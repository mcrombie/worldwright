# Distribution of Ground Flora Across Azhora

The plants catalogued in the Ibenwood ground flora surveys do not belong exclusively to the Ibenwood. Most of them exist across Azhora in forms that range from nearly identical to the forest populations to significantly adapted variants shaped by different soils, different light conditions, different rainfall patterns, and — in some cases — the simple accumulation of generations of development in isolation from the Ibenwood's particular influence. The Ibenwood is the richest expression of most of these lineages, not their only one.

What the Ibenwood appears to provide is depth: conditions so stable and so ancient that populations have had time to develop complexity that their counterparts in younger or more disturbed environments have not reached. An Ibenwood form of a plant is often larger, more chemically elaborate, more ecologically specific, and — in the cases of the anomalous species — more behaviorally unusual than the same species found elsewhere. The Caeras Valley Academy's comparative botanists describe the Ibenwood as containing the continent's oldest ecological library, and other regions as containing the same books in shorter, more recent editions.

---

## Distribution Principles

Three factors determine where a given plant will establish outside the Ibenwood:

**Moisture availability** is the primary constraint. The Ibenwood's continuous rainfall and high humidity support the full spectrum of moisture-demanding species — mosses, liverworts, ferns requiring high humidity, and the many herbaceous plants that wilt in dry conditions. Moving away from the forest and toward the arid east, these species thin progressively, replaced by drought-tolerant forms. By the time one reaches the Moroshé Desert's interior, the overlap with the Ibenwood catalogue is almost nothing.

**Canopy cover and shade** determines which species can establish in any location. The deep-shade specialists of the Ibenwood — species adapted to grow under closed canopy, including most of the achlorophyllous plants and many of the anomalous understory herbs — can only persist in forest conditions. The light-gap and edge species, adapted to variable illumination, are far more broadly distributed. Open-land species from the Ibenwood catalogue are found throughout Azhora wherever their other requirements are met.

**Soil age and continuity** is the least well-understood factor but the one that appears to explain why certain Ibenwood populations are anomalously complex compared to populations of the same species elsewhere. Very old undisturbed soils contain fungal networks, microbial communities, and chemical conditions that develop only over long periods. Plants growing in these soils have access to resources — and are subject to influences — that plants in younger soils do not encounter. This is the probable explanation for why Ibenwood forms of common plants are consistently more potent medicinally, more behaviorally unusual, and more deeply integrated into complex ecological relationships than their counterparts.

## Mittolo and the Central Heartland

The forested country of western Mittolo is the environment most similar to the Ibenwood in climate and canopy structure, and it supports the most extensive overlap with the Ibenwood flora of any region outside the forest itself. The Mittolo forests have the same basic ecological architecture — old trees, continuous canopy, rich soil, reliable moisture — and most Ibenwood species that can tolerate some degree of human disturbance are present in the Mittolo forests.

The differences are ones of degree. Mittolo forests are more heavily managed, more frequently cleared, and more regularly disturbed by human activity than the Ibenwood interior. Continuous canopy in Mittolo is centuries old at most; in the Ibenwood it may be millennia. This difference in soil age accounts for the observation, consistent across Mittoli botanical practice, that Ibenwood forms of shared species are "stronger" — a word that covers potency, flavor intensity, medicinal efficacy, and, in the case of anomalous species, the degree to which unusual behaviors manifest. An Ibenwood variant of a common herb may produce ten times the concentration of active compounds found in the Mittolo variant of the same species.

Fungal diversity in Mittolo is substantial but less than in the Ibenwood; the most demanding mycorrhizal relationships — those requiring very old, specific soil networks — are not consistently present outside the forest. Species that depend on these relationships absolutely are Ibenwood-restricted in practice even if they could theoretically establish elsewhere.

The Dark Groves of Mittolo — those patches of anomalous ancient woodland scattered through the heartland — support populations of species that are otherwise found only in the Ibenwood interior. The anomalous understory herbs, the deep-shade ferns, certain lichens that require continuously undisturbed bark: all are found in the Dark Groves at densities that confirm the Forest Mittoli assertion that the Groves are the Iben present in the heartland.

## Northern Azhora

The cold maritime north — the Bay of Lol country, the Ganoss uplands, the Orched Hills — has a climate unlike both the Ibenwood and Mittolo. It is wet throughout the year, with cold winters that regularly produce frost and occasional snow, and summers that are brief and cool. The canopy is present but more open than in Mittolo; the upland sections are often heath rather than forest.

The cold-tolerant end of the Ibenwood catalogue is well represented here: frost-tolerant ferns, winter-active mosses, cold-climate lichens, the species documented in the Ibenwood near the Oremindi margin. Many of these are present in the north at higher densities than in the Ibenwood itself, which is their southern extent rather than their center.

The heath and open upland conditions produce a flora significantly different from the forest catalogue: heathers, upland grasses, sedges adapted to cold wet conditions, moorland species. These overlap with the Ibenwood catalogue only at the cold-tolerant margins. The forest floor herbs, the deep-shade ferns, and the anomalous interior species are absent in the open uplands.

Northern Azhora's coastline supports maritime-adapted forms of many Ibenwood edge species. The Coastal Hornbeam (the one Ibenwood tree found outside the forest) reaches its northernmost extent here. Seacliff mosses and lichens in the northern bays are related to the Ibenwood's rock-colonizing species but have adapted to salt spray and wind that the forest interior never experiences.

## Pyros

West Pyros is wet, volcanically fertile, and forested in its higher sections — conditions that support a substantial forest flora. The volcanic soil produces variants of many common Ibenwood herbs and fungi with elevated mineral content and, in some cases, significantly different chemical profiles from their forest counterparts. Pyros foragers and herbalists maintain traditions distinct from both the Ibenwood forest peoples and Mittoli agricultural practice; the volcanic substrate creates unique plant forms that are neither quite Ibenwood nor quite Mittoli.

The fern flora of West Pyros is particularly rich, benefiting from the high moisture and the warm volcanic soils that remain frost-free at lower elevations through winter. Several fern species found in the Ibenwood's warmer southern sections have their largest non-Ibenwood populations in West Pyros.

East Pyros, in the rain shadow of the dividing ridge, is drier and less forested. The overlap with the Ibenwood catalogue thins sharply on the eastern side of the ridge. Edge species persist; forest interior species do not. The transitional zone along the ridge itself supports an interesting mixing layer where moisture-demanding forest species and drought-tolerant plains species occur within short distances of each other.

## The Plains

The great transitional grasslands of the continent's interior are not forest floor environments, and the majority of the Ibenwood's documented species are absent. What the Plains do support are the edge and disturbance specialists from the Ibenwood catalogue — species adapted to variable conditions, colonizers of disturbed soil, light-demanding herbs — alongside a grass and sedge flora that is specific to the open grassland conditions.

The Plains' flora is dominated by species not found in the Ibenwood catalogue at all: the tall grasses, the prairie forbs, the drought-tolerant perennials adapted to the seasonal dry periods that the forest never experiences. The Ibenwood's contribution to the Plains is primarily through the edge species that follow the forest margin as it fades into grassland, and through wind-dispersed spore-bearing plants — ferns, mosses, fungi — that establish in the wetter depressions and stream corridors cutting through the grassland.

Several of the Ibenwood's parasitic plants reach their largest non-forest populations in the Plains, exploiting the dense root systems of the grassland species that their forest relatives colonize only where grassland vegetation enters the forest margins.

## The Moroshé Desert and Arid Interior

The overlap with the Ibenwood catalogue is minimal in the desert interior. The moisture-demanding species that dominate the forest catalogue cannot establish in conditions of genuine aridity. What persists are: drought-tolerant lichens (a significant subset of the Ibenwood's lichen catalogue is present in adapted forms on the desert rock faces, where extreme temperature variation rather than extreme moisture is the defining challenge); the most resilient edge ferns in very specific seep and oasis conditions; and scattered populations of a few highly adaptable herbs in the rare wet-season windows.

The Iberos Coast, facing the Iberos Sea, has a Mediterranean climate — warm dry summers, mild wet winters — that produces a flora distinct from anything in the Ibenwood catalogue. Some of the Ibenwood's summer-dormant bulb species (including several of the early spring ephemerals) are present in coastal Mediterranean forms, their active period shifted to exploit winter rainfall rather than spring snowmelt. The coastal Iberos flora has many species that are exclusively coastal-Mediterranean and have no Ibenwood counterpart; it is the region with the lowest proportional overlap with the forest catalogue.

## Ibenale, Alezhor, and the Western Corridor

The coastal plains running along the Ibenwood's western edge — Ibenale and Alezhor — naturally carry a high density of Ibenwood edge and margin species. These are the communities living at the boundary, and their flora reflects both the open coastal conditions and the permanent proximity to the forest's influence. The ground flora of the forest edge in these regions is essentially a subset of the Ibenwood catalogue restricted to light-tolerant and disturbance-tolerant species, with the full interior flora present only where the old-growth begins.

Several species treated as common herbs throughout the western coastal trade — the Sweet-bark Shrub, the Spice Bush, Water Mint, Wild Bergamot — exist in forms traded specifically from the Ibenwood or from the forest edge of Ibenale and Alezhor, which are regarded as superior in flavor and potency to forms gathered elsewhere. The coastal trade's preference for "forest-edge" material over "mainland" material for medicinal and culinary herbs reflects the consistent difference in chemical intensity documented in comparative botanical work.

## Legemum

The Legemum peninsula — wet, cool, oceanic, without extensive old-growth forest — has a flora that shares the Ibenwood's moisture-tolerance but lacks its shade-demanding species. The open, wind-pruned conditions of the exposed western headlands support maritime-adapted mosses, lichens, and coastal cliff flora. The inland sections of Legemum, where tree cover exists, support more of the shade-tolerant fern and herb species from the Ibenwood catalogue, but the soils are younger and less continuously developed than the Ibenwood's, and the richness of fungal and mycorrhizal networks is correspondingly lower.

The boggy interior depressions of Legemum are among the best non-Ibenwood locations for the bog and wetland specialists — bladderworts, sundews, sphagnum mosses, and the sedge assemblages adapted to peat formation. The oceanic moisture regime makes Legemum's wetlands among the most extensive in Azhora outside the Ibenwood itself.

## Dinova and the Southern Peninsulas

The Mediterranean coastal strips of Dinova support species from the Ibenwood catalogue that can tolerate dry summers: the tougher orchids, drought-tolerant edge herbs, and the many bulb-forming spring ephemerals that complete their above-ground growth before summer heat begins. Several of the Ibenwood's flowering herbs are present here in forms that are smaller, earlier-flowering, and more drought-adapted than the forest populations.

The Meroshe desert interior of Dinova has essentially no overlap with the Ibenwood catalogue. The canyon soils of the canyon-south sections support seep-dependent species with affinities to Moroshé oasis flora rather than anything in the forest.

## Bouén

The Bouén peninsula's cold, grey, fog-prone climate — sustained year-round by the Deep River current — produces conditions with high moisture and low temperature that suit a specific subset of the Ibenwood catalogue. The cold-adapted mosses, liverworts, and lichens of the Ibenwood are well represented; the temperature-limited ferns are present; the flowering herbs are largely restricted to the hardiest spring ephemerals that can exploit the brief window between cold and fog.

Bouén has developed several endemic plant populations — species found only on the peninsula — that appear to be cold-climate derivatives of Ibenwood forms that colonized the peninsula in past periods and diverged in isolation. The Bouéni forms of common Ibenwood mosses are among the most extensively studied cases of rapid local adaptation, having developed features adapted to the specific salt-fog and temperature conditions of the peninsula that are not present in any inland population.

## The Oremindi Mountains

The alpine zones above the Ibenwood's upper limit support a flora distinct from the forest catalogue, primarily high-altitude specialists. The highest-elevation Ibenwood species — the Mountain-edge Fern, the Frost Needle, the Glacier Moss, the Purple Saxifrage — mark the transition zone. Above the tree line the flora shifts entirely to alpine plants: cushion-forming perennials, snow-adapted cryptogams, species adapted to extreme cold and high UV radiation that have no Ibenwood counterpart.

The Sevronese have an extensive knowledge of the Oremindi's alpine flora that they do not share systematically with outside researchers. Several plants used in Verath practice preparations are known to Academy researchers only as references in the accounts of practitioners who descended from the mountains and occasionally used unfamiliar plant names in conversation. Whether these are plants present in the lower Ibenwood catalogue under different names or genuinely alpine-only species unknown to the Academy is not established.

The meltwater streams descending from the Oremindi through the upper Ibenwood carry seeds and spores from the alpine zone into the forest's upper margins, producing transitional populations at the interface that represent ongoing contact between two distinct floras.

## On Ibenwood Forms vs. Elsewhere Forms

Academy comparative botanists use an informal distinction between what they call "Ibenwood form" and "common form" for the many species that appear in both the forest and in other Azhoran environments. The Ibenwood form is generally:

- Larger in vegetative structures
- More chemically complex and concentrated in its active compounds
- More closely integrated into its ecological community (more specific mycorrhizal partners, more specific pollinators, more specific soil requirements)
- Longer-lived as an individual
- More behaviorally unusual in the cases of species documented in the Anomalous section

The common form is generally:

- More adaptable to variable conditions
- More easily cultivated or transplanted
- Less potent medicinally or as a culinary ingredient
- Shorter-lived as an individual
- Behaviorally unremarkable

Forest Mittoli naturalists object to this framing. Their position is that there is not an "Ibenwood form" and a "common form" of a plant. There is the plant, and there is what the plant becomes when it is not in the Iben. The distinction runs the other direction from the Academy's framing: the common form is not the baseline with the Ibenwood form as an enhancement. The Ibenwood form is what the plant actually is; the common form is what remains when the conditions that make it fully itself are removed.

This disagreement about the direction of the relationship is recorded here because it has practical consequences. Forest Mittoli practitioners who have been asked to advise on cultivation of specific medicinal plants outside the Ibenwood have consistently maintained that the cultivation project is a category error — that growing the plant outside the Iben produces something that resembles the plant but is not it, in the same way that a copy of a document resembles the document without being the document. Academy horticulturalists disagree on the theory but have had consistent practical difficulty producing cultivated forms with the properties of the wild Ibenwood populations, which does not resolve the theoretical question but has slowed the cultivation program considerably.

## A Note on Anomalous Species Outside the Ibenwood

The anomalous species documented in the Ibenwood ground flora catalogue — the Counting Grass, the Listening Herb, the Direction Plant, the Memory Plant, and others — are documented only from within the Ibenwood. Whether this is because they genuinely cannot establish outside it, because they exist elsewhere but are not recognized as the same species without the Ibenwood context for identification, or because the behaviors that make them anomalous require conditions specific to the Ibenwood and are simply absent in common forms, has not been determined.

Forest Mittoli practitioners have noted, when asked, that the anomalous species are not anomalous. They are normal. The description of them as anomalous reflects the observer's framework rather than the nature of the plants. Whether this clarification is meant to reassure or to complicate is not clear from the accounts in which it appears.
