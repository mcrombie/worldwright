# Flora of the Ibenwood: A Preliminary Catalogue of Tree Species

*Compiled by the Natural History Division of the Caeras Valley Academy, drawing on field surveys, Forest Mittoli oral testimony, and the comparative botanical records maintained at Nylon and Alezhor. The word "preliminary" in this title is traditional; it has appeared in every edition of this catalogue for two hundred years. The Ibenwood has not finished introducing itself.*

The trees of the Ibenwood do not map cleanly onto the botanical categories developed for the Mittolo heartland or the Iberos coast. This catalogue uses a hybrid system: a Standard Mittoli common name, the Forest Mittoli substrate name where one has been provided (noted in *italics*), and a brief description. Forest Mittoli speakers have on several occasions reviewed partial drafts of this catalogue and offered corrections. The corrections have been incorporated. The Forest Mittoli speakers did not indicate whether further corrections remain to be offered.

---

## The Canopy Giants

The uppermost layer of the Ibenwood — trees whose crowns extend above the general canopy into open sky — is dominated by species of extreme age and scale. Many of these cannot be dated by ring-counting; the rings become too compressed past a certain point and the counting stops. What lies below the countable horizon is a matter of ongoing academic discussion.

**The Grey Vault** (*vel-thraun*) — The largest tree in the Ibenwood by most measures and perhaps on the continent. Trunks of mature specimens reach twelve to fifteen meters in diameter at the base; the bark is pale grey and deeply furrowed, almost architectural, and in old individuals the furrowing creates vertical channels that carry rainfall in defined streams. The canopy of a mature Grey Vault extends forty to sixty meters across and constitutes its own elevated ecosystem, supporting epiphytic communities of plants, mosses, insects, and birds that may spend entire generations without descending. Forest Mittoli speakers use *vel-thraun* as a term of comparison for anything considered beyond ordinary scale. "As old as a vel-thraun" means, functionally, "without a beginning anyone remembers."

**The Bloodoak** (*velnem*) — The Bloodoak is not an oak. The name was assigned by early Mittoli foresters and has persisted despite botanical consensus that it shares no family relationship with the true oaks. The bark is dark red-brown and seeps a dark crimson sap from any injury — not in quantity, but enough to color the cut surface and run slowly down the trunk. The wood is dense, the color of dried blood, and among the most sought-after timbers in the Ibenwood for the coastal cities' prestige construction; the demand is not met by the supply, and the forest peoples control access absolutely. Attempting to fell a Bloodoak without this permission is considered, in Forest Mittoli communities, an act of a severity that the Standard Mittoli legal vocabulary does not have a precise equivalent for.

**The Pale Witness** (*bethvel*) — White bark, no branches below forty meters, crown that spreads as a flat plate above the general canopy. The Pale Witness is remarkable for its stillness: in winds that move every other tree in its vicinity, the Pale Witness barely stirs. Whether this is structural — the tree's unusual mass distribution and root depth dampening oscillation — or something else is a question that botanical expeditions have consistently found themselves unable to resolve cleanly. It looks like a tree that is watching. This observation has been made independently by enough travelers to be documented in the scholarly literature, which notes it without endorsing it.

**The Ironbell** (*gorthem*) — Named for two things: the extreme hardness of its wood, which blunts tools reliably, and the seed pod, which is bell-shaped and hangs in clusters from the upper branches, and which in wind produces a sound that carries through the canopy to the forest floor as something between a tone and a percussion. The bark is grey-black and smooth, unusual in a tree of this scale. Ironbell timber is used for millstones, anvil blocks, and the load-bearing foundations of structures intended to last; it cannot be worked with ordinary tools and requires specific techniques that the Alezhor coastal cities have developed over centuries of working with what the forest provides.

**The Crowning Ash** (*shelthos*) — Not a true ash. Called the Crowning Ash for the flat, spreading crown that develops in old age, the tree flattening at its apex as though the upward growth has exhausted its ambition and spent the remaining centuries accumulating outward. Lightning strikes Crowning Ashes preferentially — whether due to crown geometry, moisture content, or the mild electrical conductivity of the bark, which has been measured and confirmed as unusual. Forest Mittoli communities track individual Crowning Ashes and maintain records of their lightning strikes going back many generations; the accumulated strike history of a specific tree is a kind of data these communities find significant, though what conclusions they draw from it have not been shared with outside researchers.

**The Deeproot** (*thranvel*) — Ordinary-looking canopy tree from above. The anomaly is below and around it: the root system extends radially for several hundred meters, and old roots emerge from the soil at the full extent of this radius as grey ridges fifteen to thirty centimeters high, arching in and out of the ground. Navigating in Deeproot territory means navigating through a subtle grid of root ridges that Forest Mittoli travelers read as positional information; outside travelers find themselves tripping. The roots do not decay in the way of normal surface roots; specimens excavated from locations where the trunk died many years ago show roots that are still intact and, by one contested report, still chemically active.

**The Smokewood** (*dorvem*) — The bark of the Smokewood sheds continuously — a constant slow desquamation of fine grey particles that in still air accumulates as a ground-level haze around the tree and in wind disperses into the canopy. A stand of Smokewoods in afternoon light looks as though it is very slowly burning without flame. The effect is visual only; the particles are not irritating and have no identified chemical activity. Scholars have been unable to establish what purpose the shedding serves. The Forest Mittoli answer, when asked, is that the Smokewood is describing itself. This answer has been recorded in five separate fieldwork accounts over two centuries, varying only in phrasing.

**The Ridgeback** (*thornvel*) — The Ridgeback's above-ground buttress roots are its distinguishing feature: flanges of root material extending from the trunk base in thin, tall sheets, sometimes three to four meters high and extending eight to ten meters outward. The spaces between buttresses are enclosed on three sides and used widely for shelter by forest travelers; Forest Mittoli communities have permanent structures built in and around specific Ridgeback root systems. The wood of the buttress roots is different in composition from the trunk wood — harder, less flexible — and has been harvested for specific purposes. The tree regrows harvested buttresses. The regrowth is slow.

**The Midnight Elm** (*velkethren*) — Named for its canopy's treatment of light. The leaves are dark green above and near-black on their undersides; a canopy of Midnight Elms absorbs light in a way that produces, on the forest floor beneath, a darkness more complete than the canopy coverage would seem to predict. Standing under a Midnight Elm at midday in a well-lit area of the forest, one experiences light conditions more consistent with an overcast evening. Botanical expeditions have measured this and found that the measured illumination values do not agree with the expected values given the canopy coverage percentage. This discrepancy is reproducible and has not been explained.

**The Stone Beech** (*gravelthos*) — The bark looks like rough-worked stone — grey, granular, entirely unlike the smooth or furrowed surfaces of other beeches. The wood is not notably heavy but floats with unusual difficulty, as though the density is distributed differently than measurement suggests. The nuts are small, hard, and sink in water; every other nut-producing tree in the Ibenwood produces nuts that float, which is the normal adaptive strategy for water dispersal. What dispersal method the Stone Beech uses instead has not been established. The nuts are edible, mealy and mild, and the forest peoples eat them in quantity. Their method of harvesting does not appear to involve water.

**The Twisted Yew** (*skelthos*) — Extremely long-lived, comparably slow-growing, and spirally twisted in a way that is consistent within populations: in a given grove, the twists all spiral in the same direction, and the direction switches between groves as though the forest alternates deliberately. The berries are bright red, decorative, and mildly toxic to visitors; Forest Mittoli communities consume them in small quantities in ceremonial contexts and report no ill effects, which suggests either adaptation, preparation, or a ceremonial dose that is genuinely trivial. The wood is almost impossible to split along the grain because the grain runs in spirals.

## The Upper Canopy

Trees that occupy the main canopy layer — the dense, continuous ceiling of the Ibenwood's interior — rather than emerging above it. These constitute the majority of the forest's biomass and the majority of its named species.

**The Listening Oak** (*vel-audren*) — Not an oak. The leaf is elongated and cupped along its length, resembling an ear, and this shape in a canopy of them produces a sound in wind that experienced travelers describe not as leaves-in-wind but as something more specific — a gathering, receiving quality, as though the canopy is orienting toward something. Whether this is the leaf geometry creating particular resonances or a projection by observers primed by the forest's reputation is not determined. The wood is unremarkable. The leaves pressed and dried are used in some Forest Mittoli document-keeping traditions.

**The Amber Pilgrim** (*goldenthos*) — A large, handsome tree with amber-yellow autumn coloration and a continuous seeping of amber resin from bark injuries and natural pores. The resin hardens slowly — a fresh flow takes several years to fully cure — and accumulates in deposits at the base of old trees. The amber produced by Ibenwood Amber Pilgrims is the source of the amber trade through Alezhor's ports, and it is notable for containing preserved insects, spiders, and small plant fragments of species not known elsewhere. Some of the inclusions are of species whose ecology is inconsistent with any environment currently present in the Ibenwood. What the Ibenwood was when those insects were alive is not established.

**The Rivercaller** (*thal-vel*) — Always found within some distance of running water — never far from a stream, creek, or reliable seepage. The root systems reach water reliably, which is not remarkable; what is remarked upon by Forest Mittoli communities is their claim that the Rivercaller is not merely finding water but contributing to its presence: that removing a Rivercaller from a drainage causes the drainage to change, the flow to shift. Expeditions investigating this have produced inconclusive results. Two cases of stream channel change following Rivercaller removal have been documented; the mechanism, if any, is unknown.

**The Hornbeam of Voices** (*vel-kormath*) — Dense, slow-growing, with a smooth grey bark and a crown of fine-branched structure unusual in trees of this height. Sounds inside a stand of Hornbeam of Voices carry differently: conversations at ordinary volume are audible at distances where they should not be; sounds from outside the stand are muffled. Forest Mittoli communities use specific Hornbeam stands as meeting spaces when transmission of sound is required; the acoustic properties are treated as a feature to be used rather than a phenomenon to be explained.

**The Widow-maker** (*korthas*) — An entirely normal-looking canopy tree with one notable property: it drops large branches without warning, without wind, sometimes in calm conditions on clear days. The drops are not random — individual trees have patterns of dropping that the Forest Mittoli communities track, the way one tracks the behavior of an individual animal. "That Korthas drops toward the north side in the third year after a cold summer" is the kind of knowledge held about specific individuals, and the knowledge is treated as reliable. Outside travelers are warned away from Korthas territory during certain seasons. The warning should be taken seriously.

**The Copper Maple** (*druventhos*) — Produces leaves that turn copper-red in autumn and do not fall. A Copper Maple in its third year of accumulation is buried under leaves to a depth that obscures its lower trunk. The leaves fall eventually — mass shed events that clear the entire tree at once, apparently triggered by structural load rather than by any biological cycle — and the floor of a Copper Maple grove in the aftermath of a shed is under three to four meters of compressed copper-red leaf matter, which takes years to decompose. The interval between sheds is not consistent between individuals. Forest Mittoli communities know when a specific tree is approaching a shed and use the timing for various purposes.

**The King Hornbeam** (*vel-shelthem*) — Enormous for its family, with a root system that produces two distinct resins: one from the male individuals and a chemically different one from the female. In close proximity — particularly in groves of mixed sex — the two resins volatilize and combine in the air, producing a vapor that is mildly disorienting. The disorientation is spatial: the affected person's ability to judge distance and direction is temporarily impaired. The effect passes within hours of leaving the grove. Forest Mittoli communities know which groves to avoid as campsites. Outside travelers who camp in King Hornbeam groves reliably wake with poor spatial memory of the night.

**The Cedar of Oaths** (*morvendreth*) — Valued for its longevity and its deeply furrowed bark, which accepts and retains objects tucked into its crevices indefinitely. Very old Cedar of Oaths specimens are encrusted with objects — small bundles, inscribed pieces of bark, carved tokens, pieces of metal — deposited over what the tree's age implies are centuries. The forest peoples use specific trees as archives of this kind; the meaning of individual deposits is known to those who made them and to community members responsible for the record. Outside scholars who have been permitted to examine these trees describe them as documents. The trees are not translated for outside scholars.

**The Dark Hornbeam** (*threnthos*) — The most common mid-canopy tree in the Ibenwood, the background species against which others are noticed. It produces no remarkable timber, no unusual fruit, no anomalous phenomenon. Its one distinguishing characteristic is that it makes no sound in wind. None. A grove of Dark Hornbeams in a storm is acoustically dead while everything around it moves and sounds. The mechanism — the leaf shape does not appear designed for silence; the branch structure is ordinary — is not known.

## The Mid-Canopy

Species occupying the middle layers of the canopy, between the great emergents above and the understory below. These are the most diverse stratum of the Ibenwood.

**The Bone Birch** (*vel-sorthren*) — Bark as white as stripped bone, continuously shedding in curling sheets that accumulate at the tree's base as a white paper litter. Bone Birch grow in rings — a mature individual is surrounded by a ring of younger individuals at a consistent radius, which are surrounded by a further ring, and so on — creating concentric circles that, viewed from above if one could achieve such a view, would look like growth rings made living. Whether the outer rings are seedlings from the center or extensions of a connected root system is contested; cutting a ring individual and the center individual in sequence has been done once by an expedition that found the two cut surfaces chemically identical despite the apparent separation.

**The Ink Tree** (*velmoreth*) — Small to mid-size, unremarkable in form, remarkable in product. The sap oxidizes on contact with air and turns black within minutes, producing one of the more consistent writing inks available in the western Azhoran region. The quality of the ink varies: summer sap is thin, prone to fading over years; autumn sap from old trees is dense, archival, and has been demonstrated on test materials to be effectively permanent — resisting water, abrasion, and light exposure past the current observational horizon of any Academy test. The oldest Forest Mittoli texts are written with Ink Tree autumn sap. They have not faded.

**The Thunderwood** (*gorvel*) — The outer wood does not burn. This has been tested thoroughly, with increasing scientific rigor over two centuries, and the results are consistent. The outer three to four centimeters of Thunderwood, when exposed to fire, chars, blackens, and insulates the interior — but does not catch and does not sustain combustion. The heartwood burns normally. The boundary between fireproof and flammable is sharp and reproducible. Forest Mittoli structural practices use Thunderwood outer cladding in contexts where fire resistance is required, and the forest edge communities of Ibenale and Alezhor pay significant prices for the material. What produces this property is not known. No constituent of the outer wood has been isolated that explains it.

**The Whisper Larch** (*sorthren-vel*) — Needle-bearing, unusual in the Ibenwood, and producing a sound in even moderate wind that sits at the upper threshold of human hearing — a thin, sustained tone that some travelers cannot hear at all and that others find intensely uncomfortable. The variation in perception is not correlated with age or ear health in any obvious way; some young people with apparently excellent hearing cannot hear it, and some older travelers with documented hearing loss find it unendurable. Forest Mittoli communities appear insensible to it in normal circumstances and offer no information about their experience of the sound.

**The Mirrored Beech** (*bethvel*) — A mid-canopy beech whose leaves have a heavy waxy coating on the upper surface that reflects light at unusual angles — not straight back, as a mirror reflects, but scattered and redirected in ways that produce shifting patches of misdirected light on the forest floor. A stand of Mirrored Beeches in afternoon sun creates a floor that seems to be lit from no consistent direction; shadows fall in contradictory orientations. Travelers moving through such stands often stop, confused about where the sun is. The effect ends immediately on leaving the stand.

**The Hollow King** (*vel-dorthren*) — Nearly all adult specimens are hollow. Not rotted hollow — the wood at the opening is sound; the hollow is a regular cylindrical space in the trunk with walls of functioning, living wood. The hollow is too geometrically regular to be explained by normal decay processes, and the inner wall surface is smooth in a way that decay does not produce. Forest Mittoli communities use specific Hollow Kings for purposes they do not explain; they maintain access to these trees and treat them as significant sites. Outside travelers who have entered unoccupied Hollow Kings report that sounds inside them do not match sounds outside — that the acoustic space of the interior is inconsistent with the exterior dimensions.

**The Pale Willow** (*vel-dreth*) — Not a willow. Called so for its hanging branches, which droop to brush the forest floor in old individuals, creating a curtained interior that is used as shelter. It grows only in specific locations, appearing suddenly in places it was not and dying in places it was, over timescales of decades. Forest Mittoli communities say it marks underground water — that its presence indicates a subsurface flow — and the correlation is sufficient for edge-community well-diggers to use Pale Willow locations as prospecting guides. Whether the tree is finding water or producing it is the question that has not been answered.

**The Weeping Thorn** (*drevel-thos*) — Thorny branches that droop, as the name suggests; a large mature specimen looks like a thorn bush with ambitions it eventually gave up. The thorns are curved backward and catch clothing and skin in ways that are not painful until one attempts to pull free, at which point they are very painful. The berries are small, dark purple, used in Forest Mittoli preparations whose purpose varies and is not consistently explained to outside visitors. The taste of the berry is, according to all available accounts, memorably unpleasant in raw form and apparently palatable prepared.

**The Spoke-tree** (*thalmoreth*) — Produces branches at regular angular intervals that are consistent across all specimens of the same age class — the same angle, the same spacing, to a precision that outside observers find unsettling in a living organism. The consistency is not approximate; it has been measured to within the precision of available instruments, and within that precision it does not vary. Whether this is a useful property, a vestige of some developmental program, or something else has not been established. Forest Mittoli speakers, asked why the Spoke-tree grows this way, have given answers that seem to be descriptive of the phenomenon rather than explanatory of it.

**The Dusk Alder** (*vel-aldren*) — Leaves that change color at sunset. Not seasonally — daily, in direct response to failing light. The leaves are a normal dark green during daylight hours and transition to a dull grey as light levels fall, beginning at the outer canopy margins and progressing inward over about an hour. The transition reverses at dawn. The mechanism appears photochemical but has not been reproduced in any laboratory setting; the compounds in the leaf surface that might be responsible change on separation from the tree. Whatever they are doing, they require being part of a living Dusk Alder to do it.

**The Canopy Fern-tree** (*thal-bethren*) — Produces large fronds rather than typical leaves; a mature specimen in full growth looks improbable, the fronds being significantly too large for a mid-canopy tree by the aesthetic conventions of the rest of the Ibenwood. It is not actually a fern — the reproductive strategy is entirely different — but the frond shape is convergently similar, and the fronds move in ways that fern fronds move rather than in ways that leaves move. Found in the wetter, lower sections of the forest, particularly where groundwater sits near the surface.

**The Smothering Ivy-tree** (*threnvel*) — A tree that grows over other trees, entirely. Unlike parasites that kill their hosts, the Ivy-tree eventually incorporates them: an old specimen that has consumed one host — grown over it, matched its form, and outlasted it — will begin incorporating the next tree in reach. The resulting organism is a tree with multiple generations of host inside it, each layer of wood identifiable by its texture and origin. Ancient Ivy-trees have been sectioned in Academy studies and found to contain four or five distinct wood types in their cross-sections, each identifiable as a different species. What the Ivy-tree experiences, having consumed multiple other trees over its lifetime, is a question that the Forest Mittoli find more tractable than scholars do.

## The Understory

Trees and large shrubs of the forest floor, growing where the canopy above allows little light. These species have solved the problem of deep shade in ways that are sometimes novel and occasionally alarming.

**The Shadow Thorn** (*kelthos*) — Grows where almost no light reaches the forest floor, which should not be possible for a plant with no obvious source of energy beyond photosynthesis. The leaves are small and dark and probably not capturing enough light to explain the tree's growth rate. The thorns curve backward and hold what they catch. The flowers open for six hours every seven years and are described by Forest Mittoli witnesses as worth waiting for: complex, pale, elaborately structured, and apparently phosphorescent in low light. The pollinator, if there is one, has not been identified.

**The Crimson Understory** (*vel-drenthel*) — Deep crimson leaves even in near-total shade, which means they are reflecting the red end of the spectrum and presumably absorbing something else, though what light source in deep understory provides usable energy in the absorbed wavelengths is not established. The Academy's botanical division has spent considerable effort on this question and produced a paper that describes the mechanism as "currently unexplained but not necessarily inexplicable," which is the closest botanical language comes to saying it has given up for now.

**The Ghost Hazel** (*sorthvel*) — White leaves with no pigmentation beyond the structural white of unpigmented cellulose. How it feeds itself is a question that the Academy has treated as identical to the Crimson Understory question and has found equally uninformative. The nuts are edible — mealy, tasteless, nutritious in the way that something can be nutritious without having any discernible flavor character. Forest Mittoli communities eat them without apparent ceremony. The trees grow in the darkest sections of the forest floor. They are easy to find in the dark because they are white.

**The One-night Flower** (*vel-threneth*) — A small understory tree, easy to overlook during most of the year, that flowers once per flowering season for a single night. The flowers are luminescent — not metaphorically, not by reflected light, but producing their own cold blue-white light, visible from a significant distance through the dark understory. A grove of One-night Flowers in bloom is described in every account that has reached the Academy as the most beautiful thing the observer has seen in the Ibenwood, and the accounts are consistent enough in this judgment to carry some weight. The following morning the flowers are gone. Attempts to preserve them for study have all failed; they collapse within hours of removal.

**The Hanging Garden** (*drevel*) — The Hanging Garden is less interesting in itself than in what it supports. Every surface that can support epiphytic growth does; large specimens carry more biomass in mosses, ferns, orchids, and other hanging plants than in their own foliage. This is partly because the bark produces a compound that appears to actively encourage root attachment from passing propagules, and partly because the branch geometry maximizes horizontal surface area for accumulation. A mature Hanging Garden is less a tree than a vertical garden platform, and the ecology of the growth it supports is its own self-contained world.

**The Amber Bush** (*goldreth*) — Small, producing a resin that never hardens. The surface of every part of the tree is perpetually tacky; insects, seeds, fragments of leaf and bark from the canopy above — all accumulate on its branches and leaves. The accumulation is managed, in some sense: the resin dissolves organic material over time, and what was trapped eventually disappears. Forest Mittoli communities use the resin for specific adhesive purposes; how they harvest it without the obvious difficulties this presents is not something that has been explained to outside observers.

**The Spine-oak** (*gorthos*) — Entirely covered in small sharp protrusions. Bark, branches, leaves — every surface. Moving through a grove of Spine-oaks without contact is technically possible and practically very difficult. Forest Mittoli communities handle the trees without apparent discomfort; the substrate vocabulary contains a word for the specific motion used, which translates approximately as "moving with the tree's grain," though "grain" is not a precise translation of the substrate root. Outside visitors who try to observe and replicate this motion experience it as insufficient.

**The Long-memory Willow** (*vel-sorth-thal*) — Another false willow; the hanging branches are the point of resemblance. Forest Mittoli communities say it remembers water: every flood level it has witnessed, every seasonal high, every drought low, recorded in its growth in ways legible to those who can read it. The Academy has examined the growth patterns of old specimens and found asymmetries and anomalies consistent with recorded flood events from the Lizeem and the Ibenwood river systems — consistent, that is, if one already knows the correspondence. Whether the correspondence is real or retrospectively fitted is precisely the kind of question that produces the most measured and careful academic disputes.

**The Drum Beech** (*gordreth-thos*) — Hollow resonant trunk in the manner of the Hollow King, but smaller and the acoustics are external rather than internal. Striking the trunk of a mature Drum Beech produces a resonance that carries through the forest floor — not through the air but through the substrate, as vibration. Forest Mittoli communities use specific large Drum Beeches as communication instruments; a practiced player can produce patterns distinguishable to other communities several kilometers away through the root-network transmission. The Academy has recorded these patterns and produced transcriptions. Reading them requires knowledge of the code, which has not been provided.

## Wetland and Riparian Species

Trees adapted to or dependent on water — rivers, standing pools, flood margins, and the swampy ground of drainage depressions.

**The River Sentinel** (*thal-goreth*) — Grows only with its roots in running water. The roots extend across the stream bottom and anchor into both banks, creating stabilized bank margins wherever the Sentinels are established; channels bordered by River Sentinels do not shift their course in floods the way unbordered channels do. Forest Mittoli river management relies on the Sentinel placement, and the siting of specific trees in specific channels is a managed practice within the communities. The trees do not colonize new channels on their own; they require planting. How the original Sentinels were established in the oldest channels is a question with no recorded answer.

**The Flood Beech** (*vel-thalren*) — Survives extended submersion without damage and marks the high-water line of past floods in its bark — irregular horizontal scars where the water surface stood, incorporated into the bark's growth record. A very old Flood Beech in the right location is a physical chronicle of floods going back as far as its age. Forest Mittoli communities read these records as historians read documents; they know which floods were significant and for what reasons. The Academy has matched Flood Beech records to Lizeem flood events documented in agricultural records and found the correspondence accurate over a five-hundred-year period where both records exist.

**The Root Bridge** (*thal-bethgoreth*) — Extends surface roots horizontally across water bodies to the opposite bank and, over decades, produces a raised root walkway above the water. A mature Root Bridge spanning three to four meters of water has an above-water root structure strong enough to carry a person. Forest Mittoli communities train them — guide the root growth to desired crossing points, prune lateral deviations, weight them in the right direction — and maintain root-bridge crossings as permanent infrastructure. The oldest and longest crossings are intergenerational projects.

**The Drowned Oak** (*vel-gorthal*) — Grows in standing water. In full submersion for most of the year, it holds its branches above the surface and produces functional canopy; the root system below creates a structure that accumulates sediment, and a very old Drowned Oak in permanent water is surrounded by a built-up mound of its own sedimentation that eventually breaks the water surface and becomes an island. The oldest known Drowned Oak stands are on islands of their own making.

**The Mist-maker** (*dorthren-vel*) — Continuous transpiration exceeding any measurement of what the leaf surface area should support. Stands produce persistent low-lying mist regardless of weather conditions; walking into a Mist-maker grove on a clear afternoon is walking into fog. Forest Mittoli communities use this for specific purposes; the groves serve as concealed spaces regardless of weather, and their locations — which do not change, since the trees are fixed — are known and used accordingly.

**The Mud Cedar** (*gorventhal*) — Grows in waterlogged ground where other trees cannot establish roots. The root system creates a raised platform of interlocked roots above the mud surface, and in dense stands the platforms connect into walkable ground above what would otherwise be impassable swamp. Mud Cedar root platforms are among the more important pieces of infrastructure in the wetland portions of the Ibenwood, and their management — which trees to promote, which to thin — is part of the forest peoples' territorial knowledge.

**The Reed-tree** (*thal-vel-dreth*) — Looks like a tree. Functions like a reed in one specific way: the trunk is hollow and open at specific points, and wind through the hollow produces a tone that carries, in still conditions, through several kilometers of forest. The tone is not loud; it is simply persistent and specifically pitched and does not decay with distance the way sounds should. Forest Mittoli communities use Reed-tree proximity as orientation when traveling — the sound tells you where the water is, which is the most important orientation information in the Ibenwood's wetter sections.

**The Long-fall** (*skorthal-thos-vel*) — Produces seeds that fall unusually slowly. In still air, a seed released from the canopy of a Long-fall takes several minutes to reach the forest floor, drifting rather than falling. The seed is not obviously shaped for this — its morphology does not show the wing, plume, or balloon structures that would explain slow descent aerodynamically. Examined seeds fall normally in laboratory conditions. The slow falling appears to require the living tree's chemistry or proximity. The seed dispersal pattern this produces is correspondingly unusual.

**The Weeping Cedar** (*drevel-goreth*) — Branches that droop to the water's surface in riparian specimens. In still-water locations, the branches create curtained chambers between the drooping tips and the water, enclosed on the land side by the trunk and on the water sides by the branches. These chambers are completely private from outside observation. Forest Mittoli communities use specific Weeping Cedars for practices they describe as requiring privacy, and describe the tree as providing the privacy. Visiting scholars who have been permitted inside these chambers report the interior as having a quality of enclosure that seems disproportionate to the physical barrier the branches represent.

## Edge and Transitional Species

Trees adapted to the margins — the transition between open land and deep forest, the ridgelines, the Ibenwood's periphery where the forest meets what is not forest.

**The Gate-tree** (*vel-kelthos*) — The commonest tree at the boundary between Ibenwood and open country, frequently the last large tree before the open. Forest Mittoli communities treat specific Gate-trees as threshold markers — not symbolic thresholds but functional ones, sites where entering the forest requires acknowledgment of the transition. Outside travelers who arrive at the Ibenwood edge and are told by a guide to "greet the Gate-tree" before entering tend to find this instruction faintly absurd, and Forest Mittoli guides know they find it absurd, and include it anyway.

**The Thorn Crown** (*gorkel-thos*) — Dense, thorny, head-height, growing in tangles at exposed margins. The Thorn Crown is not a tree in any graceful sense; it is a defense. Forest Mittoli communities manage it as a boundary marker, maintaining dense growth where they want the margin to resist entry and removing it where they want to encourage it. The thorns are serious enough that off-path entry into the Ibenwood in areas of Thorn Crown management is not simply inconvenient; it is a useful signal that one is attempting to enter somewhere one was not intended to enter.

**The Coastal Hornbeam** (*bethren-thal*) — The only Ibenwood tree that is found outside the forest. It grows on the sea cliffs above Alezhor and in exposed headland positions along the Ibenale coast, where it is tolerant of salt air and severe wind in ways that no other Ibenwood species manages. Whether the Coastal Hornbeam colonized the cliffs from the forest or represents a coastal-adapted form of something that entered the forest from the coast is a question that botanical phylogeny has not resolved.

**The Sun-seeker** (*vel-bethren-thos*) — Grows in clearings and light gaps with the fastest rate of height-gain of any large tree on the continent. A Sun-seeker in a cleared area reaches canopy height in a time that, when first reported, the Academy's botanical division considered an error in the records. The figure is reproducible. The tree disappears from the record once the canopy closes over it again, and whether this represents death, dormancy, or transformation into a canopy species that resembles something else has not been determined.

**The Boundary Ash** (*vel-sorthos*) — Old specimens grow preferentially along negotiated boundaries — the lines between different forest peoples' territories, the edges of areas under specific agreements, the margins of places with specific designations. Whether the tree has a preference for disturbed soil along paths (a mundane explanation) or genuinely marks what the forest peoples say it marks (a less mundane one) is a question that cannot be resolved without independent knowledge of where the actual boundaries are. Forest Mittoli communities will confirm the correspondence but will not provide the boundary locations for independent verification.

**The False Oak** (*thalven-thos*) — Appears to be an oak by every visible characteristic: bark texture, leaf shape, branch structure, what looks like acorns. It is not related to oaks by any analysis. The wood is different; the internal anatomy is different; the chemistry is different; the root structure is different. What it is related to is, after considerable effort, still not fully established. The Forest Mittoli name for it translates as something like "the tree that wears another face," which may be descriptive and may be something else.

## Conifers and Needle-bearing Species

The Ibenwood's needle-bearing trees are relatively few compared to the broadleaves, concentrated in the higher elevations near the Oremindi foothills and in certain soil conditions that favor them.

**The Iron Needle** (*gorvel-dreth*) — Produces needles stiff enough to pierce skin, which they do if handled carelessly. The sound in wind is high and sharp, quite different from the soft rustle of normal needle-bearing trees. The cones take seven years to open, and the specific trigger for opening has not been identified; the cones do not respond to heat or moisture in the ways that most opening mechanisms use. Forest Mittoli communities collect Iron Needle cones and use them as mechanical components in specific applications because the opening mechanism, whatever it is, can be triggered consistently by methods the communities use and has not shared.

**The Sleeping Fir** (*vel-gorthas*) — Dormant in summer, active in winter. This is the complete reversal of normal seasonal behavior, and the tree achieves it by having — uniquely — photosynthetic structures that function in the lower light intensities of winter and shut down in high summer light. The needles in winter are deep green and functional; in summer the tree looks like standing dead timber, and summer expeditions that have attempted to fell what appeared to be dead specimens have found live wood beneath the grey surface.

**The Black Pine** (*skelthal-vel*) — Bark so dark it appears black in diffuse light; it lightens to very dark grey in direct sun, and this change in appearance has caused some records to list the Black Pine as two species until specimens were found showing both appearances on the same trunk at the sun-shade boundary. The pitch is of specific consistency, used in waterproofing and sealing applications in forest edge communities. The smell of Black Pine pitch is described as the smell of the deeper Ibenwood distilled; travelers who know the Ibenwood's interior find the smell of Black Pine in lowland use disorienting in the specific way of unexpectedly encountering something that belongs elsewhere.

**The Ancient Cedar** (*gormoreth*) — Growing slowly enough that a trunk three meters in diameter may represent eight thousand or more years of growth — beyond the Ibenwood's general difficulty with dating and into a range where no known method is reliable. The oldest known Ancient Cedars are not identified to outside researchers; their locations are known within the Forest Mittoli communities and are considered significant in ways that do not translate cleanly. The Academy has identified several probable Ancient Cedars from second-hand descriptions and has been politely not confirmed on any of them.

**The Frost Needle** (*bethren-dreth*) — Grows near the upper elevation limit of the Ibenwood, in the foothills approaching the Oremindi. The needles turn silver in cold conditions — not frost-coated, but silver, a change in the needle surface itself that is temperature-triggered and reverses when conditions warm. In autumn, when the first cold comes down from the mountains, stands of Frost Needle visible from distance turn silver within hours. Forest Mittoli communities time certain activities by this change, which is a visible and reliable temperature indicator.

**The Ground Fir** (*thal-gorthas*) — Reaches a maximum height of three meters. Stays low, forms dense impenetrable canopies near the soil surface in areas of poor drainage and poor soil. The interior of a Ground Fir thicket is navigable only by something the size of a fox. What lives in them is known primarily from Forest Mittoli accounts; outside naturalists have not been able to observe the interior fauna in any systematic way.

**The Candle Tree** (*vel-bethgoreth*) — Grows in a perfect column; the lateral branches are minimal, the growth is vertical, and a stand of Candle Trees looks like a grove of columns in a building that forgot to put a roof on. The straightness is used practically — Candle Tree trunks are among the most reliably straight structural members available in the Ibenwood, and the forest edge communities use them for scaffolding, mast timber, and roof beams in construction where straight length is the primary requirement.

**The Resin Fir** (*vel-dorthal*) — The entire tree looks wet. Resin production is constant and heavy enough that the bark surface is never dry; touch a Resin Fir and your hand comes away sticky. The resin volatilizes in warm conditions and the smell in a summer stand is intense — not unpleasant, but very present. In dry conditions, stands of Resin Fir are managed carefully by forest peoples; the accumulated resin on the ground and lower trunk creates a fire risk that would be catastrophic if it ignited, and the management practices keeping it from igniting are part of the forest peoples' territorial maintenance.

## Flowering and Fruit-bearing Species

Trees that provide primary food sources for the Ibenwood's animal and human inhabitants, and trees whose flowering is notable in its own right.

**The Thousand-flower** (*vel-drenvel*) — When a Thousand-flower blooms, the ground beneath it disappears under white. The flowering periods are irregular, not following the seasonal calendar in any consistent way; Forest Mittoli communities can predict them weeks in advance by signs that have not been communicated to outside researchers. The flowers last approximately four days before falling, and the fallen carpet of petals on the forest floor is thick enough to muffle footsteps. Attempts to predict the flowering from weather patterns, growth cycles, or any other observable variable have produced no reliable model.

**The Blood-plum** (*gordreth-vel*) — The fruit is deep red to near-black when ripe, sweet past what "sweet" in ordinary usage means — a sweetness that is described by everyone who has tasted it as unlike any other food experience in their vocabulary. Outside travelers who eat Blood-plum fruit in season do not describe it as simply delicious; they describe it as memorable in a way that affects their relationship with other fruits permanently. The tree produces heavily in good years and negligibly in poor years, and the poor years follow no discernible pattern; a fruitful season is celebrated in the forest communities and a poor season is noted with concern.

**The Honey Tree** (*vel-thren-goreth*) — The flowers are unremarkable individually. Collectively, a flowering Honey Tree is surrounded by more pollinating insects than should be present given the available local population; they arrive from a distance, and the Ibenwood's insect community exhibits behavior around flowering Honey Trees that has not been fully explained by the flowers' nectar chemistry alone. The honey produced from Honey Tree-foraged material is the most valuable honey on the western Azhoran coast, and the Alezhor city-states' relationship with the forest peoples includes specific provisions about Honey Tree access that are among the more carefully maintained terms.

**The Lantern-fruit** (*bethren-vel-dreth*) — The fruit is luminescent at night when ripe, producing a pale greenish-white glow that is visible in the dark understory from a considerable distance. Groves produce enough light in full fruit to read by if one is close enough. Forest Mittoli communities use the fruiting season for nighttime activities that are otherwise impractical; the light is reliable, free, and distributed through the forest rather than concentrated in one place. The light stops the moment the fruit is picked.

**The Seven-season Pear** (*vel-dorthen*) — Fruits on its own schedule, which does not correspond to the annual calendar; individual trees have cycles running from three to twelve years between heavy fruiting, and the cycles do not synchronize across trees. Forest Mittoli communities track individual trees the way one tracks individual animals, knowing each tree's pattern. A community that knows its Seven-season Pears knows, years in advance, which years will be abundant and can plan around them. This knowledge is not written down and does not transfer to outside parties who would need to learn each tree individually.

**The Cherry of Long Night** (*vel-skorthos*) — Flowers in the shortest days of deep winter, when the Ibenwood floor is cold and the canopy is bare or skeletal. The white flowers against the bare branches in deep winter are striking enough that Forest Mittoli communities schedule specific activities around witnessing them. The fruit ripens in late winter, the only sweet fruit available in the forest's hardest months; the Cherry of Long Night is not merely a food source but a timing marker — its fruiting says the hard part is almost over.

**The Storm-fruit** (*gorventhos*) — Fruit that ripens only in the week following a significant storm — a storm above a certain threshold of wind and rain, consistently and reproducibly, not following the season or the tree's own cycle. The mechanism is not established; the fruit does not complete ripening during calm periods even if it appears externally ready. Forest Mittoli communities harvest the week after storms rather than watching the tree's appearance. The fruit itself is tart, hard-skinned, and durable — it keeps longer than most soft forest fruit, which may explain why the production strategy, unusual as it is, serves the tree's purposes.

**The Nut-spire** (*gorvel-thos*) — Pyramidal form, dense, producing nuts in quantities that create seasonal abundance for every nut-eating animal in its range. Good nut years support high populations of squirrels, certain birds, wild pigs, and deer; poor nut years — which happen, are not predictable, and are called "nut failures" in Forest Mittoli — cascade through the animal population in ways that affect predator numbers two seasons later. Forest Mittoli communities predict nut failures by observing specific early-season indicators and adjust their own food planning accordingly; the accuracy of these predictions is one of the practical demonstrations of the forest peoples' observational knowledge.

## Anomalous Species

Trees that do not behave as trees are expected to behave. The Ibenwood has more of these than any other studied forest on the continent. The Academy's position on whether this represents a genuine concentration of anomalous phenomena or a cultural effect — the Ibenwood's reputation causing observers to notice and report things they would dismiss elsewhere — is that both factors are probably present and their relative contributions cannot currently be quantified.

**The Compass Tree** (*bethren-goreth*) — Grows asymmetrically, with the majority of its canopy mass shifted in one consistent direction. The direction is not magnetic north; it is not solar direction; it is not toward the nearest large water body. It is consistent across all Compass Tree individuals within a given area, which means it is pointing at something, and the thing it is pointing at appears to be the same thing for every tree. What that thing is has not been determined. Forest Mittoli communities navigate by Compass Trees in deep interior conditions where other methods fail; the direction they are pointing is navigable to, from Forest Mittoli accounts, and leads somewhere that outside researchers have not been taken.

**The Speaking Tree** (*vel-threnthal*) — The Forest Mittoli name is unambiguous: this tree speaks. Outside observers hear sounds in the absence of wind, sounds that vary with time of day and season and circumstances around the tree. The sounds are not random; they have pattern and, to Forest Mittoli communities, meaning. Outside observers can confirm pattern but cannot access meaning. Forest Mittoli communities respond to Speaking Tree sounds in conversation. The responses appear to be in response to something specific. The conversation is not translated.

**The Counter-tree** (*sorthven-vel*) — Grows only in pairs. If one dies, the other dies within the same season, regardless of its own condition. A healthy Counter-tree whose partner has been removed will die on a schedule determined by nothing except the partner's death. This has been tested, once, by an expedition that felled one of a known pair and then monitored the survivor. The monitoring had the predicted result. The expedition's report expresses regret about the methodology.

**The Memory Cedar** (*morventhal*) — The bark shows patterns that are not growth rings: horizontal compressions, surface variations, structural changes laid down in a sequence going back through the tree's life. The patterns are consistent across individuals in ways that seem to record external events — flood years, drought years, the passing seasons — but also include patterns that do not correspond to any known climate or environmental record, that are consistent across trees too distant to have shared environmental experience in any normal sense. What these patterns are recording has not been established. Forest Mittoli communities who were shown comparative diagrams of the patterns from multiple trees stated that the trees were recording the same things, and declined to elaborate on what those things were.

**The Walking Tree** (*vel-thal-gordreth*) — Individual trees change position over decades. The movement is slow — a matter of meters per generation — and is accomplished through differential root growth, the tree advancing in the direction its newest root growth expands while older roots in the opposite direction die back. Forest Mittoli communities track specific individuals the way one tracks the seasonal movement of animals. Whether the direction of movement is random or purposive has not been established. Forest Mittoli communities consider this question already answered and do not find the Academy's uncertainty on the point confusing.

**The Ash of Ends** (*skorthen-vel*) — Grows where things ended: old clearings, the sites of significant deaths, abandoned structures, the cores of former settlements. It is a reliable indicator of human history in locations where the surface evidence has been entirely reclaimed by the forest — an Ash of Ends where no other evidence of habitation exists means a habitation existed, and Forest Mittoli communities use them as history markers. The Academy's archaeological program has dug under Ash of Ends locations at several sites and found evidence of prior occupation in every case that was excavated to sufficient depth.

**The Grief Tree** (*skorthal-vel-dreth*) — Produces a vapor when the temperature drops below a certain threshold, which in the Ibenwood's climate means the vapor is released in autumn evenings and early mornings for much of the year. The vapor causes an emotional response in humans that is not hallucinogenic — there is no disorientation, no visual distortion, no alteration of perception. It is specifically and only emotional. The emotion is described differently by different observers but shares consistent qualities: something between grief and recognition, a sense of arriving at something one has been avoiding, a quality of being seen by something that has been watching for a long time. Forest Mittoli communities manage the groves with care and use them for specific purposes. Outside travelers are warned that Grief Tree valleys should not be camped in without knowing what they are.

**The Year-ring False-oak** (*vel-thalgoreth*) — Produces false rings — additional ring boundaries within a single growing season — that make its timber appear, to dendrochronology, to be centuries older than it is. A Year-ring False-oak that is fifty years old will show growth rings consistent with five hundred years. This makes any dendrochronological survey of the Ibenwood unreliable unless the surveyor knows which specimens are Year-ring False-oaks, which requires knowing where they are, which requires Forest Mittoli guidance. The Academy's dating results for pre-Compact Ibenwood structures include an error bar for possible Year-ring contamination that makes several key dates effectively non-deterministic.

**The Pale Listener** (*vel-drethren*) — White bark, very still, and appearing to lean toward sound. Whether this is a real orientation response — some mechanism by which the tree's growth tracks toward sound sources — or an illusion produced by the Ibenwood's reputation, every observer records it. The tree does not demonstrably lean toward sound in controlled conditions; it demonstrably appears to in field conditions. Forest Mittoli communities speak quietly near Pale Listeners as a matter of course and explain this simply: the tree is listening, and it is courteous to speak clearly.

**The Hollow Oracle** (*vel-gorthal-dreth*) — Like the Hollow King in its hollow trunk but smaller and found in areas the forest peoples consider significant rather than distributed through the canopy. Forest Mittoli practitioners perform specific practices in the interior of Hollow Oracles; outside observers have been present during these practices and have produced records. The records are consistent with each other in describing sounds, postures, and durations; they are inconsistent in describing what happened as a result. Each observer describes a result that is personal to them. Whether this indicates a subjective phenomenon, a personal phenomenon, or a phenomenon that is genuinely different for each observer is not determined.

**The Marked Tree** (*vel-thren-skorthos*) — Bark that develops patterns over the tree's lifetime that are, per Forest Mittoli community consensus, individual to each tree. The patterns are described by community members as the tree's name. Not its species name — its individual name, the way a person has a name. The Academy does not have a position on what this means about the Marked Tree's inner life, as this falls outside its usual scope, but it has noted the claim in several field reports and added it to the unresolved observations file that has been growing for two hundred years.

**The Salt Root** (*bethren-thal-vel*) — Grows only where mineral salt deposits occur in the substrate. This makes it a reliable surface indicator of subsurface geology, and specifically of the minerological conditions associated with the gold-bearing substrate of the Alezhor forest hills. The Alezhor mining tradition uses Salt Root distribution as a prospecting guide; this correlation is part of the knowledge held by the forest peoples and their coastal city partners and not shared with outside commercial interests. The correlation exists and is reliable. How the tree detects the salt at depth, and why it requires it, is not known.

**The Witness Tree** (*morvendreth-vel*) — Long-lived, slow-growing, and in Forest Mittoli community understanding, recording. It grows near human activity — not seeking it, but remaining near it — and its asymmetric growth over decades incorporates the history of what has happened near it. Forest Mittoli readers can interpret the asymmetries as a record of events at specific sites: who was here, what happened, when. The Academy has verified that Witness Tree records at documented historical sites are consistent with the documentation, over the range of verified historical records (approximately five hundred years for most sites, longer for a few). What the Witness Trees have recorded at sites beyond the documentary record is accessible through forest peoples' reading of the trees. The readings have not been translated.

## A Note on Completeness

*The foregoing catalogue represents the species for which sufficient outside documentation exists to produce an entry. Forest Mittoli oral traditions reference additional species for which no outside specimen or survey exists, and species known from the deep interior that have not been observed by any outside naturalist in conditions of reliable identification. The catalogue is, as noted in the title, preliminary. Scholars who have worked longest on the Ibenwood tend to conclude that the preliminary edition will remain the only edition — not because the forest has not yielded information but because it appears to yield it at approximately the same rate it introduces new questions, and the ratio has been consistent for two hundred years. The Academy thanks the forest peoples for their cooperation in the portions of this work where cooperation was offered, notes the portions where it was not, and continues its work.*
