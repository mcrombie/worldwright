# Alezhor

Northwest of the Dinova peninsula, where the Mediterranean character of the southwestern coast begins its slow negotiation with the cooler and wetter climate of the regions above, lies the coastal strip called Alezhor. It is not a wide territory. The great forest that covers much of southwestern Azhora's interior begins within a day's walk of the shore in most places, and the land between the sea and the tree line is all Alezhor has. What it has done with that strip is build some of the most prosperous cities on the western coast of the continent, on the strength of a resource that comes not from the sea or from cultivation but from the forest rivers that flow through it on their way to the ocean.

Alezhor has gold.

---

## Geography

The region runs roughly northwest along the western coast, a coastal plain that is narrow in the south — where the forest presses close to the cliffs — and widens slightly in the center where the largest rivers emerge from the forest hills and deposit their loads of gravel and sediment into broad estuaries before entering the sea. These river mouths are where the cities are. They are where they have always been, because the rivers are both the source of the gold and the reason the gold can be worked and traded.

The sea to the west is open ocean. There are no island chains here to shelter the coast, no Iberos network of ports and archipelagos to provide navigational landmarks; Alezhor faces the open water directly, and its sailors work in conditions more demanding than those of the sheltered Iberos side. The harbors are naturally good where the rivers meet the sea — the estuaries provide depth, and the river outflow suppresses the worst of the ocean swell — but vessels leaving Alezhor's ports enter open water immediately and must be built for it.

The forest begins where the coastal plain ends, and in the south it begins almost immediately. The old-growth that makes up the bulk of southwestern Azhora's interior is ancient in the way that the western Mittoli forests are ancient — trees that predate any record, canopy that has been continuous for longer than the settlements beneath it — but here at the forest's coastal edge there is a particular quality to the margin. The light changes within twenty feet of the tree line. Travelers describe entering the southwest forest from Alezhor's coast as crossing into a different acoustic world: the sound of the sea cuts off, replaced by something that is not silence but is much closer to it than the coast.

The climate is Mediterranean, cooler than Dinova's — the summers are warm and dry, the winters mild and moderately wet, the light good — but the cooling is perceptible and the transition is under way. By the time the region's northern edge is reached, where the coast bends and begins to approach Legemum's territory, the summer warmth is already unreliable and the grey wet season is beginning to assert itself for longer portions of the year.

## The Gold

The rivers of Alezhor carry gold down from the forest hills. Not all of them, not in equal quantities, and not in a way that is obvious until you know what you are looking at — but the gravel beds of the river channels contain it in concentrations that have made the people who learned to find it, and then to mine it from the source seams in the forest hills, among the wealthiest in the region.

The gold arrives in two forms. Placer gold — small grains and flakes carried by the current and deposited wherever the water slows — accumulates in the bends of the rivers and in the gravel bars of the estuaries. This is the gold that the rivers give freely, requiring no excavation beyond wading and panning, and it is the gold that first drew permanent settlement to the river-mouths. The second form is the vein gold in the forested hills above — seams running through the rock of the hillsides where the rivers cut their channels — which requires mining in the old-growth and which is more productive, more dangerous, and more politically significant. The placer gold can be worked by anyone with a pan and patience; the vein gold requires an operation, investment, and the goodwill or controlled cooperation of the forest.

The vein gold is why Alezhor's relationship with the forest is what it is. The mines are in the hills, inside the tree line, and reaching them requires entering territory that is not quite negotiable in the way that other territories are. The forest peoples — the communities that have lived in the old-growth for longer than any record extends, whose language contains words for forest phenomena that Mittoli simply did not have before contact — know where the seams are, know which passages through the old-growth reach them efficiently, and have an established understanding with the coastal cities about what the mining means for the forest and what it costs.

This is not an understanding that was easily reached. The older records in the coastal cities reference a period of sharper conflict, before whatever arrangement now holds was established. The details of the current arrangement are not written down in any document that outside scholars have been shown, though outside scholars have asked. What is observable is the result: the mines operate, the forest around them is managed in ways that indicate long-term planning rather than extraction, and the forest peoples and coastal cities maintain a relationship of sufficient stability that neither side has found it useful to describe it publicly. Both parties, when asked about it separately, say that the other has been reasonable. They do not say more.

## The Cities

Alezhor has no single dominant city and has not had one in any documented period. The geography does not favor it — the coastal strip's resources are distributed along the river-mouths rather than concentrated at any single point, and no river is so much larger than the others that its city could naturally dominate the rest. What Alezhor has instead is a series of coastal cities, each controlling the territory from the sea to some negotiated point in the forest hills behind it, each wealthy from its own gold trade, and each conducting its affairs with a combination of commercial ambition and defensive caution that comes from knowing that everything desirable about your position is also everything another power might want to have.

The cities are not at war with each other in any sustained sense, but they are not at peace in any complacent sense either. They share the coast, they share the trade routes, and they share the practical knowledge that a city foolish enough to weaken itself by fighting its neighbor is a city that will find a third party — from outside the region — arriving to take advantage of the result. This understanding functions as a deterrent. The cities compete commercially and diplomatically rather than militarily, and the commercial and diplomatic competition is thorough enough that it has required no supplement.

The goldwork produced in the Alezhor cities is remarkable. The craftsmen here have had access to consistent gold supply for long enough to develop techniques that other regions, working with more intermittent material, have not. Alezhor gold objects — jewelry, ceremonial vessels, the decorated panels on important civic buildings — are identifiable by style across the Azhoran trade network: a particular approach to granulation and to the treatment of fine wire that represents several generations of accumulated craft knowledge. The objects are expensive. Buyers who can afford them buy them. Buyers who cannot afford them know what they look like, which also serves a purpose.

## Gold and Politics

Wealth in gold has specific political consequences that wealth in tin, copper, or agricultural produce does not. Gold is portable. It can be moved, hidden, and transferred without the elaborate infrastructure that grain requires or the industrial apparatus that metal ore requires. An Alezhor city that is threatened does not need to defend its granaries or protect its foundries — it needs to move what matters most. This has, over the long history of the region, produced a political culture that is simultaneously very wealthy and very careful about displays of dependence.

The cities of Alezhor have been targets. Marosh, from its position to the south, has incorporated parts of the southern Alezhor coast into its sphere at various points in its history, and the evidence is in the place names and architectural influence of the southernmost cities, which show Maroshi elements alongside the local style. The Mittoli interior powers have made overtures. The trading cities of the Iberos coast have funded expeditions and established residencies and attempted to build the kind of commercial dependency that eventually becomes political dependency. None of these have produced stable control of the whole region.

The most common explanation, in outside records, is the forest. Any power controlling Alezhor must also manage the forest relationship, and the forest relationship has consistently proven non-transferable. The arrangement the coastal cities have with the forest communities is specific to those cities — it involves knowledge, precedent, and personal relationships that did not pass to outside administrators when outside administrators were in nominal control. Conquerors who held the coastal strip found that the mines continued to produce only as long as they left the forest-edge arrangements undisturbed, and that disturbing them, either through pressure on the forest communities or through interference with the cities' traditional management role, produced results that were expensive to reverse.

The current political configuration is the default: independent cities, competitive but stable, with Maroshi influence strongest in the south and fading as the coast runs northwest toward Legemum's territory.

## Language

The language of Alezhor belongs to the Forest Mittoli cluster — a variety of the western language family that has been shaped by long contact with the old-growth's eastern edge. It shares the standard Mittoli grammar but contains the layer of substrate vocabulary that Forest Mittoli everywhere carries: words for specific forest conditions, for the quality of light in old-growth canopy, for the felt presence of things unseen, for phenomena that the inland Mittoli heartland never needed to name because the phenomena were not there.

In Alezhor, this vocabulary has an additional economic register. There are Forest Mittoli words used in the coastal cities that refer specifically to conditions in the gold-bearing forest hills — terms for the behavior of river gravel that indicates a productive deposit, for the specific acoustic quality of certain hillside passages that experienced miners learn to use as navigation, for the condition of forest growth over a worked seam versus an unworked one. Whether these words are original to the substrate or were developed later, within the mining tradition, is not established. They are not shared freely with outside visitors.

The cities' commercial class speaks a more standard Mittoli for trade purposes, with absorbed vocabulary from Maroshi in the south and from the Iberos coastal languages in the ports. The names of the cities themselves are in a form that shows Forest Mittoli phonology: the dropped unstressed vowels, the initial-position clusters, the substrate influence that makes certain words feel as though they were originally pronounced in a slightly different mouth.
