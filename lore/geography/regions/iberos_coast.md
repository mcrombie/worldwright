# Iberos Coast

The Iberos Coast is the eastern face of Azhora — the long stretch of coastline where the continent meets the Iberos Sea, and where the Mediterranean climate makes the land bloom despite being adjacent to or technically part of the arid eastern interior. It is a region defined by the sea at least as much as by the land: the Iberos Sea is shallow, warm, intensely blue, and strewn with islands large and small in a way that has made seafaring the natural vocation of anyone born along its shores.

## Geography

The coast itself is varied. In the north, where the green Mittoli country still has some moisture, it is rocky and forested near the water, with good natural harbors in the fjord-like inlets. Moving south, the land dries progressively, the hills become pale and terraced with dry-farmed olives and vines, and the coast becomes more cliff-faced, more dramatic, the harbors smaller and more worked-at.

The Iberos Sea is the coast's defining feature. It is not a true ocean but rather a semi-enclosed body of water, bounded by the Azhoran coast to the west, the island chains to the east, and open to wider ocean only at its north and south ends. This enclosure keeps it sheltered, warm, and navigable in ways that the open western sea is not. Ships on the Iberos Sea are almost always within sight of land or island. This made maritime culture develop here earlier and more easily than anywhere else on the continent.

## The Islands

The Iberos Sea contains hundreds of islands. Most are too small for permanent habitation but serve as waypoints, seasonal fishing grounds, and the sites of the lighthouses that the coastal cities maintain at significant expense. The inhabited islands range from small fishing communities of a few hundred people to the substantial populations of the larger islands, which have their own governments, their own histories, and in several cases their own distinct languages.

**Pebbles** — the cluster of small pale islands visible off the northeastern coast — is named for what they look like from a distance: stones scattered on the water. They are inhabited only seasonally, by fishermen who chase the migratory shoals that pass through the channel between them and the mainland. The fishing there is exceptional, the living conditions are not.

**Suval** — the mainland name for the Svaleen Peninsula, the largest southeastern extension of Azhora — appears as an island on maps made by cartographers who have never visited, because the neck of land connecting it to the main continent is narrow enough, and mountainous enough, that it is usually approached by sea. It is not an island. It is home to a loose confederation of city-states — Solis, Enebreum, Imlamdris — each with its own character and its own long history of doing business with everyone while taking direction from no one. The Elodi city of Elod is at the peninsula's southern tip. See the Svaleen entry for detail.

Beyond Suval, the map becomes uncertain. What Azhoran sailors call the **Outer Islands** are known only from reports, and the reports are inconsistent enough to be unreliable. Whether Canon — the landmass sometimes reported on the far eastern horizon — is a large island or a continent is not established.

## History

The Iberos Coast has a history as layered as the Mediterranean climates produce everywhere: long periods of cosmopolitan trade, punctuated by conflict over the chokepoints — the harbors, the straits, the island lanes — that control who can move goods and who can tax them.

The coastal cities have never been unified under a single authority for long. The sea that connects them also gives each city the ability to trade independently, ally selectively, and withdraw into maritime isolation when land-based powers become inconvenient. The Mittoli heartland has periodically attempted to extend formal control over the coast and has consistently found the exercise more expensive than anticipated — the cities are easy to threaten and difficult to hold, and their populations have a habit of simply loading their most important assets onto ships when military situations become unfavorable.

The most significant historical period of Iberos coastal civilization is called the First Island Age — a period roughly 1,200 to 1,800 years ago when the island communities were more powerful, more organized, and more outward-looking than they are now. Ruins on several of the larger islands suggest settlements of considerable size and sophistication from this period. What ended it is disputed; a combination of climatic shift, disease, and a series of conflicts over the outer island trade routes is the current scholarly consensus, but the coastal oral traditions speak of something more specific — an event, or a decision, that several communities made simultaneously and do not describe directly.

**Nyross** (also Nyrous, Nirosh in some island dialects) is the largest and most important city on the northern Iberos coast. It sits in a perfect natural harbor with a commanding view of the sea lanes, and has been continuously inhabited and continuously important for longer than most cities on the continent. Its political complexity is legendary — Nyross has a layered system of councils, hereditary offices, guild authorities, and maritime courts that has accumulated over centuries and which no one fully understands, including the Nyrossi themselves. It works, mostly, through a process of negotiation that resembles organized chaos until the moment it produces a decision.

**Zoth** is smaller and older in character — an island city technically, sitting on a low island barely separated from the mainland by a navigable channel. It has the quality of a place that has been at the edge of things for a long time and has come to prefer it. Zoth's primary trade is information — it sits at the point where news from the outer islands, the Moroshé coast, and the Mittoli interior all converge, and its merchant class has historically done very well by knowing things before other people do.

## Language

The Iberos coastal dialect is related to Mittoli but has absorbed enormous quantities of island vocabulary over the centuries — words for sea conditions, fish species, and wind patterns for which standard Mittoli has no equivalents, and commercial and legal terms from the island trading culture that have no mainland origins. The result is a language that mainlanders find poetic when they can follow it and baffling when they cannot.

The island languages are more varied. Some are clearly descended from the coastal dialect. Others are not, and their origins remain a source of active linguistic investigation.
