# Endevor

Northwest of the Acorwood, where the Oremindi's northern terminus descends into lower ground and the air begins to carry the cold salt weight of the bay, the hills of Endevor fill the space between things. Not quite the upland — the Ganoss plateau is harder and higher, to the north. Not quite the coast — the Bay of Lol is a day's descent to the northwest. Not quite the mountain country, though the Oremindi makes the southern skyline and certain clear mornings let the peaks show through. Endevor is the hilly country in between, and it has organized itself around that position for as long as anyone has recorded it.

The name has no agreed derivation. The leading proposal in Academy language files is that *Endevor* is a contracted Northern Mittoli compound, perhaps *ond* (broad water) + *vel* (return) + an archaic locative marker — "the place where the water turns back," referring to the watershed ridge where the hill streams divide between draining northwest toward the bay and north toward the Ganoss. A minority view holds that the name predates Northern Mittoli entirely and that the Mittoli speakers who arrived here simply kept the word that was already there. Both camps cite the same evidence in their favor, which the Academy has noted without resolving.

The outside world, if it thinks of Endevor at all, thinks of fell-sheep wool and reliable inns and the road toward the Bay of Lol. It does not think of the barrows on the ridgelines, which are older than the wool trade, and more numerous, and full of kings.

---

## Geography

The Endevor hills are old, rounded, and wet. They do not have the dramatic vertical of the Oremindi or the bleak exposed sweep of the Ganoss uplands; they are the kind of hills that fold into one another, valley after valley, each with its own stream, each valley floor broad enough for a strip of farmland before the slope comes up again. The highest ground is along the eastern ridge — the long slope that faces the Acorwood — which catches the worst of the weather coming in from the north and west and wrings it out before the clouds continue inland.

The result is that Endevor is wet without being waterlogged. The streams are reliable and numerous. The valley floors are damp and dark-soiled. The upper slopes are peat-moorland and short-grassed heath, suitable for grazing and not much else. The lower slopes are where most of Endevor's population lives, in the long ribbon settlements that follow the valley floors between the farmland and the hill pasture above.

The eastern ridge is wooded on its lower face — a mix of native northern species, hardy and not especially tall, transitioning as you approach the Acorwood's margin into something denser and older. Exactly where Endevor's edge-forest ends and the Acorwood begins is not a line that appears on any map; the local communities know it by feel and by the quality of the light, which changes at a threshold that most people can locate within a few hundred yards but cannot explain as a specific visible marker.

The Oremindi's northern end shapes the southern horizon and does more than frame the view. The meltwater coming off the range's northernmost snowfields feeds several of Endevor's main rivers, making them flashier in spring than their size would suggest and keeping them running in the driest summers. The passes through the Oremindi's northern extension are few and severe — harder and more seasonal than those further south — and are used primarily by the Sevron contact route and by occasional extraordinary travelers with specific purpose. Endevor has never been a mountain-crossing gateway in the way that the southern Oremindi communities are; the passes behind it are not worth the effort for ordinary commerce.

## The People

Endevor is pastoral first. The hill communities keep sheep on the upper slopes and cattle in the valley bottoms, and have done this long enough that the organization of the land around those two requirements is baked into everything else: the placement of settlements, the timing of the year, the structure of disputes, the vocabulary of status. A family's standing in any Endevor valley is legible from its position on the hill — how far up the slope their summer grazing runs, how many animals winter in their valley barns, and whether the barns themselves are stone or timber, which says something about how long the family has been in that particular valley.

The characteristic animal of the Endevor hills is the **fell-sheep** — a breed whose name is a Northern Mittoli word meaning roughly "the hill-animal" or "the high-pasture one," and which has been in these hills long enough that no records mention its arrival. The fell-sheep is not impressive beside the great animals of other regions. It is compact, short-legged, and deeply shaggy, with a fleece that produces more lanolin than any comparable breed — a natural oil that makes the wool water-resistant in the fleece and almost waterproof once spun and prepared. In a climate that is wet for most of the year, this matters. Endevor wool is traded throughout northern Azhora and down the coast road specifically because it performs in conditions that other wool cannot. The Bay of Lol fishing communities buy it for sea gear. The Ganoss upland clans have their own animals but trade for Endevor spun goods when they can get them. The Orched Hills mining settlements are regular purchasers of Endevor wool in preference to anything grown closer.

The fell-sheep also live their entire year outside, which is not true of most northern livestock. They know the upper hills the way the hills know themselves, and they will come down of their own accord before weather turns that their shepherds have not yet noticed. Endevor farmers pay attention to this. Where the sheep are on a given morning is information about the day.

The social unit of Endevor is the valley community — a loose collection of households sharing a river system, with informal but understood obligations around water rights, common hill access, and dispute resolution. Disputes between valleys are resolved at **stone-shelters** — low circular enclosures of dry-laid stone set at the ridgelines between valleys, old enough that no one remembers who built them, used as neutral meeting ground when valley communities need to negotiate. The stone-shelters are not impressive structures; they are simply very old and unmoved, which in Endevor is a form of authority. A resolution reached at a stone-shelter has a different weight from one reached anywhere else, because the shelter has been the resolution site before and will be again, and its walls have heard the settlement's terms and will keep them the same way they keep the wind off.

The shelters have one specific physical property that the Vornheld tradition says is why they were placed where they were placed and not anywhere else: speech spoken inside them at ordinary volume is heard simultaneously and with equal clarity by everyone in the circle, regardless of wind. The Academy attributes this to circular construction and the reflection properties of dry-laid stone at these specific angles. The Vornheld say this is what the sites were chosen for — that whoever built them tested the acoustics before placing the stone, and selected positions where the ground itself held the sound. A resolution reached inside a stone-shelter is heard by all parties in the same moment. This is not a formality. It is the physical condition that makes the resolution binding. You cannot claim you did not hear it.

The main market settlement is **Stroven** — positioned at the convergence of the three largest valley routes, where goods from the upper hills and goods coming northwest from the Bay of Lol approaches meet before separating again into the valley network. Stroven is not large; it is consequential because of where it is. The permanent population is several hundred, swelling in trading seasons. It has a covered market structure, a handful of inns accustomed to through-travelers, and the only standing archive in Endevor — a collection of land records and valley boundary agreements maintained by a family of hereditary record-keepers who have been doing this work long enough to hold opinions about disputes from four generations ago and will share those opinions if asked. The record-keepers are not impartial, exactly; they are the memory, and memory has its own loyalties. This is broadly understood and accounted for in how disputes proceed.

## The Acorwood's Western Face

Every Endevor community faces east toward the Acorwood. From the higher ridgelines it is visible as a dark line — darker than the surrounding hill forest, perceptibly different in color and density — that marks the edge of navigable country. The Ganoss upland communities on the Acorwood's northern margin and the Orched Hills settlements to the south have their own longstanding relationship with the forest's accessible edges. Endevor's relationship is with the western face, and it is different in a specific way: the morning sun rises behind the Acorwood, and from Endevor's hills at dawn the forest is lit from within — the edge trees stand in silhouette against the sky while the interior remains dark, the margin visible as a precise line between what is and is not entered. This is the Acorwood as Endevor knows it.

The edge timber harvest here is modest. The accessible trees on the Acorwood's northwestern fringe are similar in character to the edge timber on the northern and southern margins — large hardwoods that can be taken from the outer tree lines without going deep — but the Endevor communities have never developed a major edge-timber trade. The Bay of Lol coastal communities do not require much inland timber, having their own supplies from the coastal strips. The Ganoss use the northern margins. Endevor's edge harvest is primarily for local use: charcoal, building timber, occasional mast in good years.

What the Endevor communities have developed, across many generations, is a practice of **smoke-reading**.

The Acorwood occasionally produces smoke from its interior. The Orched Hills records note this with uncertainty and do not follow it up. The Ganoss communities acknowledge it and have made their peace with not explaining it. Endevor has spent enough seasons watching the western and northwestern face — the face that catches the afternoon light, visible from the high sheepwalks for hours at a time — that their communities have accumulated an unusual amount of observational detail.

The fell-sheep shepherds in the high pastures are positioned to watch the Acorwood's margin across long hours, with nothing pressing them to look elsewhere. Across generations, this has produced a tradition of noting, without explaining, what the smoke does. There is vocabulary for it in the Endevor dialect — words that do not appear in other Northern Mittoli varieties — for the smoke that rises thin and straight in still air, the smoke that spreads at canopy height and moves with the wind before dispersing, and the specific, acknowledged category of smoke that appears with no wind and dissipates downward rather than upward, which the Endevor fell-sheep shepherds call by a substrate word that translates into Standard Mittoli as something between "breathing-down" and "the forest taking something in."

The smoke-reading tradition is practical in two uses that have been confirmed across several generations. First: smoke from the Acorwood interior in late summer correlates imperfectly but consistently with good mast years; the shepherds who track this will predict mast yield before the edge trees show it in their growth. Second: the quality of smoke — color, height, behavior — appears to predict weather shifts in the way that cloud formations over the Oremindi also predict weather shifts, and is used the same way. Neither use requires an explanation of what the smoke is. The Endevor communities have not produced one.

When Academy scholars have visited Stroven and asked about the smoke, they receive accurate and detailed information about what the smoke does and what it predicts, and no information about what causes it. The question of cause is not treated as something the Endevor communities are withholding. It is treated as a question that belongs to a different kind of inquiry than the one they practice. The smoke behaves; they read it; that is the relationship; cause is for someone with more time and less sheep.

## History

The pastoral Endevor is recent, as these things go.

For several centuries before the current era, Endevor was the center of the most extensive political authority in northern Azhora. The source of that authority was gold — placer gold in the eastern hill streams, and beneath it the deep vein gold that the streams had been eroding for thousands of years before anyone thought to follow them uphill. When the Endevor mine-kings found those veins, the northern world reorganized around them.

At its height the Endevor realm extended further north than the Bay of Lol, incorporating the coastal port communities and the Ganoss upland clans as tributaries. It extended south through the Orched Hills and, in its largest period, pressed claims as far as the northern Mittolo margins — the first and last time a northern power came close to bridging the cultural divide that the Acorwood and the Orched Hills maintain between north and south. The mine-kings did not rule these territories as the later Mittoli city-states governed their regions, through administrators and written law; they held them through the distribution of gold, the weight of tribute obligation, and the personal prestige of a line that had been producing wealth long enough for wealth and legitimacy to become indistinguishable. The Ganoss clans supplied livestock and manpower. The Bay of Lol communities supplied ships and navigators. The Orched Hills supplied iron and copper. The gold at the center moved all of it.

For roughly two hundred and fifty years at the empire's peak — the period the Vornheld poetry calls the *Eld-Gold*, the time of gold — the mine-kings of Endevor were the most significant power in northern Azhora. Their burials are still in the hills. The ridgelines are scattered with them.

The tradition names thirty-eight kings in the mine-king period. Not all are equally present in the *Eld-Keth* — the earliest are named briefly, the middle kings described at length, the late-period kings compressed as the tradition seems to contract around the decline. Three stand apart.

**Keld** is the first-opener: the king who followed the stream gold uphill and found the vein. His *Eld-Keth* naming-poem is among the shortest in the body — he is recorded as a beginning rather than an achievement, and the tradition treats him accordingly, with respect but without ornamentation. A king who opens something gets the short poem. The gold that follows him speaks for itself.

**Orrven** is the peak. His naming-poem is the longest in the *Eld-Keth* body and is structured differently from all others: a call-and-answer form in which the first voice names what Orrven gave and the second names what was returned, eight exchanges moving from the Bay of Lol communities to the Ganoss clans to the Orched Hills to what the tradition calls simply "the deep ground." The effect, which Endevor audiences hear differently from how outside scholars read it, is of weight in motion — a redistribution so large that the land itself leaned under it. A Vornheld practitioner, when pressed by an Academy visitor to summarize Orrven's significance, said: "His word was the weight gold makes before anyone counts it." The Academy visitor wrote this down, which the Vornheld probably anticipated.

**Helm** is the surveyor — the king who sent Endevor's only recorded expedition into the Acorwood's interior, in the middle-late period when the eastern veins were thinning and the tradition of looking further was still recent enough to seem reasonable. Helm had observed, from fell-sheep shepherds watching the Acorwood's western face, that the breathing-down smoke concentrated most reliably above the Deepworks area. He concluded, or commissioned the conclusion, that the smoke was the Acorwood's surface indication of something below: an extension of the same hydrothermal system that had produced the vein gold, deeper and further east. Seventeen men entered the Acorwood's northwestern margin. Twelve returned. The five who did not are recorded by name in the *Eld-Keth*; that poem is recited at their stone at the barrow dedication and has not been offered at a public Stroven gathering in anyone's living memory.

The twelve who came back brought a word: *vel-keth-eld*. The Vornheld have not reached consensus on its meaning in the three or four generations since the expedition. Two translations circulate within the tradition: "the forest's memory of fire" and "what the dark knows that we left behind." The first suggests the Acorwood has a relationship to the ancient hydrothermal period — that the forest grew in or around or from the same geological event that made the gold. The second suggests that whatever the expedition encountered inside remembered the mine-kings in some form. Helm's own interpretation was not recorded. He did not seal the Deepworks — that was Veld, a generation later — but he did not send another expedition.

The mines did not run out all at once. That was the particular difficulty.

The gold declined over several generations, each one producing less than the last, each one also the one that the current king believed was the bottom before a new vein would open. The shaft-heads went deeper. The spoil heaps grew. The streams were panned harder. The veins were followed further into the Oremindi foothills, into ground that was more expensive and more dangerous, and which produced less. The gold was finite because it had been deposited by a period of ancient hydrothermal activity in the range that had long since ended; there was no new gold forming. What was there was what had been there, and when it was taken, the taking was over.

The kings who presided over the decline maintained their tributary relationships through prestige long after the gold couldn't sustain them economically. This worked while the living memory of abundance lasted. When the generation that had known the peak died out and the next knew only the decline, the calculations changed. The Ganoss clans stopped sending cattle and said they were using them at home. The Bay of Lol communities began trading directly with the Orched Hills, bypassing the tribute routing. The Orched Hills settlements, which had their own ore wealth and their own long memories, assessed the situation and stopped paying.

The last king of the Endevor realm was named Veld — a Northern Mittoli name meaning simply "return," which was understood at the time as a statement of ambition and in retrospect reads as something else. He held the realm together for eighteen years through the force of his personal authority and is described in the Vornheld tradition as the greatest of the later kings, which is not exactly praise: the greatest of the later kings could not undo what the geology had done. Veld died in his valley, and after him, the tributaries did not return.

What followed was not collapse in the dramatic sense. There were no invasions, no great battles for succession, no famine. The population stayed. The valley communities kept their land. The stone-shelters kept their function. What changed was scope: Endevor contracted back to Endevor, and the wider world that had organized around its gold reorganized around other things, and eventually forgot there had been an organizing.

The raiding period that the northern Azhoran regional record describes as coming after this was partly a consequence: without the empire's redistributive economy, the coastal communities went back to taking by force what they had previously received through the gold-network. The Endevor hills were the logistical hinterland of that raiding culture's staging, not its origin. The mine-kings' empire was before the raiding; the raiding filled the vacuum after.

By the time the north's transition to trade began, Endevor had been quiet pastoral hill country for long enough that neither the coastal communities nor the Orched Hills settlements remembered what it had been. No Mittoli scholar wrote down the Endevor empire while it existed; the region was too far outside the Mittoli world for systematic record-keeping. The Vornheld tradition preserved everything, and the Vornheld tradition is oral, in archaic Northern Mittoli, inaccessible to outsiders who do not seek it out.

Those who seek it out and can read what they find tend not to believe it — the scale is too large, the gold too much, the empire too extensive, for a country presently famous for waterproof sheep. This is a known difficulty with oral traditions from regions without outside corroboration. The barrows corroborate. The shaft-heads corroborate. Most outside visitors see hills and do not read them as texts.

## The Mines

The evidence for the Endevor gold is still in the hills if you know where to look.

The eastern hill streams — those draining toward the Acorwood's margin, fed by Oremindi snowfields — carry trace gold even now, at concentrations too low to bother with but detectable to anyone who knows what color to watch for in the pan. These are the runoff from the same deposits that built the empire: the deep vein gold in the Oremindi foothills, which the ancient hydrothermal activity of the range concentrated and which the long slow erosion of the hills has been dispersing into the streams for longer than anyone has been here to observe. At the mining peak, those concentrations were high enough that stream-panning alone produced meaningful yield. They have been diluting since the veins were broken open, and what remains is memory rather than resource.

The shaft-heads are more visible than the streams. The eastern faces of the three valley systems nearest the Acorwood — which the Vornheld tradition calls the **Eld-Valleys** — are marked with collapsed mine entrances at irregular intervals from near the valley floor to well above the current sheepwalks. Some are obvious: squared stonework around a shaft-mouth, subsidence depressions where fill has settled over generations. Others are subtle enough that a new arrival walking the sheep paths will occasionally put a foot through the turf into the edge of old roof-stone. The communities closest to the Eld-Valleys know where the dangerous ground is and avoid it without drama; it is simply part of the landscape.

The Vornheld tradition preserves names for seventeen distinct mine-workings, of which fourteen can be approximately located on the eastern ridge. The other three — the poems call them the **Deepworks** — are either collapsed without surface sign or are in the transition zone between the Endevor hills and the Acorwood's northwestern margin, where the Endevor communities do not regularly go. Whether the Deepworks still have accessible chambers is not known. The tradition says they were the richest of the veins, opened last and exhausted fastest, and sealed by Veld's order when the workers kept not returning from the deeper levels. The sealing instructions and the reason for the non-returns are subjects the Vornheld will discuss at different lengths depending on who is asking and what they appear to intend.

Silver was mined alongside gold in the middle period — a secondary ore from the same hydrothermal deposits, found at greater depth once the gold concentrations thinned. Silver mining continued for two or three generations after gold effectively ended, providing enough revenue to maintain something that looked, from the outside, like a functioning economy while the empire's political structure dissolved. The last silver was exhausted within living memory of the decline's final phase, and the silver shafts are visible in the northern valley systems, shallower and less numerous than the gold workings.

The Deepworks have a specific entry in the sealing record preserved by the Vornheld: Veld's decree is noted in archaic Northern Mittoli as *oss nil-vel-keth* — "mark: no return-speech" — a designation the tradition treats as distinct from ordinary closure. Other shafts were sealed when they were worked out. The Deepworks were closed *nil-vel-keth*, without the response that would indicate emptiness. The formulation implies something was there to not-answer.

The mine-worker tradition, preserved fragmentarily in the *Eld-Keth* naming-poems for the last men to work the Deepworks, refers to a phenomenon called *llos-eld* — roughly "gold-voice," though "the ore that speaks" is the translation Vornheld practitioners prefer when they feel precise. The *llos-eld* was a resonance audible in the rock at the deepest working levels: described variously as a low vibration, a harmonic in the stone that had no identifiable external source, a sound that seemed to originate not from above or below but from the rock around the worker. Experienced mine-foremen tracked it the way fell-sheep shepherds track the Acorwood's smoke — as information whose practical use was clear and whose underlying meaning was not. High *llos-eld* correlated with structural stability in the workings. The specific signature recorded in the Deepworks naming-poems is different from the stable reading, but what it was different toward is the thing the *Eld-Keth* does not say directly.

Whether the *llos-eld* and the Acorwood's breathing-down smoke are related is a question the mine-workers and the shepherds never appear to have addressed together — the workers were underground and the shepherds were watching the forest above, and their separate communities had different relationships to the relevant facts. What the Vornheld tradition notes, without drawing a conclusion, is that the breathing-down smoke is most frequently reported by Endevor shepherds above the ground that corresponds to the Deepworks' approximate location. The observation is in the tradition. No resolution has been attached to it that the tradition records.

The mines are not worked. No new vein has been found in eight generations of looking, which has not entirely stopped people from looking. There are people in every generation of Endevor history who believe the Deepworks are not exhausted — that Veld sealed something rather than nothing, that the gold is still in the ground and waiting. The Vornheld have opinions about this. They express them when asked and reserve their most specific opinions for those who have already decided to listen.

## The Barrows

The ridgelines of Endevor are not bare. They are covered with sheep and with the dead.

The barrow-building tradition spans the full mine-king period and reads in the landscape as a slow record of decline. The oldest barrows are the highest — the first mine-kings claimed the skyline, and the mounds they raised are the largest, some visible for miles in clear weather, their grass-covered profiles the most distinctive feature of Endevor's upper silhouette. The Vornheld tradition describes these burials with objects that make outside scholars careful about skepticism: gold plate, gold ornament, ceremonial vessels, weapons edged with gold — the vocabulary of a household that operated at a scale that required gold to describe itself.

The barrows descend through the centuries and through the hill. Middle-period kings are buried at the ridgeline's lower edge. These barrows are smaller, the grave goods richer in silver and bronze, with gold present but diminished. Then the late-period burials in the upper valley sides, below the crest entirely: modest mounds that a traveler might pass without noticing, holding kings who went into the ground with bronze and the memory of what their fathers had.

Veld's barrow is in the floor of the main Stroven valley — the lowest position of any king in the Vornheld record. The tradition notes that this was not a dishonoring but a request. Veld asked to be buried below rather than above. One school of Vornheld interpretation holds that this was a gesture of humility, acknowledging what had been lost on his watch. Another says the valley-floor placement was a statement of intention: a king buried facing upward rather than outward, not surveying what he once had but waiting for what would come to him. The debate has not been resolved in several generations of argument and probably will not be.

Veld's mound is unmarked. The Vornheld know where it is. Certain Stroven families, by long tradition, know where it is. Occasional outside scholars come looking and either find a different mound or find nothing useful. Whether this concealment is protective of the burial or of some other property of the location is a question whose answer, if there is one, belongs to the Vornheld.

The barrows in general are the community's ancestors, not its archaeological record. Disturbing them carries no explicit legal prohibition because the community's understanding of what the barrows are does not require explicit prohibition: they are the dead kings, their possessions are the dead kings' possessions, and what belongs to the dead kings belongs to the community's memory of itself rather than to anyone currently living. Outside treasure-seekers have attempted barrows in northern Azhora before. There are at least two documented cases in the Bay of Lol's coastal records of attempts at Endevor-adjacent mounds. In both cases the attempts were unsuccessful — one because the mound opened was not a barrow, and one because the attempt was interrupted in circumstances the coastal record describes with some vagueness and the Endevor account of the same events does not describe at all.

The second case involves three men from a Bay of Lol coastal family with documented commercial interests in the northern Orched Hills, who came to Endevor, in the account of a northern merchant who tracked the sequence of events, "to see what was in the old mounds." They opened what they believed was a late-period barrow on the upper eastern ridge, in the Eld-Valleys area. The coastal record notes their subsequent departure from Endevor as rapid and without what they came for, and that one of the three did not complete the return journey to the coast. The coastal narrative is vague on the circumstances that produced these outcomes. The Vornheld record of the same period — which does not describe what happened on the eastern ridge in any account that has been shared with outside inquirers — notes in its margin-notation system a designation applied afterward to the incident location: *nil-vel-gall nil-vel-keth*. The compound is not common. *Nil-vel-keth* appears as Veld's designation for the Deepworks: no return-speech. *Nil-vel-gall* is rarer still, appearing only twice in the tradition before this notation, both in contexts the Vornheld describe as "the closure of something that was not supposed to be opened." The combined designation places the site outside the category of locations where ordinary community protocols apply. The valley community that grazes the hill above the site now routes its sheep differently than before. No one has explained this to outside observers in a context where they have found explanation appropriate.

## The Vornheld and the Epic Tradition

The Vornheld are the keepers of the Endevor epic tradition. The name is a Northern Mittoli compound, meaning roughly "the ancestor-holders" or "those who hold the old ones" — from *vorn* (the sky-pivot; north; the ancestor-direction) and *held* (hold; maintenance; keeping). The title is old enough to be uncertain in its own derivation. By now it names the role as much as it describes it.

A Vornheld is not trained like a craft apprentice. The tradition enters through family — almost all practitioners come from households that have produced Vornheld before — but the selection is not simply inheritance. A child who shows the specific kind of memory the tradition requires is cultivated into it; a child who does not, even from a Vornheld household, is not. What the tradition requires is something between musical memory and structural memory: the capacity to hold long, metrically complex poems in reproducible form, and to hold the relationships between poems — the web of cross-references, the moment where one poem's allusion answers another's question — as a single navigable structure rather than a list. Knowing the poems is not knowing the tradition. The tradition is the map between them.

The Vornheld perform at the stone-shelters when gatherings are significant enough to require it — the major inter-valley assemblies, the seasonal land-right confirmations, the resolution of disputes that touch on ancestry, which in Endevor can mean almost any dispute between old families. The recitation at these events is not ornamental. The Vornheld's knowledge of ancestral claims is evidence. When a family asserts a right to a stretch of hill pasture based on an ancestor's service to a mine-king three hundred years ago, the Vornheld knows whether that claim is in the tradition, what the tradition says about it, and whether anything in the tradition speaks to the counter-claim. This is why Endevor dispute-resolution requires a Vornheld present for any proceeding invoking ancestry: the record-keepers in Stroven hold land agreements; the Vornheld hold lineages. The two archives do not duplicate each other.

The Vornheld lineages currently active number seven, each associated with a particular valley system and, by extension, with a particular portion of the tradition. Lineage specialization is not formalized — there is no rule stipulating that the Stroven valley lineage holds the Veld poems — but it has become de facto over several generations of practice, as each lineage has tended to recite its own valley's ancestral claims most often and therefore knows them most deeply. Full knowledge of all three bodies is the stated goal; full knowledge of the complete tradition in reproducible form is what distinguishes a master Vornheld from a capable one, and in any given generation there are perhaps two or three practitioners who can honestly claim it.

The most consequential current lineage disagreement runs between the family associated with the Keld poems and the family associated with the Orrven poems — the tradition of the first-opener against the tradition of the peak. The dispute is not about the poems themselves, which are settled, but about the primacy of their subjects: whether the origin is more essential to the tradition's meaning than the achievement, or whether the peak defines the entire arc and makes the opening merely prelude. In practical terms the disagreement affects how several disputed ancestral claims are weighted when they touch on the first-opener period versus the peak period. The dispute has an analog in a longstanding property disagreement between the two lineages' associated valley communities that has been argued at stone-shelters four times in the past century without permanent resolution. Everyone in Stroven understands the land question and the Vornheld question to be the same argument in different registers.

The most discussed recent intervention of Vornheld authority in living memory concerns an episode known in Stroven as the Drevos incident, or, among those who find the name insufficient, the salted-pan dispute. The Drevos family, a mining-descended household in the northern valley system, had for three generations asserted a right to a seasonal pasture on the basis of an ancestor's service to a late-period king. The counter-claim, from an older hill family, held that the same ancestor's service was in the *Eld-Keth* but attributed to a different king — which, if correct, would mean the pasture assignment had been made under misattributed authority and could be revisited. The dispute reached a stone-shelter proceeding with two Vornheld present from different lineages, who disagreed on the attribution. Resolution required a third Vornheld to recite the relevant naming-poem in full. This third Vornheld, who was elderly and had not recited the poem publicly in twelve years, did so without pause, in the original archaic Northern Mittoli, before an audience that included the Stroven record-keepers and approximately forty community members who had come specifically because word had gone around that the recitation would happen. The attribution was established. The pasture question was settled. The elderly Vornheld's unassisted recitation of a poem whose text two younger practitioners had disputed is still referenced in Stroven as a lesson about what the tradition requires of its keepers. What the salted-pan element of the name refers to — whether it is the name of the pasture, a detail of that day's proceedings, or something else — no one who was present has found it necessary to clarify.

The epic tradition is divided into three bodies.

**The *Eld-Keth*** ("the old-speech," the words from before): poems of the mine-king period, naming kings, campaigns, tributary arrangements, and the workings. The most extensive body and the least often publicly recited — they require context to hear correctly, and much of what they say is addressed to the Endevor community rather than to any outside listener. The *Eld-Keth* includes the naming-poems of the barrows: a short piece for each buried king describing the burial goods and the terms of his life. These are recited at the barrows on specific seasonal occasions and nowhere else.

**The *Vel-Keth*** ("the return-speech," the words of transition): poems of the decline — the closing mines, the dissolving tributaries, Veld's reign and burial. The most frequently performed body in public settings, because it does what narrative does best: it names a recognizable arc of rise and fall, and its audiences understand the shape. The *Vel-Keth* is what a traveler in Stroven who witnesses a Vornheld performance is most likely to hear. Fragments have circulated outside Endevor — two Orched Hills records and one Bay of Lol coastal archive preserve passages, apparently transcribed by travelers who heard a Vornheld recite and wrote what they could catch. These fragments are classified in the Academy's northern archive as "miscellaneous oral material, provenance unclear."

**The *Vel-Eld***: the renewal poem. Treated separately because it is not, in the Vornheld tradition, the same category of thing as the other bodies. The *Eld-Keth* and *Vel-Keth* describe what was. The *Vel-Eld* describes what will be — or what may be — or what the tradition holds is latent in the ground the way the gold was latent in the ground, waiting for its conditions.

## The Vel-Eld

The *Vel-Eld* — the Old Return, or the Ancient Coming-Back, or the Return That Was Always There — carries a title that is itself one of the tradition's genuinely contested problems. The Northern Mittoli word *vel* functions, in the archaic form the poem uses, as both "return" and "was-already." The poem is about something coming back, or about something that was always present and is finally becoming visible, or both, and the language declines to resolve the question.

Its subject, in plain summary: the restoration of the Endevor realm and the reopening of the mines. The Vornheld tradition considers this an adequate description in the way that "a watershed is where water divides" adequately describes a watershed.

The poem is not available to outside scholars in its complete form. The Vornheld position on this is not that the poem is secret — it is recited in the open twice a year, in a valley accessible to anyone who walks there — but that a complete text extracted from its recitation context and its listeners would be a different thing from the poem, and that the different thing is not what they are keepers of. The following are fragments in approximate translation, given in the order of their appearance in the full recitation. Translation is from archaic Northern Mittoli, which the full recitation uses throughout; the terse, stress-heavy character of the original does not carry.

*The streams remember / what the hills forgot — / gold is a patience / longer than kings.*

*Cold water finds / what cold water left. / The vein is not spent; / the vein is not yet asked.*

*The dead have been seen / before their door opens. / What the mounds hold / the mounds will show first.*

*The wood knows the fire / that made the gold ground. / When the wood speaks it, / Vel-Endre stands.*

Vornheld practitioners reciting the complete poem use a slower and more deliberate delivery for the third fragment than for the others, which has been noticed by everyone who has attended more than one recitation but not explained in any account that has reached outside Endevor.

The poem is structured around three signs and a title.

**The first sign**: *When the cold water finds what it left behind.* The eastern hill streams carry traces of gold even now; the tradition holds that there will come a season when this trace returns to something visible in the pan — when the water finds again what it eroded and eventually lost. One school of Vornheld interpretation holds this means the Deepworks contain a vein that Veld's sealing preserved rather than exhausted, and that the right conditions will reveal it. Another holds it means a new hydrothermal process — something in the Oremindi still active far below the surface, expressing slowly upward — that will produce new deposition. The two positions have been debating each other for several generations without decisive evidence on either side.

**The second sign**: *When the barrows wake before they're opened.* The specific language in the archaic Northern Mittoli is *vel-milloss nil-vel-gall* — "they have been seen before the crossing." The practical interpretation most Vornheld accept: someone will witness something at a barrow — light, sound, movement — before any physical disturbance of the mound. The wider interpretation: a stranger will come to Endevor knowing, without having been told, where the dead kings are. The two interpretations are not mutually exclusive, which is part of why the debate has outlasted several attempts to settle it. It is also why certain Vornheld observe the barrows with unusual attention when strangers appear in Stroven.

**The third sign**: *When the wood speaks what it knows.* The Acorwood reference is explicit within the Vornheld tradition and widely understood among practitioners: the poem refers to the smoke, or to something the smoke precedes, or to something the smoke has always been an indication of and which has not yet fully arrived. Vornheld who specialize in the *Vel-Eld* will say, when pressed, that the third sign is the one whose meaning is most certain among practitioners and most opaque to outside inquiry, and that this relationship is itself the point. The Acorwood speaks in a language the Endevor communities have been learning to read for generations. The third sign is the moment that language becomes fully legible.

The title the poem gives to the coming renewal is not a personal name. It is *Vel-Endre* — "Returning-Endevor" or "Endevor-Returned" — a name describing the condition rather than the person who embodies it. The poem does not clearly specify whether *Vel-Endre* is a king who comes from outside bringing the empire back, a quality that rises in the people themselves, or the land's own restoration coming through any of several possible channels. This ambiguity has made the title perpetually applicable to promising individuals — anyone who arrives in Endevor with any northern lineage and any apparent significance has been assessed by at least one Vornheld against the *Vel-Eld*'s criteria.

The title was applied to Veld himself, in his own lifetime, by a Vornheld lineage that believed his reign was the beginning of the restoration rather than the end of the old order. He died without the mines reopening. The lineage that backed this interpretation is still active; its current practitioners maintain that the application was not wrong but early — that Veld was the first motion of a returning tide, not its crest, and that the poem's timeline is longer than it appears. Other Vornheld lineages find this argument convenient in the particular way that arguments which cannot be falsified are convenient. The argument continues.

The *Vel-Eld* is recited in full twice a year: at the winter solstice and at the vernal equinox. Both recitations happen at Veld's barrow in the Stroven valley floor, attended by as many Vornheld as can come and whatever community members choose to follow. The solstice recitation draws better attendance than the equinox — there is something in the Endevor character that finds it appropriate to hear a poem about renewal in conditions of maximum cold, to say the thing that is latent in the ground while standing on frozen ground, to say that something will return while winter has its full weight on the hills. Not despite the conditions. Because of them.

## Fragments of the Epic Tradition

Two passages from the *Vel-Keth* have circulated outside Endevor in written form, transcribed by travelers who heard Vornheld performances at Stroven and recorded what they could. Both appear in the Academy's northern files under the classification "miscellaneous oral material, northern provenance, language unclear." The Academy scholars who catalogued them had limited familiarity with archaic Northern Mittoli and none with the tradition's conventions. Their annotations treat the passages as remnants of something larger, which is accurate, while failing to identify what that larger thing is, which is characteristic.

**From the Orrven naming-poem, as transcribed by a Bay of Lol merchant's factor, approximately four generations ago:**

*What did he give the north? — Ships for the bay and horses for the heath.*
*What did the north give back? — Men who came when called and went when released.*
*What did he give the eastern hills? — Iron's true value, which is what it becomes.*
*What did the hills give back? — Iron already becoming, waiting for the weight.*
*What did he give the country beyond? — Gold's shadow, which moves faster than gold.*
*What did the country give back? — Silence and the roads kept open.*
*What did he give the deep ground? — The right question, asked at the right depth.*
*What did the deep ground give back? — This is the exchange that is not finished.*

The Academy annotation reads: "Possible ritual exchange; appears agricultural in reference; northern dialect, incompletely rendered." The observation about incomplete rendering is correct. The rest reflects the difficulty of reading call-and-answer verse outside its acoustic context: at a stone-shelter recitation, the eight exchanges move between two voices that everyone in the circle hears simultaneously. On a page, read sequentially, the eighth exchange loses the quality that makes it different from the other seven — the moment in the recitation where the second voice does not answer.

**From the *Vel-Keth*'s Veld cycle, as transcribed by an Orched Hills trading-house record-keeper, approximately three generations ago:**

*Veld spoke last on the ridge, the long speaking.*
*He named what was owed and named who had paid.*
*He named what remained and named who would keep it.*
*He did not name a debt that had not been made.*
*The tributaries heard him. They heard him well.*
*What the tributaries did afterward is their own poem.*

The Academy annotation: "Lament fragment, possibly funerary; 'Veld' possibly a title or appellative." The second observation is the transcriber noticing that the Northern Mittoli word for "return" has been used as a proper name, and concluding it must therefore be figurative. The Vornheld tradition would say: the word was his name before it became anything else, and the Veld cycle does not require outside annotation to be understood by the people it was made for.

**From the *Eld-Keth*, the naming-poem for the five men who did not return from Helm's expedition:**

*Vel-Oram, who read the deep ground and said it remembered fire.*
*Vel-Skedhel, who heard the llos-eld and stayed to hear it again.*
*Vel-Thonn, whose question the wood did not decline to answer.*
*Vel-Orrael, who went toward the answer rather than away from it.*
*Vel-Krenth, whose name the poem does not complete.*

Each name is preceded by *vel-*, functioning here as an honorific title: "the one who returned into." The fifth entry is the one outside scholars, if they ever encountered it, would find most notable: the poem's formulation implies that Vel-Krenth's name continues somewhere, but not in the poem. Where it continues is not specified.

The Academy files do not contain this passage. It has not been transcribed by any outside visitor who has shared the transcription. It is recited at the individual stone-markers of the five men on specific seasonal occasions by the Vornheld lineage that holds their names, and nowhere else. Its appearance here is the level of description the tradition permits in this context, and is not an invitation to seek the complete text.

## Language

The Endevor dialect of Northern Mittoli is conservative in its structure and unusual in its vocabulary. It shows the expected Northern features — initial stress, final vowel deletion, flattened diphthongs — but it has preserved the *ae* diphthong in a handful of words where even Northern Mittoli has simplified it, which linguists treat as evidence that Endevor has been in less contact with the coast than the Bay of Lol communities to its northwest, maintaining an archaic form while its neighbors changed.

The substrate layer is deeper here than in the Bay of Lol speech and different in profile from the Ganoss upland dialect. The smoke vocabulary is the most studied example: words that describe the Acorwood's behavior, which have no cognates in any other Northern Mittoli variety and do not reconstruct from Proto-Western, appear to be borrowings from a language that was spoken in these hills before Mittoli speakers arrived. Academy linguists have catalogued seven such words in Endevor dialect and noted that they cluster in the domain of fire, smoke, forest behavior, and certain weather phenomena — the things that a community watching the Acorwood's western face would need to name.

The fell-sheep vocabulary is also unusually rich: Endevor herders have fifteen or more terms for wool quality, fleece condition, and grazing behavior that do not appear in other Northern Mittoli varieties and that the Academy's linguistic records have not been able to trace to any source outside Endevor itself. Whether these are old substrate words, old Mittoli words that have only survived here, or innovations developed within the community is not established. The herders consider the question academic in a register that implies the word "academic" is not a compliment.
