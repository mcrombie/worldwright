# Nethereum

The Neth flows from the northwestern uplands through country that does not hold its shape the way the Oveth does. Where the Oveth cuts a valley and stays there, the Neth spreads. It reaches the middle section of its course and finds low, poorly-drained ground — a broad depression in the interior plateau where multiple hill-streams converge and the water has no efficient route to the main Lizeem. Here it slows, fans out, and creates the Nethermere: a body of standing water that in dry years is a large shallow lake with productive margins and in flood years is a flat inland sea that covers everything below the ridgelines and does not recede until well into summer. The land within and around the Nethermere, and the communities built on the rises that remain above it, constitute the country called Nethereum.

The name is a Caeras scribal form — the Caeras compilers who first documented the inner-branch region wrote *Nethraneum* in the oldest records and shortened it in later administrative use. The people of the region call themselves the **Nethrani** and call their country *Nethran*, "the deep water country," which is what the scribal form was trying to render. The distinction does not matter in Branch Court proceedings. It matters in the Nethrani's view of how much outside authorities understand about where they live.

## Geography

The Nethermere sits at the low point of a natural drainage basin roughly twenty miles across. No single river created it; the Neth is the largest input, but several smaller hill-streams feed the depression from different directions, and the basin's flat bottom means water that enters has nowhere urgent to go. The Neth itself exits through a narrow channel to the southeast, where it drops off the plateau edge and joins the Lizeem approaches below — a short, fast lower section that gives the Neth a split character: gentle and spreading in the middle country, quick and navigable in its lower reach.

The Nethermere is not the same body of water from year to year. In a dry year it is a lake of three to four miles across, with marshland extending well beyond it into the basin margins, and drier ground at the outer edges of the basin where hill-stream communities have established themselves. In a flood year, it expands until the basin margins disappear and only the ridgelines and the higher hummocks within the basin remain as islands. The line between "lake" and "flooded plain" does not exist in the Nethermere as a fixed thing. What exists is a gradient of reliable ground, and the Nethrani have mapped every foot of it.

The communities of Nethereum are built on elevated points: the ridgelines around the basin perimeter, the higher hummocks within it, and the natural levees along the Neth's course above the Nethermere. These sites were not chosen by planners but by survival; settlements that occupied lower ground were moved or destroyed by high flood years until what remained was what could not be drowned. The result is a kingdom with no continuous territory — a constellation of elevated communities distributed across a landscape that is, for several months each year, largely water.

The climate is wetter than Ovesos and much wetter than the main Mithala plain. The basin's water moderates temperature somewhat — summers are less hot than the open plateau, winters slightly milder — but the chronic dampness shapes everything: the crops, the buildings, the health traditions, the emotional register of public life. The sky over the Nethermere is often overcast. The light has a quality that travelers describe as muffled. The Nethrani describe it as the right light for seeing clearly.

## Agriculture, Fishery, and Economy

Nethereum produces differently from Ovesos. Where the Sorten's valley floor supports grain farming and orchard slopes, the Nethermere supports what grows in and around standing water. The division is not poverty — in good years the basin is extraordinarily productive — but the productivity operates on different principles and in a different category of risk.

**Grain**: Elevated ground around the basin perimeter is farmed for barley and oats — crops suited to the damp soils and shorter reliable growing season. The yields per acre are lower than Ovesian bottomland grain; the Nethrani compensate by farming the elevated margins intensively and relying on other food sources for the rest. In high flood years even the perimeter farms are affected: the crop that was planted in spring may be inundated before harvest if the Nethermere rises late.

**Reed-grain and wet crops**: In the shallower margins of the Nethermere, the Nethrani cultivate water-adapted crops — primarily a variety of marsh grain that the Minorans classify with the reed-rice family but the Nethrani insist is different. It grows in standing water of up to a foot, harvested by wading, and produces a dense, slightly bitter grain that stores extremely well and commands a modest but consistent market from traders who know its properties. It is the Nethrani's most distinctive agricultural product and the one the Galans are least sure what to do with commercially.

**Fishery**: The Nethermere supports a large population of river fish that move into the basin during flood season and are harvested as the waters recede. The Nethrani have developed extensive weir and trap systems at the Nethermere's outlet points — constructions that channel the retreating water through harvesting structures and produce, in good years, quantities of preserved fish (smoked, salted, dried) that travel as far as Minora and occasionally Nylon. The fish trade is the Nethrani's most commercially reliable export and the one on which their Minoran factor relationships are primarily built.

**Cattle**: The basin margins after flood recession are carpeted with the fast-growing grass that the retreating water deposits. Nethrani cattle — a compact, short-legged breed adapted to wet ground — are moved onto this post-flood pasture as soon as the footing is reliable. This grazing window is not long, but the grass's quality during it is high enough that the Nethrani cattle are notably well-conditioned by late summer. The Galan meat market has known Nethrani cattle for two generations.

**Reed and marsh goods**: The Nethermere produces materials that drier countries must import — specifically the tall marsh reeds used for thatch, basketry, rope fiber, and structural framing for lighter construction. Reed goods are a secondary export, more local in reach than the fish and cattle trade, but reliable enough that several Nethrani communities specialize in them almost entirely.

In aggregate, Nethereum feeds its own population from a combination of sources that no single one of them could sustain. In a year when grain fails, fish and wet-crop carry the country. In a year when the flood is too severe for the fish weirs, grain stores and cattle are the reserve. The interdependence of multiple food systems is not an explicit policy; it is the accumulated practice of a population that has learned, over generations, that the basin does not fail the same way twice.

## The Flood

The flood is not the enemy. This is the most important thing to understand about the Nethrani and the hardest thing for outsiders to accept.

The flood makes the post-flood pasture. It deposits the silt that maintains the marsh grain's productivity. It drives the fish into the basin where they can be harvested. It fills the water stores that Nethrani households maintain in the elevated settlements. Without the flood, the Nethermere is a smaller lake with reduced margins, reduced productivity, and reduced water availability for the communities that depend on it. The Nethrani know this. Their traditions encode it. Their word for the flood — *nethvel*, "the return of the deep water" — carries no negative valence. The flood returns. This is the nature of the place.

What the flood takes is also acknowledged directly. It takes crops planted in the low margins in optimistic years. It takes buildings constructed below the reliable line. It takes animals not moved to higher ground in time. It takes people, occasionally — children who did not learn the water, the old who cannot be moved, travelers who did not know the signs. In a bad year it takes more: the high flood of three generations ago, called the Long Water in Nethrani memory, remained above normal for six weeks and destroyed structures in communities that had not flooded in living memory. These losses are remembered.

The Nethrani memorial tradition for flood loss is one of the most developed among the inner-branch countries. The lost are named in the annual **Flood Recall** — a ceremony held as the Nethermere rises each spring, in which the previous year's losses are spoken aloud and added to the running community record. The record is oral and extremely long; older communities have Flood Recall lists going back thirty or forty generations, maintained by designated memory-keepers who carry them without writing. The oldest entries are names without context — no family, no place, only the name and the approximate flood year. The lists do not distinguish between the losses of floods in different years; they are cumulative. A community's Flood Recall is its complete account of what the water has taken, delivered annually before the water takes anything more.

Outsiders who have witnessed a Flood Recall describe the experience as both solemn and matter-of-fact — the tone does not vary between an old loss and a recent one; the names are spoken the same way because the water does not distinguish. Several Mittoli scholars have compared the Flood Recall to the Telemon band recitation of the dead, which the Nethrani find mildly amusing; they consider the Telemon tradition to be a Flood Recall for violence, which is a more specific and therefore more manageable category of loss.

## Political Structure

Nethereum has no single capital and no clean hierarchy. This is not a condition it is trying to remedy.

The communities of the inner basin and the ridge settlements of the perimeter each govern themselves, organized around the elevated ground their founding families or lineages claimed and have held. The scale of self-governance varies: the largest ridge communities are small towns with their own councils and defined customary law; the smaller hummock communities are extended family groups with a senior who mediates disputes and speaks for the group in regional affairs.

What connects them is the **Flood Council** — a body of community representatives that meets on the Neth levee above the Nethermere, twice yearly, to coordinate flood management, adjudicate cross-community disputes, and make collective decisions about external relations. The Flood Council has no permanent membership beyond its coordination function; representatives come from each recognized community, and a community's weight in deliberation is roughly proportional to what it contributes to the shared flood works. Communities that maintain the largest sections of the levee system or operate the most important fish weirs speak first and longest.

The Flood Council does not produce a king. It produces consensus, slowly, with considerable argument, and the consensus lasts as long as it serves the majority of communities better than non-compliance. In flood emergencies, the coordination function activates and the process is faster — the Nethrani have extensive practice with emergency decisions and are notably quicker at them than at routine ones. An outside observer who watched a multi-day deliberation on fish weir taxation and then watched the same body coordinate an emergency levee repair in an hour would conclude they were watching a different institution. They are watching the same institution under different conditions.

Minora finds the Flood Council difficult to deal with because there is no single interlocutor with binding authority. A Branch Court ruling against "Nethereum" requires enforcement across every Nethrani community that does not want to comply, and different communities have different interests. The Caeras legal tradition has a general principle that treaty obligations bind a polity, not its component parts; the Nethrani have a general principle that the Flood Council's agreement binds the communities that agreed and not the ones that didn't, and that consensus is not unanimity. These two principles have not yet produced a resolution in any active proceedings.

## Relations with the Other Inner-Branch Countries

**Ovesos** — Northwest boundary neighbors and, by the standards of the inner-branch competition, neither allies nor active rivals. Ovesos's water-rights obsession and Nethereum's flood-management orientation produce different political languages; they compete in the Minoran Branch Court for different things and rarely find themselves in direct opposition. The Ovesian Water Council finds the Flood Council's structure frustrating — there is no equivalent body to negotiate with, no binding agreement mechanism. The Nethrani find the Ovesian fixation on documented claims slightly absurd in the context of a landscape that periodically resets. The practical relationship is workmanlike: Nethrani fish and Ovesian grain move through shared border markets; neither polity spends significant political capital on the other.

**Noras** — The most important relationship in the inner-branch system for Nethereum, since Noras controls the route by which Nethrani fish and marsh goods reach the main Lizeem market. The Nethrani have a passage arrangement with Noras, maintained separately from Ovesos's arrangement and on different terms. Noran factors have been buying preserved Nethrani fish for long enough that there is a standing commercial relationship beneath the political one; in the rare event that the Flood Council fails to reach agreement and Noras must decide who to favor, the fish trade tends to inform the decision.

**Thelom** — Upstream of Ovesos and further removed from Nethereum. The Nethrani have periodic disputes with Thelom over the western upper tributaries where the two river systems' catchments overlap; when Thelom diverts water upstream it affects the Neth's flow into the Nethermere. These disputes are slower-developing and less intense than the Ovesos-Thelom boundary conflict, because changes to the Nethermere's water level are gradual and ambiguous in cause. The Nethrani do track the correlation. They simply cannot prove it to Branch Court standards.

**Errath** — The eastern inner branch, separated from Nethereum by Ovesos. The two polities have no direct relationship. They appear in each other's political calculations only when the inner-branch alliances shift enough that an Errath-Thelom alignment threatens to isolate Ovesos and leave Nethereum with a difficult downstream partner.

## Language

Nethrani is an inner-branch Mittoli variant, recognizable to any Standard Mittoli speaker, with a vocabulary shaped by the basin environment and the flood tradition. Key terms:

*Nethvel* — the flood; "the return of the deep water." The term for the annual rise. Distinguished from *nethmorr* (a damaging flood; one that exceeds normal bounds) and *nethgell* (a low year; a flood that fails to reach its expected level, which is its own kind of problem).

*Vel-nethran* — the post-flood; the season after the Nethermere recedes. The most productive season for pasture and fishing; also the season of repair, when communities assess and rebuild what the water took.

*Haethoss* — the reliable line; the elevation above which a community's core structures are built. Not a legal term; a practical one. A family that builds below the haethoss is building in the flood. The haethoss of each community shifts slightly over generations as flood behavior changes.

*Kael-nethvel* — to address the flood in elevated register; specifically, to speak the Flood Recall formula. The Pyrosi register-borrowing (*kael-*) did not arrive through direct Pyrosi contact but through the intermediate Mittoli trade dialect. That the Nethrani use Pyrosi elevated register for their most significant ceremonial address has been noted by linguists as a remarkable borrowing; the Nethrani explanation is that the flood has the standing of the land, and speaking to the land requires elevation. This is structurally identical to the Pyrosi explanation for using *kael-* at fumarole sites.

*Nethoss* — the deep basin; the lowest point of the Nethermere. Used figuratively for any situation that has reached its lowest possible state. "We are in the nethoss of this" means: it cannot get worse; the only direction now is up.

## Character

The Nethrani are, by outside description, pragmatic about loss in a way that the other inner-branch countries find either admirable or unsettling depending on temperament. The observation is accurate as far as it goes. A Nethrani household that has watched the flood take a granary and rebuilt it in the same location the following spring is not a household that lacks feeling about the loss. It is a household that has decided that the location is worth the risk, that the loss is predictable and survivable, and that the alternative — moving permanently to the ridgeline — is worse. This is not resignation. It is the considered position of people who know their landscape.

The phrase that summarizes the Nethrani outlook, used internally and occasionally externally, is: *nethoss vel-kael* — "the deep basin is where the good ground is." What the flood covers, it also enriches. The most productive soil in the basin is the lowest, because the lowest receives the most sediment. Building above the haethoss is safe and less fertile. Building below it is dangerous and more fertile. This is a real choice that real households make each generation, and the culture built around making it is not naive about the cost.

The festivals of Nethereum are flood-organized: the Flood Recall before the rise, the Post-Flood Settlement when communities assess and apportion the post-flood margins, and the Drying Festival at the end of the fishing season, when the Nethermere reaches its lowest point and the communities hold a market across the exposed basin floor. The Drying Festival market is the one annual occasion when the entire inner landscape of the basin is accessible; communities that are separated by water for most of the year meet in the center, on ground that will be underwater again within months. The market has been described by visitors as the strangest fair they have attended, conducted on lakeside that is simultaneously open and temporary, the whole gathering organized around the knowledge that the ground it is standing on will shortly not be available.
