# Mittolo

Mittolo is the green heart of Azhora — the broad, forested, river-threaded region that occupies the center and west of the continent and has been the seat of its dominant civilizations for as long as records have been kept. The name itself is old enough that its meaning has been debated by linguists for centuries; the most widely accepted reading traces it to a pre-Compact root meaning something close to "the middle that holds," though the "holds" carries a structural sense — the middle that keeps things together — rather than the possessive. Whether this reflects an ancient geographic self-understanding or is a later rationalization of a word no one fully remembers is unresolved.

The older name for the region — or for its eastern heartland specifically — is *Mithala*. The two names coexist. Scholars from the Caeras valley cities tend to use Mittolo; older texts, ceremonial language, and the peoples of the flat river country east of the interior hills tend to use Mithala. The relationship between the names mirrors the relationship between the two parts of the region: Mittolo is the forested west, the hill country, the long-settled political heartland; Mithala is the flat eastern plains where the Lizeem spreads and the grain grows, the breadbasket that feeds everything else. In practice both names are used for the whole, and neither party finds this confusing.

## Geography

Mittolo is defined by its rivers. The three great river systems of the west — the Velm in the north, the Caeras through the center, and the slow, wide Ond in the south — drain the interior forests and hill country toward the western coast, and everything that has happened in western Mittolo has happened near their banks or along the roads that follow them. The rivers are navigable for most of their length, which meant that goods, armies, and ideas moved freely through the region long before road-building became sophisticated.

The Lizeem is the fourth great river, and it runs the other way. It drains the eastern face of the interior, gathering water from the foothills of the Oremindi and the long slopes behind them, and flows not west but east — out of the uplands and down onto the flat country of Mithala, where it spreads. The terrain shift is abrupt enough to be noticed even by travelers who do not know to expect it: the forested, rolling country of western Mittolo descends over the course of a day or two of travel into flat open plain, the trees thinning and the sky opening and the Lizeem ahead running wide and many-channeled across the lowland. Here, freed from the gradient that kept it concentrated in its upper reaches, the river branches. First one, then several, then more — distributaries peeling off from the main channel and running their own courses across the flat, linking and separating, forming a braided network of waterways across the full breadth of the Mithala plains before eventually gathering again as they approach the Iberos coast and reaching the sea at Nylon.

The land between the river channels is flat. Not the gentle roll of the western forests but genuinely, continuously flat — the kind of flat that accumulates over long periods of sediment deposition, the river having built the land it now crosses. The soil is deep and dark. It does not require much from the farmer beyond labor; it gives back in proportion to what is asked of it, and it has been asked much for a very long time. The Mithala plains are the breadbasket of Azhora, the region whose surpluses feed the cities of the coasts and the hill peoples of the interior and the desert traders of the south. When Azhoran powers calculate their strategic position, the first question is always some version of: who controls the Lizeem plain?

The land between the rivers is forested in the west — deeply in the interior, more openly toward the east where the trees thin toward the transitional zone of the Plains. The forests are old. The western groves in particular contain trees that dendrochronologists cannot fully date; the rings become too compressed, too dense, somewhere past three thousand years. What the forest was before the oldest dateable growth is not known.

The terrain of western Mittolo is gently rolling except in the northwest, where the landscape roughens into the hill country that surrounds the Bay of Lol and the Ganoss uplands. The absence of hard geographic barriers in the west has historically meant that borders here have been cultural and political rather than natural, and have moved accordingly. The Mithala plains to the east have their own answer to borders: the river channels themselves serve as boundaries, and the channels shift, and the boundaries shift with them, and the political and legal apparatus for dealing with this is one of the older ongoing preoccupations of Azhoran jurisprudence.

## Minora

Where the Lizeem first forks — where the main channel meets the flat plain and splits for the first time into two great branches — there is a city. Minora sits at this junction and has sat there for as long as the junction has been what it is, which may be longer than any other continuously occupied site on the Mithala plain. The geography explains both the city's existence and its scale: every boat descending the Lizeem from the interior must pass through Minora. Every load of grain, every block of Oremindi stone, every bolt of western forest timber that travels east by water passes this point before it can go anywhere else. The fork below the city divides the river and the trade simultaneously; the city above it collects a portion of everything before the division occurs.

This position has made Minora the most important inland city on the continent by most measures that involve counting. Not the most beautiful, not the most ancient in the sense of unbroken occupation, not the most prestigious in the ceremonial hierarchy of Mittoli political tradition — there are cities with better claims on each of those — but the largest, the wealthiest in aggregate, and the one whose disruption would cause the most immediate practical consequences for the most people in the most places. Minora knows this about itself. The city's political culture has been shaped by centuries of sitting at a point through which everyone else's interests must flow, and it has developed the characteristic outlook of such places: a practical tolerance of divergent parties, a facility for negotiation between interests that would not negotiate elsewhere, and an underlying confidence, rarely stated but always present, that whatever happens above can be held as long as the fork is held.

The city sits on both banks and on the island formed between the two departing channels — the island being the oldest settled portion, the two-bank extensions later and now much larger. The bridge system that connects them is one of the engineering achievements most cited in Azhoran civic histories, not because it is the most technically impressive but because it has been rebuilt so many times following flood damage, and each rebuilding has incorporated the available improvements of its period, such that the current structure is a palimpsest of several centuries of bridge engineering visible in its different sections.

The name Minora is Standard Mittoli. In older texts and in the speech of the river-farming communities downstream, it appears as *Minorath*, the terminal -ath a suffix that in old Mithali designates a place defined by what it does rather than what it contains. Minorath: the place of the dividing.

## The Lizeem Branches

Below Minora the river becomes many rivers. The main distributaries — there are traditionally counted as seven major branches, though hydrologists note that this count has changed as channels shift and the "major" threshold is not precisely defined — spread across the plain over the course of several hundred miles before reaching the Iberos coast. Each branch has its own name, its own character, and its own chain of riverside settlements. Each branch also has its own hydrological behavior: some are deep and slow, navigable by large vessels for most of the year; some are shallow and fast, crossable in summer but dangerous in the flood season; some have been redirected by human intervention over the centuries to serve irrigation systems that now depend on them entirely.

The flood season is the defining event of the Mithala agricultural year. When the snowmelt off the Oremindi and the spring rains of the upper interior arrive together — which happens most years and is catastrophically combined in some — the Lizeem rises and the branches spread, and the plain between the channels is briefly underwater. This flooding is not purely destructive. It deposits the sediment that has made the plain what it is and keeps the soil at its extraordinary productivity. The Mithala farmers have built their settlements and laid out their fields around the flood's requirements rather than against them. The villages sit on the highest available ground. The grain is planted after the waters recede. The calendar that the river-plain communities use is organized around the flood — its arrival, its peak, its recession — rather than around the astronomical year, and this calendar conflicts with the Caeras-city astronomical calendar in ways that have generated two thousand years of administrative friction.

## Climate

Mittolo is temperate and wet. Prevailing winds off the western sea bring reliable rainfall across most of the region for most of the year, with no true dry season. Winters are cold but not severe at lower elevations; the northern reaches approach bitter cold in deep winter. Summers are mild and green.

The forests regulate temperature and humidity in ways that are by now self-sustaining — the canopy moderates extremes, and the deep root systems maintain soil moisture through dry spells. Agriculturally, Mittolo is among the most productive regions on the continent, and has fed the populations of its cities and the ambitions of its rulers for millennia.

## History

The recorded history of Mittolo is the recorded history of Azhora, more or less, which is a Mittoli bias that scholars from other regions have noted with some irritation. The oldest surviving written texts on the continent were produced here. The first political compacts that formed the basis of later governance were drafted along the Caeras. The institutions that became the Azhoran Academy, the system of weights and measures used across most of the continent, and the calendar in common use all originated in the Mittolo heartland.

This has not made Mittolo peaceful. The region's fertility and central position made it a prize, and its history is one of repeated fragmentation, unification, conquest, and re-fragmentation. The current political configuration — a loose set of city-states and regional councils nominally bound by the Second Caeras Compact — is roughly five hundred years old and has survived primarily because no single power within Mittolo has been strong enough to absorb the others, and powers from outside have found it expensive to hold.

The oldest stratum of Mittoli history, before the first written records, is accessible only through archaeology and oral tradition. The archaeology suggests a much earlier period of high organization — coordinated construction, evidence of long-distance trade, grain storage at a scale implying central authority — that ended abruptly several thousand years ago with what the physical record describes as burning and abandonment on a regional scale. What caused this is not established. It is called the First Silence in Mittoli oral tradition, and is described not as a conquest or a catastrophe but as a departure — "the people before us left." Where they went is not part of the tradition.

## Language and Dialect

The Mittoli language family is the dominant linguistic group in central-western Azhora. Standard Mittoli — the prestige dialect used in formal writing, commerce, and cross-regional communication — is based on the speech of the Caeras valley cities. But Mittoli spoken in the northern hill country (where it shades toward the rougher consonants of the Bay of Lol dialect), the deep western forests (where it has absorbed vocabulary from older sources that linguists cannot confidently trace), and the eastern transitional zone (where it mixes with Pyric influences) are all distinct enough to cause regular miscommunication and occasional diplomatic incidents.

The name "Mittolo" itself is pronounced differently in different parts of the region — "mih-TOL-oh" in the cities, "MIT-uh-luh" in the hill country, and "meh-TOL" in the old western forest settlements, clipped and flat.

## The Dark Groves

The dense, dark patches of forest visible across Mittolo — older growth, different in character from the surrounding woodland — are called the Dark Groves by most Mittoli, though each one has its own local name. They are not avoided exactly, but they are treated with a certain care. Hunters do not make camps in them. Children are told not to play in them after dark. The explanations given for this vary: "the trees are too close," "the animals behave strangely," "it is old and the old things don't like noise." None of these explanations are satisfying on examination, and Mittoli people are generally not interested in examining them.

Academic expeditions into the Dark Groves have consistently reported two things: that the interiors are unusually quiet even by dense-forest standards, and that their maps, when compared afterward, consistently show the Groves as slightly smaller than they appeared to be when inside them. This discrepancy has not been explained.
