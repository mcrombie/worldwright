# Ovesos

The Oveth is one of five rivers that descend from the upland country north of Telemonia and west of the main Lizeem plain, gathering drainage from a rolling, half-forested transitional zone before joining the Lizeem's upper reaches some forty miles above Minora. Where the Oveth crosses its widest valley — a stretch of bottomland the people of the region call the Sorten, "the wide seat" — the kingdom of Ovesos has stood for as long as the valley has been farmed.

The name follows the river, as inland Mittoli names often do. *Oves-* traces to the root *oves*, meaning lower valley, the wide place where hill country flattens into cultivable ground; *-os* is the Mittoli collective for a settled people or political body. Ovesos: the people of the wide valley bottom. The Galans who buy Ovesian apples and barley use the name without particular feeling. The Minorans who receive Ovesian water-rights petitions use it with the slight flatness of officials who have received many such petitions and found most of them premature. The Ovesians use it with the unselfconscious specificity of people who have lived in one valley long enough that naming the valley and naming themselves is the same act.

## Geography

The Oveth is not the Lizeem. It rises in the upland between Telemonia's northern ridgelines and the more open interior plateau, fed by springs, hill-streams, and the snow-melt of several elevated ridges. By the time it reaches the Sorten, it is navigable for light boats and substantial enough for irrigation. Below the Sorten it narrows, drops through a rocky lower section, and joins the Lizeem approaches above Minora. The Sorten itself is roughly twelve miles of valley floor where the Oveth slows, widens, and deposits what it has carried from the upland — not as deep or as dark as the Mithala sediment, but productive enough to support grain cultivation, orchards on the gentle slopes above the floodline, and pastoral farming on the ridges above.

No mountain wall marks Ovesos's borders to the north, west, or south. The terrain simply rises — into the plateau country shared with interior peoples to the north, into the Telemon highland edge to the south, into the forested upland to the west. What defines the kingdom's limits in these directions is not geography but water. The line where Ovesian water rights end and another polity's begin is the real border, and it is the source of most of the kingdom's external politics.

The climate is temperate and wet, sharing the Mittolo character without the main plain's scale. Winters are cold, short-summer, with spring floods that are more localized and more variable in timing than the main Lizeem's predictable annual rise. The Oveth floods quickly, recedes quickly, and does not deliver the renewing sediment sheets that make the Mithala plain what it is. What it delivers is reliable water, good gradient for mills, and a valley microclimate that produces fruit at a latitude where the main plain cannot.

## Agriculture and Economy

Ovesos feeds itself and exports the difference. The Sorten produces barley, hard wheat, and minor grains in quantities that exceed local need in most years; the surplus travels south-east toward the Lizeem and on to Minora and Nylon, and south through the Telemon edge markets to Gala. The orchard slopes above the Sorten's floodline are the kingdom's distinctive product. The apples of the Sorten — dense, acid, long-keeping, suited to pressing or storing through winter — have a commercial reputation that has traveled further than Ovesos itself. The Galans have been buying them long enough to know the specific character of trees grown from the valley's mineral-heavy, slightly damp soil conditions. The pear varieties are less exported, more local: eaten fresh by the valley communities and pressed for a winter drink that visitors from the downstream cities find either excellent or strange depending on whether they expected wine.

The upper Oveth's gradient drives mills — grain mills and fulling mills — that the flat Mithala cannot match. Mill sites on the upper river are significant enough that their ownership has been part of noble-family disputes for several generations. Several of the most productive mills sit in or near the Middle Reach, which gives the boundary dispute its economic dimension alongside its political one.

Livestock on the upland ridges — cattle and short-legged sheep adapted to rolling terrain — provide wool, meat, and the secondary agricultural economy the valley bottomland cannot accommodate. Upland herders move seasonally between summer plateau pasture and winter valley-edge grazing, and they carry a distinct culture and dialect variant from the Sorten farmers. They are simultaneously Ovesian and somewhat separate, as pastoral people adjacent to settled kingdoms tend to be.

## The Five Branch Countries

Ovesos is one of five polities that recognize themselves, and are recognized from outside, as the inner-branch countries — the kingdoms of the tributaries that feed the Lizeem above Minora. The phrase appears in Minoran water-rights records (*vel-thris-kael*, "the returning inner tributaries") and in Galan diplomatic correspondence and means, in practice, a political geography rather than a natural one: these five polities share an upstream position, a dependence on branch-river water, a set of ongoing competition with each other, and the common downstream relationship with Minoran authority that competition has forced them to navigate.

**Thelom** sits upstream of Ovesos on the same Oveth system — smaller, higher, more pastoral than agricultural. It controls the headwaters and the upper mill sites. It is Ovesos's traditional adversary; the current boundary dispute is the latest iteration of a competition that has produced three wars in the past five generations and two treaties whose language both sides have found adequate grounds for continuing the dispute.

**Ovesos** occupies the middle Oveth — the most productive stretch by agricultural output and the most populous of the five.

**Isoruous** sits at the northern edge of the system, where the main Lizeem channel runs above Minora. It is the northernmost of the five and the one with the most fraught external relationship — effectively a Minoran client state, its politics divided between factions oriented upstream and downstream. Ovesos's arrangement with Isoruous — Ovesian goods pass through the Isoruous staging grounds on terms negotiated with the Upstream Faction of Isor's River Council — is the most important external relationship the kingdom has, and the most precarious, because the arrangement depends on which faction currently holds enough council seats to honor it.

**Errath** occupies the eastern inner branch, a separate river and a separate valley system. The oldest dispute in the inner-branch country is between Ovesos and Errath; the original grievance is not remembered with any precision by either side, which has made it remarkably durable. Ovesos and Errath have been at formal peace for forty years and share a boundary that neither side is satisfied with. Errath and Thelom have periodically cooperated against Ovesos; this cooperation has never produced decisive results but has shaped Ovesian external policy, which tends toward preoccupation with the two-front problem.

**Nethereum** occupies the northwestern inner branch — a flood country built around the Nethermere, a seasonally-expanding basin lake. Its economy runs on fish, wet-adapted crops, and post-flood cattle pasture rather than orchard and grain. Governed by a Flood Council of distributed ridge communities; no single capital, no binding interlocutor. Currently maintaining a workmanlike commercial relationship with Ovesos — neither ally nor active rival, but present in each other's calculations whenever the inner-branch alignment shifts.

These five polities have no formal federation, no shared institution, and no agreement that has lasted more than a generation. What they share is the Lizeem's upper water system and the downstream obligations Minora imposes on all who use it. The Branch Courts can theoretically adjudicate inner-branch disputes; in practice, Branch Court decisions are only enforced when the party against whom they are decided is weaker than the enforcing party. This is the basic structure of all legal systems. Here it is simply more visible.

## The Boundary Dispute

The Middle Reach is approximately four miles of bottomland where the Oveth bends westward before turning south into the Sorten. The bend creates a wide deposit — not as productive as the Sorten itself, but prime bottomland by the standards of a country that does not have much of it. It also contains two of the most productive mill sites on the upper river.

A treaty concluded three generations ago, after the second of the three Ovesos-Thelom wars, assigned the Middle Reach to Ovesos. A later treaty, after the third war, contained language that Thelom reads as restoring the stretch to joint administration and Ovesos reads as clarifying the first treaty without altering it. Both readings are in the original document. Both sides commissioned expert opinions; the experts agreed that the language was genuinely ambiguous, which the experts found intellectually interesting and both courts found useless.

Current status: the Middle Reach is farmed by Ovesian households under military protection during planting and harvest. Thelomi herders use the upland margins of the same area in summer. Both activities are conducted with the elaborate mutual awareness of people who know precisely where the other side is and what each side is capable of. Full war would be expensive for both and the king of Ovesos would prefer to settle; Thelom's council would prefer the land. What both sides want is a Minoran Branch Court ruling in their favor. The ongoing proceeding, now in its eleventh year, continues. Isoruous controls which Branch Court calendar items are prioritized, through its position as the transit point between Ovesos and Minora and the informal leverage this gives its River Council over which petitions move quickly. Both sides are aware of this and continue to pay attention to the Isoruous relationship accordingly.

The border communities along the Middle Reach itself — the farming villages that occupy both sides of the contested stretch — have family connections across the boundary and conduct the actual trade that makes the border functional. Their assessment of the dispute, when asked, is that the lawyers are keeping it alive because the lawyers are paid by duration and the farmers are losing by the same measure. This assessment has not been submitted as evidence to the Branch Court.

## Political Structure

Ovesos is a monarchy. The current king — Melos of the house Oveth-Hold — took a throne that was contested when his family won it, after the third Thelom war destroyed the previous ruling family's credibility. The present house consolidated by competence in the war's aftermath: distributing the Middle Reach allocation efficiently, settling the soldier-debt owed to families whose service had gone unrewarded, and making the Isoruous passage arrangement that gave Ovesos more commercial stability than it had seen in fifty years. They are not beloved in the heroic sense and are not expected to be.

Melos himself is cautious in the way that men who inherited contested thrones become cautious. He spent his early years in the Branch Court proceedings before he was crown-relevant and knows the treaty documents better than any living lawyer on either side of the dispute. His primary domestic challenge is not Thelom but the Water Council faction that believes the Isoruous arrangement undervalues Ovesian leverage and that Ovesos should press for renegotiation. This faction is probably correct on the merits. The king's position is that it is correct about the wrong moment; Ovesos cannot afford to lose the arrangement if the Isoruous River Council's Downstream Faction loses enough seats to honor it. Both sides of this argument make it at every Water Council session. It has not been resolved.

The **Water Council** is the governing body beneath the monarchy. Its membership is the noble families whose political standing is directly tied to their water rights — each major family holds title to a stretch of the Oveth or one of its feeder channels, and the title describes not only land ownership but allocation priority during dry years. The Council arbitrates disputes between families, advises the king on external affairs, and acts as legislative body for changes to water-right arrangements. Representation is by seniority of water-right claim, not by population; the oldest grants sit at the top. The friction between formal seniority and economic weight is the routine source of Water Council politics, because several of the most productive agricultural families in the Sorten hold relatively recent grants.

Below the noble families are the farming communities: freehold farmers with their own small water-right grants, tenant farmers working noble-family land under varied arrangements, and the craftspeople and laborers of the three main market towns. The upland herding communities sit somewhat outside this structure — they have land use agreements rather than water rights, they pay different obligations, and they have a tradition of providing military service that gives them practical leverage without formal standing.

## Language

Inner-branch Mittoli. A variant of Standard Mittoli fully intelligible to any downstream speaker, differing in vocabulary for valley terrain, water administration, and the specific practices of the branch-country environment. Key regional terms:

*Oves* — lower valley, the flat where hill country opens into farming ground; the place-name root.

*Thris-kael* — inner tributary; literally "the returning inside water." The Standard Mittoli term used in Branch Court proceedings; Ovesian speakers use it when writing to Minora and something locally specific when talking to each other.

*Vel-sorten* — bottomland allocation; the distribution of flood-renewed ground across farming claims after the annual Oveth rise. The process is managed by Water Council officials and generates the bulk of internal legal disputes.

*Osk-milis* — water-right seniority; the hierarchy by which families claim allocation in dry years. The phrase is old enough to appear in the oldest Ovesian land records and has no clear etymology in Standard Mittoli; it may be a survival of an earlier dialect form from before the Compact-period standardization.

Ovesian formal legal language differs from Minoran formal language in a recognizable way. Where Minoran legal speech tends toward the technical and precise — current measurements, certified weights, dated inspection records — Ovesian legal speech tends toward the genealogical and historical. The most important questions in a water-rights dispute are always: who was here first, and what did the document say. Documents are quoted at length. Ancestors are named. The Minoran Branch Court clerks who transcribe inner-branch proceedings have noted in their own administrative records that Ovesian submissions are typically twice as long as Minoran submissions and contain three times the genealogical material, a pattern consistent enough to have been listed in the Branch Court's standard intake guidance.

## Relations with Neighbors

**Gala** — Ovesian orchard and grain exports reach Gala through two routes: river (south-east via Isoruous, down the Lizeem approaches, south to the coast) and overland (south through the Telemon edge markets, into the Galan lowlands). The Galans know Ovesos as a steady supplier of quality apples and acceptable grain. The Guild of Assessors has twice been asked to arbitrate inner-branch disputes, both times at Minora's request; both times the Assessors produced decisions that no party found satisfying, which the Galans consider evidence of good arbitration.

**Telemonia** — South of Ovesos, adjacent but not warm. Ovesos has hired Telemon mercenary bands three times in the past five generations: twice for the Thelom wars, once for the Errath border realignment. The Telemon performed the contracted work and left when the contract ended. Ovesos paid what was agreed and found the experience impressive and not particularly comfortable. The Telemon do not linger, do not become familiar, and do not improve the odds of a campaign without changing the character of the force they augment. The Ovesian military tradition — adequate for valley defense, insufficient for sustained aggressive campaigning — accepts Telemon augmentation with clear eyes about what it means and what it does not.

**Minora** — The downstream authority and the arbitration body Ovesos most frequently invokes and most frequently finds frustrating. Branch Court rulings on inner-branch disputes are legally sound and practically enforced only when both parties agree to enforce them. Ovesos's factors in Minora are among the more experienced inner-branch lobbyists in the Branch Court system — respected for their documentary preparation, not listened to except when their interests align with what the Branch Courts want to decide anyway. The First Divider's office receives Ovesian petitions with the patience of an institution that has received similar petitions for two centuries. Ovesos knows this and continues submitting.

**Isoruous** — The most important external relationship the kingdom has. The Isoruous passage arrangement — negotiated with the Upstream Faction of the Isor River Council, which controls the staging grounds at Vel-Oss where Ovesian goods transfer to Lizeem barges — is the commercial artery through which Ovesian products reach the downstream market. Its periodic renegotiation is the event that Ovesian commercial policy organizes around. When the arrangement is stable, the kingdom is stable. When it is under pressure, everything downstream of it is under pressure. The Isoruous Upstream Faction knows this, prices accordingly, and maintains enough goodwill to keep the arrangement worth having while charging enough to make it the subject of Water Council complaints every other year. The arrangement's precariousness is structural: it holds only while the Upstream Faction holds enough River Council seats to honor it.

## Character

Ovesos is not a place that expects to be famous. Its self-image is agricultural and managerial: a kingdom that grows food in a valley, manages water carefully, and prosecutes its boundary claims through the correct legal channels because the alternative is a war that neither the grain nor the orchards can survive without damage.

The Ovesian character, as neighbors describe it, is careful, long-memoried, and slightly aggrieved. Long-memoried because the water-rights tradition requires it — you cannot argue for a claim that predates your grandfather without knowing your grandfather's documents. Slightly aggrieved because the inner-branch countries are visibly secondary to the downstream civilization, and this is noticeable to anyone who has traveled from the Sorten to Minora and back. Careful because in a place where the political stakes are perpetually bottomland and mill sites, the cost of a mistake is not abstract.

The valley festivals are agricultural and specific: the Planting Feast before the Oveth's spring rise, the Allocation Settlement when flood-renewed ground is divided among farming claims, and the Harvest Close when the Water Council meets to reconcile the year's water usage against the recorded allocations. The Harvest Close is the closest thing Ovesos has to a civic ceremony — a formal accounting conducted in public, before the full Council and whatever observers attend, in which the year's water management is reviewed and the claims for the following year are registered. It is not entertaining. It is very well attended.
