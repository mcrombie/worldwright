# The Ibenwood

Southwest of Mittolo, between the Oremindi Mountains in the north and the peninsulas of the southern coast, covering the interior of southwestern Azhora from the edge of the western sea corridor to the margins of the central heartland, lies the Ibenwood — the great forest, the old forest, the one that was here before the records and will almost certainly be here after them. It is the largest continuous woodland on the continent. It is also one of the least understood, which is not a phrase that scholars deploy carelessly when talking about geography. There is an understanding of what the Ibenwood is in the way that there is an understanding of what a deep ocean is: the surface is accessible, the edges are navigable, and the interior operates according to rules that have not been fully established.

The name is a composite. *-Wood* is Standard Mittoli — the outsiders' word for forest, practical and descriptive. *Iben* is older, from the substrate vocabulary of the Forest Mittoli peoples who live within it: their word for the forest itself, the deep interior, the living dark that predates naming. The full word *Ibenwood* is therefore something like "the Iben-forest," which is redundant in a way that reveals its history — two peoples describing the same thing, each in their own language, producing a name that layers the outside view over the inside name. The Forest Mittoli speakers do not use the compound form. They say *Iben*. The word is singular and does not require explanation within the communities that use it.

*Ibenale* — the coastal plain that runs along the forest's western edge — takes its name from the same root: the plain of the Iben, the country along the Iben's margin. The coastal peoples of Alezhor, Ibenale, and the western Legemum coast live at the edge of the Iben and have always lived at the edge of the Iben, and the edge has shaped them in proportion to how seriously they take its proximity.

## Scale and Position

The Ibenwood covers an area that is, by any reasonable measure, enormous. It runs from the Oremindi foothills in the northeast — where the forest descends the mountain slopes and meets the western sea corridor at the point where Ibenale's plain pinches out — southward along the interior of the coast, past the latitude of Alezhor, thinning as it approaches the northern edges of the Dinova peninsula and the drier conditions that the Meroshe eventually imposes. Its eastern boundary is less sharply defined: the forest thins gradually into the transitional country that borders the Mittolo heartland, and the exact line between "Ibenwood" and "western Mittolo forest" is contested by cartographers in a way that suggests the forest itself does not recognize the distinction. The trees on the Mittolo side are the same trees. The soil is the same soil. The quality that makes the deep interior what it is does not stop at any boundary a scholar has drawn.

The Dark Groves of Mittolo — those dense, anomalous patches of ancient woodland scattered through the heartland, which silence expeditions and produce maps that disagree with themselves — are, in the view of the Forest Mittoli scholars who have been asked about them, not anomalies at all. They are the Iben. The Iben once covered more of Azhora than it does now, and what remains in the Mittolo heartland are fragments of that earlier extent, cut off from the main body but continuous with it in everything except space. Whether this is literally true in a geographic sense or is a way of saying something about the nature of the forest that does not translate into geographic language is a question that Forest Mittoli scholars find less interesting than the questioner typically does.

## The Edge

The Ibenwood's edge is one of the most remarked-upon experiences in Azhoran travel literature, and this is partly because it is genuinely remarkable and partly because it is impossible to adequately describe, which makes writers try harder.

The light changes within twenty feet of the tree line. This is the first thing, and it happens before the canopy is close enough to block direct sun. The quality of the light shifts — travelers from different traditions and different eras have described it as going grey, going green, going towards something that is neither color but is a new condition of illumination that the words for colors were not designed to describe. The change is immediate and consistent. It has been reported at every point along the Ibenwood's edge from the northern Ibenale corridor to the Alezhor coast, in every season, by travelers who had not read previous accounts of it.

The sound cuts off. This is the second thing, and it is more disorienting than the light. At the tree line, the sounds of the coast — wind off the open sea, surf, birds that live at the shore — end. Not reduce, not muffle: end, over a distance of a few yards. The Ibenwood is not silent inside; it has its own sounds. But they are different sounds, and the transition is not gradual. Travelers describe it as crossing a threshold, which is the word that keeps appearing: a threshold, a crossing, a place where what was true stops being true and something else becomes true instead. Experienced guides on the western corridor treat the moment of entry as requiring a pause, a reorientation, an explicit acknowledgment that you are no longer where you were.

What waits on the other side of this threshold is the old-growth interior: canopy that has been continuous for longer than any record extends, trees whose rings become too compressed to count somewhere past three thousand years, light filtered through so many layers of leaf that it arrives at the forest floor changed beyond what any description of filtered light is prepared for. The forest floor is not dark, exactly — there is light — but the light carries no warmth from its direction and casts no shadows in the way that open-air light casts shadows. Things at the forest floor are visible. Their relationship to the light that makes them visible is not the same as above the canopy.

## The Interior

The deeper interior of the Ibenwood has not been fully mapped. This is a factual statement with several implications. The maps that exist of the interior were made by people who went in and came out, and the maps they produced when compared against each other show discrepancies that cannot be explained by the normal variations in survey technique. The distances do not agree. Two careful surveyors following the same route, starting from the same point, return with measurements that are the same in every detail except the numbers, which differ in ways that cannot be attributed to instrument error. The orientation is consistent; the scale is not.

The Forest Mittoli peoples, when this phenomenon is described to them, are not surprised. The word they use for the deep interior — *vel-iben*, the further Iben, the Iben beyond the Iben — is distinguished in their substrate vocabulary from the edge country in ways that include, among other things, the expectation that directional reckoning will work differently there. This is not described as a problem to be solved. It is described as a property of the place, like the quality of the light or the behavior of certain animal species that do not exist outside the forest. The deep interior is the kind of place where distances behave like that. You navigate by other means.

What those other means are, the Forest Mittoli communities do not explain to outside visitors in any complete way. The partial explanations that have been recorded involve attention to sound patterns, growth orientations, the behavior of water at the forest floor, and other observables that experienced residents learn to read. None of the partial explanations account for all of the navigation that interior inhabitants demonstrably perform. The remainder is presumably in the part of the explanation that was not provided.

What the interior contains, beyond trees and the peoples who have always lived among them, is a matter of significant speculation in outside scholarship. The gold-bearing hills above Alezhor are within the Ibenwood, and the veins there have been mined for centuries under arrangements that are well-attested even if their specifics are not documented. Ruins consistent with the style of the ancient coastal settlements have been reported in several deep-interior locations, which would imply that the forest was not always as dense as it is now — that there were times when the light reached the ground in places that are now perpetual canopy, and people built in those places, and the forest grew back over them. Whether these reports are reliable is uncertain; the reliable test of a report about the Ibenwood interior is whether it can be retraced, and the interior's navigational properties make retracing difficult.

The forest peoples themselves have structures in the interior — settlements, meeting places, sites that function as what scholars would call sacred spaces, though the Forest Mittoli vocabulary for what those sites are does not translate cleanly into the categories scholars use. These are not ruins. They are current and maintained and occupied on whatever schedule the forest peoples use, which is not the same schedule as the coastal settlements' calendar. Outside visitors have seen some of them. Outside visitors have written accounts of what they saw. The accounts agree on broad outlines and disagree on specifics in ways that mirror the mapping discrepancies — the same qualities of place, the different numbers.

## The Forest Peoples

The communities that live within the Ibenwood rather than at its edge are collectively called the forest peoples by the coastal regions, which is a collective label for what is in practice several distinct communities with distinct internal structures, distinct histories, and distinct relationships to the forest and to each other. They share the Forest Mittoli language family, or more precisely they share the substrate layer of that family — the old vocabulary that predates contact with Standard Mittoli speakers, the words for phenomena that do not exist outside the Ibenwood. They share a relationship with the deep interior that the edge communities do not have. They do not share a political structure, a single leadership, or an agreed external representation.

What outside parties regularly discover when trying to negotiate with "the forest peoples" is that the person or community they are negotiating with has authority over some things and not others, and the limits of that authority are not mapped in advance. The Alezhor cities have spent centuries developing working relationships with specific communities over specific resources in specific areas, and those relationships are functional, and they are not transferable to other communities or other areas. The non-transferability is not malicious; it reflects the actual structure of how authority and knowledge are distributed within the Ibenwood. No one has authority over the whole forest. No one could.

The substrate vocabulary of Forest Mittoli contains several words that outside linguists have not been able to find referents for — words that appear in speech in contexts suggesting concrete meaning, that Forest Mittoli speakers can use consistently and in ways that show they are describing something real, but that no outside observer has been able to identify as corresponding to any observable phenomenon. The most frequently cited is *vel-thraun-iben* — sometimes translated as "the living agreement" or "the forest's attention" or "what the Iben knows about you." The translations are attempts to render something that the substrate language apparently has a precise term for and the outside languages do not. When Forest Mittoli speakers are asked what it means directly, they give answers that are individually coherent and collectively varied in ways that suggest either that the concept requires contextual demonstration to convey, or that the full meaning is not something that outside parties are expected to receive, or both.

## What the Ibenwood Does Not Explain

Scholars from the Caeras Valley Academy who have studied the Ibenwood extensively tend to come away with a common observation: the forest raises more questions per answered question than any other subject of study they have engaged with. This is not a criticism. It is an observation about the density of the unresolved.

The Dark Groves of Mittolo — the patches of anomalous old-growth scattered through the heartland, the quiet interiors, the maps that shrink — have no agreed explanation. The Forest Mittoli position that they are the Iben, present in the heartland as a remnant of earlier extent, is one explanation. It is internally consistent. It does not explain the spatial behavior.

The acoustic transition at the tree line — the threshold quality, the immediate change — has no established mechanism. It has been observed too consistently to be dismissed as perception, but what it is a perception of has not been determined.

The substrate vocabulary of the oldest Forest Mittoli texts contains words for phenomena that current Forest Mittoli speakers recognize as real but cannot demonstrate to outsiders. The texts themselves are old enough that dendrochronological dating of the wood they are written on produces the same result as dating the oldest trees: the rings become too compressed, somewhere past three thousand years, and the dating stops working.

What was in the Ibenwood before the oldest dateable growth is not established. What the Ibenwood was doing during the period that Mittoli oral tradition calls the First Silence — the abrupt abandonment of the Mittolo heartland's ancient organized civilization — has not been asked in any outside record, as far as scholarship has found. It has presumably been asked inside the Ibenwood. The answer, if there is one, has not been shared.
