# Telemonia

Between Pyros to the west, the base of the Legemum hills to the south, and the Galan lowlands to the east, the terrain rises into a highland country of steep ridges, narrow river valleys, and open upland pasture that the surrounding coastal and agricultural cultures have spent several centuries learning to leave alone. This is Telemonia. Its people — the Telemon — are warriors in the specific sense that the word sometimes means and more often does not: not people who fight when pressed or who celebrate fighting as a virtue, but people for whom the cultivation of combat is the primary work of a life, the way that agriculture is the primary work of a farming culture, carried on with the same systematic seriousness and the same expectation that it will be done correctly.

They are available for hire. They do not accept foreign settlement, foreign trade in the interior, or foreign politics. They have never been successfully conquered, which is a claim that has been tested enough times for the pattern to count as established.

---

## Geography

The highland country of Telemonia covers roughly sixty miles east to west and forty north to south — not large in absolute terms, but terrain that expands considerably when you are trying to move through it with an army. The ridgelines run northeast to southwest, creating a series of parallel valleys that funnel movement and give defenders a continuous choice of positions. The valley floors are fertile enough for grazing and subsistence agriculture; the ridges are forested in their lower reaches and rocky above the treeline; the passes between valleys are few, known to every Telemon child, and defensible by small numbers against very large forces.

The rivers that drain the highland move quickly — too quick and shallow for significant navigation, useful mainly for water supply and as obstacles. They flood unpredictably in spring. The Telemon build nothing in the flood plains.

The highland borders are not marked with walls or formal fortifications. The Telemon do not build walls around their territory. The terrain is the wall, and the people are the garrison, and every outsider who has tested this arrangement has concluded it is adequate.

The climate is cool and wet in winter, short-summer, with the kind of reliability that a pastoral people can plan around. The Telemon have planned around it for long enough to have it well understood. Their provisions are always in order.

## The Telemon

The Telemon are not a large people. The population of Telemonia has been estimated, from outside, at somewhere between sixty and a hundred thousand — enough to sustain the warrior culture, the pastoral economy, and the farms, but not enough that large numbers could go to war as mercenaries while leaving the highland undefended. The numbers work because everyone is available for defense, not because the population is large.

They are a compact, dark-complexioned, highland people who have been in these hills long enough that the hills are part of how they understand themselves. Not as metaphor — the Telemon do not speak much in metaphor — but as practical fact: they know this ground, every fold of it, every reliable water source, every ridge that provides cover and every path that turns treacherous in wet weather. This knowledge is part of training and part of inheritance. A Telemon warrior at home is a different instrument from a Telemon warrior anywhere else, and the difference is the ground.

They speak their own language — *Kellith*, in their own name for it — which has not been systematically studied by outside linguists because outside linguists have not been allowed in. What can be gathered from border-market contact and from the accounts of soldiers who have fought alongside Telemon mercenary bands is that it is grammatically unlike Mittoli, shares some sound patterns with older Pyrosi dialects, and has a vocabulary for categories of terrain, weather, and physical exertion that has no equivalent in any coastal language. Translators exist at the border markets; they are Telemon people who have chosen to serve that function, and their translations are understood by both parties to be selective.

The Telemon manner is direct to the degree that outside cultures read it as bluntness and sometimes as rudeness. It is neither. It is the communication style of a people who have found that clarity is more useful than cushioning, that an unclear statement costs more to correct later than a sharp one costs in discomfort now, and that time spent in social indirection is time not spent on something real. They are not without warmth — the accounts of soldiers who have served in Telemon mercenary bands consistently describe their camp companions as capable of deep loyalty and genuine humor — but the warmth is not performed in ways that outside cultures immediately recognize as warmth.

## The Warrior Tradition

A Telemon child begins structured training at the age of seven. The training is not primarily about weapons — weapons come later. The early years are endurance: cold water, long distances, lean provisions, night navigation, extended silence. The premise is that a warrior who cannot manage their body has no use for technique. Technique is taught once the body is trustworthy.

The formal training period ends at approximately seventeen or eighteen with an individual test: several days alone in the highland, in the hardest season, returning with proof of having managed. The proof is specific and varies by tradition within different Telemon families; what is common is that the test is not witnessed by anyone else, that no help is given, and that the return is what counts. Coming back is the achievement. The account given afterward is expected to be accurate and brief.

After the formal training period, a warrior belongs to a band — a unit of between twenty and forty fighters who train together, fight together, and whose members will be responsible for the recitation of each other's deeds at death. The band is the primary social unit. Marriage, household, kinship — these exist alongside the band but do not supersede it. A Telemon warrior's band is the audience whose judgment matters most.

There is no class of professional rememberers equivalent to the Avite *veth*. The Telemon band is its own record. The members carry the dead with them in a specific recitation — names, deeds, the manner of death — spoken at the three seasonal ceremonies that structure the Telemon year. This recitation is not performed for audiences or preserved in written form. It is for the band. An outside scholar who has sought to collect these recitations for study has found the request declined with the consistency of established policy rather than individual reluctance.

The warrior tradition does not distinguish categorically between men and women. Both train. The mercenary bands that go out to fight in others' wars are predominantly composed of men, not by rule but by convention and by the household calculation that women's presence in the highland defense during a mercenary campaign's absence is more valuable than their presence in a foreign field. When the highland is invaded, no such calculation applies. Every Telemon fights. The accounts of failed invasions make this clear.

## The Mercenary Practice

Telemonia does not trade in goods. It trades in fighters.

The bands available for mercenary contract are organized through a formal process conducted at the border markets. A power seeking Telemon mercenaries sends a representative to the nearest border market site, states their need, and waits. The Telemon do not advertise; they evaluate. The representative may be kept waiting for days or may be met quickly, depending on circumstances that are not explained to them. The band leader or council of band leaders who will decide makes inquiries through the border-market translators: the nature of the conflict, the terrain, the duration, the pay, the command structure the Telemon will be expected to operate within. This last item is significant. Telemon mercenary bands serve under their own leadership on the field. They receive strategic instructions and coordinate with allied forces, but tactical command is the band leader's. Employers who cannot accept this are told so and the negotiation ends.

The terms of a Telemon mercenary contract are specific: duration, conditions, payment in goods or metal rather than political arrangement, and the departure clause. Telemon bands leave when the contract ends. Not when the war ends. Not when the employer's situation has improved. When the contract ends. This has occasionally created awkward situations for employers who allowed their planning to depend on Telemon presence beyond the agreed period. The Telemon find these situations the employer's problem.

Payment comes in the form of metal goods, specific agricultural materials the highland does not produce, and occasionally worked items of value that the warrior's household can use. Political agreements, land grants, titles, and positions are refused. What the Telemon want from mercenary service is material that improves life in the highland and nothing that creates obligation to the world outside it.

The reputation is consistent enough across generations to function as currency. A contract with a Telemon band is understood, by everyone who has worked with them, to mean what it says. They will not be bribed to change sides. They will not be manipulated through the fear of losing them — when the contract ends, they leave regardless. They will not claim to have done what they did not do or to have been present where they were not. Among mercenary forces, this is more remarkable than it sounds. The Galans, who conduct most of the border-market contract negotiations, have accumulated over generations a body of practical intelligence about how to represent a client's needs to Telemon band leaders in terms that the Telemon will engage with. This is a commercial specialty, and the Galan negotiators who hold it are valued by every coastal and inland power that has needed Telemon fighters.

## The Defense of Telemonia

Telemonia has been invaded. The word "successfully" is doing work in the claim that it has never been successfully conquered — there have been incursions, campaigns, ambitious kings who decided that a highland warrior people of manageable population was a reasonable military objective, and early-period expansions by powers that had not yet received the information.

The information, once received, is consistent: when Telemonia is invaded, the Telemon stop being the mercenaries that neighboring powers have watched in foreign service and become something else. The formal discipline is still present. The effectiveness is higher. And the terrain, which the invaders may have traveled through with some discomfort, becomes an instrument. The passes seal. The valleys produce defenders from positions that were not visible before. Supply lines for the invading force find themselves attacked by small groups that vanish. Encampments that seem secure are not.

The Telemon do not pursue invaders beyond their own borders when they withdraw. They stop at the border and return to the highland. This is not magnanimity — it is the application of the same principle that makes the defense effective: the terrain that makes Telemonia unbeatable stops at its own edge. A Telemon army pursuing a retreating force onto coastal lowland or agricultural plain is away from its advantage. The Telemon understand this and act on it. The invaders withdraw into their own territory. The Telemon return to theirs. The border markets reopen eventually.

This refusal to pursue has been misread, on more than one occasion, as weakness. Powers that have interpreted the pullback as evidence of Telemon exhaustion, limited numbers, or political constraint have occasionally concluded that a second campaign was worth attempting. The accounts of second campaigns are the most detailed available, since the commanders of forces that fail twice tend to write about it more than commanders who succeed, and the accounts share certain features: the defense was harder on the second campaign than the first, the Telemon appeared to have prepared specifically for the approach used, and the withdrawal, when it finally came, was achieved at considerable cost.

## Political Structure

Telemonia is governed by a king. The kingship is hereditary within a warrior family whose lineage claim to the position is old enough to be accepted, but it is constrained by two facts: the king must be combat-capable, and the band leaders must recognize the king's authority.

A king who cannot fight leads ceremonially. Real tactical command passes to the war-council of senior band leaders, who act in the king's name but according to their own judgment. This has happened; it is not considered a crisis. The Telemon do not have strong feelings about the distinction between formal authority and functional authority as long as the function is performed well.

The band leaders are the governing class in practice. They arbitrate disputes within and between bands, manage the border-market arrangements, decide collectively whether to accept a mercenary contract, and form the council that makes the few decisions requiring coordination above the band level. The king presides over this council and has the casting vote in genuine deadlock, which is rare because the band leaders find deadlock wasteful. A council that cannot agree is a council spending time on words when it could spend time on something real.

There is no written law code. The customs are old enough to be known, stable enough not to require constant arbitration, and in cases of genuine dispute, the council's decision is the resolution. This system would be inadequate for a complex commercial society. For a society organized around warrior bands and pastoral economy, it is sufficient.

## Economy

Telemonia is not poor. It is deliberately self-contained.

The highland produces cattle and horses — Telemon horses are a specific highland breed, compact and sturdy and well-suited to rough terrain, that the border markets sell at prices that reflect the breed's military utility. They produce grain in the valley floors sufficient for the population, supplemented by hunting and the pastoral economy. They work iron from the highland ore deposits — not the refined craft iron of the best mainland smiths, but serviceable, and in sufficient quantity that every Telemon fighter is adequately armed without depending on imported metal. The highland timber is good for building and for charcoal.

What they do not produce: tin (they buy it, minimally, at the border markets, from Legemum indirectly), certain cultivated foods that do not grow in the highland climate, and worked goods of the quality that coastal cities produce. These are the categories of import, conducted at the border markets in exchange for horses, cattle, and mercenary contracts.

The border markets are not Telemon institutions — they are meeting points at the highland's edge, operated by traders from Gala and Legemum who maintain the infrastructure and conduct the exchange. Telemon people come to the markets to trade; they do not operate stalls, do not reside at the market sites, and do not bring foreign traders into the interior. The distinction between "here" and "inside" is maintained with consistency. Traders who have attempted to follow the returning Telemon have found themselves turned around at the highland edge, with varying degrees of firmness.

## Among the Neighbors

**Legemum** — The Telemon presence to the north of Legemum's base has functioned, practically, as a buffer between the mining peninsula and the inland powers that might otherwise covet what it produces. The Legemi did not arrange this and have not attempted to formalize it into an alliance, which is wise — the Telemon do not make alliances. The relationship is one of commercially organized mutual benefit: Legemum tin reaches Telemon border markets in small quantities, Telemon horses and occasional contract fighters pass through Legemum's trade contacts outward. Both parties understand the arrangement without discussing it.

**Gala** — The Galans broker most of the Telemon mercenary contracts and conduct the bulk of the border-market trade. This is the closest thing Telemonia has to an economic relationship with an outside power. The Galan negotiators have accumulated, over generations, the expertise to represent a client's need in terms the Telemon will engage with — which requires understanding how the Telemon think about obligation, about terrain, about what a contract is and what it is not. This expertise is not easily transferred and is one of the more specific commercial assets the city of Gala holds. The Telemon, for their part, find the Galans useful and reliable in the specific sense of: they know the rules and they follow them. This is the Telemon version of a strong recommendation.

**Pyros** — West Pyros shares a cultural memory of the volcanic land and a degree of mutual intelligibility between their older dialects and Kellith that suggests some ancient common history. This has not produced closeness. The Telemon and the Pyrosi are adjacent peoples who have found that their differences are more operational than their similarities. West Pyros's agricultural tradition and the Telemon pastoral-warrior economy have minimal overlap; they trade occasionally and do not interfere with each other. The Telemon have, on at least two occasions, fought as mercenaries against Pyrosi forces in other powers' wars without this appearing to significantly disturb the border relationship. The Pyrosi read this as evidence that the Telemon have no loyalties. The Telemon read this as evidence that the Pyrosi do not understand what a contract is.

**Ascarth and Aevis** — The Avites and the Telemon are the two warrior cultures of the region, and their mutual assessment is the kind of guarded respect that develops between people who recognize quality in a domain they both take seriously, and who are too honest to pretend otherwise. The Avites find the Telemon's indifference to glory philosophically incorrect. The Telemon find the Avite elaboration of warrior culture — the epics, the bronze aesthetics, the public performance of heroic identity — an expenditure of energy on display that could be spent on capability. Neither has said this to the other's face in productive terms. Telemon bands have served alongside Avite forces in shared employers' campaigns on several occasions. By every account, the operational relationship is effective and the social relationship is minimal. They fight well together and eat separately.
