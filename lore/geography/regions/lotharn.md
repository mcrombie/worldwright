# The Lotharn Mountains

South of the Mithala plain, where the braided channels of the lower Lizeem give way to rising ground and the flat country becomes something older, the land begins to rise. Not dramatically — this is the first thing to understand about the Lotharn. There is no moment of confrontation, no wall of stone rising from the flatland the way the Oremindi rise from the Ibenale coast. The Lotharn come on gradually: gentle swells in the terrain that become longer ridges, that become the first real elevations, that eventually resolve into what can properly be called mountains, though the word lands differently here than it does further west.

These are old mountains. The distinction matters in a way that is visible before it is understood. The Oremindi are young mountains — young on the scale that matters for stone — and youth in mountains looks like drama: sharp peaks, sheer faces, glaciers still actively carving, snowfields that never melt, ridgelines that cut the sky. The Lotharn have had hundreds of millions of years to be worked on. Rain, frost, river, and the slow pressure of their own weight have had time that younger mountains have not had, and they have used it. The peaks are rounded. The ridgelines are broad-backed rather than knife-edged. The faces are not sheer; they are long, forested slopes inclined at angles that feet can manage. The Lotharn do not challenge you to cross them. They simply ask you to take the time.

What is left when a mountain range has been worn for that long is not diminishment. It is a particular kind of completeness. The Lotharn are fully themselves in a way that requires age to achieve.

## Geology

The Lotharn are one of the oldest exposed surface formations on the continent. Geologists from the Caeras Valley Academy who have studied the rock record identify stone in the deep valley cuts that was laid down before the Oremindi existed as anything other than a zone of weakness in the continental interior — before the collision events that threw up that younger range had even begun. The Lotharn are what Azhora was before it became what it currently is.

The evidence of this age is everywhere once you know to look for it. The valley walls reveal layer upon layer of sedimentary stone, tilted and folded by the events that built the range, then patiently stripped back by erosion until the cores are exposed. The exposed stone is not the dramatic basalt and granite of volcanic mountain ranges; it is ancient sedimentary rock, limestone and shale and sandstone turned on their sides and intruded by later veins of harder material, the whole record of seas that covered this land before there was any land that anyone would recognize. Fossils appear in roadside cuts and stream banks — shells of animals that lived in those ancient seas, preserved in the stone for longer than any current species has existed.

The mineralization of the Lotharn follows from their age and their geological history. The same forces that built and then eroded the range concentrated metals in the process. Iron is the most abundant, occurring in seams of rich ore throughout the central range. Coal follows — black seams visible in the valley walls and hillside cuts, carbon compressed from ancient forests that covered the range before the present forests, forests so old they predate any biological relationship to anything currently living. Copper is found in the southern sections, where later intrusive rocks brought metal-bearing fluids through the older formation. Lead, zinc, and traces of silver appear in specific geological associations that experienced Lotharn miners can identify. These are not new discoveries; the Lotharn communities have been mining for as long as there have been Lotharn communities, and the mining knowledge is as old and as specifically located as any tradition in the mountains.

## The Landscape

From a distance — from the Mithala plain to the north, watching the terrain rise into the haze — the Lotharn look like a long forested wall. This impression is accurate. The mountains are heavily, continuously, completely forested from their base to within a few hundred meters of their highest summits. There is no treeline in the Oremindi sense, where altitude and cold eventually terminate the forest against exposed rock and snow. The Lotharn's highest summits are not high enough or cold enough for that. They are simply high forested ridges from which the forest continues on both sides.

The forest of the Lotharn is not the Ibenwood. The distinction is important and the people who live in both regions maintain it firmly. The Ibenwood is evergreen, ancient, mysterious, its old-growth measuring age in millennia. The Lotharn forest is primarily deciduous — oak, chestnut, maple, hickory, walnut, beech, tulip poplar, and many others — and it is spectacular in autumn, when the entire range turns from green to a fire of reds, oranges, and yellows visible from the Mithala plain as a band of color along the eastern horizon. In winter it is bare and the ridgelines show their true shapes through the naked canopy. In spring the sequence of flowering and leafing out follows the elevation gradient, the lower valleys green weeks before the summits, so that from a distance the greening of the range appears to climb upward from the base over the course of two months.

The forest is old but old in a different sense from the Ibenwood. Individual trees in the Lotharn are centuries old; the oldest groves have not been cleared in any living memory. But the forest as a whole has been shaped by human activity — by clearings for settlements, by selective logging for construction timber and charcoal, by the burning practices of the valley peoples that maintained certain open ridgetop areas as seasonal grazing ground. This is managed old-growth, forest that knows people and has accommodated them without losing its essential character. The Lotharn forest is not mysterious. It is familiar, intimate, known in the way that a long-lived relationship produces knowing.

The valleys are the defining feature of the range. Dozens of them — some wide and agricultural, some narrow gorges through which rivers run white, some broad and flat-bottomed where glaciation left deposits now grown over with deep soil. Each valley has its own drainage, its own microclimate, its own relationship between the ridge walls above and the bottomland below. Each valley has had its own history of settlement, and the communities in each are distinct enough that Lotharn people refer to themselves by valley rather than by the range as a whole. "A Lotharn person" is not a meaningful category to the people who live there; "a Kemrath person" or "an Upper Olveth person" or "a Stonegate Valley person" are meaningful categories.

The rivers of the Lotharn flow in two directions. The northern face drains toward the Lizeem system and the Mithala plain; these streams join the Lizeem's distributaries and are part of the great river network that made the Mithala plain what it is. The southern face drains toward Amod and the country beyond it, contributing water to the streams that feed the Elagosi lake system and the rivers that cross the drier country to the east. The ridgeline that divides the two drainage systems is not sharp — the Lotharn's broad-backed summits mean that the watershed often runs through forest rather than along obvious topographic features, and the position of the divide has been the subject of boundary disputes between valley communities on either side for as long as boundary disputes have been recorded.

The southern face descends into **Amod**, a country of foothills, terraces, and pass roads that belongs neither fully to the mountains nor to the lake country below. Amod is where Lotharn streams are first captured into irrigation channels, where mountain goods are repacked for southern markets, and where the forested ridges give way to orchards, grazing slopes, and stone-walled fields. The Elagosi lake system is visible from the higher Amodian passes on clear days — a feature of some consequence, since it means the southern Lotharn also offers a view of the lake empire and everything it implies. Lotharn people often describe Amod as "downhill" rather than "south," which is geographically imprecise and culturally exact.

## The Passes

The Lotharn are crossed. This is the essential contrast with the Oremindi, which are circumnavigated. The Oremindi have four passes in their entire length, three of which are dangerous, and the fourth of which leads only to Sevron. The Lotharn have passes beyond easy counting — a range this old, with a drainage pattern this complex, cut by rivers from both sides over millions of years, has been perforated by route possibilities that the valley peoples have exploited for as long as the mountains have been inhabited.

This does not mean the passes are easy. The Lotharn crossing is not a stroll; even the most-traveled passes require a day and a half of actual mountain walking at modest elevations, with the attendant weather unpredictability and the specific difficulty of descent into an unfamiliar valley system on the far side. But they are navigable by ordinary travelers without special preparation, and most of the major passes have had permanent inns and waypoints established at their highest sections for centuries, maintained by the communities that benefit from the trade the passes carry.

The pass communities are their own cultural type within the mountains. They are not quite valley people — their orientation is horizontal, toward the movement of travelers, rather than vertical, toward the ridges above. The pass inns are among the most cosmopolitan places in eastern Azhora: a single night at a busy Lotharn pass inn in the trading season will bring encounters with Mittoli merchants, Plains traders, Iberos coastal agents, and the occasional traveler from directions that the innkeeper has trouble placing. These encounters accumulate into a culture that is broadly informed about the world in ways that the deeper valley communities are not.

The eastern Lotharn passes — those descending toward the Plains — have been especially significant in Azhoran trade history. The routes from the Mittoli interior to the Iberos maritime world all cross the Lotharn somewhere, and the communities controlling the major eastern passes have at various points been wealthy enough to be worth conquering and independent enough to resist. The Lotharn pass histories are some of the more entertaining reading in the Caeras Academy's regional archives: a consistent pattern of outside powers underestimating the difficulty of holding mountain passes against the people who know every side route through the surrounding ridges.

## The Valley Communities

Each Lotharn valley is a world. This is said as a description, not a metaphor. The distances between valleys, measured in straight lines, are often short; measured in travel time and cultural exchange, they have historically been enormous. Valleys on opposite sides of the same ridge could develop distinct dialects, distinct material culture traditions, distinct food traditions, and distinct political systems over the course of a few centuries of limited contact. The Lotharn has been encouraging this divergence since people first moved into it.

The valley communities share certain broad characteristics. They are insular by preference and practical adaptation: generations of limited contact with outside powers have produced cultures that are self-sufficient in essentials, deeply skilled in the specific resources their valley provides, and resistant to external authority in a way that is not aggressive but is extremely persistent. Lotharn valley people do not rebel against lowland administration. They simply continue doing things the way they have always done them while technically complying with whatever requirements the outside authority has imposed, and they outlast every administrative system that has attempted to change them.

Mining communities have the most fully developed material cultures. The communities in the central iron and coal districts have been working their specific seams for so many generations that the knowledge is architectural: each seam has a name, a history, a known behavior at depth, a tradition of techniques developed to manage its specific characteristics. New miners entering an established Lotharn mine do not learn general mining; they learn the lore of that mine, passed down in specific language that is partly technical and partly something closer to relationship.

The farming communities of the wider valleys are less distinctive from Mittoli practice but more culturally conservative: they preserve agricultural varieties, land management practices, and seasonal traditions that have been replaced or modified in the more dynamic Mittoli heartland. Several cultivated plant varieties now considered heritage specimens in the Academy's agricultural records were preserved in Lotharn valley agriculture while being abandoned elsewhere. The valley peoples did not make this choice consciously; they were simply less in contact with the changing fashions of the lowlands.

The crafts traditions vary by valley and by available material. The ironworking communities of the central range have developed techniques adapted to the specific qualities of Lotharn iron ore. The woodworking communities of the better-forested valleys have traditions of timber-frame construction and furniture-making using specific local wood species that have their own aesthetic character. The textile traditions of the higher, cooler valleys — where sheep have been kept on the seasonal upland pastures for as long as anyone has records — produce wool goods of a particular weight and weave that are identifiable throughout the Azhoran trade network and consistently valued for durability over elegance.

## Language

The language situation in the Lotharn is a linguist's subject of permanent fascination. The valley peoples speak Mittoli-derived languages — the family connection is clear, the grammar is recognizable, the majority of the vocabulary is shared — but each valley has had enough isolation to develop distinctive features, and the sum total of Lotharn dialect variation is wider than the variation between any two standard regional Mittoli dialects in the lowlands.

Beyond the surface variation, there is a substrate layer. Beneath the Mittoli grammar and vocabulary, in expressions and terms that resist analysis by Mittoli linguistic methods, there is an older system. The substrate is most visible in place names — the valley names, the mountain names, the names of specific geological features and watercourses — which in many cases cannot be decomposed using any Mittoli root system. These names were here before the Mittoli came. What language they belong to is not established; the Academy's Linguistic Division has a file on the Lotharn substrate that has been growing for two centuries without converging on a conclusion.

The deepest valley communities, those with the least external contact, preserve the most substrate. Linguists who venture into the upper valleys of the central Lotharn find that the speech there contains proportions of unanalyzable material that suggest the substrate language — whatever it was — was the primary language of those communities within the historical period, and that the shift to Mittoli-based speech is relatively recent, recent enough that the substrate is still a living presence rather than a fossil layer.

The pass communities, at the other extreme, speak a heavily mixed variety that draws on Mittoli, Plains languages, and the Iberos coastal trade vocabulary, with Lotharn substrate visible only in the place names and certain terms of craft. They communicate effectively with everyone who passes through, which is the point.

## The Age in the Stone

The Lotharn do something that the Oremindi do not: they make their age visible. The Oremindi's age is geological but not legible — the peaks are dramatic but they do not show their formation. The Lotharn's exposed valley walls are a chronological record open to anyone who walks past them. Every road cut, every stream-carved gorge, every quarried hillside shows layers — the sequence of what this land was before it was this land.

Valley peoples who have lived all their lives next to these walls have absorbed this chronological presence in ways that do not translate easily into formal historical consciousness but are real nonetheless. A community that has always seen, at the end of every street, stone that was formed before any creature currently alive existed has a different baseline relationship to time than communities that live in built environments with no exposed geology. The lowland towns of Mittolo are surrounded by soil and vegetation; the past, there, is historical. In the Lotharn valleys the past is geological, and it is present.

Several valley communities maintain traditions that the Academy classifies as "deep time knowledge" — oral practices of engaging with the geological record specifically, not as curiosity but as ongoing relationship. The fossil communities — those whose valley walls are rich in ancient sea creatures preserved in limestone — have traditions of reading specific outcrops as calendars, of tracking which fossils appear at which layers and what this means about sequence, of understanding what "before" means on a scale that makes the founding of Minora seem recent. The Academy's geologists have found, repeatedly, that Lotharn fossil readers can predict where in a freshly exposed cut a specific type of fossil will appear before examining the cut, using knowledge of the rock sequence that matches the Academy's own geological methods without having been developed in contact with them.

What the valley peoples believe about the age of the Lotharn, and what that age means, is not a question they find easy to answer in lowland terms. The stone is very old. They live with it. It is not surprising to them. They find the lowland habit of treating geological age as astonishing rather than simply characteristic to be a peculiarity of people who do not have old stone walls.

## The Lotharn and the Continent

The Lotharn function as a permeable southern boundary to the Mithala plains and the wider Mittolo world. They are not an absolute barrier — nothing in the Lotharn is absolute — but they are a sufficient friction that crossing them requires intention, and the friction has historically been enough to produce cultural differentiation on the two sides. The Mithala plain and the regions beyond the Lotharn have developed separately enough that the crossing is a crossing into a different world, not dramatically but definitively.

From the north, the Lotharn are the edge of the known world in a loose sense: familiar in their forest character, approachable, but ending at their ridgelines a landscape that continues into the unfamiliar. From the south and east, from the lake country and the Plains and the Iberos coast, the Lotharn are the edge of the forested world — the point past which the open country and the Mediterranean east give way to the deep, closed, ancient-tree country of Mittolo and the Ibenwood beyond it.

The valley peoples are comfortable with this position. They live in the hinge between the two halves of the continent, which means they are not fully part of either, which means they have been left alone more than populations with more obviously valuable positions are left alone. The Lotharn have never been the seat of a continental power. They have been the route through which continental powers fought each other, and the people who maintained the inns and sold the horses and mended the equipment, and who were still there after the powers and their wars had moved on.

There is a Lotharn proverb that the lowland scholars have collected in several dialect forms, all meaning approximately the same thing: *Mountains do not move. It is the fighting that passes.*
