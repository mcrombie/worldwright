# Babon

Southeast of Trogo across the Trogo Channel lies Babon — a large island of dense tropical jungle, steep interior terrain, and a coast well enough known to Hama's maritime network to appear on commercial charts, and poorly enough understood beyond the coast that those same charts simply leave the interior blank. The coastal city of Tathilium is the island's point of contact with the outside world. Everything inland of Tathilium's sight-line is, to outside knowledge, a report rather than a map.

The name appears first in Hama commercial records approximately two hundred years ago, in the context of a resin trade. It does not derive from any Mittoli root or from any Moreshi family that has been studied. Whether it is the coastal people's own word, the inland peoples' word, or a Hama trader's approximation of something else entirely has not been established. The island's own coastal people use it without explaining where it comes from, which may mean it is theirs or may mean they have used it long enough that the origin no longer matters.

## The Trogo Channel

The channel separating Babon from Trogo's southeastern coast is navigable — narrow enough that the island is visible from the Trogo shore on clear days and wide enough that the crossing requires a proper vessel and some knowledge of the currents. The channel runs fast in its middle sections, where the gap between landmasses compresses the tidal flow. Vessels that know the timing cross without difficulty. Vessels that do not know the timing cross eventually, having been pushed several miles off course and acquired an appreciation for why local pilots exist.

The channel has been in regular use between Trogo's coastal communities and Tathilium for long enough that the relationship between the two coasts is one of familiar commerce rather than exploration. Trogo's transition-zone products — forest resins, particular timber, medicinal material — move outward through Tathilium as often as through Hama directly. Tathilium knows the Trogo coast and the Trogo coast knows Tathilium; what neither side discusses in depth is what lies further into the other's interior.

## Tathilium

Tathilium sits at the northeastern end of Babon's most hospitable coastal stretch — a natural harbor backed by a broad flat coastal plain, which is unusual on an island whose interior rises steeply into densely forested hills within a few miles of the water. The city is the largest settlement on the island by a considerable margin, and its size is a function of its position: it is where the channel opens, where the coastal plain allows construction, and where the island's only reliable anchorage for deep-draft vessels is located. Everything about Tathilium's existence follows from these facts.

The city is not primarily Azhoran in character, though it has absorbed enough Hama commercial presence over two centuries that certain districts — the warehousing quarter, the pilot's hall, the factor houses along the main harbor road — are recognizably shaped by that contact. The majority population is the island's coastal people, a distinct group neither closely related to the southern archipelago peoples nor to any Azhoran mainland tradition, with their own language, their own architectural forms, and their own calendar that does not correspond to either the Mittoli astronomical system or the Mithala flood calendar. The Hama factors who live in Tathilium have learned to work with this and find it less inconvenient than expected, because the Tathilium people are commercially minded in a way that translates.

The city's commerce is built around the island's coastal products — timber from the lower forest slopes, dried fish from the channel and surrounding sea, the resin trade that first brought Hama's attention here — and around its position as the channel waypoint for traffic between Trogo's coast and whatever lies further southeast. Vessels bound for the Azhor Stones or the southern archipelago that approach from the west pass through the channel and typically stop in Tathilium. This gives the city a transit economy on top of its export economy, and the combination makes it prosperous enough to maintain a population that the coastal plain's direct productivity alone could not support.

The Tathilium people do not venture into the interior of the island. This is not law or prohibition — it is simply not what they do, in the same way that the people of the Lizeem delta do not attempt to farm the deep Meroshe. The jungle begins at the inland edge of the coastal plain and the Tathilium people recognize that edge clearly. Visitors who ask about the interior are given accurate practical information — what is known about where the edge is, what the first few miles of forest are like, what is found there — and then a silence that is not unfriendly but is complete.

## The Coast

Babon's coast is not uniform. The northeastern stretch, where Tathilium stands, is the exception — the only section where the interior terrain does not push directly to the shore. Moving around the island in either direction, the coastal plain narrows and then disappears: jungle-covered hills run directly to the sea, producing dramatic cliff coastlines interrupted by the occasional small beach or river mouth. The outer coast, facing the open southern sea, is exposed enough that the few coastal communities beyond Tathilium are small, sheltered in specific geographic pockets, and not easily visited from the water.

These outer-coast communities are related to the Tathilium people but distinct — isolated by the terrain into their own development, with dialect differences and local traditions that Tathilium cannot always translate. They fish the reef and the deeper sea beyond it. They know the coast of their section of island and not much of any other section. Communication between them and Tathilium is by sea rather than overland; going through the interior is not done.

The reef around Babon's coast is an extension of the same system that surrounds the Azhor Stones, though richer and more varied for being anchored to a larger landmass with river outflow. Southern archipelago fishing vessels are seen in the outer reef sections seasonally. Their arrangement with the Babon coastal communities appears to predate any Azhoran involvement, and the specific terms of it have not been shared with Hama's commercial representatives despite polite inquiry.

## The Interior

The interior of Babon rises from the coastal plain into steep forested hills whose topography is not clearly mapped. The few accounts that exist — from traders who attempted to cross the island overland rather than sail around it, from Tathilium guides who accompanied early Hama expeditions to the forest edge, from one Academy-sponsored survey that reached the first major ridge and produced a report — describe a jungle of unusual density and age. Not unusual for a tropical island in the abstract; unusual in the specific quality of the forest, which has the character of a place that has been continuously covered for longer than the coastal people's memory reaches or the survey party's instruments could measure.

The canopy is high and closed. Below it, the layers of the forest interior receive almost no direct sunlight for most of the day. The understory is dense with things adapted to this condition — palms, broad-leaf shade plants, the epiphytes that cover every available surface — and the ground layer is correspondingly deep in accumulated organic material. The Academy survey noted that sounds from the coastal plain do not penetrate more than a short distance into the forest, and that sounds within the forest itself behave in ways the survey's naturalist described as "not characteristic of open-canopy acoustics" without specifying further.

The river systems that drain the interior emerge at the coast as the brown, silt-carrying currents that feed the reef's nutrients. They are navigable by small boat a short distance upstream before the gradient increases and the passage becomes obstructed. What they drain from — what the interior terrain actually looks like at elevation, whether there are valleys, high clearings, water bodies — is not established from outside sources.

## The Inland Peoples

The interior of Babon is inhabited. The Tathilium people know this, mention it matter-of-factly, and do not have detailed knowledge of it. Figures are occasionally seen at the forest edge, particularly near the river mouths. Objects of clear crafted origin — specific types of worked fiber, particular resins in forms that indicate processing rather than raw harvest — sometimes appear in Tathilium's market through channels that involve several intermediary exchanges and no direct contact with whoever produced them. This is the extent of the commercial relationship between the coast and the interior.

Azhoran and Hama sources describe the interior people as primitive and their territory as ungoverned. The description reflects the limits of outside knowledge rather than what is actually the case. A people who have occupied a dense tropical interior long enough to develop their own trade goods, their own routes to the coastal market, and their own terms for contact with the outside — conducted entirely on their own initiative, through intermediaries they have selected — are not ungoverned. They have simply established a governance that the outside world is not party to and cannot observe.

What the Tathilium people say about the interior people, when asked carefully and at length: they are old here. They know the forest entirely. They do not come to the coast because they do not need to. The objects that reach the coastal market are not trade goods offered under economic pressure; they are what the interior people choose to release. Whether contact could be made directly and what the interior people would make of it, the Tathilium people decline to speculate.

The one consistent observation — from Tathilium people, from the outer coastal communities, and from the single Academy survey — is that the interior people have a relationship with the island's larger fauna that is not the relationship of a population living in fear of dangerous animals. How this works in practice has not been observed.

## The Creatures

Babon's interior fauna is the source of the island's reputation among Azhoran sailors, who use "the monsters of Babon" as a reference in the way that people use things they have heard about but not seen. The reputation is not entirely wrong, though "monster" is an outsider's word applied to things that are animals and that the insider's perspective might categorize differently.

The island's isolation and the age of its forest have produced fauna at scales not found on the mainland or on the younger volcanic islands to the south. The largest of the island's reptilian species — seen at the forest edge, observed at river mouths, identified in the coastal communities' extremely detailed knowledge of what does not come to the beach — reach lengths that the Academy survey declined to publish without corroboration, noting that the tracks and waterline markings observed were consistent with the reported dimensions and inconsistent with any species in the existing Azhoran natural catalogue. These are not mythological; they are animals, with the behaviors of animals, occupying the ecological role that large apex predators occupy. They are simply larger than the category accommodates comfortably.

A second category of creature is described by the Tathilium people with a vocabulary distinct from the vocabulary they use for the large reptiles. This second category is not characterized by size. The descriptions involve behavior: things seen at the forest edge at specific times of day or year, in circumstances that do not match foraging, territorial, or mating behavior as the Tathilium people understand these things. The naturalist on the Academy survey wrote that the behavioral accounts, taken together, "suggest an animal with a social relationship to its territory that exceeds what we associate with individual species and implies something more systematic, though I cannot specify further from the available evidence." The survey did not enter the interior. The naturalist recommended further study without volunteering to conduct it.

The inland people's apparent ease with both categories — the large predators and the second category — is observed from outside without being understood. The Tathilium people do not have a tradition of entering the interior; they do not have a tradition of being harmed by what comes out of it either. The edge is observed. Things from both sides observe the edge. Contact is not made.

Whether the interior peoples have a relationship with the second category of creature, or whether the second category of creature has a relationship with them, is a distinction the available evidence does not allow. The Tathilium people treat the question as the kind of question that people who do not live here ask.

## What Is Known

Tathilium is known. The channel is known. The outer coast, approximately, is known. The reef system and the commercial arrangements around it are partially known. The products that emerge from the interior through intermediary channels are known as objects without a clear source.

The interior terrain is not mapped. The inland peoples' language, population, political structure, and material culture are not documented. The full species composition of the interior fauna is not catalogued. The age of the island's forest cover has not been measured. Whether the island has structural relationship to the southern archipelago — geological, ecological, or in terms of deep human history — has not been studied.

The Tathilium people have lived next to all of this for long enough that they find outside interest in it somewhat academic. They have what they need from the coast. The interior has what it needs from the interior. The edge between them holds.

## The Name of the City

*Tathilium* — the name appears in its current form in Hama records from initial contact and has not changed since. The *-ilium* ending pattern is familiar from the Mittoli tradition (as in *Te Nillium*), which has led some Academy cartographers to assume a Mittoli founding or naming. The Tathilium people do not confirm this. The first element, *Tath-*, does not trace to any Mittoli root in the existing vocabulary, and the local language — which has not been formally studied — appears to use *tath* as a word with its own meaning, though the meaning has not been recorded in any document accessible to the Academy.

The commercial shorthand in Hama records is simply "Tath." The pilots who work the channel use this.
