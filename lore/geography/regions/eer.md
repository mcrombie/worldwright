# Eer

Eer is the agricultural hinterland north and east of Nylon — the flatlands and low hills that run from the Lizeem's northern distributaries to the coast road, from the edge of the city's harbor districts outward to wherever the next power has most recently decided its territory ends. It grows grain, olives, river-fish in its canal systems, and orchard fruit in quantities that have fed Nylon for as long as both have existed. It has also been conquered so many times that its people have developed, across the generations, something close to a professional competence at being conquered: they know which rooms to clear, which records to present, which local officials to keep visible, and approximately how long before whoever has come this time will leave.

The standard account of Eer's strategic situation is that foreign powers seize it to starve Nylon into submission. The account is accurate as far as it goes. What it does not account for is the second half of what follows: Nylon does not submit. The city sits behind walls that have not been breached in recorded history, draws food and trade goods from the Iberos Sea, and waits. Eer changes hands; Nylon does not. Eventually the occupying power calculates what holding Eer without access to Nylon is actually worth, arrives at an unflattering answer, and withdraws. Nylon then reclaims its hinterland, rebuilds what was damaged, and the cycle resumes its next iteration.

The people of Eer are not surprised by this. They have been the piece on the board long enough to understand the game.

## Geography

Eer occupies the coastal plain north and east of Nylon, extending roughly forty miles inland from the sea and perhaps sixty miles along the coast from the Lizeem's northern mouth. The land is flat near the coast and rises gently toward low chalk hills in the interior, which mark the region's northern limit and separate it from the dryer Mittoli upland country beyond.

The soil is the deep alluvial loam deposited by the Lizeem's seasonal floods over millennia. It is extraordinarily productive and requires relatively little effort to work compared to the hill-farming of the Pyros uplands or the irrigation-intensive desert agriculture further south. Eer does not require heroics; it requires maintenance. This has shaped the agricultural culture of the region: methodical, experienced, oriented toward the long term in a way that makes the repeated political disruptions feel like weather — unpleasant, survivable, part of the conditions one farms in.

The road north from Nylon — the main artery connecting the city to the Mittoli interior, from which it receives timber, cloth, and the goods that do not arrive by sea — runs through the center of Eer before climbing into the hills. Whoever holds Eer can tax this traffic. This is the second reason, beyond the food supply, that foreign powers find the region worth holding: it gives them the customs revenues from Nylon's northern trade without giving them Nylon.

The coast of Eer faces the Iberos Sea from a series of low headlands and small sheltered bays, none large enough to be major harbors, all useful for fishing and small coastal traffic. The Eer fishery supplements the agricultural economy without competing with Nylon's harbor commerce — the two are in different scales.

## History of Occupation

The pattern of Eer's occupation has been consistent enough across different aggressors to suggest that it is structural rather than contingent. Any power that wishes to threaten Nylon without directly assaulting its walls finds Eer accessible, productive, and strategically positioned. The calculation is not unreasonable; it simply does not account for the walls accurately.

The walls of Nylon were not built to protect Nylon's trade interests. They were built to make the loss of Eer irrelevant. This is the conclusion that Nyloni historians have eventually reached, though the early scholarly consensus was that the walls were simply good military engineering applied to a headland site with excellent natural advantages. What made the walls extraordinary — their depth, their height, the engineering of their sea-facing sections, the integration of the harbor into a defensible perimeter — went beyond what mere natural advantage required. Whoever built them knew that the hinterland would not always be held. They built for that specific scenario. Nylon has been in that scenario many times since, and the walls have not failed it.

The occupying powers have varied over the centuries. Pyrosi highland kingdoms making one of their periodic pushes toward the coast. Iberos Sea city alliances attempting to extend commercial influence into the coastal plain. Mittoli river-city confederacies seeking control of the Lizeem mouth from both ends. Ascarth in its more expansionist periods, approaching from the southwest. No single power has held Eer for more than a generation before the combination of Nylon's sea-fed independence and the expense of maintaining an occupying garrison in uncooperative farmland has made the position untenable. Nylon, for its part, has never mobilized a significant field army to reclaim Eer — it has simply waited, maintained its harbor commerce, and reoccupied the hinterland when the occupier's withdrawal created the opportunity.

The result is that Eer's political history is less a history than a list: the names of the lords its people have paid taxes to, in the order they appeared, with notes on how long each stayed. The list is long. The notes are consistent.

## The People of Eer

The Eer do not call themselves that, exactly — the name is what the Nyloni use for the region and its people, derived from an older word for "the outside" or "the outlands" that has been in use long enough that the people of the region have adopted it themselves without enthusiasm. In their own speech, they identify first by village, second by the river branch or road their village sits on, and third by the district of Eer their sub-region falls within. Political identification — Nyloni subjects, or subjects of whoever has most recently arrived — comes a distant fourth and is adjusted as required.

This local-first identity is not insularity. Eer people travel, trade, and are well aware of the wider world. The local identity is a form of stability: when everything above the village level changes every generation, the village level is where continuity lives. The names of fields, the systems of drainage, the annual rotation of crops, the relationships between families and their specific parcels of land — these are older than any overlord's claim and will outlast the next one as they have outlasted all the previous ones.

The Eer have a reputation in Nylon for being slow to trust and slow to anger in roughly equal measure. Both qualities are accurate and both are the product of the same experience: they have seen too many confident outsiders to be moved quickly by confidence, and they have learned that anger is an expense that rarely recovers its cost. They are not passive — a farmer who lets their drainage systems deteriorate or their seed stock degrade because the current political situation seems uncertain will find, when the next political situation arrives, that the farm is worse off regardless. Continuity requires active work. The Eer understanding of this is deep.

Their hospitality is practical and real. A traveler on the north road who arrives at an Eer village is fed, sheltered, and left alone; they are not expected to be sociable, and the offer of warmth and food is not an invitation to spend the evening explaining where they're from. The Eer have quartered enough foreign soldiers to have lost any romantic interest in strangers.

## Eer and Nylon

The relationship is not simple. Nylon needs Eer in the sense that any major city needs cheap, close agricultural land — it is cheaper and more efficient to have food grow nearby than to import everything. When Nylon controls Eer, the city's food is relatively inexpensive, the north road revenue flows to Nylon's treasury, and the hinterland is a source of labor and material that allows the city to focus its maritime commerce on higher-value trade.

When Nylon does not control Eer, all of this is absent — but Nylon survives. The walls and the harbor make survival possible. The Iberos Sea trade makes it comfortable. The city is inconvenienced, never defeated.

The Eer know this. They know that Nylon does not depend on them the way most hinterlands are depended on. There is something in the relationship — not quite resentment, not quite pride — that comes from being the farmland that a city could do without if it had to, but consistently chooses to reacquire when the opportunity presents. The Eer interpretation of this pattern is not usually flattering to Nylon, though it is delivered with the same equanimity as most Eer assessments: they come back, they always come back, they did not build those walls to leave us to whoever passes through.

Nylon's official view is that Eer is an integral part of the city's character and a community whose welfare the city government is responsible for. The Eer interpretation of the phrase "integral part" is that Nylon considers Eer integral when they can afford to and recovers their interest promptly when conditions change. Both positions are defensible.

## The Walls of Nylon

No description of Eer is complete without the walls, because the walls are the structural explanation for Eer's entire situation.

The Walls of Nylon are the reason the city has never been taken by the method of seizing its hinterland. They face landward as well as seaward; their design assumes that the city may at any point be surrounded, its agricultural supply cut, and its land approaches held by hostile forces, and that none of this must prevent the city's continuation. They are not merely tall — many cities have tall walls — but deep, with interior passages and provision stores built into the curtain wall itself, and sections that can be sealed from others so that a breach in one section does not determine the defense of the whole.

The sea-facing sections are the most unusual. The harbor is not outside the walls but integrated into them — ships can enter a protected inner harbor without passing through undefended space. This means that as long as Nylon's navy or allied vessels can maintain access, the city's sea trade continues regardless of what is happening outside the walls. An army that holds Eer has cut Nylon's land supply. It has not cut Nylon's sea supply. The city's survival arithmetic does not change.

Who built the walls in their current form is a question with several scholarly answers and no conclusive one. The oldest sections are older than the oldest continuous written records in the city. The most sophisticated engineering — the harbor integration, the provision systems, the sealed-section design — is attributed by different scholars to different periods and different influences, and the question of whether the current walls are one coherent design or the accumulated result of multiple building programs is contested. The Nyloni questori, characteristically, have a formal debate tradition around this question. They consider it interesting enough to be worth arguing. The Eer consider it interesting in a different way: whoever built them built them for us, the Eer sometimes say, meaning: the walls were built specifically to make the loss of Eer survivable. Whether this is flattering is a matter of interpretation.

## Language and Culture

Eer speaks a Mittoli dialect that linguists categorize as coastal-transitional — closer to the standard Mittoli of the Lizeem valley communities than to the Iberos coastal variants, but with accumulated borrowings from every occupying power that has administered the region. An Eer vocabulary is a stratigraphic record: Pyrosi administrative terms from the highland kingdoms' periods of control, Iberos commercial vocabulary from the sea-alliance occupations, Ascarth-derived words for several agricultural tools (the Ascarth occupation, though unwelcome, was long enough to leave marks on the farming vocabulary), and an old layer of pre-Mittoli terms for land features, water courses, and soil types that has no clear relationship to any identified language family.

The Eer naming tradition follows Mittoli practice in broad outline but has a local specificity that visitors sometimes notice: Eer place names are almost entirely descriptive of physical geography — soil color, water quality, slope aspect — rather than commemorating persons or events. A village called Long-Drainage or Red-Clay has a name that tells you something useful about the place. A village called in honor of a long-dead ruler who happened to pass through in the fourth generation of the current occupation would require footnotes. The Eer have historically preferred the kind of name that does not require footnotes.
