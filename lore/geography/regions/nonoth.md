# Nonoth

Most northern regions define themselves by what they produce or where they sit or what their terrain does to everyone who tries to cross it. Nonoth defines itself by whom it stands behind.

The land between the two branches of the Ond is fertile, well-watered, and clear in its borders — the rivers mark the western and eastern edges with the precision that other regions' mountains and uplands cannot match. A Nonoth community knows exactly where it is. But this geographic legibility is not what the Nonoth have made central to their identity. What they have made central is the political fact: Nonoth is the vassal of whoever holds Stonefist, and has been for as long as anyone can trace the relationship, and intends to continue. This is not resignation. It is principle, and the difference matters to them in ways that outside observers who read it as simple subordination consistently miss.

---

## Geography

The Ond descends from the Gorgi Mountains' southern face carrying the snowmelt of the northern interior — the accumulated drainage of a range extensive enough to maintain significant snowpack well into summer — and enters the Ganoss upland margins as a single, substantial channel. South of the upland edge, where the terrain flattens and the gradient eases, the Ond forks. The two branches that result are not equal: the western branch, which the surrounding communities call the Velond, is the larger and faster; the eastern branch, the Vornond, is slower and more prone to the seasonal flooding that makes its lower banks difficult to farm in wet years. Both are significant rivers by northern standards. Together they are the largest riverine system in Northern Azhora by volume, fed as they are by the most extensive highland snowpack on the continent's northern half.

Nonoth is the wedge of ground between them.

The fork is the northern point; the territory widens as the two branches diverge southward before eventually moving beyond Nonoth's settled ground and into the broader drainage basins that feed the Bay of Lol watershed to the west and the Sond-tributaries to the east. The width of the territory at its broadest — roughly where the main settlements are — is perhaps two days' travel between the riverbanks. The length from the fork to the southern edge of consistent habitation is four to five days. The shape, on any map that shows the two river courses, is unmistakable: a broadening leaf of land, water on either side, the Gorgi Mountains as its stem.

The terrain within the wedge is the product of long river influence: flat bottomland near both banks, dark-soiled and damp, transitioning to slightly higher ground in the middle strip where the soil is still deep but the drainage is better. The middle strip is where the largest Nonoth settlements have established themselves — elevated enough above both flood plains to be reliable, close enough to both rivers to use them. The bottomland near the Velond's western bank and the Vornond's eastern bank is farmed intensively where it can be managed and left as water meadow where it cannot.

The climate is colder than Lond's plateau — Nonoth sits further north, closer to the Gorgi's influence — but the river corridors moderate the worst of it, and the river valleys accumulate warmth in summer in ways the open Lond plateau does not. The growing season is shorter than Lond's, but the bottomland fertility compensates in yield per acre what it loses in weeks of cultivation time.

## The Ond and Its Branches

The Ond above the fork is navigable by small craft for a short season in late summer, when the snowmelt has settled and the channel has stabilized. The fork itself is too shallow and braided to be reliably useful for cargo. The lower Velond — south of the fork, where the gradient is gentle and the channel has deepened and clarified — is navigable for a longer season and carries modest boat traffic between the Nonoth settlements and the Bay of Lol watershed approaches to the southwest, eventually making contact with the trade routes that run through Endevor's margins.

The Vornond is less navigable than its western counterpart — the eastern branch is shallower, more prone to seasonal variation, and interrupted in places by reaches that require portage. The communities on the Vornond's eastern bank use it primarily for fishing and for the small-scale movement of goods that does not require consistent navigability. The larger commercial traffic of Nonoth moves on the Velond or overland.

The rivers are fished extensively. Cold, fast water from the Gorgi snowmelt carries the specific fish populations that the northern interior streams support — the same cold-water species that the Witherst communities harvest from the sea, here present in the upper river system in numbers that sustain a secondary food economy alongside the bottomland agriculture. Nonoth communities on both riverbanks maintain weir systems and seasonal fish camps that supplement the stored grain through winter. In a bad harvest year, the rivers feed the community; in a bad fishing year, the harvest feeds it. The two systems together provide a resilience that neither would alone.

Both rivers flood in spring, which the Nonoth manage rather than prevent. The annual flood deposits the silt and organic matter that refreshes the bottomland fertility, and the farming calendar is organized around the flood's timing — late to plant, fast to harvest, with the interval between precisely calculated by generations of observation. A family that misjudges the flood timing loses the crop. This has happened enough times in every generation that it no longer happens to the same family twice.

## The People

The Nonoth are a settled, stable, and in northern terms moderately prosperous people whose primary social identity is not the community they live in or the valley system they are part of but the political relationship they maintain. This is unusual in the north. The Lond communities know themselves by their fields. The Endevor know themselves by their valleys and their Vornheld memory. The Ganun know themselves by the river they work. The Nonoth know themselves by whom they serve.

This is not how the Nonoth would phrase it, exactly. They would say: Stonefist is the proper seat of northern authority, and Nonoth is the proper support of that seat. The framing matters. They are not the people who submit to Stonefist. They are the people who recognize Stonefist, and the recognition is a statement about the north's correct political order rather than about their own subordination.

The practical expression of this identity is a particular kind of social seriousness about political relationships. The Nonoth pay close attention to Stonefist — who holds it, how they hold it, what the current terms of the arrangement are, and whether those terms are being honored. They are not passive in their loyalty; they are attentive. A Nonoth council that has decided a new Stonefist ruler is legitimate will be among the first in the north to formally acknowledge it, and the acknowledgment carries weight precisely because it comes first and because it has historically been reliable. Conversely, if the Nonoth are slow to acknowledge a new holder, other northern powers read the hesitation as information.

They are not, and have not been, a military power in the conventional sense. The Ond's two branches do not produce the kind of armed culture that the raiding traditions of the coast or the Orgmala highland generated. The Nonoth contribution to Stonefist's authority is manpower for garrison work, agricultural surplus that supplements Lond's, and the institutional weight of a recognized and continuous loyalty that other claimants to Stonefist's authority have consistently found worth having. You can hold Stonefist against the Nonoth's resistance if you are strong enough. It is much easier to hold it with their support.

## The Vassalage

The formal relationship between Nonoth and Stonefist is the most explicit political bond in Northern Azhora outside of the Mittoli city-state system. It involves acknowledged obligations in both directions: Nonoth provides a specified share of its harvest, a specified number of men for Stonefist's garrison in call-up seasons, and the institutional recognition that legitimizes new holders. Stonefist's holders provide, in return, military protection for the river territory — specifically against Orgmala raiding from the west, against Ganoss incursions from the north, and against whatever other pressures the current era produces — and preferential access to the trade routes that Stonefist controls.

The obligations are not written in a single document. They have been renegotiated, in practice, at every major transition of Stonefist's holders, which given the frequency with which Stonefist changes hands means the Nonoth have considerable experience in the mechanics of establishing terms with new rulers who need their support. The Nonoth do not enter these negotiations as supplicants. They enter them as the party whose recognition a new holder requires, and they negotiate accordingly. The terms have not changed dramatically across recorded history — the structure is old and the values are known — but the specific form of each renegotiation reflects the current balance of need.

When a new power takes Stonefist, the question "have the Nonoth recognized?" is one of the first asked by every other northern power making its own calculation about whether the transfer is durable. A Stonefist holder without Nonoth recognition is a garrison in a rock with an uncertain future. A Stonefist holder with Nonoth recognition has a functioning tributary arrangement and the beginning of legitimate authority. The difference is significant enough that Nonoth recognition has become, over time, something that new Stonefist holders seek before they have fully consolidated their position, because the act of seeking it and receiving it is itself a stabilizing signal.

The Nonoth are aware of this leverage and use it with the care that a tool of limited applications requires. Withholding recognition entirely is an option they have exercised, in recorded history, on three occasions. On two of those occasions, the holder they declined to recognize was displaced within a generation. On the third occasion, the holder they declined to recognize managed to consolidate anyway, and the subsequent relationship between that holder and Nonoth was, by all accounts, uncomfortable for both parties for the duration of that holder's time at Stonefist. The lesson the Nonoth draw from the third occasion is not that recognition can be withheld without cost, but that the conditions under which it should be withheld require very careful assessment.

## History

The origin of the Nonoth-Stonefist relationship is not precisely traceable. The earliest records in both Nonoth's own tradition and Lond's sparse historical documentation treat the relationship as established — the question of when it began is not one that either side has found answerable from any record currently available. The Vornheld tradition in Endevor mentions Nonoth in the context of the Eld-Gold period: the mine-kings' empire required Nonoth's river territory as a grain supplement to the more distant Lond plateau, and the relationship at that time was tributary in the general sense of the empire's structure rather than the specific Stonefist-vassalage it became afterward.

The transition from mine-king tributary to Stonefist vassal appears to have happened during or after the raiding period, when the Endevor empire's structure had collapsed and the north was reorganizing around the political fact of Stonefist rather than the economic fact of Endevor's gold. The Nonoth's specific alignment with Stonefist — rather than with any individual power competing for the fortress — may have developed from the observation that Stonefist itself was more durable than any particular holder of it, and that a relationship with the position was more stable than a relationship with any person.

This insight, if it was articulated consciously at some founding moment, has been maintained through many subsequent transitions. The Nonoth position is not: we support this ruler. It is: we support the authority that Stonefist represents. The current ruler exercises that authority. When the current ruler is replaced, the authority remains, and our support continues.

The three occasions on which Nonoth withheld recognition are the most discussed episodes in their political history. The first was during a contested succession in which two claimants held Stonefist simultaneously for a period — Nonoth declined to recognize either until one had definitively displaced the other, and the resulting months of formal neutrality were later described by Nonoth community records as "the time the north was uncertain." The second was when a holder took Stonefist through methods that the Nonoth council assessed as incompatible with the obligations of the office — specifically, the destruction of a Lond community that had resisted transition rather than accommodation. The third was more recent and is not yet fully in historical perspective.

## Economy

Nonoth produces grain and fish in quantities that sustain itself and a modest surplus for trade. The bottomland agriculture is the foundation; the river fisheries are the supplement; the two together have supported the region's population through the full range of northern weather without recorded famine.

The surplus goes primarily to Stonefist's garrison as the tributary portion of the vassalage arrangement, and secondarily to the trade routes that run through the Velond corridor toward the Endevor margins and the Bay of Lol approaches. The Nonoth are not commercial traders in the sense that Ganun is commercial; they do not have a trade-house culture or a market to match Ondrath. They have the river, the grain, and the established relationships that the vassalage network provides. What they need from outside — iron, manufactured goods, the occasional specialized product from the wider northern trade system — they acquire through those relationships rather than through independent commerce.

The Velond carries more commercial traffic than the Vornond for the reasons noted: navigability, gradient, connection to the Bay of Lol watershed. The Velond-side communities are correspondingly more commercially engaged, with better access to goods moving between Endevor and the broader northern network. The Vornond-side communities are more self-sufficient, more agricultural, and slightly more isolated. The two sides of Nonoth have a gentle cultural divergence that the wider outside world does not usually notice and that the Nonoth themselves articulate as "the Velond way" and "the Vornond way" without implying that one is superior to the other. The Velond way is more connected. The Vornond way is more settled. Both are loyal.

## Language

The Nonoth dialect is Northern Mittoli with a moderate substrate layer and no particular distinguishing features that have attracted Academy attention. It falls between the conservative Endevor variety and the Lond standard, closer to Lond on most phonological measures but with a handful of vocabulary items that appear to derive from an older local layer. The name "Nonoth" itself is among these items: it does not reconstruct from Northern Mittoli, does not trace to Proto-Western, and is treated in the Academy's notes as a substrate toponym of unknown derivation — the region had a name before Mittoli speakers arrived, and the name survived while the language that produced it did not.

The word that distinguishes Nonoth speech to neighboring ears is the specific term they use for the loyalty relationship with Stonefist. Northern Mittoli has standard vocabulary for political subordination and for alliance. The Nonoth use neither when describing their relationship to Stonefist's authority. The word they use is a compound that translates most directly as something between "maintained recognition" and "the thing that holds its shape" — implying not passive submission but active structural maintenance, the way a wall holds its shape because each stone has been placed with intention and remains because the placement continues to be correct. The Academy notes this usage. It does not note an equivalent in any other northern dialect.
