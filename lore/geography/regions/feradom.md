# Feradom

Feradom is what Drent would be if the terrain gave it the option. The same culture in most of its essentials — decentralized lords, coastal orientation, fishing and timber and commercial pragmatism — but sitting behind a forested ridge line that turns an Ambronite campaign from a negotiation conducted from military dominance into an actual war. The lords of Feradom have refused Ambron before. Some of them have survived it. This is a different kind of knowledge than the Drent lords possess, and it produces a different kind of lord.

---

## Geography

North of the Drent coastal plain, the terrain rises abruptly into the **barrier hills** — a forested ridge system running along the country's inland edge, not dramatic in height but dense in tree cover and limited in crossing points. A half-dozen usable passes exist, none of them gentle in autumn and most of them impassable under hard winter snow. The hills are not the Oremindi; a determined force can move through them. What the hills do is constrain that movement to specific corridors where small defending forces have known advantages, which is more than sufficient for the purpose.

North of the hills, the coast reasserts itself in a different register from Drent's flat delta shore. Rocky headlands, sheltered inlets, harbors tucked into coves between cliff faces — the first stretches of coast that feel northern rather than Mediterranean. The sea here is colder and the currents less forgiving, and the fishing it supports requires different methods from the channel-work of the Pebbles. Feradom's northern shore faces the open water that leads toward the Bay of Lol approaches; its eastern coast catches the sea as it transitions out of the warmth of the full Iberos reach. The country is shaped like something turning its back on the south, which in certain historical contexts is not merely a geographic description.

The passes through the barrier hills are Feradom's strategic heart. Each pass capable of carrying an army is held by a fortified position — not always impressive in architecture, but placed with generations of accumulated knowledge about exactly where a small force stops a large one. A tower above the narrows. A castle at the point where the valley behind a pass opens enough to permit a staging defense. Cleared ground in front, walls at the back, and lords who have been thinking about that specific problem since they inherited the title. The pass-lords are the most politically significant figures in Feradom regardless of how small their immediate territories are, because their fortifications are what distinguishes Feradom defensible from Feradom merely defended.

## The Lords of Feradom

The structural tendency of the lordship culture is the same as Drent's — individual river valleys, harbor towns, no natural dominant center, commercial competition substituting for political unity in quiet periods. But the passes add a variable that Drent's coast lacks. A coastal lord who controls a major inlet can be bypassed by land if you're willing to pay for it. A pass-lord who controls the route through the barrier hills cannot be bypassed at all if you need to move a force in or out of Feradom from the south. This gives the pass-lords leverage over every southern campaign, and correspondingly more standing in the councils that the Feradom lords convene when outside pressure requires coordination.

The councils of Feradom are less frequent than Drent's congresses and more likely to produce decisions when they meet. The subject matter tends to be survival rather than toll disputes, and a council convened because Ambron has mobilized is working from a different premise than a congress convened to renegotiate river-passage fees. Not every council produces useful results — there have been famously unsuccessful ones — but the tradition of councils-that-matter has shaped the Feradom lords into people who understand they need each other in specific circumstances. This is more than the Drent lords have ever consistently internalized.

The pass-lords' role in these councils is structural: their cooperation is necessary for any collective defense, which means their concerns must be addressed before anything binding can be agreed. A Feradom council that fails to satisfy the pass-lords on the terms of their military contribution tends to produce nominal agreements that dissolve when the actual fighting begins. The lords who have held the passes longest tend to know this and to use it, with varying degrees of grace, to extract terms that their landholdings would not otherwise command.

## Ambron's Relationship with Feradom

Ambron wants Feradom when it is strong. The northern harbors give the Elagosi lake fleet access to the coastal routes that hug the northern shore, and the barrier hills' forests produce the best shipbuilding timber available in the northeast. What Ambron must pay for these things in Feradom is different from what it pays in Drent. In Drent, the campaign is a negotiation from a position of overwhelming military advantage — individual lords submit because the calculation is obvious and the terrain offers no alternative. In Feradom, the campaign is a campaign. Forces move through passes against prepared positions, or land on a rocky coast where the defenders have had centuries to identify the good ground, and the Feradom lords have contingency plans for both approaches.

Feradom does fall. The conditions are specific. A divided Feradom — lords at active war with each other, a succession dispute producing factions that genuinely cannot resolve without outside arbitration — can be entered by an Ambronite force that arrives as an arbiter and departs as an overlord. This is the most reliable mechanism, and the Feradom lords know it well enough that a lord seen opening communication with Ambron during a domestic dispute is making an announcement. The rest of the lords notice. The resulting social pressure has aborted several potential Ambronite interventions at the communication stage, which is not guaranteed but is more effective than it sounds.

When Ambron does hold Feradom, the occupation requires more permanent military presence than Drent demands. The same passes used to enter are the passes through which a resurgent Feradom can cut supply lines in winter. An Ambronite garrison in a northern harbor town that cannot be relieved overland between the first snowfall and the spring thaw knows exactly what its situation is. The occupation is sustainable when Ambron is attentive and provisioned; it begins to deteriorate faster than the Drent occupation when Ambron's attention shifts. Feradom is typically among the first territories recovered in the contraction phase of an Ambronite empire, not because resistance is heroic but because the logistics of maintaining it are the first to become untenable.

Whether Feradom is currently subject to Ambron is consequently one of the reliable indicators of how the empire is doing. A strong Ambron holds Drent and Feradom both. A middling Ambron holds Drent and negotiates with Feradom. A contracting Ambron loses Feradom first and holds Drent longest, because Drent requires less effort to keep. The Feradom lords have absorbed this pattern into their political self-understanding: their independence is not merely a matter of pride but a barometer reading.

## Economy

Timber is the primary export. The barrier hills' forests are old-growth in their upper reaches and actively managed on their lower slopes — the Feradom lord families have been harvesting and replanting specific forest sections for generations, not from environmental philosophy but from the practical understanding that a lord whose hills are stripped of useful timber has lost half his military advantage along with his export income. The oak and fir that come out of the Feradom hills are specifically sought by Elagosi and Drent shipbuilders for construction that must hold in northern-sea conditions. The timber trade flows regardless of political arrangements; even during Ambronite occupation, the timber moves south through the passes and the prices are negotiated between people who all want the transaction to happen.

The fishing is deepwater work — different species from Drent's channel and estuary catches, requiring larger vessels and longer voyages out past the rocky headlands into the colder reaches. Feradom fishers have historically gone further north than any other Azhoran fishing fleet for which records exist, following specific migratory species into the cold water that other fleets regard as peripheral. What they encounter at those latitudes is not extensively documented outside of the harbor communities that have been doing it for generations.

Agriculture in the river valleys is productive enough to feed the country in most years, limited by the hills that break the landscape into disconnected pockets. Feradom imports grain from Moros and Drent in poor years and exports modest surplus in good ones. It has never been a grain power and does not expect to be.

## Character

The Feradom lords carry a specific kind of self-regard that the Drent lords cannot quite share. The genealogy of resistance — particular lords who refused Ambron at particular moments and survived the refusal — functions as a living standard of reference in Feradom's aristocratic culture. Current lords are measured against it, sometimes by themselves and always by each other. This does not produce recklessness; the Feradom councils are capable of choosing accommodation when accommodation is the right calculation. What it produces is a framework in which the calculation occurs differently than it does in Drent.

A Drent lord asks: what does submission to Ambron cost me, and is that less than the cost of resistance? A Feradom lord asks that question and also a second one: what does this decision make me? Both questions are real. Only one of them appears in Drent political deliberations with any regularity.

The coastal communities of Feradom share a variant of this character at a different social register — not aristocratic honor but maritime independence, the self-reliance of communities that work rougher seas further from port in conditions that require their own competence and cannot be delegated. The harbor towns of Feradom are harder places than Drent's estuary ports in the specific way of places where the sea is less forgiving. The people are not unfriendly; they are precisely as friendly as they have reason to be, which in good conditions is genuinely warm and in ambiguous conditions is watchful in ways that strangers without context sometimes misread as hostility.

The relationship between the lordship culture and the maritime communities is not frictionless — lords and harbor towns have the usual disagreements about tolls, obligations, and military service — but it rests on a shared premise that Drent's equivalent relationship partially lacks: both know that if things go badly enough, they will need each other in ways that cannot be replaced by outside assistance. The hills close off Feradom from easy relief. Whatever happens here is settled largely by the people here. This is, depending on the situation, either a burden or a foundation.

## Language

Related to Drentish in the way of long-separated cousins — recognizably from the same source, significantly diverged. The -dom of Feradom is the same root as the Drentish word for a lordly holding or domain, which may mean the name derives from the same political concept that organizes both cultures: Feradom as "the domain country," the territory of the domains. The specific terrain vocabulary diverges considerably — Feradom has a rich vocabulary for passes, ridge terrain, and the specific conditions of northern sea approaches that Drentish does not need and does not have. The two vocabularies for sea conditions are largely different, reflecting different waters.

Elagosi loanwords appear in the formal registers, as they do in Drent, but fewer of them — Ambronite occupation has been shorter and less continuous in Feradom, which means the administrative vocabulary arrived in smaller doses and some of it did not take root. A Feradom legal document from a strong Ambronite occupation period looks substantially like its Drent equivalent. A Feradom legal document from a period of independence looks more purely Drentish-derived, which is its own kind of record.
