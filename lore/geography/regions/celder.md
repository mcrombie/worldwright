# Celder

Before anything else, the horses. They are what you notice on the road from Minora heading west: first the quality of the grass changes, and then the first herd appears, and then another, and what you are looking at is not the undifferentiated livestock of an ordinary country but something more specific. Celder horses move differently. Even at rest in a field they give the impression of being about to do something. Travelers who are not horse people and have no reason to care about the distinction find themselves slowing down anyway, looking at the animals without quite knowing why, before the road takes a turn and the herd is behind them.

The Oremindi are visible from most of Celder on clear days — not the approach hills but the mountains themselves, the full scale of them, white-capped and unignorable on the western horizon. Between the mountain wall and the flat country that becomes Mithala further east lies Celder: still technically plain, still the same river-and-sky country that defines the Mittolo heartland, but modified by proximity to the mountains in the specific ways that proximity to the Oremindi modifies everything in its reach. The rivers here run faster and colder than the Lizeem's braided lower channels. The grass is different where the mountain streams spread their mineral silt across the plain margin. The winters are broken by the dry western wind that comes off the Oremindi's eastern face and can raise the temperature twenty degrees in an afternoon. Celder is not the mountains. But it is not entirely independent of them either, and the horses are the most visible consequence of that relationship.

Somewhere on the plain west of central Celder, visible on any clear day for thirty miles in every direction, stands Canerd. It is the reason travelers realize Celder is in front of them before any other sign tells them so. On a flat country there should be nothing above eye level except weather. Canerd is above eye level. The castle stands on a mound of such height that its towers vanish into cloud on overcast days, a construction of stone and accumulated will that rises from the flat plain like a displaced piece of the Oremindi — the same scale, the same quality of simply refusing to be what the surrounding terrain is. This is, depending on whom you ask, either the seat of Celder's sovereignty or its most elaborate symbol of a sovereignty that does not, in practice, work the way it looks.

## Geography

Celder occupies the western margin of the Mittolo Plains, between the Oremindi's eastern foothills and the country that begins to flatten definitively into Mithala. The plain here is not quite the flat of the lower Lizeem — there is a gentle gradient from the mountain base that gives the rivers enough current to run clear, and the terrain has low swells that the lower Mithala entirely lacks. These swells are not significant enough to be called hills. They are significant enough to give horses purchase and give cavalry commanders sight lines, and in Celder, that distinction is what matters.

The upper Lizeem runs through the center of Celder before spreading and slowing into its lower course. In Celder, the Lizeem is still a single river, recognizable as such, running with purpose from the mountain meltwater that feeds it. The river margins here are among the finest grazing country on the continent: the floodplain soils are rich and consistently renewed, the water table is reliable, and the grass that grows on the river terraces has a particular mineral quality — traceable, so the Celder breeders maintain, to the specific composition of the Oremindi's glacial meltwater — that has no equivalent further east on the plain. Whether this is accurate as stated or accurate as shorthand for a complex ecological condition that the breeders understand without being able to articulate outside their own tradition is debated by the Academy's naturalists. The breeders are not particularly interested in the debate; they know where the good grass is and they plant their herds accordingly.

The foothills along Celder's western edge are the transition zone between the plain and the Oremindi proper. They are settled, extensively grazed on their lower slopes, and contain the quarries whose stone built Canerd and most of the significant structures of the plain. The foothills are not part of Celder's horse culture; they are the raw material and the labor source that the plain requires but that the plain's orientation is not toward. Celder people from the hill communities and Celder people from the river-plain grazing country know they belong to the same country and do not always find each other easy.

## The Horses

The Celder horse is not one thing. Breeders would correct any description that implies it is. There are at minimum five distinct bloodlines maintained in Celder, each with its own physical type, its own particular strengths, its own appropriate uses, and its own breeding community who maintains it with the same multigenerational seriousness that the Lotharn mining families bring to their specific seams. The bloodlines are as old as the written records and older; the oldest oral genealogies of the major breeding houses extend further back than any text in the Caeras Academy's archives and are considered by the breeders to be the more reliable sources.

What the bloodlines share — the property that makes Celder horses as a category the most sought-after on the continent — is harder to state than what distinguishes them. There is a structural quality in the Celder animals that outside breeders have spent centuries attempting to reproduce and have consistently failed to reproduce completely: something in the relationship between the horse's temperament and its capacity, a quality that Celder breeders call *vel-caund* in their dialect — a word that the Standard Mittoli translations render as "willingness" but that the breeders correct immediately, because willingness implies the horse is choosing to cooperate and *vel-caund* describes something prior to and deeper than choice. The horse is formed that way. It is not deciding. The quality is what the breeding produces, and it is why a Celder horse purchased by a buyer in Nyross or Ambron and kept three generations from the Celder grasslands will have horses by the fourth generation that have something adjacent but not identical to what the original animal had. The buyers come back. The prices are what they are.

The cavalry horse — the specific animal bred for military use — is the product of a crossing tradition that combines two of the five major bloodlines in proportions that vary by house and are not shared outside the breeding community. The result is an animal that can carry an armored rider at combat speeds across the kind of terrain Celder's own cavalry fights on, and that does not break its quality under battlefield conditions in the way that physically comparable horses from other countries do. This last property — the behavior under conditions that frighten horses — is the thing that military commanders from outside Celder remark on first and find hardest to account for. A Celder cavalry horse and a horse from another country, side by side in any field inspection, look roughly comparable. In the fourth charge of a long battle, they are not comparable at all.

## The Cavalry

Celder cavalry is the best on the continent. This is not a claim made primarily by Celder, because Celder does not make claims of this kind; it is a conclusion reached repeatedly by commanders from other powers who have had occasion to fight beside Celder cavalry or against it. The records in the Caeras Academy's military archive contain a pattern that experienced strategists recognize: early assessments of a Celder cavalry force emphasizing the quality of the horses, middle-battle assessments emphasizing the quality of the riders, and post-engagement assessments grappling with something that the early emphasis on horses and riders does not quite account for.

The riders are trained from childhood in a tradition as specific and as old as the breeding tradition. This is not coincidence; the two traditions are the same tradition. The Celder cavalry houses train their horses and their riders simultaneously, in a system where the young horse and the young rider develop together, the rider learning the horse's specific character and the horse learning the specific rider, producing a working partnership calibrated to each individual rather than a general relationship between a type of horse and a type of rider. The resulting pairs have a quality of mutual intelligibility — the rider does not need to communicate much and the horse does not need to receive much — that outside cavalry commanders consistently describe as difficult to fight against because it appears to eliminate the half-second of hesitation that is present even in otherwise excellent cavalry.

The political consequence of this system is that Celder cavalry is not simply a military force attached to a state. It is the possession of the horse-lord families who bred the animals and trained the riders. No Celder cavalry fight as "the cavalry of Celder"; they fight as the cavalry of House Cauvern or the cavalry of the River Mouth Lords or whatever arrangement has been negotiated for the specific campaign. A power that wants Celder cavalry must deal with the houses, not with the nominal chief lord, and must offer terms that the houses find worth the commitment. What the nominal chief lord can do is facilitate that negotiation, which is not nothing — Canerd's prestige makes the chief lord the natural venue for multi-house arrangements — but it is not the same as commanding.

## Canerd

The mound that Canerd stands on is old enough that the Celder breeders' oral traditions do not claim to know who built it. This is notable because those traditions extend to considerable depth and do not usually lose track of significant constructions. The mound is simply there in the oldest layers of the traditions, already present, already named — not built by the current horse-lord families and not attributed to any predecessor that the traditions name. Academy geologists who have examined the mound's structure from what is accessible at the base say that it is artificial — the material is compacted in ways that do not occur naturally — and that it appears to have been built in stages across an extended period rather than in a single organized effort. What occasion produced those stages, or how many there were, is not established.

The castle on top of the mound is less mysterious in origin. It was built and rebuilt by the chief-lord lineage over several centuries, each phase adding to the height of the upper works and refacing the lower sections, so that the current structure is a composite of different periods that a practiced architectural eye can read as layers. The effect from a distance is unified and impressive. Up close it is a record of successive construction decisions made by people who understood that the height of Canerd was its primary statement and that any investment in height was worthwhile.

From the top of Canerd's highest tower, on a clear day, you can see Celder's full breadth. The herds in the river-plain grazing grounds are visible as dark shapes in the grass. The Oremindi are close enough to show individual snow fields. Minora's position, thirty-odd miles east, is visible in the quality of the river's bend and the slight thickening of habitation along the bank. A commander on the top of Canerd can see armies forming and moving at ranges that make the position strategically significant in a way that complements its symbolic weight. This combination — the emblem of Celder's sovereignty that is also a genuinely useful military position — is characteristic of the best of Celder's practical intelligence. The place does two things and neither is pretense.

The chief lord who rules from Canerd rules in the traditional sense: holds court, adjudicates disputes that the horse-lord houses bring to a neutral venue, presides at the great horse fairs, and represents Celder in any dealings with outside powers. What the chief lord does not do is command the cavalry houses, direct their breeding decisions, or override their individual lord-right arrangements in the territories they control. This is the character of the nominal sovereignty — real in its domain, which is the shared institutions that require a center; absent outside that domain, which is most of what the horse-lords consider their own business. The arrangement is old enough that most of the time neither party feels the friction.

When Celder is threatened from outside, the arrangement produces something that outside powers have consistently underestimated. The individual houses are not unified forces. They are also not disorganized: the great horse fair at Canerd has been convened under emergency terms during every significant threat in the documented record, and the horse-lords have a tradition of what those emergency terms require. The chief lord presides and holds the nominal command. The houses contribute cavalry in proportions set by traditional obligation. What results is not an ideal unified command structure, but it is a cavalry force that operates with enough coordination to be coherent and with enough individual house excellence to be devastating. The powers that have invaded Celder expecting a decentralized country to fight as one have been correct about the decentralization and wrong about the conclusion.

## The Horse Fairs

The great horse fair at Canerd is the organizing event of Celder's year and of much of the broader region's commercial calendar. It runs for twelve days at the midsummer, when the grass is at its best and the new foals of the year can be displayed against the previous year's crop for comparison. The fair is simultaneously a market, a diplomatic venue, a breeding-rights exchange, and the occasion for every formal legal proceeding that Celder's distributed governance has accumulated since the last fair.

Buyers from across Azhora attend. The prices negotiated at Canerd set the regional horse market for the following year. Military agents from Ambron, commercial buyers from the Iberos coast cities, representatives of the Mittoli city-states, and the occasional traveler from further afield all converge on the mound and the great plain market that spreads from its base. The horse-lord houses display their best animals on the second and third days, in a sequence governed by seniority arrangements that the houses negotiate among themselves with more energy than they bring to most political questions. The order of display matters. It always matters.

Beyond the main market, the breeding negotiations that happen at Canerd — house to house, bloodline to bloodline, generation to generation — are the transactions that the breeders themselves regard as the fair's real purpose. A buyer purchasing a Celder horse is purchasing the product of breeding decisions made three generations ago. The breeding decisions being made at this fair will produce the horses that buyers in thirty years' time purchase. The horse-lords think in those time scales naturally, and the fair is where the long-term planning becomes visible as specific agreements written in the breeding records that each house maintains with greater precision than any other document in their possession.

## Economy

Horses are the primary export and the primary measure of wealth. A Celder house's standing is assessed in the quality and size of its breeding herds before any other consideration. Land, in Celder, is important because it is where the horses graze. Grain, produced in the river-plain communities, is important because it supplements the grass and because the grooms and stable workers eat it. The entire economy is oriented toward the breeding enterprise at the top, and the things that support the breeding enterprise are valued in proportion to how directly they serve it.

The secondary export is cavalry service — either as independent contractors who negotiate terms with outside powers or, in cases of longer-term relationship, as standing military assets affiliated with a particular state for a campaign season or a defined period. Celder cavalry in Ambronite service has been a feature of more than one period of the empire's expansion; the Ambronite military preference, in strong imperial periods, is to have Celder cavalry working with them rather than the alternative. The terms the houses negotiate for military service are not disclosed, but the prosperity of the older cavalry houses suggests the terms are adequate.

The upper Lizeem also produces significant grain in its better bottomlands — Celder is not purely a horse economy — and the valley communities of the foothills produce timber and quarried stone in quantities that supply construction throughout the upper plain. But these are secondary considerations in Celder's own understanding of itself. The Academy's commercial records list Celder as a grain-and-horse exporter; the Celder breeders, if asked to describe their economy, would describe a horse economy with grain as logistics.

## Character

Celder people have a quality that their neighbors describe, not always charitably, as patience with movement and impatience with everything else. The horse culture produces people who understand that the best outcome from a good situation requires waiting for the right moment and then being very fast, and that most situations require more waiting than action. This translates, in the political character of the horse-lord houses, into a deliberateness that can look like inertia from outside until the moment it becomes decisive.

The specific Celder virtue that the breeders invoke in other contexts is *vel-caund* — the same quality they identify in the horses. Applied to people, it means something like: not waiting to be told, not deciding either, but being the kind of person or horse who responds fully when the moment is genuinely there. The opposite of this quality is the horse that has been drilled into obedience and stops being able to respond with its own fullness; also the person who has been trained into a role so completely that they cannot act outside it. Celder people apply this to their neighbors with a consistency that those neighbors find tiresome and occasionally accurate.

The relationship with the Oremindi is one that Celder does not discuss much and maintains carefully. The mountain rivers feed the grass that produces the horses; this is understood and respected without ceremony. A small number of Celder people in each generation make the approach to Sevron's pass and some of them complete the crossing. What brings them is not uniform. What they return with, if they return, they do not describe in terms that their neighbors find fully intelligible. The Celder breeders who have made this journey tend afterward to produce horses that other breeders describe as unusual without being able to say exactly how. This is noted. It is not discussed.

## Language

Celder speaks a dialect of Mittoli that the Caeras valley cities classify as "upper plain Mittoli" — recognizably the same language, diverged enough to have a distinct character. The divergence is most evident in the horse vocabulary, which is immense by standard Mittoli measures. Celder has specific terms for gaits, temperaments, conformation features, age-related behavioral changes, bloodline tendencies, and the various states of the horse-rider relationship that Standard Mittoli must describe in phrases. Outside scholars attempting to learn these terms find that many of them are not simply labels for observable features but descriptions of relationships and dynamics that require the horse to be present and in motion to have a referent.

The word *vel-caund* resists clean translation for the same reason. It is not a description of a quality one can observe in a stable. It is a description of something that appears in the relationship between an animal and a situation, and between a person and a situation, and it requires knowing what to look for. Celder people use it in ordinary conversation and adjust their expectations when outsiders do not track it.

The name Canerd is not Mittoli in any analyzed form. The Academy's linguistic files note it alongside the other pre-Mittoli place names of the upper plain and suggest a connection to the same substrate layer that appears in the Mithala place names further east — the older language, or languages, that the plain held before the Mittoli spread across it. What the word meant in that older system has not been established. What it means now is the castle on the mound and everything the castle stands for, which is enough.
