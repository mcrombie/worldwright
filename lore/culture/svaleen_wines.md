# Svaleen Wines: A Preliminary Variety Catalogue

*Compiled by the Division of Natural History and Regional Culture, Caeras Valley Academy. Seventh edition, ongoing. Note on the seventh edition: the previous six editions were also described as ongoing. The difference between this edition and its predecessors is that the committee responsible for this one has agreed, in writing, not to restart from the beginning when a new valley community is discovered. Whether this agreement will survive contact with the Imlamdris lake-country communities has not yet been tested.*

---

The Svaleen Peninsula contains more distinct cultivated grape varieties than any other region on the continent. The number crosses one thousand by any reasonable counting method, though the counting method is itself one of the most argued questions in Svaleen viticulture. Varieties held under two names in adjacent valleys, varieties that one guild considers a single type and another considers three, varieties documented in the hill interior that have never been compared to the official catalogues — all of these keep the number unsettled.

This catalogue uses the Caeras Academy's neutral classification system. Varieties are organized by primary growing territory. Where a variety appears in multiple territories under different names, it is listed under the most-documented territory with cross-references noted. This approach has not satisfied the Solis guild, the Enebreum guild, or the Imlamdris producers' association. It has, however, produced a usable catalogue, which is more than the peninsula's own systems have achieved.

**Territory divisions used in this catalogue:**
- *Solis Western Ridge*: limestone slopes west of Solis, organized by valley community
- *Enebreum Central Plateau*: elevated interior plateau, iron-rich soil, Enebreum city's wine country
- *Imlamdris Lake Country*: the lake basin and surrounding slopes, mineral-rich, warm
- *The Hill Interior*: isolated valleys of the central peninsula, oldest cultivation sites
- *Eastern Iberos-Facing Slopes*: coast-facing terraces, drier, Mediterranean character
- *Cross-territorial*: varieties without a single dominant growing area

## Grand Varieties

*The fifty grand varieties are recognized across the Confederation — or rather, each of the three classification guilds considers them important, though the guilds dispute each other's rankings. A grand variety is defined as: having documented cultivation history predating the current Confederation; growing in at least two separate territories; and having ongoing theological significance in at least one Thareth tradition. The definition was written by a Caeras Academy committee and accepted by the guilds by failing to formally reject it.*

**1. Netholveth** (Red; Solis Western Ridge, peninsula-wide) — The great Solis red. Deep garnet, tight tannin when young, richly layered at ten years or more; the variety around which the Solis classification system was built. Val Soleth and Val Thraleth have disputed originating ownership for eleven generations. Both communities have documentation. The Academy has reviewed both sets of records twice and determined that the dispute cannot be adjudicated on evidentiary grounds, which both valleys interpret as support for their own position.

**2. Valosori** (White; Solis Western Ridge, peninsula-wide) — The great Solis white. Pale gold, aromatic, stone fruit and brine; keeps better than most whites on the western ridge. Named for its color in old Svaleen. Enebreum claims a variant under a different name; the Academy has determined they are the same variety; neither guild accepts this determination; the variety continues growing in both territories.

**3. Thareveth** (Red; Hill Interior, sacred sites across peninsula) — The oldest documented variety; the name echoes the wine god's own. Dark, full, medicinal in concentrated old-vine form; used in the First Press ceremony across the peninsula wherever the tradition has been maintained without interruption. The Solis guild's records show a longer continuous cultivation history for Netholveth. The Solis guild does not discuss what this implies.

**4. Imlaveleth** (White; Imlamdris Lake Country) — The great Imlamdris white. Pale straw, distinctly mineral, with light natural effervescence in lakeside plantings and none in hillside plantings. Whether the lakeside and hillside forms are one variety or two has been debated for forty years. The Academy says one variety. Imlamdris says the lakeside form is the real one and the hillside form is what happens when the lake is taken away.

**5. Enbraleth** (Red; Enebreum Central Plateau) — The great Enebreum red. Darker than Netholveth, broader tannin, iron-mineral character from the plateau soil; keeps thirty years without difficulty. The Enebreum guild considers this the better variety. Solis declines to engage with the comparison.

**6. Morveleth** (Red; Hill Interior) — The ancient black grape. Very dark, tannic, bitter when young; the old-vine examples from the hill interior are among the most complex wines on the peninsula. Transplanted to the western ridge it produces adequate wine and the hill interior communities consider this outcome instructive.

**7. Krevethil** (White; Eastern Iberos-Facing Slopes) — The great eastern white. High acid, citrus, mineral; made in both dry and dessert styles. The dessert version — grapes dried on mats, then fermented — is the primary eastern export. The dry version is considered unimpressive by western-ridge drinkers, which the eastern communities consider a failure of palate rather than a failure of wine.

**8. Solarneth** (Red; Solis Western Ridge) — Possibly older than Netholveth; this question is definitionally important to the Val Soleth-Val Thraleth dispute and neither community has agreed to let the Academy settle it. Lighter than Netholveth, early-ripening, often blended. Several Solis winemakers argue for single-variety bottling. The Conclave's advisory committee has disagreed with them twice.

**9. Dulannol** (White; Imlamdris Lake Country, Eastern Slopes) — The great sweet grape. Harvested very late, sometimes with noble rot; produces the honeyed dessert wines that are the primary Svaleen export to the Moroshé desert routes. Decades of age do not diminish it. Several examples in the Solis archive cellar have not been opened in sixty years and are expected to reward waiting.

**10. Ornithavel** (White; Solis Western Ridge, Enebreum Central Plateau) — The great aromatic white. Rose and stone fruit on the nose, long finish; used as the primary gift-wine because the nose communicates immediately across cultural contexts. Both Solis and Enebreum grow it. The Solis version is considered more delicate; the Enebreum more structured. Both communities object to the framing that neither is objectively better.

**11. Svarneth** (Red; Hill Interior) — Blue-black skin, extreme pigment; used throughout the peninsula as a blending grape for color and concentration. Old-vine single-variety examples from the hill interior have a collectors' following that the Academy documents without fully explaining. The Academy notes the following is disproportionate to the number of bottles available.

**12. Kelvarel** (Red; Solis Western Ridge) — Bright, fresh, lower tannin; the variety most often poured at diplomatic events where the wine should please broadly rather than impress specifically. Solis winemakers who specialize in this variety have complicated feelings about its commercial reputation.

**13. Threveleth** (White; Enebreum Central Plateau) — Full-bodied, broad, textured; the Enebreum white that ages like a red. Fermented on skins for varying durations; color ranges from pale gold to deep amber depending on producer and year. Solis considers this a vinification technique rather than a variety. Enebreum considers Solis wrong.

**14. Sorthimvel** (Rosé; Solis Western Ridge) — The great Svaleen rosé; made from careful short maceration of Netholveth; pale copper, delicate, the wine of the Solis summer festival season. Made well, it is the most elegant wine on the peninsula. Made carelessly, it is pink table wine. The Solis guild has precise protocols for the difference.

**15. Spethomar** (Red; Eastern Iberos-Facing Slopes) — Dark, pepper-spiced, fruit-forward; made in a fresh style for coastal consumption and a concentrated dried-grape style for the desert routes. The dried-grape version is one of the few Svaleen wines with consistent market presence beyond the Iberos Sea.

**16. Vethanel** (White; Hill Interior) — Young-vine Vethanel is an unremarkable floral white. Old-vine Vethanel from the hill interior's centuries-old plantings is the most complex white wine produced on the peninsula — and by several assessments, on the continent. Access to old-vine fruit is controlled by the families who own the vines. They do not sell cuttings.

**17. Flevethil** (White; Enebreum Central Plateau) — The great plateau floral white; violet and white flower rather than rose, which distinguishes it from Ornithavel. The Enebreum Tharathven festival uses this variety by preference. Solis uses Ornithavel. Both guilds treat this as a competition.

**18. Amarsolin** (Red; Solis Western Ridge) — Named for the bitter-fruit character. Full tannin, austere; the wines that require patience. The Solis guild's third most contested classification: whether this is one variety or three closely related varieties is a question the seventh committee has agreed to defer.

**19. Golvethol** (Red; Enebreum Central Plateau) — The plateau cellar red; minimum ten years recommended; minimum twenty for the premium expressions. Produced specifically for aging and not available as a young wine through any legitimate Enebreum distributor. The Solis classification system has no category for this variety's style; it has addressed this by assigning it to three categories simultaneously depending on vintage, which the Enebreum guild has used as a standing argument for the superiority of its own system.

**20. Imrathel** (White; Imlamdris Lake Country) — The late-harvest Imlamdris white; harvested after the first cold snap, when sugar concentration is high but acidity is preserved. Made in both sweet and fortified styles. The lake's microclimate produces the late-harvest conditions more reliably than anywhere else on the peninsula, which Imlamdris considers its greatest viticultural advantage and does not understate.

**21. Krevaneth** (White; Eastern Iberos-Facing Slopes) — High acid, citrus and green apple, made for fish-pairing on the coast. Not a complex wine but an exactly right one. The coastal communities consider it perfect for what it does and do not want classification systems to make it anything else.

**22. Ornavel** (White; Eastern Iberos-Facing Slopes) — Aromatic, lower-acid than Krevethil; grown for the coastal trade. Good fresh, unremarkable aged; designed for consumption within two years. The eastern coast makes this in its largest volume of any white variety.

**23. Tholavel** (White; Solis Western Ridge) — Fresh, light, green herbs and citrus; everyday table wine; the Solis domestic white consumed in larger quantity than any other variety on the ridge. Ages poorly; not designed to age. The variety has no pretension and the Solis guild values it for exactly that quality.

**24. Velimath** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — The deep south red; maximum sun exposure, ripe, generous, high natural alcohol; the variety most recognizable to outside buyers as quintessential Solis. This concerns the ridge's serious winemakers, who consider it the easiest rather than the best expression of the western-ridge terroir.

**25. Sornethal** (Rosé; Hill Interior) — Deep copper, full-bodied for a rosé, designed for food rather than summer refreshment. The hill interior diet is substantial and seasonal; this rosé accompanies it. The coastal communities do not know what to do with it and have decided not to try.

**26. Imbreleth** (Red; Enebreum Central Plateau) — Medium-weight, food-oriented; the Enebreum everyday red and the commercial foundation of the guild's income. The guild's classification committee does not discuss this variety at the Conclave. There is nothing to argue about and the committee finds the absence of argument disorienting.

**27. Dulimeth** (White; Imlamdris Lake Country) — Slightly sweet in natural form; made dry by early fermentation halt. The dry and naturally sweet versions have been classified as separate varieties by the Enebreum guild. The Academy disagrees. The Enebreum guild has not changed its classification, and the Imlamdris producers find it characteristically Enebreum that the plateau would presume to classify the lake country's wines.

**28. Kethimath** (Red; Enebreum Central Plateau) — Robust, structured, with the quality Ascarth merchants call *keth* when they encounter it in Avite wine. The comparison pleases neither community, for different reasons. The Avites consider it flattering to Enebreum. The Enebreum consider the Avite framing reductive.

**29. Vornathel** (White; Enebreum Central Plateau) — Pale gold, oxidation-resistant; the plateau's white designed for aging. Thirty years is considered its comfortable horizon; examples beyond that are the Enebreum guild's cellar archive material and not for sale.

**30. Ornethavel** (Red; Solis Western Ridge) — The aromatic red; unusual on a ridge known for aromatic whites. Val Orneth's distinction. The character is recognizable — the same valley's florals present in a structured tannic red — and unexpected enough that outside buyers often find it disorienting before finding it interesting.

**31. Threneleth** (Red; Hill Interior) — Dark, slow, shadowed character; minimum twelve years before its nature becomes legible. The old-vine examples approach Morveleth in complexity. The hill interior communities who maintain these vines consider their patience a form of knowledge.

**32. Fleveleth** (White; Solis Western Ridge) — The wedding wine of the western ridge; floral, delicate, consumed young. Presented at betrothal ceremonies and at the feast. Made in smaller quantity than the commercial whites; almost entirely consumed domestically rather than exported.

**33. Imlavelavar** (White; Imlamdris Lake Country) — The hillside expression of Imlaveleth; fuller than the lakeside expression, less effervescent, more accessible to outside buyers. The lakeside communities consider the hillside form a simplification. The hillside communities consider the lakeside form's effervescence a novelty that distracts from the wine.

**34. Solimeth** (White; Solis Western Ridge) — Clean, precise, medium-acid; the most widely exported Svaleen white and the variety that defines "Svaleen white" for buyers who have encountered only one. The western ridge's serious winemakers have the same complicated feelings about this variety's commercial success that they have about Velimath's commercial success in reds.

**35. Krevathil** (White; Solis Western Ridge) — High-acid western-ridge white; not the eastern coast's pure citrus style but the ridge's own expression of acidity — broader, more mineral, longer finish. The variety the Solis guild uses when it wants to demonstrate that the western ridge can produce serious whites alongside serious reds.

**36. Dulvethil** (White; Imlamdris Lake Country) — Precociously sweet; fully ripe before all other lake country varieties. Used for the lightest dessert wines and for the Imlamdris First Press ceremony, where the sweetness is held to be theologically appropriate. The theology has not been formally explained but the tradition has continued without challenge.

**37. Galneth** (Red; Solis Western Ridge) — Early-ripening, thin-skinned; terrible in poor years, spectacular in good ones. The Val Galneth community has three hundred years of detailed documentation on which years are which and why. They share this information only with other Val Galneth growers. Outside buyers make their purchasing decisions without it and accept the variance.

**38. Velathnil** (Red; Solis Western Ridge) — Val Velath grows nothing else. The wine has been made without interruption from the same old-vine plantings for longer than any surviving record. The variety achieved grand-variety status not through comparative tasting at the Conclave but through the simple fact of its age and the community's refusal to supplement it with anything younger or more fashionable.

**39. Threvimar** (White; Imlamdris Lake Country) — Full-bodied, naturally textured; the lake country white that requires no vinification technique to achieve its weight. Imlamdris insists this is the original variety and the Enebreum Threveleth is derived from it. The timeline evidence is inconclusive. The Enebreum guild insists on the reverse.

**40. Kelavar** (White; Solis Western Ridge) — Bright, precise, fine acid; similar in structure to Krevaneth but with the western ridge's characteristic fuller body. Good with seafood; made in quantity for the peninsula's coastal markets. The Academy has determined that Kelavar and Krevaneth share a common ancestor but are now distinct varieties. Neither community disputes this finding, which is unusual enough that the Academy noted it.

**41. Solarevel** (Rosé; Solis Western Ridge) — Summer rosé; the variety specifically made for the warm months and culturally associated with them such that year-round availability is technically possible but socially unusual. Made from Solarneth with short maceration. Solis's answer to the question of what to drink between the spring festival and the autumn harvest.

**42. Imlameth** (Rosé; Imlamdris Lake Country) — Faint pink, fresh, produced domestically for the lake country's summer festivals. Not exported in meaningful quantity; it doesn't keep as well as the whites and the demand is local. The lake country considers this appropriate. A wine for a place should belong to that place.

**43. Nethomarveth** (Red; Hill Interior) — Found in three hill interior valleys; grown on sites that predate the Confederation. Produces, in good vintages, the wine that the Solis guild — in a document it has not publicly distributed — considers the finest on the peninsula. In poor vintages it is harsh and unresolved. The hill interior communities do not release poor vintages. They simply record that no wine was made that year.

**44. Vethonath** (Red; Hill Interior) — Young vines produce undrinkable wine. Vines over a century old produce something that outside critics have described variously as profound, disturbing, and impossible to finish and equally impossible to put down. The hill interior families who own these vines regard all three descriptions as insufficient.

**45. Spethorvel** (Red; Eastern Iberos-Facing Slopes) — The pepper-spice red for the coastal trade; designed specifically to pair with the salt-preserved and spiced foods of the eastern port diet. Commercially successful and consistent. The eastern communities make it without ceremony and regard the commercial success as evidence that they understand their market.

**46. Drevethol** (Red; Hill Interior) — Named for its unusual vine growth pattern; the plant hangs rather than climbing in the standard manner and requires specific site conditions to thrive. Grows on the steepest, least-accessible hill interior sites. The wine is idiosyncratic; the community that makes it is comfortable with this.

**47. Amalveth** (Red; Eastern Iberos-Facing Slopes) — Bitter-edged, high acid; adapted specifically to the dry eastern slope conditions and unable to grow elsewhere with its character intact. The eastern communities consider this an asset; outside buyers consider this a limitation; the variety has no opinion and continues thriving.

**48. Imlamorbeth** (Red; Enebreum Western Section) — Grown in the zone where the plateau transitions toward the western ridge; claimed by both the Enebreum and Solis guilds as belonging to their classification domain. The families who grow it have not taken a position and sell to both guilds' distributors.

**49. Kreimoleth** (White; Eastern Iberos-Facing Slopes) — The eastern coast's standard commercial white; reliable, clean, without distinction; the most-exported single variety from the peninsula by volume. The eastern coast has no pretensions about this and prices it accordingly.

**50. Sorethivel** (Rosé; Solis Western Ridge, Enebreum border) — Grown in the transitional zone between western ridge and plateau. The rosé character is different from either pure territory's expression: lighter than the ridge, more mineral than the plateau. The Academy has classified it as cross-territorial. Both guilds have classified it as their own. The families produce it without acknowledgment of any classification system.

## Solis Western Ridge — Reds

*The western ridge is the most densely classified viticultural territory on the continent. Limestone substrate varying at the scale of individual terraces produces wine variation that the Solis guild's classification system was designed to capture and that it partially captures. Valley names are given where origin is firmly established; some varieties have migrated across valley lines over generations and their origin community is disputed.*

### Northern Section

**51. Solethrel** (Red; Val Soleth) — Val Soleth's secondary red alongside Netholveth; deep garnet, iron-edged. Not the prestige variety but the one the valley drinks most.

**52. Thralevath** (Red; Val Thraleth) — Val Thraleth's prestige red; smoky, full, long-aging. The heart of the valley's claim against Val Soleth regarding the true primary variety of the northern ridge.

**53. Krevethanith** (Red; Val Kreveth) — Val Kreveth's primary red; sharp, acid-driven, structured differently from any Soleth variety. When Val Soleth attempted to classify this as a Netholveth variant, Val Kreveth burned the document.

**54. Solarnithel** (Red; Val Soleth) — Early-ripening Solarneth clone; lighter, more immediately appealing than the parent variety. Val Thraleth claims they developed this first. Val Soleth has records that predate the Thraleth claim by forty years. Val Thraleth disputes the records' authenticity.

**55. Ornethavel-Red** (Red; Val Orneth) — Val Orneth's primary red; aromatic character in a tannic frame, unusual on the ridge. The valley considers this its distinction. Neighboring valleys consider it unusual. Both are correct.

**56. Imboleth** (Red; Val Imbath) — Val Imbath's primary red; dark, structured, produced in limited quantity from the small valley's best sites. Access to the valley cooperative's allocation requires a long-standing trade relationship.

**57. Velsorthim** (Red; Val Sorthim) — Dense, dark, iron-mineral; the Val Sorthim standard wine. The valley disputes its classification within Solis territory as it is geographically closer to Enebreum. Both guilds claim it. The valley collects payment from both.

**58. Threvamor** (Red; Val Thraleth) — Heavy blending grape; full-bodied, broad; the Thraleth variety used to add weight to the valley's lighter years. Bottled alone it overwhelms; Val Thraleth bottles it alone in good years as a demonstration.

**59. Kelsveth** (Red; Solis Northern Section, unfixed valley) — Northern section red; lighter character from cooler exposures; good acid and early drinking. The northern section communities consider their wines undervalued relative to the central and southern sections.

**60. Galnithavar** (Red; Val Galneth) — The secondary Galneth variety; reliable where Galneth is variable; the backbone of the valley's commercial production in years when the grand variety's yields are too low or the acid balance is off.

**61. Morvenath** (Red; Val Morven) — Dark, extracted, direct; Val Morven is in the southern reach of the northern section and receives more direct sun than the valley's position suggests. Not subtle. Very good with food.

**62. Thalethomar** (Red; Val Thraleth) — The valley's oldest recorded variety, predating the current cultivation system; grown from old-vine material on the original terraces. Low-yielding, intense, made only when the old-vine fruit meets the community's standard.

**63. Krevanathol** (Red; Val Kreveth) — Kreveth's softer variety; grown on the valley floor where the acid is lower. Val Kreveth considers this a lesser wine. Outside merchants find it more approachable. The market agrees with the merchants; Val Kreveth accepts the sales and maintains its assessment.

**64. Solibreveth** (Red; Val Soleth) — Soleth's cellar wine; minimum fifteen years recommended. The valley cooperative maintains reference bottles from before the Confederation's founding. These are not available for purchase. The Solis guild has asked twice.

**65. Velimorbeth** (Red; Solis Northern Section, multiple valleys) — Northern section dark variety; anomalously dark for cool northern exposures; the variety that surprises buyers who assume northern Solis means light and thin.

**66. Imbetholveth** (Red; Val Imbath) — Imbath's second red; lighter than Imboleth, made from younger vines and the valley's less favorable sites. Reliable commercial wine.

**67. Ornimarbeth** (Red; Val Orneth) — Orneth's heavy, tannic red; the valley's most serious wine; minimum eight years. The rest of the ridge finds Val Orneth's aging requirements excessive. Val Orneth has not adjusted them.

**68. Thralibeth** (Red; Val Thraleth) — The valley's fresh everyday red; the table wine made in the largest volume. Val Thraleth considers the existence of this wine and the existence of Thralevath simultaneously as evidence that the valley contains multitudes.

**69. Solnethavar** (Red; Val Soleth) — Ancient blending grape; found mixed with Netholveth in old plantings. The Academy classifies it as a separate variety. Val Soleth grows it alongside Netholveth and does not believe the distinction is necessary.

**70. Galdreveth** (Red; Solis Northern Section) — The northern section's structured wine; the most serious wine the northern communities produce. Not as intense as the central section but, in the northern communities' assessment, more honest.

**71. Krevethovar** (Red; Val Kreveth) — Old-vine blend component; maintained from the valley's oldest plantings specifically to add complexity to the premium blends. Not released as a single-variety wine.

**72. Velomibeth** (Red; Val Sorthim) — Sorthim's secondary red; lighter than Velsorthim, accessible, the commercial wine the valley produces for the Solis market while the prestige variety stays in the valley.

**73. Thralevimbar** (Red; Val Thraleth) — Standard commercial Thraleth; the high-volume production wine. The valley produces this in largest quantity. The valley's identity is the prestige wine; the valley's accounts are the commercial wine.

**74. Orneveleth** (Red; Val Orneth) — Orneth's spice-marked red; an unusual aromatic profile that divides the Conclave between those who find it interesting and those who find it irregular. Orneth finds the Conclave's opinion irrelevant to its quality.

**75. Imbarethol** (Red; Val Imbath) — Imbath's upper-slope variety; grown on the steepest sites; tiny production; intense character. Released only in years the cooperative considers adequate, which is fewer than half.

### Central Section

**76. Amorethvel** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Austere, tannic, age-demanding; the central ridge's serious wine. Minimum five years before opening; many producers recommend ten. The Solis guild's prestige argument against the plateau.

**77. Velimathol** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Medium-weight central section red; not the most intense but the most consistent; the variety the central section relies on for commercial reliability.

**78. Solimbarath** (Red; Solis Central Section) — High-acid blending red; the variety used in the city's standard commercial blends. Not prestigious; consistently useful.

**79. Threniveth** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Grown in the north-facing shadow-slots of the central ridge; cooler character in the middle of the ridge's warmest zone. The producers here are evangelical about under-appreciated varieties. Some converts have been made.

**80. Vethomilath** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Old-vine blending dark; the central section's analog to Val Kreveth's old-vine blend component. Adds depth without adding weight.

**81. Amorelveth** (Red; Solis Central Section) — The austere cellar red's companion; slightly lower tannin; blended with Amorethvel in the city's prestige labels to maintain structure while adding approachability.

**82. Threnolavar** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Shadow-valley's commercial red; the accessible version of Threniveth for the city market. More approachable; less distinctive.

**83. Golvimbar** (Red; Solis Central Section) — The central section cellar red; equivalent to the plateau's Golvethol in intended aging. The comparison irritates both communities, as each considers their long-aging tradition independent and more sophisticated.

**84. Velimeth** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Mid-weight central-section standard; the variety produced in the largest commercial volume from the central ridge. Clean, reliable, uncontroversial.

**85. Solrevimbar** (Red; Val Soleth, grown in central section) — A Soleth variety planted in the central section that has, over two centuries, acquired the central section's mineral character. Val Soleth classifies it as Soleth-origin. The central section classifies it as their own. Both are describing the same vines.

**86. Amarbethol** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Bitter-edged dark; acquired taste; specific to the sections of the central ridge with the highest limestone concentration. A cult variety among buyers from the eastern trading cities.

**87. Ornethimbar** (Red; Val Orneth, central section plantings) — The commercial Orneth red; the high-volume production wine the valley makes for the Solis city market while the prestige varietals stay in Val Orneth.

**88. Solimorbeth** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Darkest blending grape of the central section; rarely bottled alone; used to add color and weight to the commercial blends.

**89. Krevaveth** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Cross-influence variety; a central-section planting with Kreveth-style acid structure that arrived through generations of cuttings from the northern section. Both communities acknowledge the origin and dispute what it means for classification.

**90. Veletholveth** (Red; Solis Central Section) — Old-vine dark; the central section's equivalent of the hill interior's old-vine culture; not the same age, not the same character, but the same investment in waiting.

### Southern Section

**91. Velimavar** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — Deep, warm, generous; the southern-exposure Solis red that defines the variety for most outside buyers. High natural alcohol. Ripens fully in most years.

**92. Sornathevel** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — The southern section's structured red; from the better-positioned south-facing sites; mineral from the limestone, full from the sun exposure.

**93. Golvethimbar** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — Southern section Golvethol planting; the variety ages differently here than on the plateau; both communities consider their version definitive. The Academy has noted the tasting-profile differences without pronouncing on supremacy.

**94. Velorbeth** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — Maximum-ripeness dark; from the most direct-sun exposures on the ridge. Fruit-forward, popular with the Iberos coastal market.

**95. Golvethimolveth** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — Deep-south cellar red; maximum-expression aging from the warmest sites. One of the few southern-section wines the Enebreum guild acknowledges as serious competition for its own cellar tradition.

**96. Ombreleth** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — Named for the unusual warm amber tint of the fully ripe grape skin. Specific to the southernmost sites. The wine has a character distinct from all other southern varieties; the community that makes it grows nothing else on its best sites.

**97. Veldulavar** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — Southern-section soft red; approachable, commercial; the southern equivalent of the table wine. The valley keeps the serious wines; this serves the market.

**98. Solimethavar** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — Commercial southern red; made for broad distribution through the Iberos coastal trade. The most widely available Solis red in the southern port markets.

**99. Velimathiveth** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — Old-vine southern; from the oldest southern-section plantings on the most direct-sun sites. Low yield; high intensity; aged minimum eight years by the producers who take it seriously.

**100. Ornimoleth** (Red; Solis Southern Section) — Southern aromatic red; the south's version of Val Orneth's aromatic character, which arrived through vine cuttings and adapted over generations to the warmer exposure. Val Orneth does not recognize it as the same family. Both communities' wines are identifiably different; both carry the aromatic marker.

**101. Krevelinath** (Red; Val Kreveth) — High-elevation Kreveth red; more mineral, less fruit than the valley floor wines. The peak of Kreveth viticultural expression by the community's own assessment.

## Imlamdris Lake Country

*The lake basin and the slopes surrounding it constitute the warmest and most mineral-distinct growing area on the peninsula. The lake moderates temperature; the limestone-and-mineral substrate gives the wines their specific character; the altitude and orientation of individual hillside zones produces wide variation within a relatively small area. The Imlamdris producers' association has its own classification system that the Academy has not attempted to reconcile with the Solis or Enebreum systems, as the effort of doing so has been estimated to exceed the effort of classifying the other two combined.*

**301. Imlaveleth-Lakeside** (White; Imlamdris Lakeside Zone) — The lakeside expression of the grand variety; light effervescence, pure mineral, the most distinctive wine in the lake country.

**302. Imlaveleth-Hillside** (White; Imlamdris Hillside Zone) — The hillside expression; fuller, no effervescence, more accessible. Both communities agree the wine is different. Neither agrees which is better.

**303. Dulannol-Late** (White; Imlamdris Southern Zone) — Late-harvest version; the sweetest naturally produced Dulannol; from the southern zone where the warmest conditions allow maximum sugar concentration.

**304. Imrathel-Sweet** (White; Imlamdris Northern Zone) — Sweet version; made from post-cold-snap harvested Imrathel; the lake country's most traditional dessert wine.

**305. Imrathel-Fortified** (White; Imlamdris Northern Zone) — Fortified version; the lake country's fortified wine for the desert-route trade.

**306. Dulimeth-Dry** (White; Imlamdris Central Zone) — Dry Dulimeth; fermentation halted for dryness; the counterintuitive expression of a naturally sweet variety.

**307. Dulimeth-Natural** (White; Imlamdris Central Zone) — Naturally sweet Dulimeth; the lake country's everyday dessert wine.

**308. Threvimar** (White; Imlamdris Central Zone) — The full-bodied lake country white; naturally textured without vinification assistance.

**309. Imlavelavar** (White; Imlamdris Hillside Zone) — Hillside-accessible version of the grand variety; broader appeal, less mineral intensity.

**310. Vornavel** (White; Imlamdris Lakeside Zone) — Very pale, naturally effervescent from the lakeside conditions; made sparkling by tradition.

**311. Imlamorbeth-W** (White; Imlamdris Northern Zone) — Northern zone white; the coolest-exposure lake country white; more structured than the central zone expressions.

**312. Dulvethil-OldVine** (White; Imlamdris Central Zone) — Old-vine precociously sweet white; from the oldest Dulvethil plantings. The sweetness is deeper and the acid higher than younger-vine expressions.

**313. Imlathorol** (White; Imlamdris Eastern Slopes) — Eastern lakeside slopes white; facing the morning sun; fresh, bright, higher acid than the western-facing sites.

**314. Imlaveleth-Reserve** (White; Imlamdris Lakeside Zone) — Reserve lakeside grand variety; from the most mineral of the lakeside sites.

**315. Threvimarveth** (White; Imlamdris Central Zone) — Old-vine Threvimar; the complex, textured version from the oldest central zone plantings.

**316. Dulannol-Dry** (White; Imlamdris Southern Zone) — Dry expression of the great dessert variety; fermented fully dry; shows the variety's acid structure without the sweetness.

**317. Imlameth-Sparkling** (Rosé; Imlamdris Central Zone) — Natural sparkling rosé; the lake country's characteristic light effervescence in rosé form.

**318. Imlaveleth-Sparkling** (Sparkling; Imlamdris Lakeside Zone) — The lake country's prestige sparkling; the natural effervescence of the lakeside grand variety captured and enhanced.
