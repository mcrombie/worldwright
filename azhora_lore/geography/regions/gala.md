# Gala

Gala is the land on the western bank of the Lizeem near its mouth — the coastal plain that lies between the rocky base of the Ascarth Peninsula to the north and the river's broad delta to the east, opening south onto the Iberos Sea. By latitude and soil it is among the more comfortable places on Azhora's eastern coast: warm, well-watered by the Lizeem's distributaries and seasonal rains off the sea, sheltered from the worst of the interior weather by the Ascarth hills behind it. It grows food without great effort and has a harbor that, while never as deep or as busy as Nylon's across the river, handles enough traffic to make it a stop on any coastal itinerary. The region is named for its principal city. Both the city and its hinterland have spent most of their history in the uncomfortable position of being worth wanting by someone larger.

The King of Aevis controls Gala more often than not. This control is not always the same thing — sometimes it is garrison and direct administration, sometimes tribute and nominal acknowledgment, sometimes an arrangement so loose that Gala is in practice independent while technically not saying so. The distinction matters to the Galans in practical ways and less than expected in cultural ones. Galan culture has not become Avite. The decades under Aevis's suzerainty have left marks on the vocabulary, on the bronze fittings of the city's doors, and on the political reflexes of its merchant families, but the Galans remain recognizably and specifically Galan in a way that several generations of Avite kings have found either charming or irrelevant depending on their priorities.

## Geography

The region of Gala runs roughly thirty miles along the coast from the southern base of the Ascarth Peninsula to where the Lizeem's delta distributaries fan out into the sea, and twenty to twenty-five miles inland before the terrain rises into the foothills that mark the edge of the Ascarth Peninsula's highland interior. The coastal plain is flat, fertile, drained by a network of small rivers fed from those hills and supplemented by irrigation channels in the drier southern stretches.

The northern edge of Gala, where the plain meets the foothills, has a different character from the coast: more pastoral, with the upland farming communities that supply the coastal markets, distinct enough from the port towns to be considered almost a different region by the Galans themselves. The people of the foothills have a relationship with Aevis that is more direct — they are closer to the peninsula's territory, their young men have more often been called into Avite campaigns, their accent has drifted further toward the Avite dialect.

The coastal strip and the city of Gala itself are where Galan culture lives in its most concentrated form.

## The City of Gala

The city sits at the junction of the coastal road from Aevis and the Lizeem's western channel — close enough to the river mouth to receive goods coming down from the Mittoli interior, close enough to Nylon's harbor to be in commercial competition with it on nearly every category of trade, and close enough to the Ascarth Peninsula to be in the field of attention of every Avite king who has considered the implications of a market city on his doorstep that he does not control.

Gala is not as large as Nylon. It does not have Nylon's walls, Nylon's intellectual reputation, or Nylon's position at the river's mouth rather than the river's side. What it has is the western approach: every trader coming overland from the Ascarth Peninsula, from the Mittoli country south of the Oremindi, or from the Pyrosi lands moving toward the Iberos Sea arrives at Gala before they arrive at Nylon. The distinction matters commercially. Gala has shaped the terms of trade before the goods reach the larger harbor, and the Galans are aware of this leverage and have built their commercial institutions around it.

The city's harbor is functional rather than magnificent — a workmanlike port with stone quays, solid warehousing, and the infrastructure of a city that takes trade seriously without aestheticizing it. The upper town has more architectural ambition: the council hall is the oldest public building, built in a Mittoli civic style with later modifications — several of the facing panels are Avite bronze work from a period of particularly stable Ascarth control, when the king of the time invested in Gala's prestige as a way of investing in his own. The bronze panels remain. Three kings since then have considered replacing them with something more locally representative and have not, for the same reason they were installed: the bronze is excellent and its removal would be noticed.

The market district is the city's real center. It runs from the harbor north to the road junction and is organized by goods: the grain quarter, the oil and wine quarter, the metal-goods stalls (Avite bronze prominently featured, at prices that reflect both quality and origin), the textile section, the spice and preserved-food row that serves the maritime trade. Dispute arbitration in the market is handled by the Guild of Assessors, a Galan institution older than the current council structure, whose members are trained in commercial law and whose decisions are respected by traders from every polity that uses the market because the alternative is not using the market, which is worse.

## Galan Culture

The Galans are not warriors by aspiration. The Avite heroic tradition — the training from childhood, the glory in battle, the *veth* who remember your deeds for generations — has not taken hold in Gala despite centuries of Avite proximity and intermittent Avite rule. The Galans who end up in Avite campaigns do so because tribute sometimes takes that form, not because they consider it the highest aspiration available to them. They fight adequately when required, return home as soon as permitted, and generally regard the distinction between an adequate soldier who comes back and a celebrated warrior who does not as not difficult to resolve.

What Galan culture produces instead is competence in exchange: an extensive tradition of commercial law, a hospitality practice that functions simultaneously as ethics and as trade infrastructure, and a public culture organized around arbitration and deal-making rather than around competition for glory. The Galan guest-right tradition — which governs the treatment of any trader who arrives in the city, establishes what protections they are owed, and specifies the penalties for violation — is old enough to predate the oldest records of Avite contact and is taken seriously enough that the Guild of Assessors has jurisdiction over violations by Galan hosts as well as by visiting traders. A merchant who is robbed in Nylon has a legal problem. A merchant who is robbed in Gala has created a civic crisis.

This tradition of protected exchange is not idealism. It is the rational infrastructure of a city whose prosperity depends on traders from incompatible polities choosing to conduct business in the same place. The Galans have made it easy and safe to trade in Gala, and they have made it expensive to violate the conditions that make trading there possible. The reputation thus built is old enough to be self-sustaining.

The food culture of Gala is the region's most visible cultural expression. The coastal plain produces grain, olives, several varieties of fig and stone fruit, and a local wine that Svaleen merchants consider rough but that the Galans defend with a specificity of argument that suggests they are not entirely wrong and are certainly not uncertain. The cooking tradition is the one that most clearly distinguishes Galan households from their Avite and Nyloni neighbors: elaborate, ingredient-focused, with a tradition of communal meal preparation at festivals that visitors from Nylon find either excessive or admirable depending on how hungry they arrived. The Nyloni have a food culture of their own and are not inclined to cede superiority in it; the Galans have never claimed superiority, only sufficiency, which the Nyloni find insufficiently competitive.

The Galan calendar is organized around the agricultural year and the trading seasons rather than the flood calendar of the Mittoli interior or the navigational seasons of the Bouéni. The major festivals correspond to harvest, the opening and closing of the sailing season, and a winter feast that is specifically about the obligation of households to provide for those who have nothing to provide for themselves — an old tradition whose origin the Galan histories assign to a period of occupation when the occupying forces' requisitions left parts of the population short, and the community developed the habit of pooling what remained. Whether this history is accurate or illustrative, the Galans maintain the festival.

## The Avite Relationship

Gala's value to Aevis is commercial. Its merchant families control trading relationships with Nylon across the river, with the Mittoli cities upstream, with the island cities of the Iberos Sea, and with maritime traders from the Svaleen and beyond. These relationships are the product of generations of cultivation. They cannot be transferred to an Avite governor who does not have them. A king of Aevis who takes Gala and then governs it as he governs the peninsula's tributary cities — demanding warriors, imposing the Avite prestige economy, disrupting the commercial arrangements — will find, within a generation, that the thing worth having has become worth less.

The Avite kings who have handled Gala well have understood this. They take tribute in provisions and access — Gala's grain and oil supplement the peninsula's food supply, and Gala's market gives Avite bronze goods a commercial outlet beyond the peninsula's direct trade. They maintain garrison enough to keep Gala nominally in hand without disrupting the city's function. They leave the Guild of Assessors alone. In exchange, Gala's political institutions do not press the question of independence, the roads are maintained, and the trade flows through channels that Aevis is positioned to tax.

The Avite kings who have handled Gala badly have demanded that it behave as a conquered city — warriors, conscription, tribute in forms that suit the Avite prestige economy rather than the Galan commercial one. This produces a version of Gala that is less useful and resentful, and eventually produces a period in which the Kingdom of Ascarth is in one of its dissolution phases and Gala reasserts its council governance and begins the slow work of rebuilding the commercial relationships that were disrupted.

The Galans observe this cycle with the equanimity of people who have outlasted several iterations. They know how Avite kings tend to end. They know how long the Kingdom of Ascarth has historically persisted before it comes apart. Their council records, which go back further than their city's independence does, contain assessments of every period of Avite control — what was taken, what was left, what needed rebuilding afterward. These records are not vengeful documents. They are maintenance logs. The city that built them knows it will be in the same position again and wants to know what to expect.

## Gala and Nylon

The river separates them; commerce connects them. Nylon sits at the Lizeem's mouth on the eastern bank, drawing the river's goods and the Iberos Sea trade into its harbor and its salons. Gala sits on the western bank a few miles upstream, drawing the overland trade and the coastal road traffic before it reaches the river.

The two cities are in commercial competition and know it, and they manage the competition through a combination of formal treaty arrangements that are renegotiated every twenty years or so and a culture of measured respect between their merchant and diplomatic classes. Nylon's questori and Gala's Guild of Assessors have a professional relationship — the questori find the Assessors rigorous in ways they respect, if not in ways they entirely agree with, and the Assessors find the questori sophisticated in ways that are occasionally useful and more often than not beside the practical point.

The Nyloni consider themselves the grander city, which is accurate in most of the ways grandness is measured. The Galans do not contest this. They note, privately, that grandness and commercial position are not the same thing, and that the road west from Aevis reaches their city before it reaches Nylon. The Nyloni, when this is put to them, have several responses prepared, all of them technically correct and none of them quite the point either.

## Language

Gala speaks a Mittoli-derived mainland dialect — related to the speech of the Lizeem valley communities upstream, distinct from Nylon's coastal variant, and leavened with accumulated vocabulary from every political and commercial contact the region has sustained. Avite borrowings appear in the legal and administrative registers: terms for tribute, garrison, the protocols of royal acknowledgment, and several words for categories of military obligation that Galan has no independent term for because Galan culture did not develop the need. Iberos Sea vocabulary appears in the maritime and commercial registers. A layer of pre-Mittoli terms persists in the names of geographical features — the small rivers, the coastal inlets, the specific soils of the agricultural plain — in the way that the names of things that were there before the current speakers arrived tend to persist.

The name *Gala* itself is from this older layer. What it meant to whoever named the place before the current population arrived is not established.
