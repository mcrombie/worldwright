# Pyros

Pyros is both a name and a concept — two distinct regions that share a word, a geography, and a complicated history. West Pyros and East Pyros are adjacent territories separated by a ridge line and a cultural divide that is, in some respects, older than either name.

The word "Pyros" appears in multiple Azhoran language families with similar meaning: fire, heat, or more specifically the residue of fire — ash, char, the aftermath. This is not coincidental. The Pyros regions sit above a zone of ancient volcanic activity that, while geologically quiet now, left its mark on the land in ways that are still visible: the soil is dark and mineral-rich from old ash deposits, certain hillsides expose layers of basalt and pumice, and in the deep valleys there are occasional hot springs and fumaroles that produce wisps of sulfurous steam even in summer. The land remembers what it was.

## West Pyros

West Pyros is the greener, wetter, more populated of the two. It sits between the Mittolo forest country to the north and west and the ridge that divides it from its eastern counterpart. The volcanic soil makes it extraordinarily fertile; the hillside terraces of West Pyros, built up over centuries of careful agriculture, are among the most productive agricultural land on the continent per unit area.

The terrace animal of West Pyros is the ashback, a compact volcanic-slope bovid described in **Livestock of Azhora**. Ashbacks are not impressive beside river-horns or tallhorns, but they are sure-footed, manure-rich, and stubborn enough to work ledged fields that would break larger cattle. Pyrosi farmers value them less as spectacle than as proof that a household has enough patience to make steep land answer.

West Pyros has historically been the most contested agricultural zone in Azhora. Mittoli powers have invaded it, held it briefly, and been expelled from it at regular intervals throughout the historical record. The Pyrosi have never been a significant military power, but they have an almost pathological capacity for outlasting occupation — governing their own affairs quietly through any external administration, preserving institutions beneath the surface, and resuming full self-governance the moment pressure releases. Scholars of political history call this the Pyrosi Method and study it with a mixture of admiration and fascination.

The people of West Pyros are compact, dark-haired, and speak a dialect that mixes Mittoli grammar with Pyrosi vocabulary in roughly equal measure. They are known for their food — the volcanic soil and reliable rainfall produce fruits and vegetables of unusual sweetness — and for a cultural formality that outsiders mistake for coldness until they are inside it, at which point it resolves into warmth of an unusual intensity.

**Gala** is the principal city of West Pyros — old, walled, built on the terraced hillside above a river confluence. Its markets have been continuous for recorded history. Its political institutions are ancient enough to predate the Caeras Compact. The Galans are proud of both facts and will mention them to you.

## East Pyros

East Pyros is different in almost every respect from its western neighbor, which is remarkable given that they are separated by only a ridge. The difference is made by rain: the ridge blocks the moisture-bearing winds from the west, and East Pyros sits in a rain shadow that grades quickly from mixed grassland to dry scrubland to the first tongue of the Moroshé Desert as you move east.

The land here is beautiful in a spare way — open, pale-grassed hills, scattered stands of drought-adapted trees, the occasional red-rocked outcrop. In spring, after the thin winter rains, it blooms briefly and dramatically. The rest of the year it is tawny and dry and stark.

East Pyros is a transit zone. The great trade routes between the Mittolo heartland and the eastern seaboard and islands pass through here, and the towns that grew up along those routes have a cosmopolitan character unlike anything in the more insular west. You can hear five languages in an East Pyrosi market town. You can buy goods from the Iberos coast, the deep desert, and Mittolo all in the same afternoon.

The people of East Pyros — where they have a distinct identity at all, rather than simply considering themselves traders first — are lighter in their cultural commitments than the Westpyrosi. They have a reputation for adaptability, pragmatism, and a certain ethical flexibility that the more settled peoples around them have found useful and also worrying at various points in history.

**Talermolis** is the name of the upland district in the northern part of East Pyros, where the ridge is high enough to catch some moisture and the land is greener and older. Talermolis has a different character from the rest of East Pyros — more traditional, more closed, older money and older grudges. The Taler dialect is considered archaic even by Pyrosi standards and preserves forms that linguists use to reconstruct the pre-Compact Pyrosi language.

North of the Pyrosi transit country, before the land rises fully into the Lotharn, lies **Amod**: a foothill country of terraces, water courts, and pass markets. East Pyros relies on Amod more than East Pyrosi merchants like to admit. Goods descending from the Lotharn usually become southern trade goods in Amodian warehouses first, and the Amodian habit of measuring every channel, mule-load, and road fee has quietly shaped the accounting practices of the northern Pyrosi routes.

## The Fire Memory

Across both Pyros regions, there is a tradition — partly religious, partly historical, impossible to cleanly separate — of what is called the Fire Memory. It holds that the land itself was once awake, that the fires below it were not merely geological but intentional, and that something happened a very long time ago that put those fires to sleep.

The Fire Memory is not a single text or tradition but a family of them, varying in detail between communities. In West Pyros it is associated with agricultural ritual — seasonal ceremonies that ask permission of the land before planting, that leave offerings at the fumarole sites, that treat the volcanic soil as a gift from something that could have chosen not to give it. In East Pyros it has been absorbed into the trade culture and become more metaphorical — "the fire beneath the deal," meaning the long-term consequences that an agreement will have when it eventually surfaces.

The fumaroles and hot springs scattered across both regions are considered liminal places — places where the interior of the world is close to the surface, and where the distance between what is and what was is thinner than elsewhere. People make requests at them, leave objects, and in some communities, speak words that are not meant for other people to hear.
