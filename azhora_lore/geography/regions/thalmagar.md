# The Cape of Thalmagar

The Cape of Thalmagar extends from the northwestern terminus of the Oremindi Mountains into the sea at the point where the western coast of Azhora bends and then stops making sense as a connected landmass. On three sides: water. On the fourth, southeast: the Oremindi, which at their northern end do not decline graciously into the coastal plain but simply arrive at the sea, leaving the cape cut off behind them as a consequence of geology rather than any political arrangement.

The result is a piece of Azhora that is, for all practical purposes, an island. Ships can reach it. Overland travelers cannot — the northern Oremindi passes are among the most severe on the range, seasonal at best, impassable in the worst years and most years are the worst years. What lies on the other side of the mountains from the rest of the continent sits in its own world.

The cape is on the southern shore of the bay that separates the main body of Azhora from the northern extension — the same bay whose northern coast holds the Bay of Lol and the ports of northern Azhora. From the north coast of this bay, Thalmagar is visible on clear days as a dark line of cliff and forest: close enough to know is there, far enough that few bother to cross. Northern Azhoran sailors call the bay itself *the Outer Deep*. The water between is cold, rough in most seasons, and offers nothing to repay the crossing except whatever Thalmagar offers, which until recently has not been much that outside traders need.

The name "Thalmagar" is the outside name, carried by the sailors who mapped the cape from the water. What it means has been argued without resolution. The leading proposal is that *thal* is a northern coastal-dialect word for a headland that juts into cold water, and that *magar* is a corruption of a term carried back by the first sailors to make contact with the cape's inhabitants. The people of the cape do not use it. What they call their land in their own language has not been recorded in a form that outside scholars have agreed on how to spell.

## Geography

The cape is not large — several days' walk from its junction with the mountains to its outermost point, perhaps two days across at the widest. But it is varied in the compressed way of landscapes that have been shaped by exposure on every side and connection to nothing.

The mountain barrier at its back is the defining feature. The northwestern terminus of the Oremindi does not diminish into hills before meeting the sea; the range ends in sea cliffs, a sudden vertical junction that leaves no passable coast between mountain foot and water on the southern side. This means there is no route from the cape to the rest of Azhora that does not cross either the mountains themselves or the bay. Both crossings are serious undertakings. The mountains are not offered at their gentlest at this end of the range; the passes here are the ones the Academy's Oremindi records describe as technically possible and practically avoided. The bay crossing requires vessels and weather that cooperate, neither of which is reliably available.

North of the mountain junction, the cape opens into its own geography. The land here is lower than the adjacent mountains but not flat — a series of rounded headlands and sheltered embayments along the northern coast, facing the Outer Deep and the distant northern shore; a wilder, more exposed western coastline that takes the open ocean without interruption; and an interior of moorland and ancient forest that has not been significantly cleared. The rivers are short and fast, draining from the elevated interior into either the bay or the open sea, and they run cold in every season.

The forests of the interior are not the Ibenwood in character — they are northern forests, dominated by species adapted to cold and exposure — but they have the quality of forests that have been undisturbed long enough that the disturbance-adapted species have ceded back to slower, longer-lived forms. The oldest trees here are large. The understory is dense. The light on the forest floor is the reduced, filtered quality of canopy that closes over most of the available sky.

The northern coast, more sheltered than the open west, is where the cape's handful of human settlements concentrate. A protected inlet, a freshwater source, a beach that can be safely worked in most winds — these are the requirements, and they are met in perhaps eight or ten places along the bay-facing shore. These are where the people are. The rest belongs to other things.

## Climate

Cold maritime. The Outer Deep moderates the worst extremes — the cape does not experience the continental cold of the Ganoss uplands, which have no ocean to buffer them — but the moderation is to cold-and-wet rather than to mild. The prevailing weather comes from the west and northwest, off the open sea, carrying persistent moisture and the kind of grey overcast that is not the temporary grey of incoming storms but the structural grey of a climate that has organized itself around the condition.

Snow falls in winter at all but the lowest coastal margins and persists at elevation through spring. The summers are short, cool, and — when the weather clears, which it does in brief windows — unexpectedly luminous. Visitors who have crossed the bay in summer describe the light on Thalmagar on a clear day in terms that recur across accounts without coordination: a quality of illumination that makes the landscape seem more present than usual, details more precise, distances more legible. The Academy has proposed this is a function of the clean maritime air and the low sun angle. The Northern Azhoran sailors who cross seasonally say it is the cape showing itself when it chooses to.

Fog is frequent at the cape's extremities in the warmer months, where cold sea surface and warming air produce it reliably. The lighthouse tradition of the northern Azhoran coast does not extend to Thalmagar. Ships that approach the outer cape in fog do so with the navigation methods of people who understand the risks.

## The People

There are, by the most recent external estimates, between three and five thousand people on the cape. The range reflects the difficulty of counting rather than any particular ambiguity about whether people exist. They live in small settlements on the northern bay coast — fishing villages, essentially, supplemented by hunting and gathering on the moor and in the forest — and they do not aggregate. The largest settlement known to outside sailors is perhaps two hundred people. There may be larger concentrations in the interior that bay-oriented visitors have not encountered, but if so they have not made themselves known.

How long people have lived on the cape is unknown. The people themselves make no claim, in any form that outside visitors have understood, to having arrived from anywhere. The oral traditions recorded in second-hand accounts describe the cape in terms that suggest a very long presence — events set against geological features, an integrated knowledge of the megafauna's long-cycle behaviors, names for individual landscape features that include the slow and catastrophic alongside the immediate — but no founding narrative, no arrival story, no record of a first generation. They have, in the phrasing of one Northern Azhoran merchant who spent a winter on the bay coast, "always been here, and they know it."

Whether they came by sea from the north before the Outer Deep became the dangerous crossing it currently is, or whether they crossed the Oremindi during a period when the northern passes were navigable, or whether they are a remnant of a population that once occupied the whole northwestern coast before the mountains achieved their current severity — none of this is established. Their own account, as far as it has been transmitted to outside observers, is that the land remembers them and they remember the land, and that is sufficient description of how they came to be where they are.

They are a quiet people, by outside report. Not unfriendly — sailors making the crossing for trade in seal oil, walrus ivory, and preserved fish have been received and traded with for several centuries. But not forthcoming. The outside world's interest in them is commercial and occasional; their interest in the outside world appears, from their conduct, to be roughly equivalent. The question of whether their reticence is cultural, linguistic, or simply the demeanor of people who have found that outside visitors are usually passing through has not been determined.

## Language

The language of the cape is not a dialect of Northern Mittoli. Every outside linguist who has obtained vocabulary samples has established this and then found themselves unable to say what it is instead. It is not Moreshi, not Grassic, not any of the known Iberos coastal languages, and the comparison with Sevronese — which was proposed by a scholar who obtained samples of both — produced no correspondences that could not be attributed to chance.

The one consistent observation is that the language appears to be old. Not simply conservative in the way Taler is old — that is, preserving ancient forms of a documented ancestor — but old in the way Grassic is old: no ancestor in any surviving record, no relatives among any documented family, the product of a history that is not traceable from the outside. If the Prior Presence Hypothesis developed for Grassic has merit — that the language family's isolation reflects pre-dating the arrival of the Mittoli, Pyrosi, Moreshi, and Bouéni families rather than extreme internal change — then the language of Thalmagar may represent a third such case. Or it may represent the same population in a different location. Whether the cape people and the Plains peoples share a common ancestor is a question that the Academy has noted twice as worth investigating and has not investigated.

What outside scholars have recorded of the language reflects the limits of brief trading contacts. The cape people learn enough Northern Mittoli or trade-dialect for commercial purposes. They do not teach their own language to visitors, which is not unusual, but they also do not translate it — when asked what something means, they tend to describe its use rather than render it into Mittoli equivalents, which is either a teaching philosophy or a statement about the impossibility of the task.

## The Megafauna

The northern Azhoran sailors who began trading with the cape several centuries ago returned with two reliable observations: the seal oil and walrus ivory were good quality, and the animals on the cape were like nothing they had seen elsewhere on the coast. Specifically: they were large. Specifically: not all of them were recognizable.

This has since been documented more carefully. The cape's isolation from the rest of Azhora by both the Oremindi and the Outer Deep has produced a fauna community that shares its origins with broader Azhoran megafauna but has been developing independently for a very long time. Hunting pressure has been minimal — the sparse human population does not, in general, hunt the large animals at all; the question of why is discussed below. The result is animals that have reached sizes no longer seen elsewhere, in a landscape where the ecological relationships between them have not been disrupted by the large-scale habitat modification that has reshaped the rest of the continent.

What the cape has, in a phrase that has circulated among naturalists at the Caeras Valley Academy since the first formal account reached them a generation ago, is what Azhora used to be.

### The Shore Elder

The Shore Elder is the first thing arriving sailors see that convinces them they are somewhere different. It occupies the flat rocky beaches of the northern and western coast in groups of three to eight animals. It is semi-aquatic — capable in the water, slow and deliberate on land — and roughly four meters from the broad face to the end of the tapering tail, with a build that is neither seal nor bear but suggests both without being either. The forelimbs are long enough to allow it to raise the front portion of the body upright; it does this frequently, apparently for observation. The head is large, broad-muzzled, and the eyes are well-forward in the face, with the binocular orientation more typical of predators than of grazers. The teeth, when visible, are the teeth of something that processes large prey — but its primary food, from what has been observed, is fish, large mollusks worked from the rocks at low tide, and the occasional seal.

It is not aggressive toward people under normal circumstances. It is simply large enough that "not aggressive" and "not dangerous" are not the same thing. A Shore Elder that feels blocked will defend the space available to it. The people of the cape do not approach them closely. They fish from the same beaches and have specific knowledge of which locations are occupied at which seasons. The knowledge is detailed, generational, and taken seriously.

Outside sailors who did not know this have occasionally found out the consequence. The incidents are recorded in trading-house logs as warnings rather than complaints; the shore elder's response to encroachment on its resting beach is not attack in the sense of pursuit, but the consequences of standing ground at that scale are equivalent.

The Shore Elder does not appear in Azhoran fauna records from any other location. The nearest candidate species — the **bay walrus** of the Bay of Lol and Smunders Bay — shares some coastal habits but is otherwise not closely similar in scale or form. Whether the two share common ancestry or are convergent adaptations is a question for which sufficient material has not been collected.

### The Cape Palmant

The interior of the cape, and the moor and forest margins where they open into wider ground, is the territory of the Cape Palmant — the largest ungulate documented in the northwest. It is elk-like in general structure and in the confirmed relationship of male to female to juvenile, but its scale exceeds mainland elk by a factor that requires recalibration. Mature males stand above two meters at the shoulder and carry flattened, palmated antlers that span three meters from tip to tip — not the branched form of mainland elk, but broad, shovel-shaped, and ridged in a pattern that Caeras Valley Academy naturalists who have examined shed examples describe as more archaic than any living cervid form they know. The sheds are collected by the cape people and traded; the antler material reaches northern Azhoran markets as a curiosity.

The Palmant moves in small mixed groups in the warmer months and breaks into larger herds for the winter lowland concentrations. The cape people know their wintering grounds precisely and route around them. Whether this routing is primarily practical — the Palmant, encountering a human settlement in its winter concentration territory, would be destructive without any intent — or whether it reflects a protocol older than the practical calculation is something the people have not explained to outside visitors in terms that translate cleanly.

The Palmant is not hunted. This is stated by the cape people as a fact rather than a prohibition, in the same register they use to say that the sea is cold or that the passes are closed in winter. Why it is not hunted — whether by active decision, by some ancient agreement, by pragmatic assessment of the difficulty, or by something else — has not been established from outside.

### The Rack-Wing

The sea cliffs of Thalmagar's western and northwestern face are the nesting ground of the largest bird documented in Azhoran natural history. Northern Azhoran sailors named it the Rack-Wing for the profile it makes when seen against the cliff face — the wings stretched thin and angled, the body hanging between them in a way that looks, from the water, like a frame hung against the rock. The wingspan is between three and four meters in adults. The weight is substantial enough that it cannot hunt from a perched launch; it requires the cliff updrafts for takeoff and landing, and it spends most of its active hours in extended soaring over the bay and the open sea, hunting at the surface.

It is not closely related to any other Azhoran raptor. The Academy's most recent assessment is that it represents a distinct lineage with a morphology suggesting very long isolation — the bone structure of the wing, as examined from a found carcase, shows proportional differences from all known birds of comparable size that are too systematic to attribute to individual variation. It is not merely a large version of something known. It is something else, scaled to the conditions that the cape and the cliffs and the cold upwelling of the Outer Deep have selected for over a very long period.

The Rack-Wing has taken children. This is recorded in the trading-house accounts and confirmed by the cape people's conduct: approaching the cliff nesting areas is prohibited in specific seasons, and the prohibition is treated with the same seriousness as the Palmant routing protocols. The people do not take Rack-Wing eggs or young. Whether the reverse prohibition holds by design or coincidence — Rack-Wings have not, in recorded trading contact, attacked adults in the open — is not something the cape people have offered an analysis of.

### The Standing Hul

The forest interior of the cape has a large land predator. It is called the Hul in the nearest phonetic approximation of the local name that has been rendered in outside records; Northern Azhoran sailors who have seen it call it the Standing One, which describes the most immediately striking feature of its behavior. The Hul is built like a very large bear — it is bear-like in the general plan of the body, heavy in the forequarters, capable of sustained bipedal stance — and in mature males it reaches weights that field estimates put between six and eight hundred kilograms. This is substantially heavier than any bear species documented elsewhere on Azhora.

It stands upright frequently and for extended periods. Not in the alarm posture of mainland bears but in the observation posture of something that habitually surveys from height and has built a physical structure that makes this effective. When standing, a large adult male is the tallest land animal on the cape, visible above the moorland brush, turning its head to read the landscape with the patience of an animal that does not have predators rather than the urgency of one that has.

It is the apex land predator. The cape people give it the specific attention one gives a permanent and serious fact of the landscape. There are protocols for what to do when one is encountered at close range, and the protocols are in regular use rather than theoretical. The settlements are sited and structured partly in reference to Hul territory — which areas of the coast they use seasonally, which valleys their paths cross, which forest margins they occupy through the breeding season. This knowledge is old enough and detailed enough to function as cartography.

The Hul is not hunted. Like the Palmant, this is stated as fact. Unlike the Palmant — whose size and herd character make hunting a purely theoretical exercise in courage — hunting the Hul would be physically feasible for a coordinated group. That it does not happen, and that this does not appear to require ongoing communal enforcement as a prohibition, has not been explained to outside observers in terms they have found fully satisfying.

### The Long-Back

A browser of archaic form, found in the interior forest and the forest margins. The Long-Back is smaller than the Palmant — the size of a large horse at the shoulder — but its neck is proportionally long in a way that no living Azhoran ungulate's is, and the effect at first encounter, even for observers who know to expect it, is disorienting. It feeds from the upper forest canopy on a level that other browsers cannot reach, and its range and movement are organized around the distribution of specific tree species whose upper-crown growth it prefers. The lower vegetation is largely untouched by it.

The Academy's one formal account of a Long-Back specimen — a skeleton obtained through trade, provenance uncertain — produced significant correspondence among natural historians. The proportions do not match any living species they know. They match, in partial and imprecise ways, forms described in the fossil record of several Azhoran regions: illustrations and descriptions of ancient remains found in the deep sedimentary layers of the Lotharn Mountains' fossil-bearing valleys, in the canyon country of the Moroshé margin, and in one northern Azhoran coastal deposit. The animals those fossils represent are understood to have been gone from the continent for a very long time.

Whether the Long-Back is a direct descendant of those ancient populations, or an independent development of a similar body plan in a similar ecological role, is not established. The Lotharn Mountains' oral tradition of deep-time geological reading includes accounts of animals that no longer exist, rendered in enough physical detail that several Academy naturalists have been prompted to ask whether these accounts might be describing the Long-Back from the other end of its range. The Lotharn valley communities who hold these traditions have been asked this question and have given answers that the Academy has recorded as significant and difficult to interpret.

## Living with the Animals

The cape people's relationship to the megafauna is the thing that outside visitors find hardest to characterize. It is not worship — the people do not appear to address the animals ceremonially, and the protocols governing behavior near them are practical in character rather than ritual. It is not domestication — there is no management, no feeding, no breeding selection. It is not simple avoidance — the settlements and the animals' territories overlap, and the people navigate the overlap with constant and detailed attention rather than by leaving a safe margin around the unknown.

It is something closer to cohabitation at a scale of mutual awareness that requires everything both parties know about each other. The cape people know the megafauna the way farmers know weather — not controlling it, not ignoring it, but reading it with the continuous attention of someone whose life's conditions are shaped by a force that operates on its own terms. The seasonal routes of the Palmant herds, the Hul's territorial ranges by year, the Rack-Wing's nesting calendar, the Shore Elder's beach occupancy patterns — all of this is in active use at any given time in any settlement's decision-making.

The resulting culture has organized itself around the capability required for this attention. The knowledge of the megafauna is the central body of practical and social knowledge on the cape. What other cultures organize around agriculture, trade, or military capacity, the cape organizes around knowing where the large animals are and moving through the landscape that belongs to all of them. The elders who hold the most detailed and accurate knowledge of long-cycle animal behavior are the people whose counsel is most sought in every other domain.

Why the megafauna are not hunted is a question that outside traders and naturalists have asked and received incomplete answers to. The most consistent response, in the Northern Mittoli required for trade, is a construction that translates approximately as: "They were here first." This may be a statement about history. It may be a statement about priority. It may be something that requires the cape's own language to say precisely, and the Mittoli approximation carries the shape of the meaning without its full weight. Outside visitors have tended to accept it as the most complete answer available.

## Contact with the Outside

The cape's trading relationship with northern Azhora is old but thin. Seal oil and preserved sea mammals, walrus ivory from the Shore Elder's beach neighbors, rare materials from the cliff-nesting Rack-Wing — feathers, occasionally a found specimen — and the shed antler of the Palmant, traded as curiosity and worked material. In exchange: iron, which the cape has no source of; cordage and fabric materials that can be made elsewhere but whose production requires time the cape's sparse population cannot spare from the ongoing business of knowing the landscape; and occasionally, requests for information that can only be filled by making the crossing.

The crossing is made in summer, when the Outer Deep is as calm as it gets. A few ships per season. The contacts are brief and practical. The cape people are not interested in expansion, in trade debt, in the political arrangements of northern Azhora, or in any offer that requires sustained outside presence on the cape. Several attempts have been made over the centuries to establish a more permanent outpost — a trading station, a seasonal camp, a Caeras Valley Academy observation post — and all have ended in the same way: the outside party found the cape's conditions difficult, the locals' assistance present but minimal, and the megafauna's presence more immediately relevant than anticipated. The trading contacts that have persisted are conducted on the cape's terms, which are: come in summer, trade fairly, leave before the season closes the crossing.

The Academy's formal file on Thalmagar contains accumulated trading-contact accounts, the sketches made by naturalists who crossed the bay, the skeleton and shed-antler samples obtained through trade, and several vocabulary lists of uncertain quality. The file is classified as high interest and low documentation. Several expeditions have been proposed. The most recent proposal noted that the crossing, the climate, the season window, and the likely cooperation of the inhabitants would make a full survey possible in perhaps three to five seasons if sufficient resources were committed.

The proposal has not been funded. The Academy has other priorities, and the cape has shown no sign of changing in the interim, which is the kind of patience that belongs to places that have been waiting longer than any institution whose priorities they are not.

## On the Age of the Place

The megafauna of Thalmagar are, in aggregate, the evidence that the cape has been isolated for a very long time. Animals do not become what the Shore Elder is, or the Palmant, or the Rack-Wing, in a few centuries. The body plans require either very long local development or preservation of forms no longer found elsewhere — and both options imply an isolation that measures in thousands of years rather than hundreds.

The Long-Back, specifically, raises the possibility that the cape has been cut off for long enough that animals present on its terrain before the final closure of the passes have had sufficient time to diverge substantially from their mainland cousins — or to survive, in a landscape where human pressure has been absent, while those cousins were hunted to local extinction across the rest of the continent.

The natural historians who have engaged most seriously with the Thalmagar materials have tended to reach for the same framework applied to the Ibenwood: not that the cape is unusual, but that the continent around it has changed. The megafauna that the bay-crossing sailors find strange were not always strange. They were, or something like them was, the fauna of a larger landscape that is no longer what it was. The Rack-Wing soared over more coastline. The Palmant's antler form appears in fossil records from regions where no living cervid of comparable scale exists. The Shore Elder is an archaic form that the Academy's records from the deep Lotharn deposits suggest may have been widespread.

What Thalmagar preserves is what the rest of Azhora lost, and the vehicle for that preservation is the bay, and the mountains, and the sparse people on the sheltered northern coast who have lived alongside the large animals long enough that neither finds the other's presence remarkable.

This is either the most complete picture of what ancient Azhoran coastal ecology looked like, or a remnant so far from its context that it cannot be used to reconstruct that context. Both interpretations are currently in play.
