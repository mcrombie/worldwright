# The Ascarth Peninsula

Southwest of Nylon, the Iberos coast bends and narrows into the Ascarth Peninsula — a rugged finger of land that juts into the southern Iberos Sea, rocky and forested in its interior, cliff-faced along much of its coast, with good anchorage only in the sheltered bays on its eastern and northern shores. It is not, geographically, a fertile or easy place. The Avites who have lived here for as long as record extends have made this irrelevant by being people to whom difficulty is, at worst, a neutral condition and, at best, the point.

The peninsula contains many small cities and towns, each with its own traditions, its own warrior lineages, its own pride. The largest and most powerful is Aevis, which sits on the northern coast where the peninsula connects to the main body of Azhora — positioned to receive trade from Nylon and the Lizeem River mouth while controlling the overland access to the rest of the peninsula behind it. When Aevis is strong enough, and its kings ambitious enough, the other cities of the peninsula swear allegiance and the entity that results calls itself the Kingdom of Ascarth. When it is not, they do not, and the peninsula returns to a state of competitive coexistence that the Avites consider normal and that outside observers tend to describe as chronic low-level warfare.

## The Avites

The people of Aevis, and by extension the dominant culture of the peninsula, are called the Avites. The name is theirs; it predates the city it comes from, or is claimed to — a point the Avites would make and the historians would dispute and the Avites would regard the dispute as evidence of the historians' unsuitability for the things that actually matter.

What matters to the Avites is glory. Not glory in the abstract, not glory as metaphor for civic achievement or commercial success, but glory in the specific and personal sense: the kind that is won in contest, witnessed by others, and sung about afterward. The Avite heroic tradition is old enough to have accumulated a substantial body of epic poetry — cycles of verse that name individual warriors and their deeds going back to a period that may be partially legendary and is certainly at least partially historical, depending on which scholars you ask and whether the Avites are in earshot when you ask. The epics are performed publicly at festivals, recited by specialists called the *veth* — the rememberers — who train for years in the oral tradition and who occupy a social position roughly equivalent to a very well-regarded general: honored, listened to, expected to perform, not expected to fight.

The epics' function is not merely entertainment. They are the cultural record of what an Avite life well-lived looks like. They document which families have produced heroes, what those heroes did, how they died. An Avite who knows their lineage is expected to know which of their ancestors appear in the cycles, and in what context, and whether the context reflects well on the family. The *veth* are consulted on genealogical disputes as readily as on poetic questions. This is not considered unusual.

Death in battle is the optimal outcome of a heroic life in Avite tradition, which sounds worse from the outside than it feels from the inside. The tradition does not celebrate dying specifically; it celebrates the kind of living that makes dying in battle possible rather than accidental — the quality of attention, the physical cultivation, the willingness to be present at the moment the moment arrives. An Avite who lives to old age is not disgraced; they are simply a person whose moment did not come in the form of battle. This happens. It is not the point.

Women in Avite culture do not fight in the formal sense, but they occupy the epic tradition as principals rather than ornaments — queens who direct strategy, mothers who shape the character of heroes, figures whose judgment determines outcomes. Whether this reflects ancient political reality or is a later idealization is not established.

## Bronze

The Avites work bronze. Every culture in the region is aware of this; fewer are aware of what it means in practice until they encounter the product.

Avite bronze is not technologically primitive. It is technologically exceptional. The Ascarth Peninsula has two things that most of the Iberos coast does not: a reliable source of the right copper ore in the interior hills, and several centuries of accumulated craft knowledge about what to do with it. The alloying ratios the Avite smiths use are precise enough and refined enough that their bronze outperforms what their neighbors produce with iron — harder at the edge, less brittle at the core, with a working quality under the hammer that experienced smiths describe with something close to reverence. The knowledge of exactly how this is achieved is not widely shared. It is organized within a guild system that is also a religious institution, and the information moves from master to apprentice in conditions of considerable formality.

The Avite commitment to bronze over iron is therefore not sentiment and not tradition in the pejorative sense. It is the rational preference of people who are better at one thing than anyone else, applied to the case where they are better. Iron, in Avite use, is for tools and fasteners — practical material for practical purposes.

The bronze economy has one persistent vulnerability: the peninsula produces copper but not tin, and without tin there is no bronze. Tin comes primarily from Legemum, the peninsula immediately to the west, whose hill mining settlements have supplied the Ascarth smiths for as long as the guild records on either side extend. The Legemum tin price is not a number any serious Avite strategist ignores. Every king of Ascarth who has considered expanding his territory has also considered, at some length, what it would mean to control that supply directly rather than purchase it. None have managed it permanently. See the Legemum entry for the reasons. Bronze is for the things that matter: weapons, armor, the great gates of Aevis that are the city's most famous physical feature, the grave goods that accompany warriors to the burial mounds on the peninsula's highland interior, the enormous bull-headed figures that stand at the entrances to the city's major public spaces, verdigris-green now after generations of sea air, staring toward the water.

Bronze architecture — specifically bronze-clad architecture — is the visual signature of Avite achievement. The great buildings of Aevis are stone at their foundations and structure, but their facing, their doors, their decorative panels, and in the most important structures their roofing elements are clad or cast in bronze. The effect, when the buildings were new, would have been extraordinary — an entire city gleaming at the harbor's edge. The patina changes this over time: the newer bronze-work of more recent construction shines golden-orange, while the older districts have gone deep green, so that the city reads, from the sea, as two colors depending on how old the neighborhood is.

The Avites export bronze goods throughout the Iberos Sea trade. Avite-made weapons command premium prices from buyers who can afford them. The merchant cities of Nylon and the Svaleen buy Avite bronze; the Moroshé trade routes carry it east. This commercial dimension is not lost on anyone, including the Avites, who understand perfectly well that their military culture and their craft economy reinforce each other. The reputation of Avite weapons sells the weapons. The weapons maintain the reputation.

## Aevis

Aevis is built on and around a rocky promontory at the northern end of the peninsula — a site chosen, if the epics are believed, because it was the highest defensible point available and the founding king wanted to be able to see anyone coming from any direction. The city has grown down from that original citadel height over centuries, spreading into the lower town around the harbor, but the citadel remains the political and symbolic center: the palace-complex of the King of Ascarth, when a king exists, or of the high lord of Aevis when it does not.

The citadel is the most completely bronze-clad structure on the peninsula. Its gates are the famous ones — two full stories of cast bronze, depicting in relief the campaigns of the kings of the first Ascarth period. The detail is extraordinary and was clearly produced over multiple generations of craftsmen. The gates are not purely symbolic; they are functional, and they are heavy enough that opening them requires a mechanism and several men. Visitors are made aware of this.

The harbor below the city is active with trade — bronze goods going out, tin and other necessary metals coming in (the peninsula has copper but must import tin, and the management of tin supply is a persistent strategic concern for any Ascarth king), timber, foodstuffs, and the miscellaneous requirements of a substantial urban population. The harbor district has a different character from the citadel: busier, more mixed, with resident merchants from Nylon and the Svaleen quarter-districts who have found that being present in Aevis is useful for knowing what is happening before it happens to them.

The *veth* maintain their archives in a building adjacent to the citadel — technically part of the palace complex but architecturally distinct, lower and wider, with a courtyard used for public recitation during festivals. The *veth* archives are the closest thing to a formal history that Ascarth culture produces. Scholars from Nylon have petitioned to study them and have been politely informed that the archives are for the *veth* and the *veth* are for the Avites, and that this arrangement has served both well.

## The Kingdom of Ascarth

The Kingdom of Ascarth is not a permanent political entity. It is a condition that the peninsula enters periodically, when Aevis is powerful enough and its king is capable enough to compel or convince the smaller cities to acknowledge suzerainty. The smaller cities acknowledge suzerainty by sending warriors when called, paying tribute in bronze goods and provisions, and allowing Aevis's markets to set the prices for the peninsula's external trade. In exchange they receive protection, participation in the glory of the King's campaigns, and the economic advantages of being part of a larger and more feared polity.

The Kingdom dissolves when the king dies without a strong successor, when the tributary cities accumulate grievances beyond the manageable level, or when an external situation — a failed campaign, a trade collapse, a plague — damages Aevis's prestige enough that the smaller cities conclude the calculation has changed. It has dissolved and reformed several times within recorded history. The current period is one of partial unity: Aevis controls most of the northern and central peninsula under terms that the southern cities are actively contesting.

The Avite heroic tradition complicates royal succession in ways that are culturally interesting and politically unstable. The king must be worthy — this is not a formal criterion but it is a genuine one. A king who cannot lead warriors, who cannot be praised by the *veth* in terms that the warriors accept, who cannot maintain the reputation of the house, will find the tributary cities reviewing their arrangements. Several kingdoms have ended this way, without formal conflict — the king simply found, one day, that the allegiances he thought he had were no longer operative.

## The Peninsula's Other Cities

The smaller cities and towns of the Ascarth Peninsula are not merely Aevis in miniature. Each has its own tradition, its own lineage claims, its own contribution to the epic cycle. Some are coastal fishing communities that supplement their income with bronze trade and have their own small warrior aristocracies. Some are inland hill towns that produce the bulk of the peninsula's agricultural output and negotiate carefully for their independence. A few are old enough to have pre-Avite foundations — evidence of earlier inhabitants whose language and culture have been largely absorbed, preserved only in place names and certain ritual practices that the *veth* have incorporated without fully explaining.

The southern tip of the peninsula is the part most consistently outside Aevis's control. The cities there are small, remote, and have survived by being not worth the effort of conquering — their harbors are poor, their agricultural land thin, their bronze production limited. They maintain their own warrior traditions, speak a dialect of Avite that the northern cities consider archaic, and regard Aevis with a mixture of cultural pride and political wariness that summarizes the peninsula's general relationship to its dominant city.

## Among the Coastal Cities

South of the peninsula's base, on the western bank of the Lizeem, lies Gala — a market city and agricultural region that the kings of Aevis control more often than not, through tribute and access arrangements rather than direct garrison. Gala is Ascarth's provisioning ground and its commercial outlet on the mainland side; its grain and oil supplement what the rocky peninsula cannot grow, and its market gives Avite bronze goods a trading floor that reaches the Lizeem's river network. The Galans are not Avites and do not become Avites, which the Avites find either irrelevant or mildly grating depending on the king. What matters strategically is that the arrangements hold. See the Gala entry for detail.

When the Kingdom of Ascarth is strong, the cities of the Iberos coast — Nylon, the Svaleen confederation, Nyross — pay attention. A united Ascarth controls a stretch of coastline that any ship moving north or south must pass, and a king who decides that taxing that passage is a good idea has the military capacity to enforce the decision. This has happened before. It is not remembered fondly in the maritime trading records of the coastal cities.

The management strategy has been consistent across several generations: keep Aevis supplied with what it needs from outside the peninsula — tin, in particular, which the city requires in volume and which the coastal trade controls — at terms favorable enough that disruption is not in Aevis's interest, while maintaining diplomatic relationships with the tributary cities that give coastal merchants early warning when the Kingdom's internal politics are shifting. The Nyloni are, characteristically, systematic about this; their harbor records contain what amounts to a continuous assessment of Ascarth's internal coherence, updated whenever a trading ship returns from Aevis with news.

The coastal cities admire the Avites in the way that people admire something that is genuinely excellent at what it does, and fear it for the same reason. Nylon's salons do not produce many defenses of the Avite heroic tradition — it is too alien to the city's values — but they also do not produce many comfortable dismissals of it. The Avites are inconveniently difficult to dismiss.
