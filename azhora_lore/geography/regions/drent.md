# Drent

The lords of Drent have never agreed on anything long enough to matter. This is not for lack of trying; there have been Drentish unification projects in most generations, some of them producing impressive results for a decade or two before the coalition exhausts itself on a succession question or a river-toll dispute and the individual lordships drift back to their natural state of armed commercial competition. The land does not encourage unity. Every significant river in Drent has its own mouth and its own lord, and each river-mouth port is valuable enough to sustain an independent ambition and not valuable enough to compel deference to anyone else's. The result is a region of genuine wealth — the rivers are productive, the coast is rich, the Pebbles fishery is extraordinary — that has never successfully converted its wealth into the kind of unified power that might have kept Ambron out.

Ambron is consequently in, more often than not.

---

## Geography

Drent occupies the northeastern coastal slope of Azhora, east of the Lake Lands and north of where the coast begins its long turn toward the warmer Iberos Sea latitudes. The terrain descends from the uplands shared with Elagos's eastern edge through a series of river valleys — a half-dozen significant rivers and many more minor ones, all running east or southeast to the sea. None of them is as large as the Ros or the Lizeem; all of them are navigable far enough inland to give the lords of the river towns meaningful reach into the agricultural hinterland.

The coast itself is low. Where the northern Azhoran coast has cliffs and hard headlands, Drent's coast flattens as it goes south, the rivers spreading into shallow estuaries, the land losing its argument with the sea in a long contested fringe of marsh, sandbank, and beach. This is where the Pebbles come from — the same accumulation of longshore sediment that the rivers deposit, shaped over centuries by the prevailing currents into the long thin chains of barrier islands that parallel the coast half a mile to a mile offshore.

Between the Pebbles and the Drent mainland lies the **Stills** — the collective name for the sheltered water of the sounds, shallow and calm and bearing almost no resemblance to the sea on the Pebbles' open face. Fishing boats work the Stills in weather that would keep them off the coast entirely. Flat-bottomed transport barges move river cargo between ports along the sound without needing to risk the approaches. The Stills are a secondary transportation network, slow but reliable, that the Drent river-mouth communities use when the outer coast is difficult and the roads through the hinterland are worse.

The main rivers of Drent — the **Avrel**, the **Torn**, the **Caloss**, and the smaller ones — are each associated with a lordship, sometimes one per river and sometimes two or three competing for the best landing sites. The fertile valley floors support the same kind of grain and orchard agriculture as Moros to the south, though the climate is somewhat wetter and more variable, and the flood behavior of Drent's rivers is less predictable than the Ros's steady seasonal rise. The upland between rivers is forested, difficult, and the territory of no lord in particular, which has historically made it useful for moving things and people that lords are not supposed to know about.

## The Pebbles

The barrier islands run for nearly eighty miles along the Drent coast, interrupted in three places by the navigable inlets that connect the Stills to the open sea. At a distance they look like what they are named for: a scatter of pale stones dropped on the water, low enough that the sea regularly overwashes the smaller ones in storm season, shifting the sand and remaking the landform slowly but continuously. No Drentish lord holds the Pebbles in any meaningful sense. The islands do not stay still long enough for a fortification to become permanent, and the communities that have lived on them for as long as anyone remembers are not the kind of communities that a lordship manages well.

The Pebble communities — scattered across a dozen of the larger, more stable islands — are fishers and pilots and salvagers, and they are one of these more than the others. The channels through the barrier islands that connect the Stills to the Iberos Sea approaches are navigable in good weather by anyone with a competent eye and in bad weather by almost no one, with the exception of people who grew up reading the particular color of the water over the bars, the specific behavior of the current at each marked channel, the way the light falls differently on safe water and shoal water at a particular hour of a particular kind of day. This knowledge is old and it is not written down. It lives in the Pebble families and nowhere else.

The consequence is that anyone wishing to bring a substantial vessel into the Drent coast — a trading ship with any significant draft, a Elagosi lake barge refit for coastal work, anything that cannot afford to strike a bar in moderate swell — must take on a pilot. The pilots come from the Pebbles, and the pilots set their fees. This is the quiet foundation of the islands' independence: no lord's authority is worth more than the pilot's knowledge, and the pilots have never seen a reason to fix that arrangement. They are not hostile to the mainland lords; they are simply not subject to them in the way that people who need something from you are not quite subject to you regardless of what the formal hierarchy says.

The Pebble fishing grounds — the channels and the open approaches just east of the islands — are among the most productive on the northeastern coast. The migratory fish that transit the waters off Drent's coast pass through a narrowing between the open sea and the barrier island chain that concentrates them in ways that fishers elsewhere cannot replicate. The catch is salted and dried on the islands and moved through the Stills to the river-mouth markets and from there into the broader trade network. In years when the migratory shoals come through in volume, the Pebble catch exceeds what all the Drent river fisheries combined produce.

The salvage operations are not publicized. Ships that miss the channels and strike the bars become property of the nearest Pebble community by a custom older than any Drentish lordship. The mainstream position is that the pilots prevent wrecks. The Pebble communities' position is that they prevent wrecks whenever they are hired to, and that the bars do what bars do regardless. Both positions are accurate.

## The Lords of Drent

Drent is governed by a loose collection of river-mouth lords, each controlling a port town and the agricultural hinterland of its river valley, and each related to the others through a web of marriage alliances, trade agreements, historical grudges, and competitive commercial relationships that outside observers have consistently found too complicated to summarize without getting something wrong.

The number of significant lordships has varied across the recorded history of the region. At any given time there are between six and a dozen who matter, with the Avrel and Torn lordships typically the largest — the Avrel because its river valley is the widest and most fertile, the Torn because its mouth is positioned where the main coastal trade route runs along the Stills and any ship moving between the Pebble approaches tends to pass it. The lesser lords hold smaller valleys, inland castles above the flood line, and the sort of defensive positions that the Drent terrain — not spectacular, but usable — provides.

The great Drentish political project, attempted in some form every two or three generations, is a congress of lords sufficiently unified to act as a single authority — to set common tariffs, maintain a shared coastal defense, negotiate with outside powers from something approaching a collective position. Every such congress has produced results. None has held together for more than a generation. The obstacles are structural: the lords' interests are too immediately commercial to sustain the compromises that collective governance requires, and any lord who grows powerful enough within the congress to make it function efficiently has grown powerful enough that the others begin calculating whether the arrangement still serves them.

When no congress exists and no outside power is present, Drent's political reality is a series of bilateral agreements between individual lords, renegotiated constantly, honored approximately as often as they are convenient, and supplemented by the Pebble pilots' practical authority over the coast. This is not stable in a way that administrative scholars find satisfying, but it has been the default condition of the region for most of its history, which suggests that it is stable in some other sense that administrative scholars have not found a good framework for.

## Ambron's Dominion

Ambron finds Drent useful. The northeastern coast provides harbors that the Elagosi lake fleet can use when it needs access to the open sea rather than the lake system. Drent's river valleys produce grain, timber, and fishing catch that supplement the lake country's domestic production. The Pebble approaches give Ambronite commercial interests access to the Iberos Sea trade routes without having to negotiate passage through the Svaleen Confederation or the Nylon harbor authorities. These interests are consistent and have driven Ambronite attention toward Drent through every empire cycle.

The conquest of Drent is not militarily difficult. The decentralized lords cannot field a combined force capable of resisting a substantial Ambronite mobilization, and the Elagosi lake fleet can suppress coastal resistance that might otherwise sustain a prolonged campaign. What individual lords can do — and consistently do — is negotiate their own submission before the army arrives, accept Ambronite overlordship on terms they find acceptable, and continue governing their own rivers in exchange for tribute and nominal fealty. Ambron's administrative preference is exactly this: the lords remain in place, the river-mouth ports continue to function, the Pebble pilots continue to pilot, and Ambronite tax collectors appear annually to collect the empire's share. The disruption of conquest is minimized because both sides understand that what Ambron actually wants is revenue, not direct administration of fishing villages.

The formal arrangement during empire periods is a **Lord Protectorate**: an Ambronite-appointed official, typically from a family with Drentish and Elagosi connections both, who sits at the Torn mouth as the empire's representative, hears disputes between lords that the individual lordships cannot resolve, and ensures that the tribute flows. The Lord Protector's actual authority over the individual lords depends on how much Ambron is paying attention at any given moment. In the strong imperial periods, the Protectorate has genuine teeth — Ambronite forces within reach, trade concessions withheld as leverage. In the periods of imperial distraction, the Protectorate becomes a formality that the lords observe punctiliously in language and ignore substantially in practice.

When Ambron contracts, the Lord Protectorate is the first institution to lose substance. The official may remain in residence for years after the actual Ambronite military presence has withdrawn, presiding over congresses that he can no longer compel anyone to attend. Eventually the position either ceases to exist or becomes a ceremonial title that one of the major lords absorbs as a prestige addition to their own styling. The records of Drentish congress minutes from the late contraction periods contain entries like "the Lord Protector was unable to attend and sent his compliments" filed across thirty-odd consecutive years — a monument to the polite fiction that the structure still functions while everyone present acts as if it does not.

When the cycle turns and Ambron expands again, the new Protectorate begins with a period of energetic reassertion — old tribute debts renegotiated, roads and harbor facilities that fell into disrepair during the gap brought back to standard, lords who accumulated private power during the free period reminded in material terms of what Ambronite backing costs. This period is uncomfortable. It does not last. Within a generation the arrangement normalizes into the standard extractive equilibrium, and Drent returns to being a productive and loosely governed province that Ambron prefers to having a problem at its eastern approaches.

## Economy

The river valleys produce grain and orchard fruit with the reliability of well-watered fertile soil. The Avrel valley in particular has been called the best agricultural land in northeastern Azhora by sources who may be exaggerating but are not inventing it. Timber from the forested upland between rivers provides raw material for ship construction — Drentish oak and fir are specifically valued for coastal and sea-going work, and the Pebbles pilots' own boats are built from local timber by local craftspeople who have never been surpassed at the specific task of building something that can spend years in the intersection of sea spray, salt wind, and hard use.

The coastal and river fisheries together make Drent one of the most productive food-producing regions per capita in eastern Azhora. Much of this production moves south through the Iberos Sea trade or north along the coast to the markets of Northern Azhora, rather than inland — the sea route is faster and cheaper than overland travel through the forested upland, and the lords of Drent have historically found that keeping trade maritime keeps their revenues higher and their dependence on Elagosi road infrastructure lower.

The Stills support a specific trade in the shellfish and estuarine species that the sounds produce in quantities the open coast cannot match. Drent oysters and the specific species of flat-water crab that lives in the Stills' southern reaches are known by name in Solis and Enebreum markets. The Pebble pilots carry these in their own small trading vessels as a sideline, which is one of several commercial activities the pilots conduct that mainland lords are aware of and choose not to involve themselves in.

## Character

Drent is not ambitious in the way that Ambron or the Svaleen cities are ambitious. Its ambitions are local — this river-mouth against that one, this lord's tolls against his neighbor's, this congress arrangement versus last generation's congress arrangement — and they are pursued with considerable energy and ingenuity at the local scale without ever quite rising to a scale that would change the region's fundamental situation. This is not a cultural failure. It is a structural condition of terrain and commerce that the lords of Drent inhabit rather than choosing.

The people of the river valleys and the people of the Pebbles are recognizably from the same general northeastern coastal culture and are specifically very different from each other in the way that people who live on opposite sides of a sound and interact mainly for commercial reasons become different. Mainland Drentish culture is agricultural and feudal and suspicious of the sea in the specific way of people who need the sea for trade but do not trust it for living. Pebble culture is marine and communal and suspicious of the mainland in the specific way of people whose independence depends on a skill that the mainland cannot replace and has periodically tried to control.

When Ambron is present, both communities accommodate it in their own way. The lords of Drent negotiate. The Pebble pilots charge.

When Ambron is absent, both communities return to their defaults. The lords of Drent congress, fail to agree, and compete. The Pebble pilots pilot, fish, and salvage, and no one asks too carefully about the third part.

## Language

Drentish is not Elagosi and it is not the eastern Mittoli of the Moros plain. It is its own thing — related to Elagosi in the way that neighboring peoples who have been in commercial and political contact for centuries tend to develop similarities without becoming the same. An Elagosi speaker and a Drentish speaker can achieve mutual understanding with effort and goodwill; they cannot follow each other's formal proceedings without translation. The Pebble islanders speak a variant of Drentish that has diverged further through relative isolation, and which mainland Drentish speakers describe as fast and folded in on itself — the same vocabulary, but compressed in ways that suggest a community that does not waste words on people who already know what they mean.

Ambronite administrative presence has introduced Elagosi loanwords into the formal and commercial registers of Drentish that persist through the contraction periods. The congress minutes of Drent, regardless of which generation wrote them, contain Elagosi-derived terms for tribute categories, dispute procedures, and property classifications. Like the walls Ambron builds on the Torn and the road surfaces it lays along the Avrel valley, the administrative vocabulary survives the empire that produced it. It is useful; it stays.
