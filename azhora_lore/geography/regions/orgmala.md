# Orgmala

The traveler who has only read the accounts does not expect the valleys.

The accounts describe what the accounts can describe: the approach, the grey rock, the terrain that climbs and breaks and offers no welcome, the silence of a highland that does not have the wind-noise of the Ganoss uplands or the water-noise of the Ganun river country — just the cold and the stone and the feeling that the landscape is aware of being entered. This is true. The approach to Orgmala's highland is exactly as the accounts describe it, and the accounts are not exaggerating for effect.

What the accounts cannot describe accurately is the valleys, because the people who have been inside the valleys and returned to write about them are very few, and the people who have been inside the valleys and not returned cannot contribute. What filters back to the outside — to Ganun's trade houses, to the Riesov coastal records, to the Academy's northern files — is inference and secondhand description and the occasional account of a traveler who saw, from above, a valley floor that looked wrong for the surrounding terrain. Wrong in the sense of: too green. Too inhabited. Too much evidence of organized productive life in a landscape that is supposed to contain only the most difficult kind of people.

The valleys are real. The people inside them are the same people who produce the raiding parties that have given Orgmala its reputation. This conjunction — the lush valley and the raider — is not a contradiction. It is the explanation.

---

## Geography

The Orgmala highland is the rocky massif north of Ganun and northwest of the Skeld range, occupying the elevated ground between the Ganoss upland margins to the north and the headwaters of the Sond tributaries to the south. It is not one unified plateau but a broken, fractured landscape: irregular ridges and rock-faces separated by gorges, the occasional open heath where the wind has scoured the soil to bare rock, and the specific sites — perhaps eight or ten of them in the entire highland, more by some accounts, fewer by those who have looked harder — where the geology has produced something entirely different.

The highland is elevated enough to be cold by northern standards, which are already demanding. The snowpack sits on the upper ridges from late autumn through late spring. The exposed rock radiates cold on clear nights in a way that makes camping on the highland in winter a commitment rather than a choice. The communities that have tried to establish themselves on the upland surface — as opposed to in the valleys — have not done so in any lasting way. The highland surface is not inhabited. The valleys are.

The relationship between the highland's geology and its defensibility is straightforward and has not changed. The terrain approaching any Orgmala valley entrance involves ground that is difficult to cross in good order, impossible in winter, observable from above for miles, and organized in such a way that the approach narrows before any entry. An army attempting to move on an Orgmala valley in force must cross highland that exposes it to observation for days, compress through a bottleneck that the valley's defenders can hold with a fraction of the attacking force, and then enter a valley that is not large — which means a large force inside it is actually a disadvantage. The terrain was not designed. It does not need to have been.

## The Valleys

Each Orgmala valley has the same structural property: a narrow entrance that widens behind it, surrounded on its remaining sides by rock faces steep enough to make approach without the entrance impractical. The heights above are passable for individuals and impassable for any organized group. The valley floors are sealed from the worst of the highland wind by the rock walls on either side, collecting instead the afternoon light that the surrounding upland does not receive at the same angle or duration.

The fertility that results is, to the outside eye, inexplicable from the surrounding terrain. The valley floors have accumulated soil over centuries from the drainage off the surrounding rock — organic matter, minerals, the slow decomposition of everything the walls shed downhill — and what collects is dark, deep, and productive in the specific way that enclosed and sheltered ground is productive: not a large extent, but unusually rich for the latitude and elevation. The streams that emerge at the valley floors run clean from the rock filtration and do not dry in summer.

The contrast with the highland approach is the thing travelers who have seen the valleys from above consistently try and mostly fail to communicate adequately. You cross hours of grey broken rock that offers nothing, and then the valley opens below you and there is — green. Deep, dark, intentional-looking green. Fields. Structures. Evidence of a community that has organized its space with the confidence of people who have been exactly here for a very long time and expect to remain.

The valley communities are self-sufficient in everything except metal and certain goods that their terrain cannot produce. They grow grain on the valley floors in quantities sufficient for local needs with a modest surplus. They keep animals on the lower valley slopes where the forage extends. They have winter-preserved stores in the rock chambers that the valley walls provide naturally and that have been extended with human effort. They do not need to raid to eat.

This is the part that outside accounts consistently misunderstand: the Orgmala are not raiding because they are starving. They are not raiding from desperation. The valley economies are stable and have been stable for as long as the Orgmala have been in the highlands, which is longer than the neighboring regions' records go back. The raiding is not the poverty. It is something else.

## The People

The name "Orgmala" does not appear in any of the languages that the people who live in the Orgmala highland speak among themselves. It appears in Ganun records, in Riesov coastal accounts, and in the general Northern Azhoran vocabulary as the established name for the highland and its people. What the Orgmala call themselves in their own language is known to outside scholars who have spent time at the highland margins, and these scholars note that the self-name translates approximately as "the valley-holders" or "those of the enclosed ground." The term is neither proud nor humble; it is descriptive in the way that names for places often are in communities that have been there long enough to name themselves after the geography rather than after the ancestors.

The outside name "Orgmala" has disputed origins. The leading interpretation in the Ganun trade-house records — which are the most extensive outside documentation of the highland — is that "Org" derives from a Northern Mittoli word root meaning roughly "closed" or "sealed," and that "mala" is a pejorative suffix in an older Ganun dialect form meaning something like "those who cannot be dealt with." Closed and sealed and beyond negotiation. Whether this etymology is correct or backformed from the reputation it claims to explain is a question the Academy's linguists have not resolved.

The Orgmala character, described by the few outside traders and travelers who have had extended and relatively peaceful contact with valley communities, is not what the raiding reputation predicts. Inside the valleys they are organized, careful, and property-conscious in the manner of people who have had to maintain productive systems in difficult terrain for many generations. The labor organization of the valley communities is sophisticated: the seasonal requirements of highland agriculture and animal-keeping are managed by a community structure that distributes work without obvious formal hierarchy, or rather with a hierarchy that is not visible to outsiders because it operates through competence and reputation rather than declared status. A valley elder who commands the planting schedule does so because everyone present has seen what happens when the schedule is wrong, and this elder has not been wrong in a way that cost the community yet.

They are not unfriendly in the ordinary sense. They receive strangers with the specific hospitality of isolated communities that have occasionally found strangers useful: food, shelter, the minimum that courtesy requires. What they do not provide, and what the accounts consistently note, is information. An outside trader who comes to a valley entrance and trades — and this does happen, on specific terms, in specific seasons — receives fair value for what he brings and a complete absence of anything he did not bring. What is in the other valleys, how many people, what resources, what the community's plans are: none of this is available, and the absence is so complete and so consistent that it reads not as secretiveness but as discipline.

## The Raiding Tradition

The Orgmala are the reference point for raiding in the north in the same way that the Skeld is the reference point for a mountain range: other ranges exist, but when a northern community is trying to communicate the category of "difficult barrier," the comparison is to the Skeld. When a northern community is trying to communicate the category of "organized predatory intent," the comparison is to Orgmala.

What distinguishes the Orgmala raiding tradition from the general northern raiding culture that preceded the transition to trade is not the violence, which is present but not exceptional by historical standards. It is the preparation.

An Orgmala raiding party, in the accounts of communities that have been raided and have survived to document it, demonstrates knowledge of its target that is not consistent with a first encounter. The party knows which household stores what. It knows the patterns of the watch, if there is one. It knows where the animals are penned and which structures are stone and which are timber. This knowledge is not magic. It is the product of reconnaissance — of earlier visits, in different forms, that established the information before the raiding party arrived to use it.

The Orgmala are not easy to identify as Orgmala in advance. They are northern people who look like northern people and who speak Northern Mittoli in the manner of northern people, with an accent that is recognizable to specialists and not distinguishable to most communities. They pass through market towns. They take employment as drovers or cargo handlers. They accept hospitality as travelers. And then, later, the community that hosted them experiences a raid whose character reflects the specific intelligence that a guest would have gathered.

This is the reputation's core. It is not that the Orgmala are unusually brutal in their raiding — they are not, particularly. It is that they are unusually knowing. The violation is not only of property or safety but of the trust that hospitality implies. The communities that have been targeted this way describe the experience with a specific vocabulary: not betrayed, exactly, since betrayal implies a prior relationship of some acknowledged kind, but something closer to the sense of having been studied and found useful in a way that you were not consulted about.

The response to this reputation — the practical precaution that northern communities have developed — is a practiced wariness toward unknown northerners in the specific period before they are known well enough to have established reliable identity. The Ganun trade-house records include, in the margin notations of several ledgers from the current era, a shorthand reference to "valley checking" — a vetting practice for new workers and traders that the Ondrath merchant community has apparently developed and does not advertise. Whether this practice effectively identifies Orgmala scouts is not established by the records. Whether Orgmala scouts are in Ondrath in the current era is also not established, but the question is not treated as theoretical.

## Captives

The Orgmala take people.

This is noted in every account of their raids that includes a complete accounting of what was taken and what was not. It is not the primary thing taken — goods, animals, and portable wealth are the primary targets, consistent with the economic logic of a raiding culture operating in a resource-limited highland. But in a significant proportion of raids, one or more people are also taken, and these people are not ransomed.

The ransoming of captives is standard practice in the northern raiding tradition. It is economically rational: a live captive from a family with means is worth more as a negotiating token than as a laborer. The Bay of Lol raiding culture of the historical period ransomed captives as a matter of routine. The various northern raiding groups that operated during the transition period ransomed captives. The Orgmala do not ransom captives, or ransom them so rarely that the rarity has become part of the reputation.

What happens to people taken in Orgmala raids is not established. No account from inside an Orgmala valley community addresses it. The communities that have lost people to Orgmala raids do not have information about those people's fate beyond the moment of the raid; they have never been in a position to acquire it. The Academy's northern files contain a notation from a scholar who spent time on the Ganun margins asking about Orgmala captives, which reads: "No account of return. No account of message. No account of contact of any kind after the taking. The communities I spoke with had stopped expecting an account."

Outside interpretations of the captive practice range through: labor in the valley economies, political hostages held for leverage that has never been exercised, something worse, and something that outsiders have not correctly characterized because they are reasoning from outside assumptions about what captured people are for. None of these interpretations have evidence in their favor beyond the fact that all of them are consistent with the absence of information.

The absence of information is itself the most significant fact. The Orgmala are very good at not having information leave their valleys. Whatever the captive practice is, it is part of the valley interior that the highland terrain and the community's discipline together protect.

## Relations with Neighbors

**Ganun** has the most contact with Orgmala and the most carefully structured wariness about it. The Ondrath market is not closed to highland traders who identify as from the Orgmala regions; the Ganun commercial principle of doing business with whoever has something to sell extends to the highland in practice, while the "valley checking" margin notation suggests that the extension is not unconditional. Raids on Ganun communities are documented in the trade-house records across several generations, and the records' tone, which is generally precise and unemotional, treats Orgmala raiding as a recurring cost of doing business in the northern interior — unpleasant, budgeted for, not resolved.

**Riesov** has a different relationship because the terrain between them is the Skeld. The northern Skeld pass — the hard pass, the one "used by those with specific reasons to avoid the other two" — runs through the Orgmala highland's eastern margin. Riesov communities on the northern Skeld spur have experienced raids from the western highland, and Strendal's records include several accounts of highland-origin raids on coastal settlements that Riesov attributes to Orgmala parties who crossed the northern pass. Whether these attributions are accurate is not established; they may be Orgmala, or they may be other highland communities whose raids are attributed to Orgmala because Orgmala is the name for raiding-from-the-north in the regional vocabulary.

**The Ganoss upland clans** have a relationship with Orgmala that the outside world understands least clearly. The Ganoss are pastoral and mobile; the Orgmala are valley-fixed and raiding. Both occupy elevated terrain. They have been neighbors for a very long time. The expected outcome — persistent violent conflict, or alternatively, accommodation through negotiated terms — is present in neither the Ganoss clan records nor the Orgmala-adjacent documentation from Ganun's northern margins. What is present is a notable silence: neither side documents the other in ways that suggest active hostility, and neither side's practices seem consistently directed at the other. Whether this represents a longstanding accommodation, mutual deterrence, or simply that both sides have found more productive targets elsewhere is not established.

**No southern power** has successfully sustained a military operation into the Orgmala highland in any recorded period. The attempts that are documented — two from Bay of Lol coalitions in the historical period, one from the Ganun river community network in the current era's earlier generation — stopped at the highland margin and did not attempt the valley approaches. In each case the recorded reason is terrain: the cost of moving a force across the highland surface to a valley entrance, and then forcing that entrance, exceeded whatever strategic purpose the operation was meant to serve. The Orgmala have not needed to make the highland difficult to enter. It came that way.

## The Interior Economy

The valley communities produce what their enclosed terrain allows and trade for the rest through the specific channel of the highland margin — the edge communities where Orgmala traders appear in the Ganun northern settlements and the Ganoss autumn trade gatherings, selling highland goods for what the valleys cannot produce.

The highland goods include: dense, cold-climate wool from a small-bodied sheep breed adapted to the rocky upper slopes; preserved meat of high quality, dried and salted in ways specific to the highland communities; medicinal plants from the valley floors whose specific properties are not well characterized in any outside botanical record but whose value is established by demand; and a category of carved bone and stone objects — tools, weights, decorative objects — that appear in northern trade records with enough regularity to suggest that either there is a consistent demand for them or the Orgmala specifically introduce them into the trade as part of their presence management.

What the valleys cannot produce and must acquire: iron in working quantities, certain agricultural implements, salt in quantity, and goods from the wider Azhoran network that the highland cannot grow or make. These requirements are finite and manageable, which is why the raiding is not about survival. A valley community could meet its needs through trade alone, with the goods its terrain does produce. The raiding adds to rather than constitutes the economy.

The conclusion that follows — that the raiding is cultural rather than purely economic — is the one outside scholars have arrived at and then found unsatisfying, because it raises more questions than it answers. Cultures do not maintain expensive practices without function. What function the raiding serves in the valley communities, beyond the goods it produces, is the question that requires being inside the valleys to understand, and the outside world has not been inside the valleys in any documented way that produced an account.

## Language

The Orgmala speak a variety of Northern Mittoli that the Academy classifies as significantly diverged from the standard, with a substrate layer that is more extensive than in any other northern variety except possibly the Ganoss upland dialect. The substrate proportion is high enough that some Academy linguists have proposed that the highland was inhabited before Mittoli speakers arrived by a population that had a different language family entirely, and that the Mittoli overlay is incomplete — that significant amounts of vocabulary, and possibly some structural features, survive from the pre-Mittoli layer.

The evidence for this is the distribution of vocabulary items that cannot be traced to Proto-Western and that cluster in specific domains: the names for valley geography and enclosed terrain; terms for certain agricultural practices and food preservation methods specific to highland conditions; and, notably, a set of terms related to observation and concealment — words for sight lines, for the specific qualities of light at different highland elevations, for the difference between a visible approach and an invisible one — that are more numerous and more precise than anything comparable in the neighboring dialects.

Outside scholars who have attempted to analyze the Orgmala dialect face a specific methodological difficulty: their informants are Orgmala traders at the highland margin, who speak the variety they choose to speak to outsiders, and the variety they choose to speak to outsiders is not the variety they speak inside the valleys. The difference is not a formal register distinction in the way that the Pyrosi system distinguishes formal and intimate speech. It appears to be a practical separation: there is the language that is used with the world outside the highland, and the language that is used inside. Which language carries which content is not a distinction the outside world has been able to observe directly.

The Academy files note this under "methodological limitation" and do not return to it. The note reads: "Further study requires access to valley communities that has not been obtained and whose obtaining presents difficulties the file does not address."
