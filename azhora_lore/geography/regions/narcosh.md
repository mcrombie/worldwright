# Narcosh

The first thing most travelers encounter from Narcosh is not a person. It is a goat standing on a ledge that should not bear weight, on a cliff face that should not be climbable, at an elevation that the ledge in question does not appear wide enough to permit. The goat is near-black, with pale facial markings in a pattern that, from a distance, the brain insists must be deliberate. Its horns sweep back and outward from the brow in a long, heavily ridged arc. Its eyes — pale yellow against the dark face, the iris large enough to be visible at distance — are oriented toward you with an attention that does not read like the attention of an animal that has decided you are not a problem. By the time you have completed this assessment, there are three more goats on ledges above and beside the first, and they are all looking at you with the same quality of attention, and then a Narcoshi herder appears from somewhere the cliff face offered no indication of concealing.

This is the normal introduction to Narcosh. The goat comes first. The person second. The understanding of either comes much later, if it comes at all.

---

## Geography

The Narcosh valleys occupy a section of the northern Oremindi where the mountain system does something unexpected: rather than running as a single spine, the range fractures into a cluster of parallel ridges that create, between them, a network of enclosed valleys oriented differently from the typical glacial carve. Most of these valleys run roughly east-facing, catching the early sun that the range's geometry would otherwise deny them. A few have geothermal springs — the northern Oremindi are young mountains, and youth in mountains means unfinished geological business, and the unfinished business here includes vents and upwellings that keep specific valley floors ice-free in the winters that close everything around them.

These two accidents of geology — the unusual orientation and the thermal activity — make the Narcosh valleys livable at altitudes where livable is not a word that would otherwise apply. The terrain is still extreme: the ridges between the valleys are not the gentle saddles of an old range but the sharp, fractured rock of young mountains still in the process of deciding their final form. What makes the Narcosh valleys a coherent inhabited system rather than a series of isolated pockets is the goat routes.

The Narcoshi call the routes between valleys *vel-narcoth* — a term that translates clumsily as "the ways the goats go" but carries the implication that the goats found them and the people learned them rather than the reverse. The routes are not paths. From outside, they are not visible as anything. They traverse cliff faces at specific sequences of handholds, cross ridges at specific points where the rock has fissures that accept human and goat feet, descend through chimney-cracks in the granite that outsiders standing at the base of the same rock face cannot identify from below. A Narcoshi moving between two valleys crosses terrain that a lowland observer, watching from a distance, would describe as a person climbing a wall, moving sideways across a wall, and descending another wall on the other side. This is accurate. The key difference is that the Narcoshi know which sections of the wall hold and which sections do not, and that knowledge — accumulated over generations of following the goats — is the effective geography of Narcosh in a way that maps cannot represent.

The valleys are six in number, ranging from the largest — **Narcoth-main**, the central valley that serves as the gathering place of the whole system — down to the two smallest eastern pockets that outsiders are not sure are inhabited but whose cheese and fiber appear in Narcoshi trade. They are connected by the vel-narcoth and by a shared awareness that the valley system is one thing and that being one thing has been the difference, at several moments in Narcosh's history, between surviving the winters and not.

## The Oremindi Above

Narcosh is the last inhabited country before the northern passes. Beyond the Narcosh valleys, the Oremindi do what the Oremindi do in their northern section: they close. The passes above Narcosh are among the most severe on the range — open in perhaps one year in three, and in the years they are open, open briefly and unpredictably. What lies beyond them is the Cape of Thalmagar, cut off from the mainland as thoroughly as geography can manage.

The Narcoshi are aware of what the terrain above their valleys contains. They have terms for the large browsing animal that the Academy's naturalists have attempted to catalog from secondhand accounts — the Long-Back, in the Academy's provisional name — and their awareness is not academic. The creature's range extends into the upper reaches of the northern Oremindi in specific seasons, and the Narcoshi have encountered it often enough that their relationship to it is neither mystified nor casual but is the specific orientation that people develop toward large animals that share their terrain and require respect without requiring fear. The Long-Back does not come into the inhabited valleys. The goats' behavior changes when one is in the upper reaches, and that change in behavior is how the Narcoshi know.

What else the northern passes conceal, on the Cape side, is not a question the Narcoshi find more interesting than the question of whether the passes will be navigable this year. They have their own terrain to manage.

## The Goats

There are three distinct Narcoshi breeds, and the distinction matters to anyone who works with them and is invisible to anyone who does not. To an outside observer, all three share the features that produce the slur: the dark coloring that ranges from charcoal grey to near-black, the pale facial markings in the high-contrast patterns that map onto the brain's pattern-recognition in ways the brain finds hard to dismiss, the swept-back horns that carry the length and curvature of something larger than a goat seems entitled to, the pale-yellow eyes that in a dark-faced animal appear to glow in low light, and the specific quality of the attention the animals direct at whatever interests them — a quality that lowland visitors consistently describe as wrong without being able to say exactly what is wrong about it.

What is actually producing these reactions is the result of very long adaptation to specific conditions. The dark coloring absorbs the limited solar radiation available at altitude. The pale eyes are adapted to the lower light levels of north-facing rock and the long dawn and dusk that the valley orientations create. The horn conformation that reads as aggressive from below is the specific geometry that allows a heavy-horned animal to move through rock chimneys and narrow passages without the horns catching; they sweep back because that is what fits through the vel-narcoth. The markings, on close examination, are not uniform — they vary individually and are used by Narcoshi herders as identification markers in the same way that a brand or an ear-notch is used elsewhere, except that they are natural and present from birth and have apparently been consistent enough in the breeding population to be diagnostic.

The posture of attention — the quality that visitors describe as the goat seeming to evaluate rather than merely observe — is the hardest feature to explain without watching a Narcoshi goat in its actual environment for long enough to understand what it is doing. The goat is assessing the terrain. The goats of Narcosh have been selecting, across generations, for the specific cognitive quality that makes a cliff-face navigable: the ability to assess a complex sequence of footholds several moves ahead and commit to the sequence without hesitation. This requires a quality of attention applied to the environment at all times that, from outside, reads as the goat looking at you with calculation. The goat is not particularly interested in you. It is doing what it always does: reading the terrain. You happen to be in the terrain.

The three breeds serve different functions. The **Narcoth-main** breed is the largest, producing the most fiber and the most milk but restricted to the central valley's easier slopes. The **vel-ridge breed** — no outside name has been standardized — is the specific mountain-face animal, smaller and lighter with the most extreme version of the terrain-navigation ability; it rarely comes to valley floor and some of its range overlaps with the upper reaches where the Long-Back occasionally descends. The third, the **eastern-valley breed**, is the oldest in the oral records and produces the cheese that is most prized in lowland markets, a sharper and more complex flavor than the standard Narcoth product, from a different diet and a slightly warmer valley environment.

## The Herding Culture

The relationship between the Narcoshi and their goats is not one thing. It is a relationship between people and animals who have been cohabitants of the same extreme environment long enough that the practical and the cultural have become impossible to separate cleanly.

The practical: goat herding in the northern Oremindi requires knowing what the goat knows. A herder who cannot follow the vel-narcoth cannot manage a herd that uses them. The training of Narcoshi herders is the training of Narcoshi people generally — every person in the valleys learns the routes as children learn to walk, which is to say by following adults who know them across surfaces that look, from below, like they should not work. By early adulthood, a Narcoshi person can move between any two of the six valleys by three or four different routes, depending on season and conditions, carrying a load or driving animals. This mobility is what makes Narcosh a single country rather than six separate valleys. The goats taught the routes. The people learned them. The country is the sum of what both know.

The cultural: the Narcoshi breed-keeping tradition is among the most detailed on the continent. The oral records of individual bloodlines in the major breeding families extend to a depth that the Academy's animal husbandry division, when it received a partial translation, described as "genealogical records more carefully maintained than those of most noble houses, and more consequential." The markings of each animal are memorized and recorded. The seasonal ceremonies that mark the transitions of the herding year — the spring climb to the high pasture, the breeding season pairings, the autumn descent — involve rituals that the Narcoshi describe, if asked by outsiders who manage not to be offensive about it, as "keeping the record" rather than worship. The record being kept is the relationship between the breeding lines and the terrain, which specific families know which sections of which routes, which animals have shown the route-reading quality in the most reliable form, which pairings produced the vel-ridge animals with the strongest high-altitude range. Keeping the record correctly is understood as keeping Narcosh intact. Not keeping it is how valleys fail.

The goats are given individual names. They are attended at birth. Old animals past their productive years are kept until natural death. There are specific ceremonies when a breeding-line matriarch dies that the Narcoshi decline to describe in detail to outsiders, citing not secrecy but the inadequacy of Mittoli vocabulary for what the ceremony involves. This is, in the Narcoshi understanding, simply true. Whether it reads as worship from outside is not a question they find interesting.

## The Slur

*Devil Goat Worshippers* is not a phrase the Narcoshi use for themselves. It is what happens when a lowland traveler who has never seen Narcoshi goats encounters them at a livestock market, looks at the coloring and the markings and the eyes and the horns and has a specific reaction, and then notices that the Narcoshi herders who brought these animals have a relationship with them that does not look like the way livestock handlers elsewhere handle livestock, and draws a conclusion.

The Narcoshi are aware of the slur. Their awareness has had sufficient time to move through the stages that awareness of a persistent insult typically moves through — initial offense, then contempt, then a settled dryness that is not quite amusement but is in that direction. A Narcoshi trader at the Celder horse fair or the Vaundrel market knows, before the conversation begins, what the lowlander opposite them is trying not to say. The usual approach is to let them not say it as long as they want, conduct the transaction, and save the observation about the slur for the walk back to wherever the Narcoshi are sleeping that night.

The more patient Narcoshi, in contexts where explanation seems worth attempting, will note the following: the animals are dark because high-altitude solar absorption is a survival trait. The markings are natural identification patterns, not symbols. The eyes are adapted to low-light mountain conditions and appear luminous because they are reflecting whatever available light exists. The horns sweep back because that is the geometry that fits through narrow passages; an animal with forward-pointing horns cannot navigate the vel-narcoth and would not survive the terrain. The attention the animals give to their environment is constant because the terrain they navigate requires constant terrain assessment. None of this is demonic. All of it is competence.

The less patient Narcoshi do not offer this explanation. They have found that outsiders who arrive at the "devil goat" reading from one glance at the animals do not reliably revise it on the basis of information. They find this philosophically interesting in a way that does not make the conversation more enjoyable.

Whether the Narcoshi have, at some level, a certain sardonic affection for the slur — whether there is a version of the "devil goat" framing that they use in private to describe their position in the world — is a question that the Narcoshi decline to answer for outsiders and that their behavior occasionally implies an answer to.

## Governance

The six valleys govern themselves and each other through the **Valley Assembly**, which meets in Narcoth-main at the spring gathering and the autumn descent, and which any adult Narcoshi who can be present may attend. The spring gathering is when the allocation of high pasture is determined — which breeding groups go to which sections of the upper ranges, which family takes responsibility for which vel-narcoth route this season — and the autumn descent is when the season's outcomes are assessed and the planning for the following year begins. The Assembly does not have a standing authority between meetings; the individual valleys manage themselves on the practical consensus of the people in them.

The closest thing to a standing office is the **route-keeper**, a role held by the elder with the most comprehensive knowledge of the vel-narcoth network. The route-keeper's authority is entirely technical — they arbitrate disputes about route usage and safety, their assessment of whether a specific crossing is dangerous this season is binding, and they train the next generation of herders in the full network — but this technical authority produces a kind of institutional standing that exceeds most formally political positions. The people who know the routes most completely are the people whose judgment about the routes most governs what is possible, and what is possible in Narcosh is defined by the vel-narcoth. The route-keeper knows the whole country in a way that the other valley-level elders do not.

Narcosh has never been successfully administered by any outside power. This is partly terrain and partly a specific characteristic of the connected-valley system: there is no single point whose control constitutes control of Narcosh. Narcoth-main can be entered, with sufficient military effort, by a force willing to take the casualties of mountain terrain at altitude. Narcosh cannot. The other five valleys continue.

## Economy

What Narcosh sells is specific and not easily replaced by other sources. The cheeses — particularly the eastern-valley variety — are traded south through Celder and Yunethre and eventually to the Iberos coast markets, where they command prices that outsiders consistently find hard to reconcile with the quantity they represent. The premium is not irrational: the flavor profile of the eastern-valley cheese cannot be reproduced outside the conditions that produced it, and the buyers who know it know this. The cheese trade is managed by a specific family in the eastern valley whose route knowledge and lowland commercial connections have been maintained across generations; they are the people who show up at Vaundrel's market with the product that causes the toll official to note the weight carefully and the merchant opposite them to try not to appear too interested.

The fiber from the vel-ridge and main breeds is a secondary export — heavy, dense, with an insulating quality that northern buyers value for specific applications, and a texture that requires processing methods the Narcoshi maintain that outside craftspeople have not successfully reproduced. The goat fiber trade is smaller than the cheese trade and more local, mostly going to the Celder and Yunethre wool markets.

Narcosh imports grain. The valley floors produce some crops in the warmer eastern pockets, but not enough to sustain the population through the winters, and the cheese and fiber trade exists specifically to sustain the grain imports that the valleys cannot replace from their own resources. The balance has been maintained for as long as the records exist. It is exact in the way that mountain economies that cannot afford inexactness become exact over sufficient generations.

## Character

Narcoshi people are, by most outside accounts, difficult to offend and difficult to impress. These two qualities have the same root: they have been navigating extreme terrain since childhood and managing their goats' reputation since adolescence, and both experiences produce a specific kind of equanimity about what the world offers. The terrain is what it is. The slur is what it is. The cheese price is what it is, and they know what it is better than the buyer does, and they are prepared to wait.

The one thing that reliably produces Narcoshi interest is technical competence. An outsider who demonstrably knows what they are doing — at anything, the subject is not important — receives from a Narcoshi a quality of attention that is noticeably different from the patient neutrality extended to everyone else. The vel-ridge animals show route-reading competence in the terrain; the Narcoshi value route-reading competence in people. Someone who is good at their particular equivalent of the vel-narcoth is someone a Narcoshi finds worth talking to.

The dry quality of Narcoshi humor is consistent enough to be described as a cultural feature. The standard joke format is the literal answer to a question whose actual import was different from its stated form. Asked whether they found the accusation of devil worship offensive, one famous Narcoshi elder said that they found it imprecise, and declined to elaborate. Whether the imprecision was the offense or the description was worth keeping is a question that the answer left open, which was the point.

## Language

Narcoshi is a Mittoli-derived mountain dialect — recognizable grammar, shared core vocabulary — with a goat vocabulary that specialists have found more extensive than the vocabularies that other herding cultures maintain for their primary animals. The reason is not that the Narcoshi are more attached to their goats than other herders are to their animals; it is that the vel-narcoth requires precise communication about terrain and behavior that Standard Mittoli must describe in phrases. There are specific words for the moment when a goat pauses before a section of route to assess it (distinct from pausing for other reasons), for the specific behavior that indicates a vel-ridge animal has found a new route section (distinguished from simply wandering), for the sound the herd makes when one of the large animals from above has come into range, and for the quality of attention a breeding-line matriarch has versus a young female in her first season on the high routes. Outside scholars who have obtained vocabulary lists describe these as extremely specific. Narcoshi herders describe them as barely adequate.

The substrate layer that appears in Narcoshi valley names and terrain terms is from the same pre-Mittoli layer as the Mithala place names and the Canerd/Yunethre names — the same older phonological profile, the same resistance to Mittoli root analysis. The Academy has noted this connection without being able to use it. The name Narcosh itself is from this substrate; proposed derivations include "northern pocket," "the held place," and "the country of the dark ones," the last referring to the goats rather than the people, or possibly to both.
