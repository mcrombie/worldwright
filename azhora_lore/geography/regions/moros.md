# The Moros Plain

East of the inner-branch hills and north of the Lizeem's coastal hinterland, the land drops and flattens and does not stop flattening. The Moros plain is one of the most uniformly level territories on the continent — not the flat of river deltas, where channels and levees interrupt the surface, but the structural flat of deep ancient sediment laid down without folding, without disruption, across an area large enough that the eye reaches the horizon in every direction without finding relief. On clear days the horizon is the only line in the landscape. The sky is approximately everything.

The comparison that travelers from the Pyros uplands or the Lotharn valleys reach for is open water — the plains give the same sensation of being exposed on a surface with no edges. The people of Moros find this a strange thing to say. There is obviously ground underfoot. The travelers are clearly not at sea. But the disorientation the comparison points at is real: the Moros plain strips away the sense of enclosure that most of Azhora provides, and people raised in hills and valleys do not immediately know what to do with their attention when there is nothing in it.

## Geography

The Moros plain occupies northeastern Azhora's interior, bounded to the west by the upland country of the inner-branch river systems — the hills rise gradually rather than sharply, but they rise — and to the north by low ridges that mark the limit of the productive agricultural belt before the climate tips toward steppe. To the south, Eer's chalk hills separate Moros from the Lizeem's northern distributaries and the coastal plain around Nylon. To the east, the plain runs to the Iberos Sea, where several river mouths open into a coastline of low sandy headlands and estuary flats.

The rivers of Moros are the Ros and its branches — a network of slow, wide-floored rivers that gather the plain's drainage from west to east and deposit it in the sea north of Eer's coast. The Ros does not cut a deep valley. On this terrain it cannot; there is not enough gradient to entrench. It spreads instead — braiding across its floodplain in wet years, pulling back to a single channel in dry ones, leaving behind a wide corridor of seasonally-flooded bottomland that is simultaneously the most productive soil on the plain and the least hospitable to permanent settlement. Moros communities sit on the slightly elevated ground above the flood line and farm the bottomland when conditions allow.

Suval is visible from the eastern coast on clear days — the island sits close enough to the mainland that the Ros estuary communities maintain regular boat traffic, and distant enough that crossing in bad weather is an experience people remember.

## Climate

The Moros plain has no protection. The Oremindi keeps the western side of Azhora in rain shadows and orographic patterns that give the interior its climate character; Moros, east of the inner-branch hills and open to the north and east, gets whatever comes from across the Iberos Sea and whatever descends from the cold uplands to the north without significant modification.

The result is seasons of considerable force. Winter arrives with cold continental air and produces conditions that surprise people from Mittolo and Eer: snow that lies flat on the open plain instead of catching in hollows and sheltered slopes, ice on the Ros and its branches, wind that encounters nothing to break it for miles. Summer brings heat and dramatic afternoon storm systems — the thunderstorms that build over the plain in the warm months are visible approaching from thirty miles in the clear air, which gives them a particular quality: you see them coming for an hour before they arrive. The people of Moros know the weather a long time before it is upon them, which is not the same as being able to do much about it.

The growing season is adequate for grain. The soil is deep and dark, built over millennia from the same sediment processes that made the Mithala plain fertile, and grain on the Moros plain yields generously in years when the rainfall is cooperative and ruins quickly in years when it is not. The agricultural memory of Moros communities runs to forecasting: families that have farmed the same ground for generations have accumulated a body of practical knowledge about which weather patterns in early spring predict what summer will bring, which river behaviors signal a productive flood year and which signal damage. This knowledge is held at the household level, transmitted informally, and not systematically recorded — which means it is also regularly lost to migration, war, and the interruption of farming families.

## Political Character

Flat land is difficult to hold. There are no defensible positions on the Moros plain — no ridge to command, no river-gorge to anchor a fortification, no high ground that puts one community above another. The rivers are slow and their crossings are numerous; blocking a crossing on the Ros prevents traffic at that crossing, but the next ford is within a day's walk. Political power on the Moros plain has always come from wealth and relationship rather than from terrain, which means it is assembled slowly and comes apart quickly.

The plain has no dominant city. It has a number of market towns, each controlling a stretch of river and the roads that radiate from it — useful positions that many powers have found worth holding and none has found sufficient to project authority over the whole plain. Every market town on the Moros plain knows that if it grows influential enough to attract the attention of Pyrosi highland confederacies or Iberos sea powers or Mittoli river interests, it will likely spend a generation as someone's garrison before reverting to its own management when the cost of maintaining the garrison exceeds the revenue it produces. The largest of the Moros towns have gone through this cycle enough times to have reduced it to institutional practice: when the occupier arrives, they occupy; when the occupier leaves, they resume. The transition procedures are almost ceremonial at this point.

This does not make the Moros towns politically passive. Between major occupations, the plain's market towns compete over river-crossing tolls, seasonal market rights, and the grain trade with Nyloni merchants who come north to buy. The competition is commercial and legal rather than military — nobody fights over flat land because nobody wins that fight — and the legal institutions that manage commercial dispute between towns have accumulated more practical authority over the plain's economy than any individual town council. The arbitration assembly at the Ros midpoint crossing is, by most measures, the most consequential political institution on the Moros plain. It has no army. It has the ledgers.

## Economy

Grain is the foundation. Wheat and barley in the good bottomland years; rye and oats in the drier years; fodder crops for the cattle that range the higher-ground pastures north of the Ros main channel. The Moros plain feeds itself and exports surplus south through Eer to Nylon and by sea from the Ros estuary communities to Suval and the coastal trade.

The river fishery is significant. The Ros's slow-water environment produces species that do not appear in the faster highland rivers — large flat-water fish that are salted and exported in quantities that make Moros salt-fish a known commodity in Minoran markets and at Nylon's fish docks.

Cattle. The higher pasture ground north of the productive bottomland is cattle country — large herds of the plains stock breed that Moros has developed over generations of selection for endurance in open-weather conditions. Moros cattle are not the high-value mountain beef of Nescor or the dairy breeds of the inner-branch valleys; they are large, hardy, tolerant of cold and storm, and produce quantities of beef and leather that Nylon and the Iberos coast trade cities reliably buy. The cattle drives south to Nylon's market — down the old road through the northern hills and into Eer — are a seasonal rhythm as established as the grain harvest.

The Ros estuary communities are a different economy entirely: fishing, Suval-trade, coastal traffic, and whatever carrying work the sea offers. They have more in common with Nylon's outer harbor communities than with the grain farmers of the plain's interior, and their politics are correspondingly more oriented toward Iberos coast affairs and Suval's commercial interests than toward the arbitration assembly at the Ros crossing.

## The Plain's Particular Knowledge

Living on terrain that hides nothing produces a specific attentiveness. Moros people watch the sky the way hill people watch the paths — it is where information arrives. Weather-reading on the Moros plain is a practical skill developed to a degree that visitors from elsewhere find either impressive or unsettling: farmers who can identify tomorrow's storm pattern from cloud behavior and wind shifts at noon today; cattle herders who can estimate a blizzard's arrival time within hours from conditions that look to an outsider like ordinary cold. The Caeras Valley Academy has periodically sent scholars to study this knowledge, finding in the practical meteorological tradition of the Moros plain methods that precede and in some cases surpass what formal Academy study has derived.

The knowledge is not written. It is not systematized. It exists in families and in the informal networks of farmers who exchange observations at market. It is damaged every time a war disrupts the farming communities; it regenerates more slowly than the crops do.

The same quality of attention extends to anything moving on the plain. An army three days out is visible — its dust, if the ground is dry, or its smoke if it camps. A merchant caravan from the Pyrosi foothills is visible half a day before it arrives. The Moros plain does not surprise people with arrivals; it announces them. This is a feature the plain's inhabitants regard as essential and its conquerors consistently underestimate. By the time an occupying force arrives at a Moros town, the town has been watching it approach for a day and has already made the relevant decisions.

## Language

Eastern Mittoli — the transitional variant of the Iberos contact zone, similar to the dialect that develops wherever Mittoli and the Iberos coastal trade vocabulary have been in long contact. The Ros estuary communities shade toward the Iberos-influenced forms; the inland grain farmers shade toward standard Mittoli.

Place names on the Moros plain preserve an older layer that does not derive from Mittoli or from any identified neighboring language. The topographic terms in oldest use — names for specific bends of the Ros, for certain areas of reliable upland pasture, for the weather phenomena that characterize the plain in winter — follow a phonological pattern distinct from current speech. Linguists who have studied the Eer naming tradition (which has a similar pre-Mittoli substrate in its land-feature names) have noted that the two substrates share characteristics, which would imply that before the Mittoli expansion, the coastal plain from Eer north through Moros was occupied by a people who spoke something else. What they spoke and where it went is not established. The names survive in the ground; the language that made them is gone or has not been asked.

*Morath* — the local word for the spring flood on the Ros, from the oldest substrate layer. Has entered colloquial Mittoli on the plain in the specific meaning of any situation of coming abundance that requires preparation before it arrives and good sense after it recedes.

## Character

Moros is not dramatic. It is very large and very flat and the weather does what it does and the grain grows or it does not, and whoever arrives eventually leaves, and the Ros floods and recedes on its own schedule. The people who have farmed the same bottomland across generations have the patience of people who have found impatience to be agriculturally useless.

Outsiders sometimes mistake this patience for lack of feeling. The Moros people are aware of this and find it an odd mistake — you do not watch storms approaching for an hour before they arrive and develop no relationship with what you're watching. The attentiveness is the feeling. The plain is not indifferent to what moves across it; it simply sees it coming a long way off.

The one thing the Moros plain does not provide is the feeling of being enclosed, protected, held by terrain. There is sky and there is ground and there is the horizon, and everything that arrives or departs does so in full view. People raised here and living elsewhere often describe a persistent sense, in hill country, that they cannot see far enough — that the walls of a valley are keeping them from knowing what is beyond them. This is accurate. The Moros plain is not a comfortable place by the standards of enclosed landscapes. It is extremely legible. These are, to the people who grew up in it, the same thing.
