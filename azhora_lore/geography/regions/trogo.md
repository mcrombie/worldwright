# Trogo

Trogo is the name given to the southeastern face of the Dinova Peninsula — the territory that begins where the Meroshe's canyon country ends and descends toward the southern ocean through what is, by any geographic expectation, the wrong kind of landscape. The Meroshe is desert. The southern ocean is warm and wet. Between them, on the slopes and river valleys of the peninsula's southeastern reach, the desert's last water meets the coast's first moisture and produces a consequence that neither the desert peoples to the northwest nor the Maroshi kingdom to the north has entirely figured out what to do with: tropical rainforest.

The name does not come from any language family associated with the main Azhoran traditions. The Moreshi of the canyon south call it something that renders approximately as "the wet below." The Maroshi court in Dinelv calls it Trogo in their administrative correspondence and notes this as a term used by the people there; where the term originates, the correspondence does not say. It does not appear to be Mittoli. It does not appear to be Moreshi in the triconsonantal root structure. It may be the local peoples' own word for their own place, which would be the most sensible explanation and the one that the administrative records are slowest to offer.

## The Transition

Stand at the southern edge of the Meroshe's canyon country and face southeast. Below you, the canyon walls drop and the stone changes color — the red-orange of the high desert giving way to a darker, wetter rock where the canyon rivers are running. Follow the rivers down, and within a day's walk the vegetation changes faster than seems reasonable. Thorn scrub gives way to broadleaf growth. The broadleaf growth gives way to something taller. The air changes — humid first in the morning, then constantly, then oppressively to anyone who arrived from the desert. The stone is still there, still dramatic, still carved canyon-country geology, but the canyons are now full of canopy rather than heat shimmer.

The transition is not gradual everywhere. The topography creates edges: a ridgeline that catches the southern moisture and drops a fog wall on its windward face while the leeward side stays desert. A canyon that has been sheltered enough to hold humidity for long enough that the vegetation inside it is ten kilometers ahead of the vegetation on the surrounding plateau. And then the reverse — gaps in the ridge where the desert air pushes through in the dry months, creating corridors of sparse growth cutting into the forest. The transition zone is not a line. It is a territory with its own character, belonging to neither system fully.

The fog is significant. Where desert air meets ocean-loaded humidity along the southeastern ridge, fog forms and stays, sometimes for days. This is not the cold sea-fog of Bouén's coast. It is warm and thick and close, and it waters the middle elevation forest through the dry months when the rainfall alone would not be enough. The fog-forest of the upper transition zone is its own ecosystem, distinct from the canyon desert above and the full tropical canopy below — an intermediate world of mosses and cloud-dependent plants that exists only because two atmospheric systems are pressing against each other at this latitude.

## Geography

The southeastern drainage of the Dinova peninsula runs through Trogo to the coast. Three named river systems collect the meltwater — such as it is, in a desert — from the canyon country and gain strength as they descend into the wetter south. By the time they reach the coast, they are full rivers carrying substantial volume year-round. The largest, which Maroshi records call the Trogoreth ("the Trogo river," a construction that acknowledges they have no better name for it), has a wide delta mouth that has silted into a shallow estuary system. Fishing communities live on the estuary. The river is navigable for small vessels some distance upstream before the canyon narrowing stops boat traffic.

The land itself is steep. Trogo is not a lowland rainforest in the manner of the river basins of the southern archipelago; it is a slope forest, on the descending face of the peninsula's interior plateau, and the terrain reflects this — rivers run fast, elevation changes quickly, and the forest grows on ground that would challenge cultivation even if anyone were trying to cultivate it in the conventional sense. The coastal strip, where the rivers slow and the land flattens near the southern sea, is the most hospitable section. The upper slopes are forest on grades that make travel difficult.

The southeastern coast of Trogo faces the southern ocean and the Azhor Stones, which are visible from the higher coastal headlands on clear days. The coast is not extensively sheltered — no deep natural harbors on the scale of Hama — but several river mouths have been developed into small anchorages adequate for the coastal and regional trade that Trogo's communities conduct.

## The Ecology

Trogo's ecology is layered by altitude. The upper edge, where the canyon meets the fog zone, is adapted to intermittent moisture: plants that can survive dry months and wet ones, that take the fog when it comes and store it. The canyon fig species that grow along the upper river courses root in the rock faces and extend canopy far beyond what the soil would support. This forest is old by Azhoran standards and dense in its species composition at every layer.

The mid-slope forest is full tropical rainforest by the measures that matter — closed canopy, high humidity year-round, the layer structure of tall emergents over a canopy over understory over ground. The species composition overlaps partially with the forests of the southern archipelago's larger islands, suggesting either a shared ancient forest cover or long-distance seed dispersal along ocean and river routes. Whether the same species arrived in Trogo from the south or whether the southern islands' forests descend from stock that once covered this coast is a question that requires a botanist willing to spend years here, and the Academy has not yet sent one.

At the coast and along the lower river systems, the mangrove and estuary ecology takes over — brackish-adapted vegetation, root systems that make the river mouths look solid but are not, and the bird and fish populations that the estuary communities depend on.

The transition zone supports animals from both the desert and the forest at its edges. Meroshe species that follow the canyon rivers down encounter the forest edge and some go no further; some have adapted to the wet over generations and are now found in both zones, distinguishable from their canyon relatives by behavioral and slight physical differences that the local peoples identify and name. Tropical species from the forest proper come to the desert edge for specific resources — particular insects, particular plants, water — and return. The carnivores that hunt both zones are the most studied by the communities here, because they present the most immediate practical interest.

## The Peoples

Trogo is inhabited by communities that do not fit cleanly into any major Azhoran category. The canyon peoples of the southern Meroshe follow their river systems down into the upper reaches of Trogo — the transition zone is inhabited by families who know both desert and forest and work seasonally between them, moving up with the dry season and down with the rains. These are Moreshi in language and ancestry, and their connections are northward, into the canyon country.

The forest itself — the mid-slope and coastal sections — has its own inhabitants, whose ancestry does not trace to the Moreshi tradition and whose language belongs to neither the Moreshi family nor any Azhoran mainland family that has been classified. Whether this forest people's language relates to the southern archipelago languages, as some vocabulary patterns suggest, or whether it is an independent development, is not established. The southern peoples of the archipelago, who have made contact with the Trogo coast through the Azhor Stones route, treat the coast here with a recognition that suggests familiarity — they have words for the local plant species that are neither Azhoran approximations nor locally borrowed, which would require explanation.

The transition zone itself is inhabited by a third population that is neither clearly Moreshi nor clearly forest people — communities that have been in the ecotone long enough to have drawn from both and now constitute something of their own. They speak a contact language with vocabulary from multiple directions, operate as intermediaries between the canyon and forest communities above and below them, and have a practical knowledge of the transition zone's resources that neither the canyon Moreshi nor the forest peoples fully replicate.

The relationships between these groups are functional but not frictionless. The canyon Moreshi follow river rights they consider traditional; the forest peoples maintain territorial arrangements the canyon communities do not always acknowledge; the transition population navigates between both and does not always feel recognized by either. There is no political structure that encompasses all three, and Marosh's administrative claim to Trogo does not resolve this — a kingdom that governs by desert road has limited practical authority over forest slope populations that have no roads and no reason to use them.

## Commerce

Trogo produces things the desert cannot and the Mediterranean coast does not. Tropical timber of species not found further north — dense-grained, rot-resistant woods suited for ship construction that Hama has been quietly sourcing for a century. Resins from the upper forest that the Moreshi trade network carries north into the caravan routes; their uses in preservation, medicine, and scent have established a market as far as the Caeras cities, where the origin is imprecisely known. Dye plants from the lower river systems that produce colors in the blue-black and deep red range not achievable from Azhoran mainland sources. Medicinal material from the transition zone, where the overlap of desert pharmacopeia and tropical pharmacopeia creates compounds that neither tradition alone has access to.

Hama's Merchant Houses source much of this through intermediaries who do not explain their supply chains. The resins appear in Maroshi court records as tariffed exports, which means Marosh has noticed them and has found a way to claim administrative revenue without fully understanding what it is taxing. The timber appears in Hama's shipbuilding records with a geographic attribution that is vague in ways that suggest the vagueness is intentional.

The transition zone communities are the primary commercial interface. They move goods from the forest into the canyon river system and from the canyon system to the coast, extracting intermediary value from both directions and maintaining the relationships that make both flows possible. They do not discuss the internal workings of these arrangements with Maroshi administrators, whom they regard with the specific patience of people who have learned to be patient with claims that do not match the practical reality.

## Marosh's Relationship with Trogo

Marosh claims Trogo as the southeastern territory of the Dinova Peninsula. This claim is recorded in the kingdom's administrative documents and appears on Maroshi maps with the same boundary conventions as the Meroshe interior. What the claim means in practice depends on which part of Trogo you are discussing.

The canyon river systems in the upper transition zone, where Moreshi-speaking communities operate, are administered — loosely, at a distance — through the southern canyon governance structures that Marosh has established for the deep Meroshe. Tax collection is intermittent and conducted by intermediaries who retain a portion of what they collect; this is considered a reasonable administrative cost for reaching a population that is not easily reached otherwise.

The forest itself and the coast are governed, in practice, by no Maroshi institution. The kingdom's administrative reach stops at the lower canyon country. The forest communities have no obligation to Marosh that is currently enforced, no road that connects them to the Maroshi administrative structure, and no particular incentive to develop one. Marosh does not have the institutional capacity of a forest-governance kingdom — it is a desert-road power — and a program of forest administration would require building that capacity from nothing.

The current equilibrium is that Marosh collects what revenue it can from the upper transition zone, allows the Hama trade network to function in the coastal region without formal acknowledgment, and classifies Trogo as administered territory on maps that the forest communities do not see and would not find accurate if they did.

## Language in the Contact Zone

The languages of Trogo form a spectrum rather than a set of discrete varieties. At the canyon edge, recognizable canyon Moreshi. At the coast, the forest language and its apparent southern-ocean relatives. In the transition zone, a contact register that draws from both and has been developing its own character long enough to have grammatical features that are neither Moreshi nor forest in origin.

One feature of the transition language that has drawn attention: it has a grammatical category that neither Moreshi nor any Azhoran mainland language possesses — a verb aspect marking actions that are conditional on the current state of the fog. Whether the fog is present or not changes the form of certain verbs describing movement, visibility, and resource access. The category is semantically precise and grammatically regular, which means it was not invented recently. It names a distinction that is genuinely important to people whose decisions depend on whether the fog wall is up. The canyon Moreshi find it unnecessary. The coastal forest people find it imprecise. The transition zone communities find the criticism from both sides unsurprising.

The forest language of the mid-slope communities has not been systematically studied. The vocabulary that has been collected shows consistent phonological patterns — heavy use of nasal consonants, long vowels, and a series of tone distinctions that Azhoran languages do not have — and the vocabulary clusters that have been compared against southern archipelago languages show enough correspondence to require explanation. Whether this represents deep common ancestry, ancient contact, or something else is open. The question is not currently being investigated.

## The Canyon South and Trogo

The southern Meroshe's canyon country — the oldest-inhabited section of the desert, where the canyon communities do not excavate by choice and the ruins of something pre-Moreshi are present in the rock — drains directly into Trogo through the river systems. The water that makes Trogo possible comes through those canyons.

Whether anything else comes through is a question the canyon communities address by changing the subject. They follow the rivers seasonally and return from the Trogo direction with forest goods they trade through the caravan network. What they observe in the lower canyon reaches, and how far they follow the rivers into the forest, and what they encounter there — they are informative about the goods and quiet about the rest.

The forest peoples of Trogo proper, asked about the canyon country above them, describe it as the place where the water comes from and where certain things live that they do not follow upriver. They do not use the word "ruins" because the word in their language for what is in the upper canyon carries different connotations — something closer to "what was placed there" than "what was built and abandoned." The distinction may be a translation artifact. It may not be.
