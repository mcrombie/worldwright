# Selemis

Southwest of the Ascarth Peninsula, across a channel narrow enough that on a clear day you can read smoke from the other shore, sits the island of Selemis: a midsized island whose position at the southern mouth of the Iberos Sea has made it, over several centuries, the most consequential single point of maritime power on the continent. The city that occupies most of its northern coast shares the island's name. The people are called the Selemi.

Selemis does not have the largest navy in the Iberos Sea. It does not control the most territory. It does not produce the most goods. What it controls is position: every ship moving between the Iberos Sea and the open southern ocean passes within reach of its harbor, and every port on the sea has, at some point in the last two centuries, found itself doing business with a Selemi outpost whether it planned to or not. The Selemi have translated this geographic accident into a commercial empire built not on ownership but on presence — the carefully maintained ability to be at every significant port with something to offer and something to withdraw.

This makes them the most successful maritime traders on Azhora. It also makes them, to the Ascarth kings across the channel, an irritant of the first order.

---

## The Island

The island of Selemis is shaped like a crescent, its concave face turned toward the Azhoran coast, forming a natural sheltered harbor of considerable size. The city fills this crescent from headland to headland — a dense, layered settlement that has been building over itself for long enough that the oldest foundations in the lower harbor district are several stories underground and accessible only to the maintenance workers who keep the drainage systems functional.

The harbor is the city. Everything else — the residential districts climbing the island's interior hills, the warehouses and chandlers' yards, the quarter districts where foreign merchants maintain permanent residences, the schools and the meeting-halls and the archive building with its copper roof gone green — is organized around and in service to the harbor. A Selemi who cannot give you a clear account of the current tide schedule, the state of the wind, and what ships are in port knows they are going to hear about this.

The channel between Selemis and the Ascarth peninsula is narrow but not trivial — enough that a fleet can cross it but not so little that an army can wade. The Selemi have positioned their harbor fortifications to make the crossing as expensive as possible and have stationed their fastest ships at the channel's northern end, where they can intercept anything moving toward the island before it reaches the crescent harbor. These defenses have not stopped Avite conquest, historically. They have made each conquest expensive enough that it has eventually been abandoned as more trouble than it was worth.

## The Selemi

The Selemi are the most widely traveled people on the continent and they carry this fact lightly. A Selemi merchant in Nyross, in Nylon, in the ports of the Svaleen, in the Moroshé coastal towns, or in the Elodi quarter of any city has usually been in several of those places in the same season, and has adjusted their manner to each without apparent effort. They are genuinely easy to talk to, curious about other people's situations, knowledgeable about whatever the local preoccupation is, and generous with their time in the way of people who know that the investment will return.

This cosmopolitan fluency is not an affectation. The Selemi have been absorbing foreign vocabulary, foreign cooking, foreign music, and foreign religious practices for long enough that the question of what is originally Selemi culture is genuinely difficult to answer. The food in Selemis itself is, by consensus of most visitors who have eaten extensively in other Azhoran cities, the most interesting in the continent — not because it is elaborate or precious, but because it combines techniques and ingredients from a dozen different culinary traditions in combinations that should not work and do. The music is similar. The architecture of the city shows four or five distinct stylistic traditions coexisting on the same street, accumulated across the centuries of Selemi commercial reach.

What is genuinely and distinctly Selemi is the quality of attention they bring to understanding other people. A Selemi in a new port does not merely learn the prices. They learn who sets the prices, why, who benefits from them being set at that level, and who would benefit from them being different. They learn the social geography of the city — which families are rising, which are declining, which disputes are currently active, which are superficially resolved but still generating pressure. This information is not always immediately useful. The Selemi are patient enough that this does not bother them.

The combination — charming surface, comprehensive attention, long memory — is why other Azhoran merchants sometimes find the Selemi pleasant to deal with and difficult to trust. Both assessments are accurate.

## The Outpost Network

The Selemi maintain outposts at strategic points along the coasts of the Iberos Sea and, in several cases, beyond it. These are not primarily commercial operations — there are Selemi merchants who use the outposts as bases, but that is secondary. The outposts exist to give Selemis a card to play in every major port.

In practice, this means: a Selemi outpost is a small, permanent, legally recognized Selemi presence with its own building, its own staff, its own relationship with local authorities, and its own established role in local commerce. The outpost handles Selemi ships, stores Selemi goods, and maintains the relationships that allow Selemi merchants to operate with advantages — favorable berths, early information about cargo, introductions to the people worth knowing — that outside merchants cannot easily replicate.

When Selemis wants to apply pressure to a coastal city, it does not send a navy. It adjusts its outpost's behavior. Ships are delayed rather than expedited. Introductions are not made. Information about cargo availability becomes, suddenly, slightly less current. The goods that the outpost has been smoothly providing become subject to delays that cannot quite be pinned on anything specific. This pressure is deniable, incremental, and effective. Most coastal cities with Selemi outposts have learned what it feels like and have adjusted their policies accordingly.

The outpost network also means that the Selemi know, in near-real-time, what is happening at every significant port in their reach. This is not what the outposts are for — it is simply an inherent consequence of having well-placed, attentive people everywhere. The Selemi do not advertise this capability. They use it.

The outpost locations are chosen by a combination of strategic necessity and historical opportunity — some exist because that port controls a critical sea lane, others because a Selemi merchant a century ago established a relationship that proved durable. The network has gaps, and maintaining it requires continuous investment. Selemi diplomacy is substantially occupied with keeping outpost rights in place when local politics change, and with establishing new outpost rights in cities that are becoming important before those cities are certain they want Selemi presence.

## With Aevis

The narrow channel between Selemis and the Ascarth Peninsula is one of the most examined stretches of water in the Iberos Sea, from both sides.

The Avites and the Selemi are natural antagonists: one culture built around land, hierarchy, and the individual pursuit of martial glory; the other built around sea, lateral commercial networks, and the collective management of leverage. They have been neighbors for long enough that the antagonism has acquired the familiarity of a long argument — still genuine, still capable of producing violence, but structured by centuries of knowing exactly how the other side thinks.

The conquest history is real and sits in the Selemi cultural memory with the weight of something that must not happen again. Aevis has taken the island twice in the documented period. Both times, the Selemi response was the same: the island fell, the outpost network continued to function, and the Selemi civilization carried on from the sea. Selemi ships did not stop sailing because Aevis held the harbor. Selemi merchants did not close their outposts because an Avite garrison occupied the city. The revenue streams continued; the relationships continued; the empire of presence persisted while the physical island was temporarily someone else's.

This is the essential asymmetry: Aevis can take Selemis's island, but Selemis is not its island. The Selemi understand this about themselves. The Avites, both times, eventually understood it as well, after the cost of occupation exceeded what the occupation was producing. The occupation ended. The Selemi returned.

The current period is independent. The channel is watched. There is trade across it — Avite bronze and Selemi sea-goods, mutually useful enough that neither side wants complete disruption — and there are diplomatic missions of varying frequency and, usually, limited warmth. The Selemi outpost in Aevis is the one outpost in the network that is managed with the most careful attention to local politics, because the local politics are the most capable of turning into an army on short notice.

The Ascarth kings, for their part, are aware that the Selemi outpost in their city is watching them. Both sides find this arrangement more useful than the alternative.

## Among the Coastal Cities

Selemis is known everywhere on the Iberos Sea in the way that a fact of geography is known — not because you chose to learn it but because it is simply present and relevant. Every city that trades on the sea has an opinion about the Selemi. The opinions range from genuine admiration through cautious appreciation through the particular wariness of people who have seen what happens when the outpost in their city becomes, suddenly, less helpful.

Nylon's relationship with Selemis is among the warmer ones on the coast. The Nyloni questori find the Selemi interesting to argue with — one of the few groups that treats negotiation as an intellectual discipline rather than a contest. The Selemi find Nylon useful as a place where information flows openly enough to be worth collecting. There is a Selemi quarter in Nylon's harbor district that is old enough to be considered part of the city's permanent texture.

The Svaleen cities deal with Selemis competently. Solis has a Selemi outpost; Enebreum has had complicated negotiations about whether it wants one (it has wanted one when it was losing trade and not wanted one when it was suspicious about what the outpost was doing). Imlamdris has no outpost and, being inland, does not need one.

Outside the Iberos Sea, wherever Selemi ships have reached, the reputation travels ahead of them. The most successful maritime traders on Azhora is a description that functions as both a commercial recommendation and a social warning, depending on what you are trying to do and whether you want Selemis to know about it.

## Summary

Selemis is the continent's master of maritime leverage — an island city-state whose actual power is not in its harbor or its fleet but in the distributed network of presence it maintains at every significant port in its reach. The Selemi are charming, cosmopolitan, attentive in ways that take time to notice, and constitutionally patient. Their independence from Aevis is precarious in the narrow sense that the channel can be crossed, and has been; it is secure in the broader sense that the Selemi civilization is not located on any island that can be taken. What Selemis is, the Avites have not yet found a way to hold.
