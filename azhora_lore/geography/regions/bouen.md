# Bouén

Bouén is the southern peninsula of Azhora — a long, tapering arm of land that extends southward from the main continental mass and ends in a rocky coast facing open ocean in three directions. By latitude it should be the warmest region of the continent. It is not. A cold ocean current sweeps up the western and southern coasts of the peninsula from deep southern waters, dragging cold upwelling with it, and the result is a climate that surprises everyone who arrives expecting tropical warmth and finds instead grey skies, cold water, and a wind that comes off the open ocean with nothing to slow it.

The Bouéni have lived with this for long enough that they consider it normal. They are consistently amused by the disappointment of visitors.

## Geography

The peninsula is roughly 600 miles long and narrows progressively from its connection to the main continent in the north to its southern tip, which ends in a series of rocky headlands and the island clusters of the Azner Shores. The interior is hilly rather than mountainous, covered in dense coastal scrub adapted to wind and salt spray in the south, transitioning to a mix of grassland and woodland further north.

The western coast is cliffed and exposed — the cold current runs closest here, and the sea off the western shore is cold enough that swimming is inadvisable except in high summer, and is inadvisable in any serious sense regardless of season. The eastern coast is more sheltered, facing the southern extension of the Iberos Sea system, and is where the majority of the peninsula's population lives.

The rivers of Bouén are short and fast — fed by highland rain, they run directly to the sea with little of the broad valley character of the Mittoli river system. They are rich in fish.

## The Cold Current

The current that defines Bouén's climate has no universally agreed name; the Bouéni call it the Undercurrent or the Deep River, and Azhoran oceanographers call it the Southern Draw. Whatever its name, it is one of the dominant features of the southern ocean, moving cold, dense water from the deep south northward along the western Azhoran coast before curving away into the open ocean to the west.

The current creates the persistent overcast, the cold-water fishery, and the reliable winds that Bouéni sailors have exploited for centuries. It also creates the fog — dense, persistent sea-fog that rolls in off the coast in spring and autumn and can reduce visibility to a few feet for days at a time. Navigating in Bouéni fog is a skill that the peninsula's sailors are renowned for throughout Azhora, and which outsiders consider to be somewhere between impressive and inexplicable.

The Deep River also moderates temperature extremes. Bouén does not have the brutal winters of the north or the true heat of the Moroshé summer; it has an endless, cool, overcast grey that is its own kind of extreme and that produces a population with a particularly developed relationship with indoor life, fire, and one another.

## History

Bouén's history is the history of its isolation. Connected to the main continent by a narrow neck that historically served as a natural chokepoint, and surrounded on three sides by open ocean, the peninsula developed largely on its own terms. Mittoli powers reached it late and held it loosely. The Iberos coastal culture reached it at the same time by sea and found a people who were curious about trade but deeply uninterested in incorporation.

The Bouéni identity is old and specific. They have their own origin traditions — distinct from both the Mittoli "the people before us left" and the Pyrosi Fire Memory — that describe their ancestors as having come from the sea rather than having always been on the land. Whether this is historical memory, metaphor, or something else is not established. The Bouéni treat the question with mild amusement rather than the weight that outside scholars bring to it.

The peninsula's most significant historical contribution to the wider continent has been navigational. Bouéni sailors, trained in fog navigation and accustomed to the unpredictable southern seas, were the first Azhorans to map the southern ocean routes, to make contact with the Azner Shores islands, and to attempt the western crossing that led to the reports — still contested — of the Western Shore beyond the Silence.

**Te Nillium** is the principal city of the northern peninsula, sitting at the neck where Bouén connects to the mainland. It is a gateway city in every sense — the place where goods, people, and ideas from the wider continent enter Bouén and are processed through a culture that will selectively absorb some of them and quietly ignore the rest. The architecture of Te Nillium reflects this: orderly, compact, built for weather, with public spaces that are enclosed or windbreaked in ways that strike visitors from the more open-climate regions as claustrophobic and strike Bouéni as sensible.

**Blue Stones** is not a city but a site — a coastal location on the western shore where a grouping of large, distinctly blue-grey standing stones has been maintained since before recorded history. The stones are of a type not found in the local geology and were brought from somewhere else, though from where is unknown. They are not actively worshipped but they are treated with care. The Bouéni do not build near them, do not remove them, and do not explain them to outsiders in any detail. "They were here before us" is the standard response to inquiry, delivered in a tone that closes the subject.

## The Azner Shores

The island chain that extends southward from the tip of the Bouén peninsula into open ocean is called the Azner Shores — a different word root from "Azhora," though the superficial similarity has caused occasional confusion. The islands are volcanic in origin, green and dramatic, and inhabited by a people who are related to the Bouéni in language and culture but have diverged considerably over the centuries of island isolation. The Azner islanders are the southernmost Azhoran people known to exist. What lies further south is ocean, as far as anyone who has returned can report.

## Language

The Bouéni language is the most divergent of the Azhoran mainland tongues. It has Mittoli roots but has absorbed so much from older substrates — the origins of which are unidentified — that linguists sometimes debate whether "divergent dialect" or "related language" is the more accurate description. It sounds, to Mittoli ears, like Mittoli spoken with a different rhythm and half the vocabulary replaced. To Bouéni ears, Mittoli sounds like their own language spoken by someone who has forgotten most of what words originally meant.
