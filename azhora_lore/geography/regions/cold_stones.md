# Cold Stones

Cold Stones is an archipelago of pale, rocky islands sitting in the cold sea between Azhora's northern coast and the Ice Spear peninsula of the northern continent. It is the furthest north that most Azhoran sailors have gone, and for many expeditions it represents not a destination but the last reliable waypoint before the unknown.

The name distinguishes it from Blue Stones in Bouén — a common source of confusion in navigational records, where "the Stones" without qualification has sent at least two documented supply missions to entirely the wrong place.

## Climate

Cold Stones sits at the intersection of two ocean current systems. The warm current that wraps around northern Azhora's western coast loses most of its warmth before reaching this latitude. The cold current flowing south along the eastern side of the northern continent brings Ice Spear's climate directly to the archipelago's northern edge. The result is cold in ways that defeat simple description, because temperature is not the primary fact. Wind is.

Storms off the northern continent are not like Azhoran storms. Azhoran storms build gradually and offer sky-signs that experienced sailors read hours in advance. Cold Stones storms arrive quickly, often without the warning cues that Azhoran navigational training relies on. The permanent residents have at least six named categories of winter storm, distinguished by wind direction, sea surface behavior, and the quality of the cold itself — whether the cold is wet or dry, whether it comes with pressure drop or pressure rise, whether it means ice in the channel or simply misery on the shore.

**Summer** lasts approximately three months. Extended daylight accompanies it, but not warmth. Average summer temperatures sit just above freezing at night and reach perhaps ten degrees above freezing at midday on the warmest days. The southern channel to Azhora clears. The northern channel toward Ice Spear opens to careful navigation. The vegetation flush is rapid and intense — coastal grass grows to knee height in the warmest weeks, lichen blooms turn the pale rock visibly green, and the seabird colonies on the outer stacks reach their fullest density. Visitors from Azhora consistently describe summer on Cold Stones as beautiful and cold. Residents do not find the description complete.

**Winter** lasts approximately seven months. At the solstice, the sun rises for a few hours and does not climb high. Sea ice closes the northern channel by late autumn. By deep winter, the southern channel becomes unreliable. The islands do not typically freeze — the surrounding sea moderates the temperature — but they become wind-scoured and largely impassable in the worst storms, when visibility drops to a few feet and exposed skin freezes within minutes.

The permanent residents spend deep winter in their settlements, which are built low to the ground and partially below it. Winter is not described as hardship in the Cold Stones communities. It is described as *neth* — the local word for the cold season, unrelated to any Mittoli calendar term. What the Mithala plains calls *nilcaelith* (the dry river, winter) and what the Caeras cities mark on their astronomical calendar have nothing to do with this. *Neth* is a different thing.

## The Islands

Cold Stones comprises roughly thirty islands of notable size and several hundred smaller outcrops and sea-stacks. The larger islands are low and wind-scoured, their surfaces covered in pale rock, hardy coastal grass, and the grey-green lichen that is one of the few things that thrives in the archipelago's conditions.

The rock itself is the feature that gave the islands their name — a pale, almost white sandstone unusual for this latitude, where darker igneous rock dominates. The pale stone is visible from considerable distance and gives the archipelago a ghostly quality in low light, particularly in the long winter dusk when the islands glow briefly before going dark.

Sea ice does not typically form on the islands themselves except in the worst winters, but the sea between them and Ice Spear carries drifting pack ice in all but the warmest months. The channel approach from the south, from Azhora, is clear in summer and difficult in winter. The northern approach is rarely attempted.

## The Sea

The water around Cold Stones is cold, rough, and productive. The same current system that makes the climate severe makes the sea rich: cold, well-oxygenated water supports dense populations of the species on which the archipelago's food economy depends. The fish are large and numerous by Azhoran standards. The communities do not fish far from shore — the outer sea is too difficult in most conditions — but the inshore waters yield enough year-round to sustain the population without significant supplementation.

Sea mammals are present in numbers. Seals use the outer rock stacks as haul-out points seasonally and the residents hunt them, primarily for oil and fur rather than meat. Larger animals — something in the walrus family, never closely described in Academy records — have been reported on the northern rocks, though the residents are noncommittal about their habits and do not name them in the vocabulary they share with outsiders. Seabirds are prolific: a dozen nesting species occupy the outer stacks in summer, and the egg-harvest from accessible colonies is an important part of the spring diet. In winter most species leave; a few remain year-round, and the residents know them individually by behavior if not by name.

Whales transit the deeper waters south of the archipelago. The residents do not hunt them — their boats are not built for it — but they have detailed knowledge of the transit patterns, seasons, and species composition, which they communicate to Academy expeditions in exchange for specific goods and without commentary on why the Academy wants to know.

## Habitation

Cold Stones has been intermittently inhabited for as long as Azhorans have been sailing north. Whether it was inhabited before Azhorans arrived is an open question — certain features of the larger islands, including a series of stone enclosures on the second-largest island that predate any known Azhoran presence, suggest the answer may be yes.

Currently, Cold Stones supports two small permanent communities on the largest island — fishing families whose ancestors came from the Smunders Bay coast and who have been here long enough to constitute a distinct sub-culture. They speak a dialect of Northern Mittoli that has drifted considerably from the mainland version and has absorbed vocabulary from sources no one has identified. They are accustomed to hosting Academy expeditions with the specific combination of hospitality and mild contempt that comes from repeatedly accommodating people who think they are the first to notice something you have lived next to your entire life.

### The Communities in Winter

The two communities — separated by about three miles of coastline and a ridge — maintain distinct household traditions but share resources during the hardest winter months, which function as a single extended community period. The buildings are stone-walled and partially dug into the hillside, with turf-covered roofing that sheds wind better than any pitched surface. Interior spaces are small. Multiple families share a single building during the coldest period; the arrangement is practical and unromantic about it.

The diet in winter is stored fish (dried or cold-smoked over fuel that has to be brought from Azhora, since the islands provide almost none), seal oil, preserved eggs, and a starchy root crop that grows in the thin soil in summer and stores well underground. Fresh food arrives with the first navigable days of late spring. The permanent residents do not treat the winter diet as deprivation; they treat it as the winter diet. Visitors who arrive expecting to supplement Academy supplies with local provisions are told clearly what is available and at what price.

The oil lamps burn through winter. The buildings are dark and warm and smell of fish and seal fat and people. Academy expedition members who have wintered on Cold Stones describe the experience with a range of responses, none of which the permanent residents find particularly interesting.

## The Old Enclosures

On the second-largest island, a ridge of the pale sandstone running east-west has had its southern face shaped into a series of enclosures — roughly rectangular, roughly the same size, built from the same stone that forms the ridge but worked rather than natural. The working is good: the stones are fitted without mortar and have not shifted perceptibly despite centuries of frost cycles. The enclosures are open to the sky. Their interiors show no evidence of roofing and no obvious evidence of function — no hearth deposits, no occupation layer, nothing that suggests habitation, storage, or animal keeping.

What they are for is not known. The permanent residents do not know either, or say they do not. They do not avoid the enclosures, but they do not particularly use them. They are described, in the local dialect, as *sketh-nor* — a compound that does not translate neatly but carries something like "the placed stones" or "the stones that were put there," with an implication that the putting-there was deliberate and that the purpose of the deliberateness has not been communicated. When pressed, residents say the enclosures were here before their grandparents' grandparents came, and before whoever was here before that.

The Academy's northern expedition spent two days examining the enclosures and produced a report that describes their physical characteristics accurately and declines to speculate on their purpose. The lead archaeologist's field notes, which were not included in the official report, describe a feeling of being examined that they attribute to wind through the stone gaps and then cross out.

The alignment of the enclosures — open faces all oriented at the same bearing, slightly west of south — has been noted but not explained.

## As Waypoint

Cold Stones is the staging ground for any serious attempt on the northern continent. Both Academy expeditions used the largest island as their winter base. The permanent residents have strong opinions about this, which they express through the pricing of supplies and the quality of information they choose to share.

From the northern shore of Cold Stones, Ice Spear is visible on clear days as a pale presence on the horizon, roughly sixty miles away. The water between is cold, often rough, and in winter, heavily iced. The sixty miles might as well be six hundred in terms of the difficulty of crossing them reliably.

## The Dialect

The variety of Northern Mittoli spoken on Cold Stones has drifted from the mainland version in ways consistent with long isolation and then absorbed vocabulary from a source that no linguist has identified. The grammar is recognizable Northern Mittoli — fixed initial stress, final vowel deletion, collapsed *ae* diphthong — but the phonological inventory includes sounds that Standard and Northern Mittoli do not have, suggesting either the influence of an older contact language or independent phonological development in isolation. The former is more likely, given that several of the unidentified vocabulary items cluster around specific semantic domains: sea ice, storm types, light conditions, and the old enclosures.

Selected dialect vocabulary not traceable to any Mittoli root:

*neth* — the cold season; winter. Distinguished from the Mittoli *nilcaelith* by covering both the depth of winter and the months of approach and departure on either side. *Neth* begins when the light changes, not when the temperature drops. Residents say they can feel *neth* arriving in the quality of late-summer light before any thermometric change.

*sketh* — ice that is on stone or land, as opposed to sea ice. The distinction matters: *sketh* on a path is a danger, *sketh* on a roof is structural load, *sketh* on the ridge is a weather indicator. Sea ice has a different word.

*droven* — sea ice; specifically floating pack ice, as opposed to ice that is anchored to the shore or fixed. The word carries the sense of the ice being in motion — *droven* that has stilled is *sketh-droven*, "landed ice." Navigators use these terms precisely and find Azhoran navigational language for ice conditions imprecise.

*skelver* — a storm that arrives without the usual sky precursors. The sudden type. Distinguished from storms that give warning not because the storm itself is different but because the local knowledge cannot be applied: *skelver* is what catches you out. A *skelver* in summer is unusual and discussed afterward. A *skelver* in winter is expected several times per season.

*thrag* — the quality of light at the winter solstice, when the sun is present for only a few hours and never climbs above the ridge line. Not darkness; a specific dim that comes from the south, flat and cold, without casting shadows in any useful direction. Visitors find it disorienting. Residents describe it as *thrag* and leave it there.

*ungvel* — the open northern sea when it is completely clear of ice; a rare condition that the residents treat as notable. Also used as a term of provisional good fortune: "it is *ungvel* today" carries the sense of "conditions are favorable, do not rely on this."

The Academy's position is that these terms are borrowings of unknown origin. The residents' position is that they are words for things that needed naming.

## Compass Anomaly

Navigation within and around Cold Stones requires local knowledge because compass needles behave inconsistently in the archipelago. The deviation is not constant — it shifts across the day in ways not attributable to any identified magnetic deposit, and it worsens significantly as you approach the northern edge of the archipelago near the open channel to Ice Spear. In that area, needles have been observed to rotate slowly in a clockwise direction, to hold a bearing that is approximately ninety degrees off magnetic north, or to oscillate without settling. The pattern does not repeat predictably between visits.

Navigators who rely on instruments rather than dead reckoning and star observation have run into trouble here regularly enough that all Academy expedition reports include explicit warnings. The second expedition's navigator lost two days in fog with a misbehaving compass and recorded a level of frustration that reads through the formal language of the expedition log.

The permanent residents navigate by star, current, wave pattern, and local knowledge of the channels between islands. They do not use compasses. They find the outsiders' reliance on them faintly baffling, in the specific way that people who have never needed a thing find baffling the distress of those who cannot function without it.

Whether the compass anomaly is related to whatever produces the anomalous features of the enclosures on the second-largest island is a question the Academy has not formally posed. The two phenomena are documented in separate reports by separate expeditions and have not been discussed in the same paper. The residents, asked directly, say the enclosures have nothing to do with it and then change the subject to something practical.
