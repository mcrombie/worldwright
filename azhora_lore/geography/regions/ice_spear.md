# Ice Spear

Ice Spear is the name Azhoran sailors gave to the southernmost peninsula of the northern continent — a long, tapering arm of glaciated rock that extends further south than any other part of that landmass, pointing toward the Cold Stones archipelago and, beyond it, Azhora's own northern coast. The name is straightforward: it is shaped like a spear, and it is made of ice.

Whether its inhabitants — if it has any — call it something else is not known.

## Appearance

From Cold Stones and from Azhora's northern headlands on clear days, Ice Spear presents as a pale shape on the horizon, distinct from the darker coast on either side of it. The peninsula is predominantly glaciated — ice flows from the high ground at its base down to the sea on both flanks, producing the white and blue-grey coloration that makes it visible at distance. The point itself, where the peninsula tapers to its narrow southern tip, is a succession of sea-cliffs and glacier-fronts that calve regularly into the water, producing the field of drifting ice that surrounds the tip in most seasons and makes close approach difficult.

The scale is difficult to estimate from a distance, but the accounts that have come closest suggest the peninsula is at least 150 miles long from its base to its tip, and that the glacial cliffs at the point rise to several hundred feet above the waterline.

## The Approach

The sea between Cold Stones and Ice Spear is not reliably navigable. Pack ice in winter closes it entirely. In summer, the calving from Ice Spear's glaciers produces floating ice in quantities that make the approach hazardous for wooden-hulled ships. The weather is unpredictable — storms come off the northern continent without the warning that Azhoran sailors are accustomed to reading, and the magnetic interference that affects compass needles in the Cold Stones archipelago is reportedly stronger closer to the peninsula.

One small party, from the second Academy expedition, came close enough to see the glacier face at the point clearly. Their account describes the ice as unusually blue — deeper in color than glacial ice on other Coravian landmasses — and notes a sound they describe as a low, intermittent resonance coming from within the ice itself, like a very large instrument played at the edge of hearing. They attributed this to wind passing through ice formations. The expedition's lead geologist wrote in the margin of the account: "possibly."

## The Base and Beyond

Where Ice Spear joins the main northern continent at its base, the glacial coast gives way to rockier terrain, and the mountains of the interior are visible. No Azhoran has traveled inland from this point. What lies beyond the visible coastal strip — whether the interior is glaciated throughout, whether there are lower-elevation habitable zones further from the coast, whether the mountains conceal valleys of any kind — is not known.

The northern Azhoran traditions describe the interior of the northern continent, in their more expansive moments, as a landscape of ancient things: old forests below the glacial line, structures half-buried in ice, and a quality of age that makes even Azhora's oldest sites feel recent. How much of this is observation and how much is the elaboration that oral tradition naturally accumulates over centuries is not separable at this distance.

## The Name in Other Languages

The Bay of Lol coastal dialect has its own word for Ice Spear — "Smethvar" — which does not translate directly but carries connotations of something pointed and intentional, as if the peninsula was made to point at something rather than simply shaped that way by geology. This interpretation is not taken literally by most people who use the word. The Ganoss upland clans, who have their own relationship with the North, call it something that translates as "the finger that counts," which raises more questions than it answers and which the clans decline to elaborate on.

The name that appears in the oldest Smunders Bay tradition — before the Mittoli period — is a word that linguists have not been able to etymologize from any known Azhoran language root. It may be a borrowing from an earlier contact. It may be something else.
