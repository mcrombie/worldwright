# Riesov

The coast at Riesov does not welcome arrival. The cliffs that back the Skeld range come down to the water in most places without transition, and the navigable gaps between them — the bays and inlets where the coastal plain meets the sea at a landing angle — are not numerous and not obvious until a sailor has come to know them. The northern sea here is grey and cold for more of the year than it is blue and cold, and the light sits low even in summer, arriving at the angle of a place that is far enough north to know it.

The Riesov will tell you this is an advantage. They mean it.

Every sailor who learns this coast learns it correctly, because the cost of learning it incorrectly is prohibitive. The Riesov communities have been the custodians of that knowledge — the tides at the Strendal approaches, the current that sets toward the rocks past the Skeld's northern spur, the specific behavior of the ice in an early winter versus a late one, the winds that come off the range in autumn and the winds that come off the sea in spring, and what the difference is between the two when you need to know it — for long enough that the knowledge is not written down anywhere that outsiders can read it. It is in the sailors. This is how they prefer it.

## Geography

Riesov is the coastal plain that lies between the Skeld range and the northeastern sea. The Skeld is not as high as the Oremindi — it is a harder and more compact range than the continent's southern spine, built differently, fractured differently — but it is high enough to constitute a barrier, and its eastern face descends steeply to the plain without the transitional hill country that softens the Oremindi's northern approaches. The range is a wall. The plain is what the wall protects.

The plain itself is narrow by the standards of Lond or even Ganun — ten to twenty miles between the Skeld's footing and the coast, widening at the major river mouths and narrowing again where spurs of the range come down to the water. The rivers that come off the Skeld's eastern face are short, fast, cold, and numerous: snowmelt and rain draining in parallel channels to the sea, depositing sediment where they slow and spread across the coastal flat. These river mouths are the most fertile ground Riesov has, the places where the soil deepens and the farmland is worth cultivating rather than simply possible to cultivate. The coastal community pattern follows them.

The narrowness that would seem like a limitation is partially answered by the sea. Riesov does not need its plain to be large in the way that an inland agricultural culture needs land to be large. It needs the plain to be productive enough to support its coastal population between fishing seasons, and this it manages. The sea provides protein in quantities that the plain's growing conditions — short seasons, cold, the shadow of the Skeld blocking the afternoon sun for a significant portion of the year — cannot match from agriculture alone. In the north, a community that can eat from the sea is not limited by its arable acres in the same way that a community that cannot is limited.

The cold here is categorically different from Ganun's cold. Ganun is northern by the standards of the central continent; Riesov is northern by the standards of the north. The winter that Ganun prepares for, Riesov considers unremarkable. The winter that Riesov prepares for is the kind that a Lond farmer hears described and wonders whether the Riesov are exaggerating for effect. They are not. The sea moderates the temperature enough to prevent the absolute cold of a truly arctic coast, but "moderated" here means "survivable with the right preparation," and the preparation is extensive.

The Skeld range is not primarily a military obstacle. Its passes are few and seasonal — open reliably only from late spring through early autumn — but they are not fortified in the way that a strategic pass would be fortified, because Riesov's relationship to its interior is commercial rather than defensive. The passes are how goods cross between Riesov and Ganun. They are contested economically rather than militarily, and the communities that sit at their mouths on both sides are in the business of handling that transit, not preventing it.

## The Skeld Passes

There are three passes through the Skeld that have been used with any regularity. The southernmost is the most accessible and carries the most traffic: a valley route that crests at a height manageable for loaded pack animals through perhaps six months of the year. The middle pass is higher and serves as an alternative when the southern pass is blocked by late snow or contested by whoever currently wants to complicate that crossing. The northern pass is the hardest and is used by those with specific reasons to avoid the other two.

The question of who controls the passes is not a question about Riesov or Ganun as polities. It is a question about which communities sit at the pass mouths — on the Riesov side, settlements that have grown around the transit function, handling the transfer of cargo from pack animals to coastal transport; on the Ganun side, communities that have grown around the same function in reverse. These communities are not agents of their respective coastal powers. They are independently minded, economically necessary to both sides, and extremely good at being neutral in a way that serves their own interests. When the rivalry between Riesov and Ganun sharpens into something that affects the passes — a season of elevated tolls, a closure declared on one side or the other — the pass communities lose income, and they make this loss audible to both parties in language that is not diplomatic but is specific.

The most significant recent use of pass politics was a winter when the Riesov coastal merchants, facing a period of particular Ganun price aggression at Ondrath, coordinated a suspension of southbound transit through all three passes during the autumn carting season. The Ganun respond to this kind of action with a calculation rather than with anger: what is it costing us, what is it costing them, what does the other side need badly enough that they will end it first. The calculus of that winter came out approximately even, which both sides found unsatisfying, and the passes reopened on a compromise that satisfied neither party and has been referred to with displeasure ever since.

## Strendal and the Shipping Industry

**Strendal** is the largest settlement in Riesov and the center of its commercial life. It sits at the mouth of the longest of the Skeld's eastern rivers, where the plain is widest and the natural harbor — a bay formed by two converging headlands — provides shelter on three sides. The harbor is deep enough for the largest Riesov vessels and most of the Bay of Lol-built ships that occasionally call there; the approach from the sea is not trivial to learn but is safe once learned.

Strendal is where the argument about Northern Azhoran shipping is most audible and most continuous. The argument, stated plainly: Riesov believes that the natural conduit for northern goods destined for the wider trading world is the coast, not the river. A loaded vessel out of Strendal can reach the Bay of Lol, the Smunders Bay ports, the coast road's northern terminus, and, in the right season, ports far down the continent's eastern margin. The Sond route reaches Mittolo's interior efficiently, but Mittolo's interior is not the whole market, and the Sond's seasonal limitations — pack ice closing the upper river, low summer water slowing the middle reach, the portages that cannot be bypassed in high water — are not limitations the sea imposes to the same degree.

The counter-argument, which Ganun makes with equal force and more patience: the Sond reaches the continent's largest commercial population directly, without the additional voyage from Strendal down the coast and around to the river mouths. The Sond delivers grain and wool to Mittoli merchants who come to Ondrath; Riesov's sea route delivers the same goods to coastal ports where a second handoff is required. Every handoff is a margin. The Ganun take one margin; Riesov and its downstream coastal partners take two.

Both arguments are correct in different conditions. The market they are arguing about — the northern surplus, and who gets to carry it south — is large enough to be contested indefinitely, and has been.

What Riesov does well that Ganun cannot match is the coastal distribution network itself. The Riesov shipping industry serves the northern coastal communities as a system: the Smunders Bay ports, the smaller settlements along the northeastern shore, the communities on the Skeld's northern spur that have no river access and no overland connection to the south. These communities require sea service or they lack it entirely. Riesov provides it. Ganun, whose inland orientation leaves it with Smunders Bay access that is grudging at best, does not. This captive coastal market is Riesov's economic foundation beneath the rivalry with Ganun, and it is not going away.

The Riesov merchant fleet is real and is maintained accordingly. The vessels are built for the northeastern coast: deep-drafted, cold-weather capable, designed for the specific challenge of a coast that is cold enough to ice up in hard winters and rough enough to test seamanship in mild ones. They are not the Bay of Lol's open-water ships, which are built for the deep ocean; they are built for the northern coast, which is a different thing. A Riesov ship in the open ocean beyond the continental shelf would be capable and uncomfortable. A Bay of Lol ocean-going vessel working the Riesov coastal approaches in early winter, with the Skeld's katabatic winds and the harbor ice and the tidal variation that catches visitors wrong, would have an instructive time.

## The Rivalry with Ganun

The rivalry is old enough that neither side remembers an era before it. The structural fact is simple: both Riesov and Ganun want to be the primary conduit through which the north's surplus reaches the wider trading world, and the surplus is not divisible by mutual agreement because the parties who produce it — Lond, Endevor, the Ganoss clans, the Orched Hills mining settlements — will route their goods toward whichever option costs less, and competition keeps both options perpetually undercutting each other.

The cultural expression of this is more complicated. Riesov and Ganun have had enough contact, over enough time, that each side understands the other with more accuracy than either side finds comfortable to admit. Riesov knows Ganun is not simply a collection of river boatmen operating outside their competence on coastal water. Ganun knows Riesov's sea routes reach markets that the Sond does not directly serve. The contempt each side performs for the other is not ignorance. It is the contempt of competitors who understand the competition well.

What Riesov says about Ganun, in terms that appear in coastal trade-house records and that Ganun sailors encounter regularly enough to be indifferent to: that the Sond is a ditch with pretensions, that Ondrath is a market town with a boat landing calling itself a port, and that Ganun's "seafaring tradition" is the tradition of a river pilot who once saw the bay. What Ganun says about Riesov, with equal regularity: that Strendal is a harbor waiting for a hinterland, that Riesov's shipping network exists to serve communities too peripheral to route through a real trade center, and that the only reason anyone crosses the Skeld passes in November is because the Sond is more reliable and they have no choice.

The third party in this dispute is Lond. Whoever holds Stonefist can make Riesov or Ganun's position substantially better or worse by deciding which direction Lond's grain flows first. Riesov and Ganun have both invested heavily in maintaining favorable relationships with Stonefist's current garrison, which the Lond communities observe without comment and the Stonefist garrison has historically found useful. The leverage runs both ways: Stonefist can tilt Lond's grain toward either route, but it cannot grow more grain, and both routes need the grain to have something to carry. The mutual dependency is the only reason the rivalry has not become something more explicitly destructive.

## The People

The Riesov are shaped by cold and by the sea in the way that cold and sea-adjacent people are shaped: practically, conservatively, with a specific pride in competence that is not interested in praise from people who have not demonstrated they can do the same thing. Visitors from the interior — Lond farmers, Ganoss upland clanspeople, occasional southerners who have made the Skeld crossing — find Riesov communities hospitable in a functional sense and opaque in almost every other. Information is available if you know what to ask for. Everything else is presumed to be your own business.

The Riesov standard for competence is, specifically, nautical and cold-weather competence, because those are the competencies that matter for survival and commerce here. A southern merchant who is commercially sophisticated but has no feel for the coast will find the Riesov patient with his inexperience and firm about the terms of what he can or cannot expect them to do about it. He will receive good service and will not be consulted about operational decisions. This is not condescension. It is the same logic that a Sond river pilot applies to a passenger: you paid for the boat, not the judgment.

Families in Riesov are organized around the vessel as often as around the household. A family with a ship has a productive unit that is legible, assessed, and respected in proportion to the ship's range and the family's record with it. A family without a ship is not poor — the fishing and farming communities on the plain are not marginal — but they are not in the central conversation. The Strendal merchant families who own large vessels and maintain the coastal network are the governing class in practice if not always in theory, and the governance they exercise is commercial in character: route rights, berth rights, cargo priority, the seasonal schedules that determine when which goods move on which ships.

The Riesov fishing tradition is the cultural substrate beneath the commercial surface. Most Riesov families with ships began as fishing families. The knowledge of the coast and the cold water that makes a Riesov sailor competent in coastal trade was accumulated first in the context of feeding people, before anyone had anything to sell to a southern merchant. The fishery is still active and still significant — the cold water fisheries of the northeastern coast are productive in a way that warmer coastal water is not, and Riesov salt fish is traded throughout the north and as far south as the Caeras valley in good supply years. The fishing fleet and the merchant fleet are not the same vessels or the same people, but they share knowledge, family, and harbor, and the line between them is crossed frequently enough that neither side of it considers the other separate.

## Agriculture

The Riesov plain is cold and produces accordingly. The growing season runs perhaps twelve weeks reliably, more in a warm year, less in a bad one, and the margin for error is narrow enough that Riesov agriculture is organized around storage and diversification rather than around optimizing any single crop.

Rye dominates where grain is grown at all — it will germinate in soil temperatures that other grains will not, and it tolerates the Skeld's shadow in ways that more demanding varieties cannot. The river mouth farmlands produce reasonable yields in good years; the ground away from the rivers is not primarily arable and is used for cold-tolerant root vegetables and the coastal cattle that graze the salt marsh edges and are sold as a secondary product to the passing trade.

The plain is "fairly fertile" in the specific sense that it supports the Riesov population without requiring constant import, which in this climate and on this terrain is not a small achievement. It does not produce the surplus of Lond or the varied plenty of the Sond bottomlands. What the plain does not provide, the sea does, and this balance — soil producing carbohydrate, sea producing protein and fat — is so deep in Riesov practice that the two food economies are organized as a single system. Riesov communities track the fishing calendar and the harvest calendar together, and the decisions about labor allocation between them are not made in isolation.

## History

Riesov and Ganun have not always been rivals in the current organized sense. The structural competition — coast versus river, sea route versus river route — is inherent in the geography, but for much of the north's early history both regions were too underdeveloped commercially to contest the same market. The raiding period brought them into conflict in a different register: Riesov ships, when the north's coastal culture was organized around raiding rather than trading, were part of the same raiding complex that Bay of Lol and Ganun's coastal communities participated in. The Riesov did not raid with particular distinction or particular infamy relative to their peers. They were northern coastal people doing what northern coastal people did in that period.

The transition to trade changed the calculation. As Ondrath developed into a functioning market and the Sond route to Mittolo became regular, Ganun's commercial position solidified faster than Riesov's, because the river route was already in use and the infrastructure — the boats, the portage arrangements, the southern relationships — was already paid for. Strendal developed its commercial identity later, and for a generation or two operated partly as a secondary market feeding into Ondrath's network. Riesov merchants of that period are not prominent in the Ondrath records; they are there, but they are buying and selling on Ganun's terms.

The moment when the rivalry formalized — when Riesov decided it was not a secondary participant in Ganun's commercial network but a competitor with its own network — does not correspond to a single event in either side's records. It corresponds to the development of the coastal distribution system: when Strendal began providing sea service to the northeastern coastal communities as a primary carrier rather than routing their goods through Ondrath, Ganun lost a portion of the northern coastal market it had assumed was part of its natural territory. The assumption had never been tested because the competition hadn't existed. When it existed, the assumption turned out to be wrong.

What followed was the rivalry as it now operates: commercial competition, periodic pass-politics, mutual cultivation of the inland regions' goodwill, and a persistent background contempt that both sides have maintained long enough that it has become, in its way, a form of acknowledgment. You do not sustain a rivalry this long with a competitor who does not matter.

## Language

The Riesov dialect of Northern Mittoli is conservative in its core grammar and unusual in its nautical vocabulary — a profile different from Ganun's, which is conservative in its nautical terms (having developed them in isolation) and relatively open to southern loan words in commercial terminology. Riesov has the opposite distribution: commercial vocabulary absorbs terms from Ganun, from Bay of Lol traders, and occasionally from southern merchants, while the maritime vocabulary is closed, archaic, and resists standardization.

The Academy has noted that certain Riesov terms for sea conditions, currents, and ice behavior have no cognates in any other Northern Mittoli variety and do not reconstruct from the Proto-Western base. The substrate layer in the maritime vocabulary is older than the Northern Mittoli settlement of the coast, suggesting that whoever was here before used the same coast and left their names for what it does in the mouths of the people who came after. The Riesov acknowledge this when asked and do not find it interesting. The names are the correct names. The fact that they are old is the proof of that, not the problem with it.

What makes the Riesov dialect immediately recognizable to a northern ear is the compression under cold — sentences already short in the Northern Mittoli standard get shorter still, articles drop, the question form loses its rising inflection and becomes declarative with an expectation of answer. "You came through the southern pass" is how a Riesov speaker asks whether you came through the southern pass. The Bay of Lol communities do something similar, which has been used as evidence by Academy linguists that both coasts were settled from the same population at approximately the same period. The Riesov and Bay of Lol communities, when this theory is presented to them, agree that it is probably correct and show no particular interest in the implications, having managed their relationship without the Academy's philological framework for as long as they have been on their respective coasts.
