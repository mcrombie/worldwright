# Amod

Amod is the country south of the Lotharn Mountains, lying in the long belt of foothills where the old forested range descends into warmer, more open country. It is not a mountain region in the Lotharn sense: the ridges are lower, the valleys wider, the roads more forgiving, and the sky less often hidden by trees. It is also not Pyros, though Pyros lies close enough to shape it. Amod is the slope between them, the place where mountain water, pass trade, old stone, and southern heat meet.

The name *Amod* is used by outsiders for the country as a whole. Amodians use it too, but with the usual foothill qualification that the whole country is less real than the valley, town, terrace, road, or household one is actually standing in. A person from Amod will call themselves Amodian when speaking to foreigners and then immediately become more specific when speaking to anyone who might understand the distinction.

## Geography

Amod runs along the southern foot of the Lotharn in an uneven crescent. To the north, the land rises into the forested Lotharn slopes. To the south and southeast, the foothills descend gradually into the lake country of Elagos — the transition from terraced hillside to lake-margin flat is one of the great visual moments of northeastern Azhora, visible from the upper Amodian passes as a shining interruption in the distance. To the west, the country opens toward the Mittoli river plains and eventually Pyros. Its western end is greener and more deeply wooded, catching rain that spills around the mountain shoulders. Its eastern end is drier, stonier, and more open, with pale grasses between terrace walls and villages built around springs.

The land is made of descending ridges. Each ridge throws a valley southward; each valley carries a stream out of the Lotharn; each stream has been made to work. Amodian fields are terraced wherever the slope allows it and irrigated wherever the stream can be persuaded. The terraces are the country's most visible human mark: long stone ribs following contour lines across the hills, patched and rebuilt by the same families for generations.

The upper foothills are chestnut, oak, beech, and walnut country. Lower down, the slopes carry orchards, vines, pulses, grazing goats, and the small hardy grains that tolerate thin soil and uncertain rain. Amod is not one of Azhora's great bread regions, but it is rarely hungry. It has too many elevations, exposures, and microclimates for a single bad season to ruin everything at once.

The rivers are small but reliable. They come cold from the Lotharn, gather warmth on the south-facing slopes, and drain in two directions depending on the valley: the western rivers run toward the Mittoli plain network, the eastern and southern rivers feed into the lake system of Elagos. A watershed divide runs through Amod without anyone having formally agreed where it is, which has generated its own category of boundary dispute. This makes water rights the central fact of Amodian law. Land can be inherited, bought, married into, or abandoned. Water must be remembered correctly.

## Climate

Amod has four seasons, but the mountains bend them. Spring starts at the lower terraces while snowmelt is still feeding the upper streams. Summer is hot in the lower country and mild in the upper villages, with afternoon winds that fall from the Lotharn after sunset. Autumn is the Amodian season: dry air, clear ridgelines, chestnut smoke, grape harvest, and the great movement of animals down from the high grazing.

Winter is not harsh by northern standards, but it is complicated. Cold air drains through the valleys at night, frost gathers in pockets, and a village halfway up a slope may be warmer than one below it. Amodian farmers read frost the way sailors read current: by direction, shape, and habit rather than by a number on a calendar.

## Settlements

Amodian towns are built on shoulders of land above valley floors, high enough to avoid flood and low enough to reach water without wasting half a day. They are compact, stone-built, and vertically organized: storage and animals below, households above, drying rooms and work lofts under steep roofs. Streets tend to follow contour rather than compass direction, curving around the hill in ways that confuse lowland visitors and make complete sense to anyone carrying a load.

The capital, if Amod can be said to have one, is **Mavren**, a terraced town at the meeting of three valleys near the central passes. Mavren is where the valley councils meet, where pass tolls are reconciled, where outside envoys are received, and where disputes large enough to involve more than one watershed are argued until everyone is too tired to continue being elegant. Amodians do not describe Mavren as ruling the country. They describe it as "where the ledgers meet."

Other towns matter in their own domains:

**Kelmod** guards the western road and is known for timber, chestnuts, and mule breeding.

**Sareth-am-Vel** sits above the central south road and controls the best descent from the Lotharn pass inns into Amod.

**Ostel** is the eastern dry-slope town, famous for stonecutters and hard white wine that outsiders pretend to enjoy before learning to actually enjoy it.

**Tir Amel** is less a town than a chain of spring villages sharing one water court. Its legal records are among the most carefully maintained in the country, because a missed spring-right clause can start a feud that outlives the scribe.

## Government

Amod is a country without a single crown. Its governing structure is the **Terrace Compact**, a federation of valley councils, water courts, and pass authorities that has existed in recognizable form for at least eight centuries. The Compact is not democratic in the modern philosophical sense, but it is participatory in the practical Amodian sense: anyone whose field, spring, mill, herd, road, or terrace wall is affected by a decision has standing to be heard, and failing to hear them makes the decision unenforceable in practice.

The fundamental unit of governance is the **water court**. Every stream system has one. It records rights of diversion, mill time, flood repairs, terrace obligations, and the order in which fields receive water during shortage. The court's memory is partly written and partly embodied in specialists called **measure-keepers**, who know the channels, gates, and seasonal behavior of the stream by inspection. A measure-keeper's testimony can override a written record if the record describes a watercourse that no longer behaves as written.

Pass tolls are administered separately by road houses and pass families, many of whom have kin in the Lotharn valleys above them. This gives Amod unusual leverage over trade moving south out of the mountains. The Lotharn communities control the heights; Amod controls the descent, the mule changes, the lower warehouses, and the first markets where mountain goods become lowland goods.

## Economy

Amod sells what slopes produce well: chestnuts, dried fruit, goat cheese, wool, timber, charcoal, wine, medicinal herbs from the upper ridges, and stone worked finely enough to be used in door lintels and irrigation gates throughout the south. Its iron is limited compared with the central Lotharn mines, but Amodian smiths are known for tools rather than weapons: pruning hooks, channel knives, hinge-work, mill fittings, and the small precise metal pieces that keep water gates honest.

The country is also a passage economy. Goods from the Lotharn and Mittoli interior come down through Amod before turning toward East Pyros, the Plains, or the Iberos routes. Amodians are not grand merchants in the Nyrossi style. They are handlers, measurers, insurers, mule-breeders, warehouse keepers, road cooks, and toll accountants. Their genius is not making a trade route glamorous. Their genius is making it arrive with fewer losses than expected.

This has made them quietly wealthy. Not visibly wealthy; Amodian architecture spends money on walls, drains, roofs, and springhouses before it spends money on display. But the ledgers are good, the granaries are rarely empty, and an Amodian village that looks plain from the road may have three generations of credit extended across half the eastern trade network.

## Culture

Amodian culture is built around maintenance. Terraces fail if they are not repaired. Channels clog if they are not cleared. Roads wash out if no one watches the culverts. Orchards require pruning before they reward anyone. This produces a people who are suspicious of brilliance when it has no maintenance plan attached.

The central virtue is **keeping grade**: holding the correct slope, neither too steep nor too flat. Literally, this refers to irrigation channels and roads. Socially, it means proportion, continuity, and the refusal to let urgency ruin a system that must last after the urgent people have gone home. An Amodian compliment that translates poorly is "they hold grade in weather." It means someone can be trusted when conditions change.

Hospitality is practical and exact. A guest is fed, dried, placed near heat, and asked where they are going only after the first needs are met. A guest who volunteers to help with an obvious task without being asked is remembered well. A guest who compliments the view while standing next to a broken retaining wall has misunderstood the situation.

## Religion and Custom

Amod shares religious vocabulary with both the Lotharn valleys and Pyros, but its practice is its own. Springs, culverts, old terrace walls, and pass stones receive small offerings at seasonal transitions. These are not usually grand ceremonies. A sprig of herb in a water gate, the first chestnuts left at a spring, a cup of wine poured on a repaired wall: the customs are modest because the relationship is ongoing rather than theatrical.

The dead are buried above the villages, never below them. This is partly practical — the lower terraces are too valuable — and partly conceptual. Ancestors should be uphill. They look down the watercourse. They see what is being neglected.

## Language

Amodian speech is a Mittoli-derived foothill dialect with heavy Lotharn substrate in place names, water vocabulary, and terrace terms. Pyrosi influence is strongest in the south and east, especially in agricultural and hospitality vocabulary. An Amodian merchant can usually make themselves understood in East Pyros and in the Lotharn pass communities, though both sides will hear the slope in the speech immediately.

The dialect preserves a set of water-measure words that Standard Mittoli lacks. These words describe not only quantity but behavior: water that cuts, water that seeps, water that can be trusted overnight, water that looks abundant but will vanish into gravel before reaching the lower fields. Lowland administrators translate all of these as "flow" and then wonder why Amodian contracts become long.

Several scholars have argued that the name *Amod* is not Mittoli. Proposed derivations from Mittoli roots are unconvincing, and Pyrosi explanations fare no better. The oldest Lotharn substrate lists include related forms meaning "south slope" or "held water," but the evidence is fragmentary. Amodians have a simpler answer: Amod is what the country is called because that is what it was called when their grandparents learned where the roads went.

## Relations

With the Lotharn, Amod has kinship, trade, and irritation. Mountain communities think Amodians are lowlanders who talk too much about walls. Amodians think mountain communities are admirable people who would forget half their income if no one downhill wrote it down. The relationship works because both claims are partly true.

With Elagos, Amod has its most commercially consequential relationship. The lake country below is the primary destination for Amodian exports — chestnuts, wine, stonework, dried fruit, and the pass-refined goods that come down through Mavren and reach the Elagosi lake markets by the southern river routes. The Elagosi lake towns are large enough to absorb Amodian production volumes that no other regional market can, and the return trade — lake fish, lowland grain in poor years, Elagosi metalwork — fills gaps that the hills cannot fill from their own resources. Amod and Elagos have been commercially interlocked long enough that the ledgers in Mavren contain Elagosi-derived accounting forms that arrived through trade and stayed.

Ambron is a political fact that Amod has navigated in most generations rather than resolved. The empire that controls Elagos wants the Lotharn passes — the routes through Amod connect the lake system to the Mittoli interior and to the northern mountains, and whoever holds Elagos finds Amod's cooperation worth purchasing or, periodically, compelling. In the strong imperial periods Amod has paid tribute to Ambron and received the usual imperial package in return: road maintenance, trade protection, military presence that functions as both security and occupation depending on the moment. The Terrace Compact's institutional depth makes Amod harder to directly administer than the Moros plain or even Drent — the water courts, measure-keepers, and pass families all have legitimate authority that Ambronite prefects cannot simply override without breaking the systems they need to function. Ambron has learned this in practice more than once. The characteristic resolution is a negotiated tribute arrangement that leaves the Compact nominally intact, which suits both parties well enough to persist across contraction cycles. Amod does not keep two sets of legal forms the way Drent does; the Terrace Compact is too old and too embedded to be replaced. It simply continues, with or without an Ambronite representative in Mavren, and the presence or absence of that representative is noted and adjusted for.

With Pyros, to the west, Amod maintains older commercial ties that predate the Elagosi connections. The Pyrosi admire Amodian hospitality and find Amodian councils exhausting. Amodians admire Pyrosi formality and find Pyrosi latent certainty difficult to invoice. Trade between them is real but slower than the Elagosi connection — overland through the Mittoli plain country, with the usual friction of moving goods between highland and lowland economies without a convenient lake network to do it on.

With the Plains peoples, Amod's relationship is seasonal. Herding groups use the lower foothill grazing in certain years, and the older agreements governing that access are maintained through Amodian water courts and Grassic oral law in parallel. The two systems do not translate cleanly, but they have coexisted for long enough that both sides know which parts must not be simplified.

## Amodian Saying

*A wall remembers every winter.*

Said when repairing something before it fails; said when refusing a convenient compromise; said, with particular dryness, to young officials from outside who explain that this year's emergency requires ignoring old maintenance obligations.
