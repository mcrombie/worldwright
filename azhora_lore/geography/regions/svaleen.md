# The Svaleen Peninsula

The Svaleen Peninsula is the largest southeastern extension of Azhora, a finger of land that pushes into the Iberos Sea and separates its southern reach from the open ocean to the east. On Azhoran maps made in the Mittoli heartland, the peninsula appears at the edge of confident cartography — marked as *Suval* in the mainland rendering of the name, described sometimes as an island when the mapmaker has never been there and sometimes as a peninsula when they have. The people who live there call it Svaleen and do not consider the distinction remotely ambiguous.

It is a peninsula. It is attached to the continent. The confusion arises because the neck of land connecting it to the main Azhoran body is narrow enough, and mountainous enough, that crossing it is a significant undertaking — easier in most seasons to arrive by sea than by land. This isolation has given the Svaleen a character more maritime than continental, and a certain mild contempt for people who show up having crossed the mountains and act like they've accomplished something unusual.

---

## Geography

The Svaleen Peninsula is shaped roughly like a blade angled toward the southeast. Its western coast faces the Iberos Sea — warm, sheltered, dotted with good harbors. Its eastern coast faces open water; the current there is stronger and the weather less predictable, and the fishing settlements along the east coast have a harder character than the western cities. The inland is hilly and arid in a way that Mediterranean climates produce reliably: pale limestone ridges, thin soil, scrub and aromatic plants, occasional cultivated terraces where the slope and the water permit it.

The southern interior of the peninsula contains the only substantial lake in the region, fed by aquifer springs rather than seasonal rainfall, which makes it unusual in the way that water independence is always unusual in arid country. A city sits on its shore. The lake does not run dry.

The climate varies with position. The western coast is classic Mediterranean: warm, dry summers, mild winters, rain arriving in season from the sea. The eastern coast is exposed to storms that the western cities never see. The inland hills are hotter in summer and colder at night than either coast suggests. The southern lake country is its own microclimate — cooler, with morning mist off the water that the rest of the peninsula does not experience.

## The Svaleen Confederation

The Svaleen cities are organized under an arrangement called the Confederation, which is less a government than a mutual agreement not to fight each other when trade would be more profitable. It has existed in some form for at least four centuries, has been revised substantially seven times, and has been on the verge of collapse continuously throughout its history without ever actually collapsing. This has led to a body of Svaleen political philosophy about the productive tension between competition and cooperation that mainland scholars find either sophisticated or exhausting depending on their patience.

The Confederation does three things with reasonable consistency: it maintains shared lighthouses and navigation markers throughout the peninsula's coastal waters, it operates a common system of weights and measures for trade between member cities, and it convenes a meeting twice yearly called the Conclave at which delegates from each city argue about everything else. The Conclave has no binding authority except on lighthouse maintenance and weights. This is either a fatal flaw or the entire point, depending on who you ask.

There are smaller towns and fishing settlements throughout the peninsula that participate in the Confederation as lesser members — they use the weights, maintain the lighthouse allocations assigned to them, and attend the Conclave when they have a specific grievance, which is not always. The three major cities are the ones that matter for the Confederation's actual balance of power.

## The Cities

### Solis

Solis is the largest city on the Svaleen Peninsula and has been for as long as anyone can reliably document. It sits on the west coast at the point where the peninsula widens slightly — a good harbor, protected by a natural headland to the north, with a flat coastal shelf behind it wide enough to build on comfortably. The city has used this space thoroughly: Solis is dense, layered, built over itself across centuries in the way that cities do when they run out of easy ground but do not stop growing.

The character of Solis is civic. It is a city of plazas and institutions, of public argument and formal debate, of government that takes itself seriously enough to construct elaborate buildings for it. The Conclave sessions held in Solis — they rotate — are the most attended and the most formally structured. Solis does not consider this proof that it takes the Confederation more seriously than the other cities do. Solis considers this proof that it is better at it.

The city's wealth comes from trade, like most of the peninsula, but Solis has historically been better than its neighbors at turning trade advantage into institutional permanence — into archives, academies, public works. There is a library in Solis that is old enough to contain documents in languages that no one in the city can currently read. This fact is considered a mark of distinction rather than a problem.

Solis worships Laeron, a sun-associated deity of civic order and clear light — not a god of war or commerce specifically but a god of things being what they appear to be, of agreements being kept, of the day being legible. The cult is old enough that its theology has grown complicated in the way of all old theologies, and the Laeron priesthood has developed a reputation for producing very good jurists.

### Enebreum

Twenty miles south of Solis on the same western coast, Enebreum has been Solis's rival since before either city was large enough to make the rivalry interesting. Its harbor is somewhat smaller and its hinterland somewhat poorer, but it is older than Solis by a margin that its citizens do not hesitate to mention. The oldest foundations visible in Enebreum predate anything in Solis by at least two centuries, and in certain districts they predate anything the current inhabitants can explain — foundations cut from stone that was neither quarried nor moved using any technique current Svaleen builders use, settled several feet below the current ground level by the slow accumulation of what time does to cities.

Enebreum is sharper in character than Solis — more wary, more aware of angles, more inclined to consider what a situation costs before considering what it offers. Its architecture is less publicly grand and more functionally dense: workshops and warehouses and counting-houses in the lower city, residential quarters climbing the hill behind in tight terraces, the temples on the ridge rather than in the center of things, watching rather than presiding.

The rivalry with Solis is conducted on several fronts simultaneously. There are ongoing disputes about the Confederation's lighthouse maintenance allocations, which Enebreum believes unfairly favor northern sea lanes. There are historical claims and counter-claims about which city first settled the fishing grounds off the western coast. There is a trade dispute about wool tariffs that has been technically unresolved for sixty years because resolving it would require both cities to acknowledge the terms on which the previous dispute was settled, and those terms make both cities look bad.

None of this prevents Enebreum and Solis from doing extensive business with each other. The rivalry is genuine and the trade is genuine and the two coexist without difficulty, because both cities are run by people who understand that these are separate categories.

Enebreum's religion is older than Solis's and less organized — a set of practices and prohibitions rather than a coherent theology, associated with a figure the texts call the Grey Warden, who is neither a god of the dead nor a god of the sea but something between them, associated with thresholds and the moment before decisions are made. The Solis Laeron priesthood considers this theology primitive. The Enebreum response to this opinion has historically been to produce competent sailors while the Laeron priests produce competent jurists, and to observe that both have their uses.

### Imlamdris

Inland, on the southern lake that has no common Azhoran name but that the Svaleen call the Stillwater, sits Imlamdris: the oldest continuously occupied city on the peninsula and the one that most completely ignores the Confederation when it finds the Confederation inconvenient.

Imlamdris is not a maritime city. It has no harbor. Its trade moves by road through the hill passes, which is slower and harder than the coastal routes and has given the city a different relationship to commerce: less of it, more controlled, conducted on the city's terms rather than the market's. The Stillwater provides water independence that neither coastal city can match — the spring-fed lake does not dry up in drought years, and Imlamdris has outlasted regional droughts that damaged the coastal cities' agricultural hinterlands by simply not being dependent on the same rainfall.

The city's character is quiet in the way of places that have learned not to need to prove anything. Its streets are wider than Solis's, its building less frantic. The lake is visible from most of the upper city, a flat grey-green surface in the morning mist, blue in the afternoons, black at night. The Imlamdrissi relationship with the Stillwater is not merely practical — it is architectural and theological. The city faces the lake more completely than it faces any road or market. The great hall of the Stillwater Temple, which is not primarily a temple but serves several functions, opens on its lakeside face and is closed on its landward side. Visitors arriving overland enter from the back.

The Stillwater Temple maintains the peninsula's most significant archive of astronomical records — centuries of sky observation recorded by a tradition that predates the current temple structure. Why an inland lake city became the peninsula's center of astronomical scholarship is one of those questions that the Imlamdrissi find obvious (you can see the stars clearly in the inland hills, far from the coastal haze) and that coastal visitors find insufficiently explained.

Imlamdris participates in the Conclave inconsistently. It attends reliably when the agenda concerns the inland roads — the mountain passes, the trade routes through the interior — and sends its apologies when the agenda is primarily about lighthouse maintenance.

## Common Threads

The Svaleen are not a single culture, but they are recognizably from the same place in the way that people who share geography and trade and two centuries of grudging cooperation eventually resemble each other even when they are trying not to. They are pragmatic about religion in a way that outsiders sometimes read as skeptical but that is more precisely described as practical — the gods are treated as real and as having their domains and as being worth the proper attention in those domains, without the expectation that theological consistency across all three cities is either achievable or particularly important.

The Confederation calendar and the shared weights and measures have produced a common commercial vocabulary across the three cities that outsiders doing business on the peninsula quickly adopt. Svaleen merchants trading in Nyross or Zoth are recognizable partly by accent and partly by the way they handle numbers — a Svaleen commercial transaction moves through its steps in a particular order that reflects the Confederation's standard trading protocols, which have been refined across four centuries of inter-city commerce into something close to a ritual.

To Azhorans from the mainland interior, the Svaleen generally — all three cities — present as confident, somewhat clannish, and mildly amused by anyone who tries to explain how trade works.

## In Azhoran Context

The peninsula is known on the mainland primarily through its olive oil, its wine, its particular grades of dried fish, and its role in Iberos Sea commerce. Solis maintains active trade relationships with Nyross and Zoth. Enebreum trades more directly with the Moroshé coast through overland connections to East Pyros. Imlamdris is the least known of the three in mainland contexts — its goods arrive by road and its citizens travel less.

What lies east of the peninsula — across the open eastern ocean — is a question the Svaleen have thought about in the way that coastal people think about open water: as a practical matter of what's been reported, not a philosophical one about what might be there. The reports are inconsistent. The eastern coast fishing communities have seen distant landmasses in unusual weather conditions, or say they have. The coastal cities' archives contain several such accounts without drawing conclusions from them. The question of what's east is less interesting to the Svaleen than the question of what's west, where their actual trade and politics exist.

Elod, the Elodi city at the peninsula's southern tip, is a distinct community with distinct institutions. The Elodi are Confederation members in the narrowest technical sense — they observe the weights and measures, they maintain their lighthouse assignment — but they do not attend the Conclave and have not been expected to for approximately a century. The arrangement is politely unspoken. The Svaleen have learned, over several generations, that the Elodi are reliable neighbors and intensely private ones, and that these two facts are probably connected.
