# Nescor

The Oveth rises in Nescor. This is the first fact of the relationship between Nescor and Ovesos, and neither side has found a way to make it politically irrelevant. The headwaters of the river that feeds the Sorten, drives the mills, and underwrites Ovesian agricultural civilization emerge in the upland country that Nescor controls. The Oveth does not emerge as a single spring; it gathers from snowmelt, from upland rain, from a dozen contributing streams across the highland basin that constitutes Nescoran territory. But it exits Nescoran ground as a river, and the people downstream depend on what comes through.

Nescor is small. By population, by agricultural output, by any measure the Branch Court uses when weighing the five inner-branch countries against one another, it is the least of them. It has lost three wars with Ovesos. It has survived all three. The measure by which it is not small is the one the Branch Court lacks a standard for: Nescoran musicians are known from the Ibenale corridor to the Minoran court receptions, and the ones who have heard them tend to reach for a vocabulary that exceeds what the occasion seems to call for.

The Telemon bands that Ovesos has hired for its border conflicts — practical, unsentimental people — call the upland folk *Thelom*, their own rendering. The Nescorans are aware of this and use it when speaking with Telemon without correction.

## Geography

The headwater basin of the Oveth is a highland bowl north of the Sorten, elevated several hundred feet above Ovesos's valley floor, colder by season and sharper by terrain. Several ridge systems converge here, and the drainage from their eastern and southern slopes gathers in the basin and exits as the Oveth's main channel. The basin supports pasture, short-season grain farming on the sheltered lower slopes, and the highland forest that provides the specific hardwood from which Nescoran instrument makers work.

The terrain does not invite the intensive bottomland farming the Sorten supports. What it has is altitude, water control, and the acoustic environment of narrow highland valleys where sound carries differently than on the open plain — traveling further, reflecting from rock faces, developing resonances that a voice or an instrument can be tuned to exploit.

The lower edge of Nescoran territory runs into the contested zone. The Middle Reach — the four miles of bottomland where the Oveth bends before entering the Sorten — is where Nescoran territory, by one reading of the treaty documents, extends. Above the Middle Reach, the country is unambiguously Nescor's. Below it, the Sorten is unambiguously Ovesos's. The bend is what the Branch Court has been deliberating for eleven years.

The upper Oveth above the contested zone holds the most productive mill sites on the river. The gradient there is stronger than anywhere downstream, and the mills that exploit it produce more than the Sorten's slower water can drive. Nescor controls these mills. This is worth more than the Middle Reach bottomland in gross annual output, which the Ovesian Water Council knows and does not find comforting.

## Music

Nescoran music is not the only music in the inner-branch country. Every polity has its festival traditions, its communal songs, its professional entertainers. What distinguishes Nescor is the depth of the tradition and the specific technique — developed over generations in the upland valleys — that Nescoran musicians carry and that lowland musicians cannot replicate without years of sustained immersion.

The technique has two parts that outsiders describe separately and Nescorans consider one thing. The first is *vel-thren-oss* — "the valley's carrying" — a vocal method that uses the resonant properties of highland terrain as a shaping environment. Nescoran singers learn to hear the valley's response and to tune their voice into relationship with it: not competing with the echo but working with the delay, the reflection, the specific pitch-reinforcement that rock faces and confined spaces provide. A trained Nescoran singer performing in a Minoran banquet hall is doing something technically different from the same singer in the highland basin, and both performances are different from what a Minoran singer produces in either setting. The Minorans can hear the difference. Explaining it requires a vocabulary Minoran musical theory does not have.

The second part is the *kethran* — a bowed instrument of two strings, made from the highland hardwood that grows only in the upper Oveth's forest band, with a sound chamber cut and dried according to methods the instrument-making families maintain within their own households. The *kethran* is not tuned to a standard pitch. Each is tuned to the player, in a process of adjustment that takes months when a new player takes up an instrument and years when a player commissions their own. No two *kethran* produce exactly the same sound, which makes ensemble playing between multiple instruments demanding and the result, when it works, unlike anything produced by instruments tuned to a common standard.

Minoran court musicians who have attempted the *kethran* describe it as uncooperative. Nescoran players describe it as requiring a different kind of listening — not to the note you are producing but to the note the instrument wants to produce, which is not the same thing. Several prominent Minoran court composers have concluded that writing for Nescoran musicians rather than for the *kethran* specifically is the only practical approach, since the instrument and the player cannot be separated in a way that allows systematic notation.

Nescoran musicians travel. They always have — the upland pastoral economy has a lean season when there is less to do and more reason to leave, and the musical tradition converts readily to income and hospitality in the lowland markets. A Nescoran musician in Gala can work. A Nescoran musician in Minora can work well. A Nescoran musician at the First Divider's seasonal reception is not unusual and is paid accordingly.

This produces a class of Nescorans who have seen more of the inner-branch system than anyone else in their polity — who have sat in Ovesian feasts and Minoran courts and Galan market squares and been treated as guests of honor in places where the political relationship with Nescor is adversarial. The Ovesian Water Council hires Nescoran musicians for the Harvest Close. This is not discussed in the same session as the boundary dispute. Both things happen at their appropriate moment in the annual cycle. Neither side raises the incongruity.

Those who travel and those who stay are not in formal tension, but the distinction is recognized. The ones who remain maintain the tradition at its source — in the highland valleys whose acoustic character shapes what the music is. The ones who travel carry the tradition into contexts where it will never be heard in its native environment. What they carry is real. The Nescorans who have not left are not certain it is the complete thing.

## Agriculture and Economy

Nescor feeds itself from short-season grain on the sheltered slopes, upland cattle, and the compact mountain sheep the highland terrain produces. In a poor grain year — which is more common at this elevation than in the Sorten — Nescor buys surplus from Ovesos under arrangements both sides find awkward given the boundary dispute. The highland cattle and sheep reach the downstream markets reliably; Minoran meat buyers know Nescoran highland beef and pay for the specific quality that upland pasture produces.

The mills are the economic backbone. The upper Oveth's gradient drives operations that the Sorten cannot match, and Nescoran milled grain and fulled cloth carry a quality premium in the downstream markets. The mill-owning families are the economic elite; their interest in the boundary dispute is straightforwardly material — the lower mill sites in the Middle Reach were productive before the disputed treaty transferred them to Ovesian control, and recovering them is not an abstract principle.

The instrument-making families occupy their own niche: small households maintaining specific knowledge of highland hardwood selection, drying, and finishing. An exported *kethran* without a trained Nescoran player is of limited use to the buyer, which keeps the export market small and the price high.

## The Boundary Dispute — Nescoran Reading

The Middle Reach is, from Nescor's perspective, a matter of treaty language. The first treaty gave the stretch to Ovesos under conditions the second treaty modified. The modification is in the document. Ovesos reads it as clarification; Nescor reads it as what it says. The expert opinion agreed the language was ambiguous. Nescor's position is that ambiguity in a treaty Ovesos won a war to produce should favor the party who was not winning.

The Highland Council's deliberation on the dispute has, over eleven years of Branch Court proceedings, moved from optimism about a ruling to a working assumption that the ruling will come when Isoruous has reason to let it come. Maintaining good terms with the relevant Isor River Council faction is therefore understood as the primary diplomatic task. The musician households, who pass through Isoruous regularly and know its council families better than anyone else in Nescor does, are the principal source of intelligence on this question.

The water leverage is real and is not used. Nescor does not divert the Oveth. It does not threaten to. What it does is exist upstream, which requires no statement and produces no Branch Court record. The Ovesian Water Council's chronic anxiety about the headwaters is a feature of every session, and the Highland Council is aware of this. Neither side has found it productive to say any of this aloud.

## Political Structure

Nescor is governed by a **Highland Council** of leading families — the mill-owning lineages, the major pastoral households, and the senior musician families. The last category is unusual by inner-branch standards; no other polity gives musician households formal council standing. The Nescoran justification, when articulated, is practical: musicians are Nescor's most widely traveled citizens and the ones who know the most about the downstream political environment. The council listens to them because they are useful, which is a more durable basis for standing than prestige alone.

The council produces a **Speaker** — analogous to Caricas's Voice of the Council — who manages external affairs and Branch Court representation. Speakers are typically from mill families, whose material stakes in the boundary dispute are clearest, but convention has established that a Speaker consults the musician households before major diplomatic initiatives. This is not a formal requirement. It has become routine enough to function as one.

## Relations with Neighbors

**Ovesos** — Three wars. Two ambiguous treaties. Eleven years of Branch Court proceedings. Formal peace for the duration of all of it. Nescor sells grain to Ovesos in poor years and sends musicians to the Harvest Close every autumn. Ovesos controls the Middle Reach and its mill sites. Neither side is satisfied with this arrangement. Neither side has found a less costly alternative.

**Isoruous** — The passage arrangement at Vel-Oss. Nescoran mill output, wool, and highland goods travel on terms the Highland Council renegotiates periodically and finds consistently less favorable than warranted. The musician households maintain better informal relationships with both Isor River Council factions than the mill families do, for the simple reason that musicians travel through and have learned which households to cultivate. This intelligence informs Highland Council strategy in ways the mill families do not always acknowledge and could not replicate.

**Caricas** — Neighboring inner-branch polities without shared border. Nescor and Caricas have twice produced coordinated Branch Court submissions against Ovesos — once effectively, once not. The relationship is cooperative when Ovesian pressure gives both a reason to coordinate, and distant otherwise. Carican council members who have attended Nescoran performances describe the *kethran* as beautiful and unnerving, specifically in that order.

**Minora** — The Branch Court has ruled against Nescor on the Middle Reach for eleven years. Nescoran musicians are a fixture at Minoran court events. The First Divider's household keeps a Nescoran musician in permanent residence. These facts coexist without apparent tension on Minora's side. The Highland Council has noted all of them.

## Language

Highland Mittoli — the most phonologically distinct of the inner-branch variants, shaped by the acoustic environment of the upland basin. Consonant clusters that Ovesian Mittoli has simplified are preserved in Nescoran speech; the vowel system shows older upland dialect features that the Sorten communities lost through downstream contact. A Standard Mittoli speaker can follow Nescoran speech with attention; a Nescoran speaker produces Standard Mittoli without difficulty and with the slight adjustment of someone speaking a closely related language rather than their own.

*Vel-thren-oss* — the valley's carrying; the acoustic phenomenon of sound reinforced and shaped by upland terrain. Extended to the vocal technique Nescoran singers develop in relationship with it.

*Kethran* — the two-stringed bowed instrument, made from highland hardwood, tuned to the player rather than to a standard pitch. Not transferable between players without significant re-tuning, which changes the instrument.

*Osk-thren* — headwaters tenure; the Nescoran framing of their upstream position as a prior water claim that supersedes downstream allocations. The term appears in Branch Court submissions and is consistently found inapplicable. It continues to appear.

*Thelom* — the Telemon name for Nescor and its people, from the Telemon word for an upland or high reach. The Nescorans use it without correction when speaking with Telemon, as it would be strange to correct someone for using their own language. Ovesian historical records use *Thelom* throughout the accounts of the three wars, since the wars predate Ovesian familiarity with the self-name; "the Thelom wars" is historical Ovesian phrasing that Nescorans accept and do not prefer.

## Character

Nescor is small and knows it. The three wars were lost. The boundary dispute has not been resolved in Nescor's favor. The Branch Court produces rulings in proportion to Ovesos's ability to make them stick. The Highland Council is not innocent about any of this.

What Nescor has decided is that the measure it is ahead on is not the one the Branch Court counts. The musicians who sit at Minoran court receptions are not there because Nescor won a war. They are there because the music is what it is, and no alternative has produced anything comparable. This is not compensation for political weakness. It is a different kind of fact.

The highland communities — the herders, the mill families, the farmers of the sheltered slopes — do not spend much time thinking about the cultural reputation. They are aware of it the way people are aware of something their family is known for: not indifferent, but not defined by it from the inside. What defines them from the inside is the water. The Oveth rises here. Everything downstream uses what rises here. This is a fact that requires no statement, produces no Branch Court record, and is the first thing a Nescoran learns about the world they live in.
