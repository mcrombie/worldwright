# Caricas

The Carica River descends from the eastern upland shelf southeast of the main Lizeem transition, collecting drainage from the hill-ridges that form the inner-branch country's eastern limit. By the time it reaches the junction where it joins the Lizeem's upper channel within Isoruous's territory, it has passed through two distinct kinds of country: a fast, rocky upper section where the eastern plateau breaks into the inner drainage basin, and a slower, wooded middle valley where the river loses gradient and the banks thicken with the mixed riverine forest that is the Carica's characteristic landscape. This wooded middle valley — the Carica corridor — is the homeland of the *vel-caric*, the river fox.

The people who have lived in this valley for as long as the valley has been farmed call themselves Caricas, after the river. The Minoran Branch Court calls them Errath, rendering an older form of the river name through Caeras administrative script. The Caricas use Errath in formal proceedings and Caricas among themselves, without particular feeling in either direction; the name you use tells the other party which context you think you are in.

## Geography

The Carica rises on the eastern upland shelf — a broad elevated plateau east of the Lizeem's main drainage, rougher and less well-watered than the western inner-branch country. The upper Carica is a hill-country river: quick, cold, running over rock and gravel, prone to sudden rises after upland rains and quick drops when the rain passes. Below the upper section the river enters the middle corridor, where the terrain softens and the valley opens into the alternation of wooded bank and cultivable margin that defines most of what outsiders mean when they say Caricas.

The Carica corridor is not the same kind of valley as the Sorten. Where the Sorten is open, flat, and agricultural in character — bottomland managed for grain and orchard — the Carica corridor is narrower and more forested. Good farming ground exists in the valley margins and on the terraced slopes above the flood line, but the corridor's distinctive feature is its woodland: old-growth mixed forest along the immediate riverbanks, breaking into managed woodland and farmland above it. This is the fox's habitat. The Caricas have not cleared it.

The eastern edge of Caricas's territory rises again toward the upland shelf, where hill-country communities farm the elevated margins and where some mining activity — iron, some lead — draws on the geological character of the eastern slope. The western border with Ovesos runs through country that neither polity finds entirely satisfactory, for reasons with a documented history.

The climate is variable by inner-branch standards. The upper Carica drains upland rain, so the river rises unpredictably — more than the other inner-branch rivers, which are more reliably seasonal. A summer storm on the eastern plateau produces a flash rise in the Carica two days later. The valley communities have learned the signs and take them seriously.

## The Fox

The *vel-caric* — the river fox — is a small, semi-aquatic carnivore with a distinctive dark-tipped tail and the narrow, mobile face of a creature that lives in river margins. It is found reliably only in the Carica corridor and in small populations on two of the Carica's upper tributaries. Whether it once ranged more widely across the inner-branch river system is a question the Caricas consider settled and everyone else considers open.

The fox does not behave like most foxes. It hunts along the water's edge rather than in fields, taking small fish, crayfish, and the occasional water-bird. It uses the same territory as the Carica's otter population without competing with it — occupying a slightly different niche in the same habitat, using a narrower band of the bank. It is not domesticated and is not tameable in any sense the Caricas have found. When approached, it does not flee unless directly threatened. It watches.

The Carican word for this experience — *vel-caric-oss*, literally "the river fox's regard" — has extended into a broader usage meaning a particular kind of witnessing: the quality of being seen by something that does not need you. A *vel-caric-oss* on the morning of a significant decision, a birth, a death, or a contract is registered by the person who experienced it and noted in the household record. The accumulation of these records, maintained by specialist families across generations, constitutes a body of material that the Caricas treat as a form of evidence about what the river has been paying attention to.

The fox keepers — *vel-caric-hass*, "those who hold the fox's ground" — are the families responsible for maintaining the corridor. This means, in practice: keeping the bank woodland intact, managing the lower flood zone to ensure the foxes' habitat is not degraded by agricultural encroachment, and recording sightings according to the standardized notation that has been in continuous use for as long as the records exist. The fox keepers are not a priesthood. They have no ritual function that the Caricas recognize as distinct from their practical one. They keep the ground. The ground keeps the fox. The fox does what it does.

The protected corridor runs approximately six miles of the Carica's middle reach. Within this stretch, land use is managed by the fox keeper families under customary arrangement recognized by the Caricas Water Council. Agricultural activity occurs at the corridor's edges; the immediate riverbank is fox ground.

Three times in recorded Carican history, the *vel-caric* population has dropped to the point where sightings ceased for a full season. All three events are associated in Carican memory with subsequent disasters — two floods, one plague. Whether this is correlation or cause is the kind of question the Caricas find it unnecessary to resolve. The fox's absence is an event. Events have implications.

The Minoran Branch Court has received one petition arguing that the corridor's customary status constitutes a prior water-right claim superior to Ovesian agricultural expansion into the western border zone. The Branch Court found the argument creative and without applicable precedent and ruled accordingly. The Caricas submitted the ruling to their corridor records with the annotation: *vel-caric-oss vel-kael* — "the fox's regard, without elevation." Meaning: noted, without ceremony.

## Agriculture and Economy

Caricas feeds itself from a combination of valley-margin grain farming, terraced slope cultivation, upland pastoral activity, and the secondary economy of the corridor woodland. The valley margins produce barley and rye suited to the Carica's variable flood character — crops that recover better from irregular inundation than the wheat varieties favored in Ovesos. The terraced slopes, where water can be managed more precisely, produce vegetables and soft fruit. Orchards exist but are not the Carica's commercial identity; the valley lacks the Sorten's mineral-heavy soil conditions that give Ovesian apples their character.

The corridor woodland produces timber, coppiced wood products, charcoal, and harvested forest goods — fungi, gathered plants — that the Carica's ecology makes available and the Sorten does not. This is not a major export but a consistent one; the inner-branch markets take Carican timber and charcoal reliably.

Mining on the eastern upland edge — small-scale iron workings, some lead — provides raw material for a local metalworking tradition that produces agricultural tools and goods that travel to the inner-branch markets. The workings are not extensive enough to define Caricas economically the way the Sorten defines Ovesos, but they give the eastern upland communities an identity distinct from the valley farmers.

The fox corridor has an economic dimension the Caricas discuss carefully. The corridor's ecology produces riparian resources — specific medicinal plants found at the forest-water boundary, spawning-ground fish in the protected stretches — that are harvested under fox keeper supervision. This is not officially a commercial operation. The harvested material moves through private arrangement and appears in accounts as gifts, exchange, and consideration. The Branch Court has noted this pattern and not yet found a procedural frame for it.

## Political Structure

Caricas is governed by a council of valley communities — a **Water Council** in form, similar to Ovesos's, but with the fox keeper families as a distinct class whose standing in council deliberations is not defined by water-right seniority but by corridor tenure. A fox keeper family that has held its section of the corridor for six generations speaks in council in proportion to the corridor length it manages and the quality of its records, not in proportion to its irrigated acreage.

This dual structure — water-right seniority for the farming communities, corridor tenure for the fox keepers — produces regular friction, because the two systems use different metrics and different evidentiary standards. A water-right dispute follows the standard inner-branch genealogical-documentary practice. A corridor dispute is argued through the sighting records and the ecological assessments of the keeper families. When a dispute involves both — as the ongoing Ovesos boundary contest does — the council's deliberative process becomes complex enough that external parties describe it as slow. The Caricas describe it as complete.

There is no king. There is a **Voice of the Council** — a rotating position held by the senior family representative for a period of years, with responsibility for external correspondence and Branch Court representation. The Voice has no unilateral authority. It speaks. The council decides.

The fox keeper families hold council seats through their valley community membership, the same as farming families. In practice, when corridor matters arise, the keeper families speak with a coordination that non-keeper council members find notable. They have been meeting separately, before council sessions, for longer than the council's formal records go.

## Relations with Neighbors

**Ovesos** — The oldest active rivalry in the inner-branch system, though "active" requires qualification; the formal boundary has held for forty years. The original grievance predates living memory on both sides and is documented only in records each side claims the other has interpreted incorrectly. The current boundary places the western limit of Caricas's farming territory at a point that Ovesos finds more favorable than Caricas does. Ovesos has twice attempted Branch Court proceedings challenging the corridor's customary legal status — arguing that a water-right claim cannot be anchored in the habitat of an animal — and been ruled against both times. The boundary satisfaction has not improved. Both sides observe the formal peace with the specific attention of people waiting for the other side to provide grounds for renegotiation.

**Isoruous** — The passage arrangement through Vel-Oss. Carican goods — timber, charcoal, grain, metalwork — move through Isoruous's staging grounds on terms the Caricas find marginally acceptable and periodically renegotiate. The Isor River Council's factional instability is frustrating in the same way it is frustrating to Ovesos: the Voice of the Council prefers to know whom it is dealing with and whether the agreement will hold. The Downstream Faction's terms are worse for Caricas than the Upstream Faction's; Caricas maintains working relationships with both and tries not to appear to be choosing.

**Minora** — The Branch Court's consistent failure to find applicable precedent for corridor-related claims has made Carican legal strategy primarily defensive: protecting what is established rather than expanding it. The First Divider's office treats Carican petitions with somewhat more attention than it gives Ovesos's, because Carican submissions tend to be shorter and more focused. The Caricas have noted this and continued the practice.

**Nescor** — Upstream of Ovesos and geographically distant from Caricas; the two have no direct relationship. Nescor and Caricas have occasionally found themselves aligned in three-way Branch Court proceedings involving Ovesos, which has produced working communication without warmth. The Nescor-Caricas alignment that Ovesos has historically been concerned about has never produced decisive coordination, partly because Nescor and Caricas have no more reason to trust each other than they have reason to cooperate against Ovesos.

**Nethereum** — Separated from Caricas by Ovesos and the main Lizeem channel. No direct relationship. They appear in each other's political calculations only when the inner-branch alliances shift enough to matter.

## Language

Carican inner-branch Mittoli. Recognizable to any Standard Mittoli speaker; distinct from Ovesian Mittoli in vocabulary for woodland management, fox corridor practice, and the specific register used in sighting records.

*Caric* — the river fox. The word is old enough that its etymology is not recoverable from Standard Mittoli. Whether the river took the fox's name or the fox took the river's name is a question the Caricas treat as senseless; they are the same name because they are the same thing.

*Vel-caric-oss* — the fox's regard; the experience of being observed by the fox. Extended meaning: witnessing by something indifferent to the witness's intention.

*Vel-caric-hass* — the fox keepers; those who hold the corridor.

*Osk-vel-caric* — corridor tenure; the Carican analog to Ovesian *osk-milis* (water-right seniority), applied to keeper family standing rather than water allocation. The two terms appear in the same council discussions and operate on structurally different logics. This has not been resolved.

*Erreth* — the older local word from which the Minoran *Errath* is derived. It appears in older Carican records as a name for the entire valley — possibly predating the *vel-caric* tradition as the primary naming frame, possibly a survival of a different dialect layer. The Caricas do not use it in everyday speech. It appears in the formulaic opening of formal documents — "In the valley of Erreth" — before the document's actual content begins.

Carican formal legal speech combines the inner-branch genealogical-historical approach with a specific strand of ecological testimony — the sighting records, the corridor assessments — that has no equivalent in Standard Mittoli legal practice. Minoran Branch Court clerks have described Carican submissions as including "natural history material of arguable relevance." The Caricas include it because the argument is not arguable.

## Character

Caricas is a place that has decided something about what it is and is not negotiating on it.

The fox corridor is not the only thing the Caricas are, but it is the thing they will not trade away. The fox keeper families are not the dominant families in the council by wealth or acreage; several are modest farming households by Ovesian standards. What they hold is tenure, and what tenure gives them is continuity. A keeper family that has maintained the same stretch of corridor for eight generations is making a claim about what kind of place the Carica valley is — that it is the kind of place where the river fox lives, and where it is seen, and where what it sees is recorded. This claim is prior to the political structure and does not depend on it.

Outsiders from the other inner-branch countries tend to find Caricas difficult to read — not unfriendly, but organized around priorities that do not map cleanly onto water-right seniority, economic output, or military capacity. The Caricans are not contemptuous of those measures; they use them. They simply have another measure that they consider primary, and they are not particularly interested in explaining it to people who will find the explanation insufficient.

The fox itself, when encountered — and travelers through the corridor occasionally encounter it — is widely reported as disconcerting. Not threatening. Not remarkable in any visible sense. It looks at you with the attention of a creature that is not afraid of you and does not want anything from you and is not going to move on. This is the experience the Caricas have been recording for generations. They are not surprised that it disconcerts outsiders. They are not sure what to do with outsiders who are not disconcerted.
