# Flora of the Ibenwood: Ground Layer Species
## A Supplementary Catalogue

*The Caeras Valley Academy's tree catalogue covers the canopy. This supplement addresses everything beneath it. At time of compilation, 1,089 species have been documented to the Academy's satisfaction. Forest Mittoli naturalists who reviewed a draft estimated this represents approximately a third of what is actually present. The Academy disputes neither the estimate nor the implication.*

---

## Part I — Ferns and Fern-Allies

The Ibenwood is one of the richest fern environments on the continent. The combination of permanent moisture, deep shade, and whatever additional properties the forest possesses has produced a fern diversity that the Academy's Cryptogam Division has studied for four generations without running out of novelty.

**1. The Shadow Fern** (*vel-thren-kel*) — Black-stemmed, dark green fronds, the most common fern in the deep interior. Grows in carpets under closed canopy.

**2. The Bone Fern** (*sorthven-kel*) — Pale white stipe, fronds that bleach nearly white in deepest shade. Nutritious, eaten by forest peoples in early spring.

**3. The Copper Fern** — Fronds that turn copper-red in autumn while remaining alive and productive. The color persists through winter.

**4. The Ghost Fern** (*bethkel*) — White fronds in low light, transitioning to green near any illumination. Found only under the deepest canopy.

**5. The Blood Fern** — Red undersides that stain fingers if the fronds are crushed. The stain does not wash out easily.

**6. The Iron Fern** (*gorkel*) — Stiff, nearly rigid fronds that creak in wind rather than flex. Extremely long-lived for a fern; individual crowns may be centuries old.

**7. The Ink Fern** — Fronds that turn black when dried. Pressed and dried specimens used in some Forest Mittoli decorative traditions.

**8. The Widow Fern** — Named for the habit of growing only where a tree has recently fallen. Colonizes light gaps within days of opening, then retreats as the canopy closes.

**9. The Torch Fern** — Tall, upright fronds that glow faintly phosphorescent in the first hours of darkness. The glow fades by midnight.

**10. The River Fern** (*thal-kel*) — Grows with roots submerged, fronds above water. Stabilizes stream banks. Tougher than it looks.

**11. The Stone Fern** — Grows directly on rock faces with no apparent soil. Fronds lie flat against stone in a way that looks ornamental.

**12. The Night Fern** — Fronds that close during daylight and open fully only after dark. Active photosynthesis mechanism unknown.

**13. The Winter Fern** — One of few ferns that grows actively in the coldest months. Fronds push through snow.

**14. The Flame Fern** — Orange-red fronds in all seasons. Extremely drought-tolerant by Ibenwood standards.

**15. The Deep Fern** (*vel-kel*) — Found only in the interior of large hollow logs. Cannot survive open conditions.

**16. The Crown Fern** — Fronds arranged in perfect rosettes so geometric they have been mistaken for manufactured objects.

**17. The Hanging Fern** — Grows from tree bark and branches, fronds hanging downward in curtains up to two meters long.

**18. The Mist Fern** — Found exclusively in the fog zones of Mist-maker tree stands. Dies within a week when removed from fog conditions.

**19. The Pale Shield Fern** — Grey-green, tough, found on exposed ridges where no other fern establishes. The preferred material for certain Forest Mittoli basket forms.

**20. The Tongue Fern** — Undivided fronds, thick and fleshy, resembling tongues. Grows from rock crevices and trunk bark.

**21. The Ladder Fern** — Fronds with pinnae arranged in ladder-like regularity. Small, found at base of large trees.

**22. The Velvet Fern** (*threnkel*) — Fronds covered in fine dark hairs that give a velvet texture. Do not touch after rain; the wet hairs cause mild skin irritation.

**23. The Hand Fern** — Deeply lobed fronds resembling open hands. Each frond has five lobes. No exception has been recorded.

**24. The Brittle Fern** — Fronds that shatter when frozen rather than wilting. The fragments root where they fall.

**25. The Ember Fern** — Appears to smoulder; the croziers (unfurling fronds) are deep orange-red. First-year growth looks like cooling coals.

**26. The Ink-cap Fern** — Not related to the ink-cap fungi despite the name. Spore cases are black and burst, staining nearby surfaces.

**27. The Listening Fern** — Fronds that cup slightly, gathering sound. Named by analogy with the Listening Oak; whether the analogy is merely visual is unresolved.

**28. The Water-memory Fern** — Grows only in locations that were under water within living memory. Reliable indicator of past flooding.

**29. The Long Fern** (*vel-kel-dreth*) — Fronds can reach three meters. Found in the wettest forest sections, forming dense stands.

**30. The Spider Fern** — Fronds so finely divided they resemble spider webs. Delicate; collapses in wind, recovers in calm.

**31. The Horned Fern** — Fertile fronds with elongated spore-bearing projections that curve outward like horns.

**32. The Pressed Fern** — Grows flat against bark in a two-dimensional plane, appearing pressed from outside.

**33. The Double Fern** — Always grows in physically connected pairs, two crowns sharing one root system. Singles not recorded.

**34. The Dagger Fern** — Pinnae taper to sharp points. The fronds are used as needles in some Forest Mittoli sewing traditions.

**35. The Wax Fern** — Fronds coated in a waxy substance that repels water. In rain, droplets bead and roll off. Very shiny.

**36. The Smoke Fern** — Spore release creates a visible cloud. Spore release timing appears deliberate; not triggered by wind.

**37. The Counting Fern** — Frond divisions follow a consistent mathematical pattern. Scholars have counted; the pattern holds.

**38. The Drowned Fern** — Fully aquatic. Fronds only above water; roots in stream bed.

**39. The Grave Fern** — Colonizes sites of animal death specifically. Forest peoples do not harvest from it.

**40. The Amber Fern** — Fronds resinous, amber-colored in autumn, sticky to touch. Traps small insects.

**41. The Copper-back Fern** — Green upper frond surface, metallic copper on the underside. In wind, alternates visibly.

**42. The Old Fern** (*morv-kel*) — Grows only on the oldest trees. Absent from young growth entirely. Age indicator species.

**43. The Tooth Fern** — Sharply serrated frond margins. Can cause small cuts when handled carelessly.

**44. The Blue Fern** — The only true blue pigmentation documented in Ibenwood ferns. Small, rare, deep interior.

**45. The Knot Fern** — Stipes that grow in tight spirals before straightening. Each plant spirals differently.

**46. The Weeping Fern** (*drevel-kel*) — Fronds that droop completely, tips touching the forest floor regardless of frond length.

**47. The Fan Fern** — Fronds undivided and fan-shaped. Very large; individual fronds exceed one meter across.

**48. The Rust Fern** — Fronds that look diseased but are not; the rust coloring is normal pigmentation.

**49. The Thread Fern** — Fronds reduced to thread-like segments. Looks skeletal.

**50. The King Fern** (*vel-gorkel*) — The largest fern in the Ibenwood, occasionally exceeding four meters. Old crowns are hollow at the center; sheltered within by forest peoples.

**51. The Salt Fern** — Found near Salt Root trees. The correlation with minerological deposits holds for this fern as well.

**52. The Drift Fern** — Fragments of old fronds are used as propagules; the plant distributes itself by intentional fragmentation.

**53. The First Fern** (*morvenkel*) — Considered by Forest Mittoli tradition to be among the oldest plants in the Ibenwood. No verifiable dating.

**54. The Mirror Fern** — Fronds that are perfect mirror-images of each other on either side of the rachis. Uncommon.

**55. The Collar Fern** — Grows in a ring around tree bases, encircling the trunk. Always circular.

**56. The Hidden Fern** — Found exclusively under other large ferns, never in open conditions. Whether it needs the cover or requires the shade is not established.

**57. The Feather Fern** — Frond texture genuinely feather-like; pinnae soft and plume-like.

**58. The Burning Fern** — Named not for fire but for the sensation of brushing against it; the frond hairs cause temporary skin burning without visible mark.

**59. The Bright Fern** (*bethren-kel*) — Leaves remain vivid green in conditions where all nearby plants go dull. Color indicator of soil conditions.

**60. The Sunken Fern** — Rhizome grows deep underground; fronds emerge directly from soil with no visible stipe.

**61. The Walking Clubmoss** (*vel-thal-dreth-kel*) — A fern-ally; produces runners that root ahead, giving the colony directional movement over years.

**62. The Lantern Clubmoss** — Produces luminescent cones. The glow is used by forest peoples navigating at night.

**63. The Ground-star Clubmoss** — Flattened colonies that look like star-shaped green medallions pressed to the soil.

**64. The Rope Clubmoss** — Long, rope-like, hanging from branches. Can reach five meters.

**65. The Silver Clubmoss** — Metallic silver sheen on the leaves. Most common in the mist zones.

**66. The Stone Clubmoss** (*gorkel-vel*) — Colonizes bare rock; survives total desiccation and revives on wetting.

**67. The Night Clubmoss** — Strobili glow very faintly at night. The glow is consistent but dim.

**68. The Hollow Clubmoss** — Stems hollow; make a thin whistle when wind passes through.

**69. The Copper Clubmoss** — Copper coloration on the older portions. The youngest growth is green.

**70. The Deep Clubmoss** (*vel-kel-dreth*) — Found in the root-space beneath old trees. Requires deep shade.

**71. The Flat Spikemost** — Grows in dense flat mats pressed to tree bark. Very difficult to remove.

**72. The Trailing Spikemoss** — Long, trailing stems that root at intervals, forming connected colonies.

**73. The Stiff Spikemoss** — Upright, unbranched. Grows in perfect vertical clusters.

**74. The Blue-grey Spikemoss** — Unusual coloration. Found only near specific water chemistry.

**75. The Ascending Quillwort** — An aquatic fern-ally; found submerged in still pools, resembling small rushes.

**76. The Mudflat Quillwort** — Found in seasonal pools; dormant in dry periods, fully active when submerged.

**77. The Forest Horsetail** (*vel-kel-goreth*) — Taller than most horsetails; found in the wetter forest sections.

**78. The Black Horsetail** — Stems dark grey-black rather than the typical green.

**79. The Branching Horsetail** — Profuse lateral branches; the usual whorled structure multiplied several times.

**80. The Giant Horsetail** — Can reach two meters. Found in boggy depressions.

**81. The Winter Horsetail** — Green in winter, dormant in summer. Reverse of typical behavior.

**82. The Creeping Horsetail** — Rhizome extends horizontally; single colonies cover large areas.

**83. The Beaded Horsetail** — Nodes visibly enlarged, giving a beaded appearance.

**84. The Silent Horsetail** — Makes no sound in wind; the hollow stems, which in other horsetails whistle, are sealed.

**85. The Fertile Horsetail** — Produces strobili year-round. No distinct sterile and fertile period.

**86. The Fine Horsetail** — Branches so fine as to be almost invisible; looks like a green mist at distance.

**87. The Climbing Fern** — Produces fronds that twine around other plants to reach higher light.

**88. The Mat Fern** — Forms dense flat mats over the forest floor. Can cover several square meters from a single crown.

**89. The Root Fern** (*thal-kel-vel*) — Grows into the exposed root systems of large trees. Photosynthesizes above, roots below, in both senses.

**90. The Spotted Fern** — Fronds with pale spots that may or may not be a natural pattern. Forest peoples treat spotted specimens differently from unspotted ones of the same species.

**91. The Cold Fern** (*skortkel*) — Found only in cold microclimates: north-facing rock faces, the cold air drainage valleys near the Oremindi foothills.

**92. The Reed Fern** — Grows in reed-like upright masses in wet conditions.

**93. The Net Fern** — Frond surface patterned with a visible network of veins that look deliberate.

**94. The Green-fire Fern** — Spore release appears as a flash of green light. The mechanism is contested; several Academy reports describe it as illusion.

**95. The Rooting Fern** — Frond tips root when they touch soil, producing a new plant. Spreads rapidly.

**96. The Ear Fern** — Basal lobes of each pinna shaped like small ears. Consistent across all specimens.

**97. The Wound Fern** (*vel-dreth-kel*) — The fronds, when crushed, produce a substance used by Forest Mittoli communities for wound treatment. Not shared with outside medical traditions.

**98. The Flask Fern** — Rhizome inflated, flask-shaped. Found only in the wettest ground.

**99. The Forked Fern** (*skelthos-kel*) — Each frond forks exactly once, producing a Y-shape. No exceptions.

**100. The Mountain-edge Fern** — Found at the upper limit of the Ibenwood, at the Oremindi transition. Frost-resistant.

## Part II — Mosses, Liverworts, and Hornworts

Mosses dominate the forest floor, the bark of every tree, and many surfaces that would be bare in forests with less moisture. The Ibenwood's moss layer is continuous across much of the interior; the Academy estimates that removing the moss layer would reduce the forest's water retention by a factor that would render the current tree distribution impossible.

**101. The Velvet Moss** (*vel-thren-drev*) — Deep green, fine-textured, the most common moss in the Ibenwood. Forms continuous carpets. Walking on it is genuinely pleasant.

**102. The Bone Moss** (*sorth-drev*) — White to pale grey. Grows on the bones and shells of dead animals. Present in abundance at large carcass sites years after soft tissue is gone.

**103. The Fire Moss** — Bright red capsule stalks visible above the green cushion in fruiting season. Common on disturbed ground.

**104. The Silver Thread Moss** — Hair-like, silvery, grows in dense tufts from bark. A single tuft contains thousands of individual plants.

**105. The Copper Feather Moss** — Copper-colored, feather-branched, grows in flat mats on the forest floor. Soft enough to sleep on; forest peoples do.

**106. The Luminous Moss** (*vel-bethren-drev*) — Very faint bioluminescence. A dense mat in complete darkness is visible to dark-adapted eyes.

**107. The Hanging Curtain Moss** (*drevel-drev*) — Hangs in long pale strands from branches in the mist zones. Creates a curtain effect in stands of Mist-maker trees.

**108. The Snaking Moss** — Grows in branching patterns that look like frozen lightning on rock surfaces.

**109. The Black Mat Moss** — Very dark green to black. Found under the densest canopy where almost nothing else grows.

**110. The Step Moss** — Grows in layered tiers on steep slopes. The tiers are roughly uniform in height.

**111. The Wind Moss** (*skort-drev*) — Found on exposed ridges. Tiny, dense, completely wind-resistant by design.

**112. The Feather-plume Moss** — Branched to resemble a bird's feather. The resemblance is exact enough to cause confusion.

**113. The Tree-hair Moss** — Long, hair-like, drapes over branches. The most common epiphytic moss in the canopy layer.

**114. The Cord Moss** (*gorvel-drev*) — Extremely tough; the stems do not break when pulled. Forest peoples use strands as cordage.

**115. The Prism Moss** — Capsules refract light in a way that produces tiny rainbow patches on the forest floor in morning sun.

**116. The Stone Fur Moss** — Forms fur-like coatings on stone surfaces. Old stone in the Ibenwood is never bare; this is partly why.

**117. The Cold Spring Moss** (*skort-thal-drev*) — Found in cold spring seeps. Indicates water temperature.

**118. The Trailing Fern-moss** — Looks like a tiny fern; not related. Trails along the forest floor from a creeping stem.

**119. The Chalk Moss** (*beth-drev*) — White-grey in dry conditions; turns vivid green when wet. Rainfall indicator species.

**120. The Lantern Moss** — Transparent leaf tips that concentrate and redirect light to the chlorophyll layer below. The optical arrangement has been studied and is genuinely functional.

**121. The Ribbed Dome Moss** — Forms perfect hemispheres. Colonies of Ribbed Dome Moss look like a field of green spheres.

**122. The Cup Moss** — Central cup collects water. Not technically a moss-cup (which is a lichen); this is a true moss with a convergent structure.

**123. The Spiral Moss** (*skel-drev*) — Leaves arranged in visible spirals. The spiral direction is consistent within populations.

**124. The Ancient Peat Moss** — Sphagnum-like, found in the boggy depressions of the forest. Individual colonies may be centuries old.

**125. The Blood-cap Moss** — Red capsule caps the most vivid red found in any Ibenwood moss. The color is not present in the vegetative structure, only the capsule.

**126. The Weft Moss** — Grows in interwoven layers with other mosses, creating a composite mat that resists disturbance more than either species alone.

**127. The Whisper Moss** (*vel-drev-thren*) — Makes a sound in wind that experienced travelers describe specifically as a whisper. The acoustic mechanism — the leaf shape creating a specific turbulent frequency — has been modeled but not fully explained.

**128. The Stag-horn Moss** — Branching pattern resembles antlers. Grows upright rather than flat, unusual for forest floor mosses.

**129. The Creeping Fire Moss** — Red pigmentation in all vegetative structures, not just capsule stalks.

**130. The Thread-vein Moss** — Leaves so transparent the internal cell structure is visible, creating a visible network pattern.

**131. The Glacier Moss** (*skortkel-drev*) — Found only at the highest elevations of the Ibenwood, approaching the Oremindi transition. Tolerates temperature extremes.

**132. The Night-glow Moss** (*vel-bethren-drev-vel*) — Stronger bioluminescence than the Luminous Moss. A dense patch genuinely illuminates the immediate area in complete darkness.

**133. The Leather Moss** — Thick, leathery leaves; water-resistant. Used as a packing material by forest peoples.

**134. The Flat-iron Moss** (*gor-drev*) — Grows so flat it appears ironed onto its substrate. Even vertical surfaces show it perfectly flat.

**135. The Branching Cord Moss** — Like the Cord Moss but branched; used for finer cordage work where flexibility is required.

**136. The Fan Moss** — Fronds (not technically fronds, but so described in common use) spread in flat fans. Grows from tree bark.

**137. The Twisted Cord Moss** — Stems naturally twisted; appears braided even as a single strand.

**138. The Mounding Moss** — Forms mounds rather than flat mats; individual mounds can reach knee height over decades.

**139. The Copper Thread Moss** (*skel-bethren-drev*) — Coppery sheen from metallic compounds in the leaves. Found near ore-bearing substrates.

**140. The Soft Felt Moss** — The most texture-pleasant moss in the Ibenwood. Forest peoples use it as bedding material.

**141. The Shelf Liverwort** (*vel-thal-drev-kel*) — Grows in shelf-like layers on wet rock faces. Each shelf level is a separate generation of growth.

**142. The Ribbon Liverwort** (*drevel-beth-drev*) — Long, ribbon-like thalli that flow along the substrate following water drainage. Traces the history of water movement on rock surfaces.

**143. The Spotted Liverwort** — Thallus covered in pale spots. The spots mark the positions of underground air chambers.

**144. The Umbrella Liverwort** — Reproductive structures resemble tiny umbrellas. Used as a visual landmark by forest peoples navigating specific routes.

**145. The Scented Liverwort** — Produces a persistent sweet smell. Recognizable from several meters away.

**146. The Stone Liverwort** (*gor-beth-drev*) — Encrusts rock surfaces so tightly it appears painted on. Cannot be removed without taking the rock surface with it.

**147. The Silver Liverwort** — Metallic silver thallus surface. Common on stream banks.

**148. The Transparent Liverwort** (*vel-beth-drev*) — Very thin thallus; the substrate is visible through it. Looks like a layer of glass.

**149. The Long Liverwort** — Thallus extends in long unbranched strands, sometimes reaching a meter from the attachment point.

**150. The Mat Liverwort** — Forms extensive flat mats on forest floor, visually indistinguishable from carpet moss to the non-specialist.

**151. The Snake Liverwort** — Thallus with a pattern resembling scale markings. Forest peoples treat it differently from other liverworts.

**152. The Cup Liverwort** — Thallus margins roll upward to form a cup. Collects debris, water, and small invertebrates.

**153. The Blood Liverwort** (*dren-drev*) — Deep red thallus. Found on recently disturbed soil, one of the first colonizers after disturbance.

**154. The Rough Liverwort** — Thallus surface distinctly rough; the surface texture is used to distinguish it from lookalike species.

**155. The Night Liverwort** — Produces oil bodies that fluoresce under certain light conditions. Forest peoples use it as a nighttime path marker.

**156. The Pore Liverwort** (*vel-drev-gor*) — Upper thallus surface covered in visible pores that open and close with humidity changes.

**157. The Coil Liverwort** — Young thalli coiled like watch springs; uncoil as they mature.

**158. The Frond Liverwort** (*kel-beth-drev*) — Leafy liverwort with frond-like leaves; resembles a tiny fern. Most common leafy liverwort in the Ibenwood.

**159. The Feather Liverwort** — Pinnately branched; the branching pattern resembles a feather.

**160. The Ghost Liverwort** (*beth-vel-drev*) — Pale to almost white. Found in the darkest sections of deep Ibenwood.

**161. The Flat Hornwort** — Smooth, flat, dark green thallus with characteristic hornwort sporophytes (horn-shaped). Common on wet soil.

**162. The Branched Hornwort** — Thallus deeply lobed. The lobing creates a rosette pattern.

**163. The Spiny Hornwort** — Sporophytes armed with small spines. Function of spines not established.

**164. The Long-horn Hornwort** — Sporophytes elongated, reaching several centimeters. Visible above the thallus from a distance.

**165. The Twin Hornwort** — Always found growing with one specific liverwort species. The association may be symbiotic or may be shared habitat preference.

## Part III — Lichens

Lichens cover nearly every solid surface in the Ibenwood — bark, rock, soil, bone, old glass if any were available. They are the first colonizers, the last survivors, and the surface layer through which most visitors first experience the forest's character.

**166. The Map Lichen** (*vel-gor-drev-beth*) — Crustose, grey-yellow, with crack patterns that resemble maps. Forest peoples use specific Map Lichen colonies as actual navigational references.

**167. The Blood-spot Lichen** — Bright red spots on grey thallus. The red is apothecia (fruiting bodies). Very visible.

**168. The Shield Lichen** (*kel-beth-drev*) — Foliose, grey-green, covering tree bark in overlapping layers like scales or shields.

**169. The Beard Lichen** (*drevel-drev-kel*) — Fruticose, hanging in long pale grey-green strands from branches. The mist zones are heavily bearded.

**170. The Gold Dust Lichen** — Powdery, yellow-orange. Covers rock surfaces in the brighter sections of the forest edge.

**171. The Black Crust Lichen** (*skort-gor-drev*) — Colonizes rock where nothing else establishes. Black, thin, the first life on bare stone.

**172. The Nail Lichen** — Fruiting bodies protruding like tiny nails from the thallus. Found on dead wood.

**173. The Kidney Lichen** — Lobes kidney-shaped. Distinctly foliose, found on bark in the mid-canopy where it can be seen.

**174. The Blue-grey Rock Lichen** (*vel-skort-drev*) — Colonizes shaded rock faces. The blue-grey color is produced by a pigment that resists UV radiation.

**175. The Coral Lichen** — Three-dimensional, branched like coral. The most architecturally complex lichen in the Ibenwood.

**176. The Ember Lichen** — Red to orange; looks like cooling coals on dark bark. Very striking.

**177. The Ghost Lichen** (*beth-vel-drev-kel*) — White, almost luminous. Found on dying trees specifically; appears before visible decline and after recovery.

**178. The Iron Lichen** (*gor-drev-kel*) — Brown-black, extremely slow-growing, the oldest-living organisms measurable in the Ibenwood. Certain rock face colonies have been growing continuously for thousands of years.

**179. The Pebbled Grey Lichen** — Thallus surface covered in regular projections like small pebbles. Found on bark.

**180. The Ink Lichen** — Produces a black exudate when wet. One of the oldest sources of writing material in the forest edge communities.

**181. The Powdered Gold Lichen** — Sorediate; produces powdery granules that function as dispersal units. The granules look like gold dust on bark.

**182. The Tar Lichen** (*gorvel-drev-kel*) — Black, shiny, appearing wet. Colonizes the smoothest bark surfaces.

**183. The Rosette Lichen** — Circular growth with lobes radiating from center. Individual colonies are geometrically round.

**184. The Mustard Lichen** — Bright yellow-green. Common on nutrient-enriched bark near bird perch sites.

**185. The Red Beret Lichen** — Grey thallus with bright red apothecia shaped like berets. Unmistakable when fruiting.

**186. The Ribbon Lichen** — Fruticose, flat, ribbon-like. Hangs from bark in pale grey ribbons.

**187. The Thread Lichen** (*vel-drev-kel-dreth*) — Extremely fine, hair-like. Can only be distinguished from actual hair or thread under magnification.

**188. The Scale Lichen** — Squamulose; thallus broken into small scales. Covers the ground in certain forest floor sections.

**189. The Cup Lichen** (*vel-thal-drev-kel*) — Produces upright cup-shaped podetia. Colonies look like a field of tiny grey cups.

**190. The Pixie Cup Lichen** — Smaller than the Cup Lichen, the cups more precisely formed.

**191. The Matchstick Lichen** — Podetia capped with red, like matchsticks. Very photogenic; frequently illustrated in Academy botanical reports.

**192. The Pale Shield Lichen** (*beth-kel-drev*) — Larger lobes than most foliose lichens; pale grey-green. Used by forest peoples as a dye source.

**193. The Rough Bark Lichen** — Its presence makes bark feel rougher than it is, adding its own texture to the surface.

**194. The Foiled Lichen** — Very thin lobes that look like folded silver foil. Found on smooth bark.

**195. The Burnt Cork Lichen** — Dark brown-black; looks like charred cork. Found on old-growth bark specifically.

**196. The Star Lichen** (*vel-beth-drev-kel*) — Lobes radiating precisely from central point. Individual colonies measure from centimeter to meter across, same pattern at all scales.

**197. The Chalk Lichen** — White, powdery surface. Colonizes calcareous rock outcrops in the Ibenwood.

**198. The Smoke Lichen** (*vel-dorvem-kel*) — Accompanies the Smokewood tree. Found only on Smokewood bark and seems to require the Smokewood's specific bark chemistry.

**199. The Bleeding Lichen** — Produces a red exudate when damaged. Used in small quantities for dyeing by Forest Mittoli communities.

**200. The Copper Lichen** — Metallic copper coloration from compounds absorbed from mineralized substrates.

**201. The Pale Moon Lichen** — Round, white, found on large horizontal branches where rain can pool. Associated with water accumulation.

**202. The Forest Floor Lichen** (*thal-kel-drev*) — Grows on soil, covering patches of forest floor in a continuous grey-green mat.

**203. The Net Lichen** — Thallus surface patterned with a network of raised ridges, like a net cast over the surface.

**204. The Antler Lichen** (*vel-gor-drev-dreth*) — Fruticose; branched like antlers. Grows in the drier, better-lit sections near the forest edge.

**205. The Varnish Lichen** — Produces a shiny varnish-like surface compound. The varnish has been used by forest peoples for waterproofing applications.

**206. The Eye Lichen** — Apothecia that appear eye-like. Bark covered with Eye Lichen looks as though it is watching.

**207. The Skeleton Lichen** — Branched to resemble bone structure; the branching pattern follows what appears to be an internal template.

**208. The Frost Lichen** (*skort-kel-drev*) — Silvery-white; grows at higher elevations. Survives complete freezing and thawing cycles.

**209. The Dark Crumble Lichen** — Black, granular, colonizes bare soil in the deepest forest shade.

**210. The Pale Finger Lichen** (*beth-vel-kel-drev*) — Fingers of pale grey rising from a flat base. Found exclusively on certain species of Ibenwood bark.

**211. The Golden Film Lichen** — Thin as paint on rock surfaces, gold-yellow, invisible in dry conditions and vivid when wet.

**212. The Rock-hair Lichen** — Long, black, hair-like, growing from rock crevices. Very old specimens; individual "hairs" may be centuries old.

**213. The Pale Green Floor Lichen** — Covers the forest floor where mosses cannot establish. Smooth, continuous, pale.

**214. The Hanging Rope Lichen** (*vel-drevel-kel-drev*) — Long, rope-like, can reach three meters. Found in mist zones.

**215. The Spiny Rock Lichen** — Tiny spines over the entire thallus surface. Function unknown.

**216. The Copper Cup Lichen** — Cup-shaped like the Cup Lichen; copper-colored.

**217. The Brown Crust Lichen** — Most common crust lichen on Ibenwood rocks. Covers millions of square meters of stone surface.

**218. The Ancient Rock Lichen** (*morv-gor-drev-kel*) — Believed by Forest Mittoli communities to be among the oldest living organisms in the Ibenwood. The Academy has not disputed this.

**219. The Dripping Lichen** — Grows on overhangs where water drips. Long, pendant, oriented by the water flow direction.

**220. The Feather Lichen** (*beth-drev-kel*) — Foliose with feather-like lobes. The most common lichen on the lower trunks of the Canopy Giants.

## Part IV — Shrubs and Woody Understory Plants

The woody understory of the Ibenwood is dense where light permits and sparse where it does not. The shrub layer is the primary habitat for many of the forest's birds and mammals, the source of a large proportion of Forest Mittoli food and medicine, and the layer most directly managed by the forest peoples.

**221. The Shade Berry** (*vel-dren-kel*) — Deep purple berries on dark-leaved shrub. Primary food source during the forest peoples' late-summer gatherings. Not cultivated; harvested wild.

**222. The Bitter Bark Shrub** — Bark so bitter it deters grazing; used in small quantities as a digestive medicine by Forest Mittoli communities.

**223. The Red-twig Dogwood** — Vivid red stems visible in winter when leaves have dropped. Navigation marker in the leaf-bare season.

**224. The Forest Hazel** (*vel-thos-kel*) — Produces nuts of better quality than its edge relatives, attributed to the specific soil chemistry of the deep interior.

**225. The Spine Bush** — Thorny, used by forest peoples as a living fence around specific sites.

**226. The Pale-leaf Shrub** (*beth-kel-thos*) — Leaves silver on both surfaces; a grove of these lights up the understory in any direction of sun.

**227. The Creeping Gorse** — Low, thorny, covers the ground under Bone Birch stands where the leaf litter is too thick for most other plants.

**228. The Arrow Shrub** (*vel-kel-goreth*) — Produces extremely straight young shoots; harvested for shafts.

**229. The Dream Shrub** — Leaves produce a mild sedative effect when burned. Forest peoples use in specific ceremonial contexts. Not for general use.

**230. The Sweet-bark Shrub** — Bark with a sweet flavor used as a cooking spice throughout the forest edge communities and traded to coastal cities.

**231. The Ink Elderberry** (*velmoreth-kel*) — Berries produce a permanent black ink when crushed. Related to the Ink Tree in product if not in taxonomy.

**232. The Witch Hazel** (*skelthos-kel*) — Flowers in deep winter, the only flowering shrub in the Ibenwood's coldest months. The flower smell carries unusually far.

**233. The Red Osier** — Used for basket weaving. The red stems are harvested in spring; flexibility highest in the first years of growth.

**234. The Silver-leaf Gooseberry** — Berries edible, tart. Leaves used as an astringent in Forest Mittoli wound care.

**235. The Forest Elder** — Flowers used in a preparation that Forest Mittoli practitioners describe as clarifying; outside scholars have not determined what is being clarified.

**236. The Copper-leaf Viburnum** — Copper-colored autumn leaves that persist through winter. Dense, used as windbreak in forest peoples' settlements.

**237. The Broom Bush** — Flexible stems used for sweeping. Also used in construction; woven walls that flex in the wind without breaking.

**238. The Deep Buckthorn** (*vel-thos-gor*) — Thorny, found in disturbed areas and light gaps. Berries purgative; used in small doses medicinally.

**239. The Bog Myrtle** — Aromatic; produces a scent that repels certain insects. Forest peoples plant it around sleeping areas.

**240. The Bird-cherry** (*dren-thos-kel*) — Abundant small cherries, intensely sour. Eaten by birds; fermented by forest peoples into a preparation used in specific contexts.

**241. The Snowberry** — White berries that persist through winter. Toxic; forest peoples know this and mark stands.

**242. The Black Chokeberry** — Astringent berries used in Forest Mittoli food preparation. Requires specific treatment to render palatable.

**243. The Wayfaring Bush** — Traditionally considered a path marker; found along old routes through the forest. Whether planted or naturally occurring in these positions is unresolved.

**244. The Leatherleaf** — Thick, leathery leaves that persist through winter on the stem. Covers boggy depressions in the eastern sections.

**245. The Spice Bush** (*vel-thos-dren*) — All parts aromatic. One of the most traded forest products; the dried bark is used as a spice across western Azhora.

**246. The Low Juniper** — Prostrate in deep shade, upright near light. The berries are used in food preservation.

**247. The Bristle Shrub** (*gor-kel-thos*) — Covered in fine bristles. Produces a mild irritation on contact; forest peoples are habituated to it.

**248. The Forest Raspberry** (*dren-thos-vel*) — Much larger fruit than coastal varieties. The flavor is described by visitors as uncomfortably intense.

**249. The Woody Nightshade** — Berries red, toxic. Common in disturbed areas near forest settlements.

**250. The Gale** — Aromatic leaves used in food preparation and as insect repellent. Very common in the wetland margins.

**251. The Wood Sorrel Shrub** — Not a true shrub; perennial herbaceous plant reaching shrub size in the interior. Sour leaves eaten in salads.

**252. The Trailing Bramble** — Forms impenetrable mats in light gaps. The berries are large, sweet, and difficult to reach.

**253. The Ghost Currant** (*beth-dren-kel*) — White berries unusual in a forest berry. Sweet. Found under the palest patches of canopy.

**254. The Forest Gooseberry** — Very large, sweet. Forest peoples compete with bears for them in season.

**255. The Crowberry** — Black, found at higher elevations toward the Oremindi margin. Used in cold-weather food preparation.

**256. The Heather** — Found only near the Ibenwood's eastern edge where the canopy thins. A marker that the true interior is ending.

**257. The Ground Ivy** (*vel-thal-thos*) — Spreads aggressively in disturbed areas. Forest peoples use it to indicate which areas have been recently cleared.

**258. The Blue-bead Lily** — Blue berries in summer, highly toxic. The flowers are white and beautiful; the berries are a trap.

**259. The Mountain Alder** — Found at the Oremindi transition. Fixes nitrogen; forest peoples plant it to improve soil in the highest-elevation settlements.

**260. The Dark Willow-herb** — Colonizes disturbed soil rapidly. The presence of large stands indicates recent large-scale soil disturbance.

**261. The Stink Bush** — Unpleasant smell when bruised. Deer avoid it; forest peoples use this as a deterrent.

**262. The Catkin Shrub** — Produces enormous catkins in spring; the pollen release is visible as a yellow haze in the understory.

**263. The Sweet Gale** — Used in the production of certain forest peoples' fermented preparations.

**264. The Wax Myrtle** — Berries coated in wax; the wax is harvested and used for candles and waterproofing.

**265. The Woolly Shrub** — Leaves covered in dense wool-like hairs. The insulating quality of the leaf covering is exceptional; used in packing materials.

**266. The Pale Spiraea** — White flowers in dense clusters. One of the few shrubs that flowers profusely in deep shade.

**267. The Featherleaf Spiraea** — Leaves feather-like; produces a fine root system that works unusually well on thin soils.

**268. The Bog Rosemary** — Found in the boggy interior sections. Not true rosemary; similar appearance.

**269. The Swamp Laurel** — Toxic; forest peoples mark stands clearly. The smell is pleasant, which makes it a trap for unwary visitors.

**270. The Leather-bark Shrub** (*gorkel-thos-vel*) — Bark used in leather tanning by forest edge communities. The tannin content is extraordinary.

**271. The Snowball Bush** — White flowers in dense spherical clusters. Common in the better-lit margins.

**272. The Rattle Bush** — Seed pods that rattle audibly in wind. Forest peoples use the rattle as a wind direction indicator.

**273. The Burning Bush** (*vel-dren-thos*) — Not a euphemism; in dry autumn conditions, the volatile oils in the leaves can briefly ignite and burn out. Forest peoples manage stands carefully.

**274. The Red-leaf Cinquefoil** — Red leaves; grows in dense mats near water.

**275. The Sticky Shrub** — Covered in glandular hairs that trap small insects. The trapped insects provide the plant with nutrients.

**276. The Blue-stem Shrub** — Blue-tinged stems. Found in mineralized soils.

**277. The Cork-bark Shrub** — Bark that has the texture and insulating properties of cork. Used in construction.

**278. The Silver-bark Willow** (*beth-thal-thos*) — Small willow found along streams in the interior. Bark silver; very bright in stream-side conditions.

**279. The Compact Snowberry** — Smaller than the regular Snowberry; berries same color, same toxicity.

**280. The Heart-leaf Bush** — Leaves heart-shaped; grown by some forest peoples in ceremonial gardens.

**281. The Lace Shrub** — Leaves deeply cut, lace-like. Found in the better-lit edge sections.

**282. The Copper-bark Shrub** — Bark copper-colored; used as a copper-tone dye.

**283. The Pepper Bush** (*vel-kel-dren*) — Crushed berries produce an intense pepper-like sensation. Used as a spice. Very valuable in trade.

**284. The Stiff-leaf Shrub** — Leaves rigid; do not move in normal wind. Make no sound.

**285. The Tansy-leaf Bush** — Aromatic; repels insects. Planted around forest peoples' settlements.

**286. The Whorled Shrub** — Leaves in precise whorls of five. The mathematical regularity is consistent.

**287. The Red Stem Dogwood** — Crimson stems visible across large distances in bare-canopy season.

**288. The Black-berry Elder** — Produces an elderberry variant with black fruit. Used in food and in Forest Mittoli medicinal preparations.

**289. The Woolly Willow** (*vel-thal-kel-thos*) — Leaves covered in white wool. Found at the upper Ibenwood elevations.

**290. The Damp Alder** — Requires permanently moist soil. A reliable wet-ground indicator.

## Part V — Wildflowers and Herbs

The herbaceous layer of the Ibenwood is the most visually varied in the forest. Flowers appear in pulses throughout the year, tracking the variable light conditions through which canopy gaps open and close, and the Ibenwood's herbaceous flora is adapted to opportunism — rapid response to light availability — in a way that the tree layer cannot match.

**291. The White Windflower** (*beth-vel-dren*) — Earliest spring flower. Carpets the forest floor before canopy leafout provides shade.

**292. The Blue Wood Sorrel** — Sky blue flowers; more vivid than any coastal blue flower. The blue is a navigational reference.

**293. The Ghost Orchid** (*beth-vel-dren-kel*) — White, leafless, entirely saprophytic. Appears and disappears unpredictably from the same sites over years.

**294. The Nodding Trillium** — Flower hangs below the leaf canopy. Pollinated by flies; the smell attracts them.

**295. The Deep Violet** (*vel-thren-dren*) — Deeper purple than surface violets. Edible leaves and flowers.

**296. The Spotted Lungwort** — Spotted leaves. Used in Forest Mittoli respiratory preparations for its perceived resemblance to lung tissue.

**297. The Gold Star Lily** (*vel-dren-bethren*) — Six-petaled, gold. One of the most striking flowers in the Ibenwood's better-lit margins.

**298. The Blood Root** (*dren-kel-vel*) — Orange-red sap from the roots. Highly toxic; vivid orange dye. Both properties used.

**299. The Forest Anemone** — Small, white, grows in the transition zone between Ibenwood and open country.

**300. The Wake Robin** — Deep red flower, foul smell. Pollinated by carrion insects.

**301. The Solomon's Seal** — Arching stems with hanging white flowers. Used in Forest Mittoli joint preparations.

**302. The Star-of-the-Deep-Forest** (*beth-vel-thren-dren*) — White, five-petaled, glows very faintly at night. Found only in specific interior locations.

**303. The Creeping Jenny** — Golden yellow, prostrate, spreads rapidly in disturbed areas.

**304. The Wild Ginger** (*vel-thos-dren*) — Aromatic root used as a spice throughout the Ibenwood trade.

**305. The Crimson Bee Balm** — Bright red; significant nectar source. The bees that work it are rarely seen outside the Ibenwood.

**306. The Forest Phlox** — Pink to purple, fragrant. Flowers in gaps where some light reaches the floor.

**307. The Yellow Trout Lily** — Mottled leaves resembling fish. Flowers in early spring before canopy leafout.

**308. The Dark Columbine** (*vel-thren-dren-kel*) — Flowers so dark purple they appear black in low light.

**309. The Spider Orchid** (*vel-kel-dren*) — Named for the spider-like appearance of the labellum. Pollination mechanism involves deceiving specific spiders.

**310. The Wood Violet** — Common; edible; used as a salad herb throughout the forest edge communities.

**311. The Shy Flower** (*vel-skort-dren*) — Closes immediately when touched; reopens in ten minutes. The response is entirely mechanical; even shadow contact triggers it.

**312. The Foam Flower** — White flowers in frothy masses. Common in the better-lit stream margins.

**313. The Shadow Lily** (*vel-thren-dren-vel*) — Flowers only in complete shade. The flower structure is adapted for very low light pollination conditions.

**314. The Twisted Stalk** — Stem segments twisted alternately left and right; the stalk weaves through the air.

**315. The Wild Sarsaparilla** — Aromatic root. Traded widely to coastal cities for flavoring preparations.

**316. The Blue Cohosh** — Blue berries that look edible; moderately toxic. Forest peoples mark stands.

**317. The Baneberry** — Red or white berries; highly toxic. Stands require specific warning systems in Forest Mittoli territory management.

**318. The Jack-in-the-Pulpit** (*vel-kormath-dren*) — The spathe and spadix structure gives the appearance of a figure in a pulpit. Forest peoples consider it significant; they do not elaborate to outside observers.

**319. The Pink Lady's Slipper** (*dren-kel-vel-beth*) — Spectacular orchid; requires a specific fungal relationship to germinate. Cannot be transplanted.

**320. The Yellow Lady's Slipper** — Related to the Pink; same germination requirement; different color and habitat range within the forest.

**321. The Showy Orchid** — The most visually impressive orchid in the Ibenwood's better-lit sections. Pink and white. Pollinators arrive from significant distances.

**322. The Rattlesnake Plantain** — Mottled leaves resembling snakeskin. Used in some Forest Mittoli preparations for snakebite.

**323. The Downy Wood Mint** — Aromatic, wooly-leaved. One of the primary tea herbs used throughout the western Azhoran coastal trade.

**324. The Wild Bergamot** (*vel-thos-vel-dren*) — More intensely aromatic than its edge relatives. The Ibenwood variety is specifically sought after.

**325. The Blue Lobelia** — Bright blue; found near water. The color is one of the most vivid in the forest.

**326. The Cardinal Flower** — Brilliant red; found in stream margins. Hummingbirds and certain insects are drawn from outside the forest.

**327. The Swamp Milkweed** (*vel-thal-dren*) — Pink flowers; grows in the wet interior sections. Significant insect plant.

**328. The Wild Geranium** — Deep pink; very common in the transitional zone. Reliable colonizer of disturbed areas.

**329. The Rue Anemone** — White or pink; delicate; spring-flowering. Very easily damaged; presence indicates undisturbed soil.

**330. The Wild Columbine** — Orange-red; flowers with five hollow spurs. Much more vivid than similar plants outside the Ibenwood.

**331. The Fire Pink** — Bright red, star-shaped flowers. Very striking against dark forest background.

**332. The Long-spurred Violet** — Extended spur on the flower; adapted to a specific long-tongued pollinator that is rarely described by outside naturalists.

**333. The Yellow Trillium** — Less common than the white; found in richer soils.

**334. The Red Trillium** — The most distinctive; deep red flower; strong smell; spring.

**335. The Giant Trillium** (*vel-goreth-dren*) — Much larger than other trilliums. Single flower the size of a spread hand.

**336. The Nodding Wild Onion** — Edible; harvested. Flowers drooping rather than upright.

**337. The Wild Leek** (*vel-thos-kel-dren*) — Forest peoples harvest the leaves in spring and the bulbs in autumn. Very significant food plant.

**338. The Blue Wild Indigo** — Blue-grey leaves; white to blue flowers. The root has been used as a blue dye.

**339. The Wild Blue Phlox** — Lavender-blue; spring; one of the most photographable flowers on the forest floor.

**340. The Glade Fern** — Very different from other Ibenwood ferns; found in specific glade conditions and listed here among the wildflowers for organizational reasons.

**341. The Partridgeberry** — Twin white flowers; paired red berries. Edible. Common ground cover.

**342. The Trailing Arbutus** — Pink or white; early spring; fragrant. The first fragrant flower after winter.

**343. The Goldenseal** (*vel-bethren-dren-kel*) — Used in Forest Mittoli medicinal preparations for a range of conditions. Traded to coastal cities where it is expensive.

**344. The Wild Ginseng** (*vel-thos-morv*) — Found in the interior; extremely long-lived; used in preparations that Forest Mittoli communities describe as restorative. Not shared with outside practitioners.

**345. The Mayapple** — Large, tropical-looking leaves; single white flower hidden beneath them. The ripe fruit is edible; other parts toxic.

**346. The Indian Cucumber Root** (*vel-thal-thos-dren*) — Edible root with cucumber flavor. Found in specific soil conditions.

**347. The Purple Trillium** — Distinguished from the red by specific pigmentation differences.

**348. The False Solomon's Seal** — Similar in form to Solomon's Seal; produces white flowers in terminal clusters rather than hanging from the stem.

**349. The Wild Strawberry** — Small, very sweet. The flavor is described by visitors as the most strawberry-like thing they have ever eaten.

**350. The Wood Anemone** — White, spring. One of the most common forest floor flowering plants.

**351. The Yellow Wood Sorrel** — Small, yellow, common. Sour leaves eaten as a green throughout the forest range.

**352. The Creeping Phlox** — Low, spreading, purple-pink. Forms mats in the transition zone.

**353. The False Lily of the Valley** — Small, white, fragrant. Very common under certain canopy types.

**354. The Gold Thread** (*bethren-dren-kel*) — Named for the vivid yellow threadlike roots. Used in Forest Mittoli preparations for mouth ailments.

**355. The Trout Lily** — Mottled leaves; yellow flowers; spring ephemeral. Gone before the canopy closes over it.

**356. The Wild Bleeding Heart** — Pink; heart-shaped flowers. Toxic; Forest Mittoli communities know where the stands are.

**357. The Dutchman's Breeches** — White flowers with yellow tips; described as resembling upside-down trousers. Toxic; pollinated by early bumblebees.

**358. The Squirrel Corn** — Similar to Dutchman's Breeches; deeper interior habitat. The tubers are yellow, corn-grain-sized.

**359. The Wild Lily of the Valley** (*beth-dren-vel*) — White, fragrant, spring. Less abundant than its lichen-zone relatives.

**360. The Bunchberry** — White flowers; red berries in tight bunches. Common ground cover in the mid-range zones.

**361. The Blue-bead** — Blue berries in summer, toxic. Flowers white in spring.

**362. The Fairy Slipper Orchid** — Small, pink, single flower. Very specific habitat requirements; present only in certain old-growth moss beds.

**363. The Striped Coralroot Orchid** — Entirely leafless, saprophytic; brownish-purple with striped petals.

**364. The Spotted Coralroot Orchid** — Same habit as striped; purple spots rather than stripes.

**365. The Pale Coralroot Orchid** — Paler coloration; slightly different habitat range within the forest.

**366. The Deep Forest Orchid** (*vel-vel-dren-kel*) — Unknown outside the Ibenwood; entirely saprophytic; red-brown; found only in the oldest portions of the interior.

**367. The Three-leaf Trillium** — Variant of common trillium with three distinct leaves held horizontally.

**368. The Showy Trillium** — Large, white, the most spectacular trillium in the Ibenwood.

**369. The Painted Trillium** — White petals with pink veining. Very distinctive.

**370. The Wakerobin** — Common name used locally for several red trillium variants; specific taxonomy disputed by Academy.

**371. The Autumn Gentian** — Blue, flowers in the autumn when almost nothing else does. Very striking.

**372. The Fringed Gentian** — Blue-purple, fringed petals. Found in specific open conditions.

**373. The Forest Monkshood** (*vel-thren-dren-goreth*) — Dark purple, toxic, used in Forest Mittoli preparations under strict conditions. Considered one of the most significant medicinal plants.

**374. The Wild Ginger Mint** — Cross between the aromatic properties of both. Valuable spice.

**375. The Blue Wood Aster** — Late autumn, blue-purple. One of the last flowers before winter.

**376. The White Wood Aster** — White autumn-flowering aster. Common in the transition zone.

**377. The Rough-stemmed Goldenrod** — Gold flowers in late summer; very common in light gaps.

**378. The Zigzag Goldenrod** — Stems zigzag between nodes. Found specifically under certain canopy types.

**379. The Broad-leaf Goldenrod** — Broader leaves than other goldenrods; deeper interior.

**380. The White Snakeroot** — White flowers; highly toxic. One of the most dangerous plants in the Ibenwood to domestic animals.

**381. The Wild Bergamot Mint** — Aromatic, lavender flowers. Significant pollinator plant.

**382. The Cow Parsnip** (*vel-gor-thos-dren*) — Enormous; the largest herbaceous plant in the Ibenwood. Edible when young; causes skin photosensitivity when the sap contacts skin in sunlight.

**383. The Water Hemlock** — Highly toxic. Found near water. Forest peoples mark stands with consistent warning signals.

**384. The Wild Parsnip** — Edible root; invasive in disturbed areas. Managed by forest peoples.

**385. The Forest Carrot** (*thal-thos-vel*) — Edible; smaller and more strongly flavored than cultivated carrots.

**386. The Sweet Cicely** — Anise-scented; edible; used as a sweetener and flavoring.

**387. The Wild Celery** — Found in wet conditions. Edible; used in Forest Mittoli cuisine.

**388. The Angelica** (*vel-morv-dren-kel*) — Very tall; aromatic; used in preparations that Forest Mittoli practitioners describe as protective.

**389. The Bur Parsley** — Seeds that attach to clothing and fur. Effective dispersal mechanism.

**390. The Forest Fennel** — Anise-scented; used as a spice and in preparations.

## Part VI — Grasses, Sedges, Rushes, and Reeds

The grass layer of the Ibenwood is thin compared to open-land systems but present wherever sufficient light reaches the forest floor. Sedges dominate the wet conditions; rushes mark the margins of standing water.

**391. The Deep Forest Grass** (*vel-kel-thal*) — The only grass adapted to complete shade conditions. Very fine; forms thin but persistent ground cover.

**392. The Melic Grass** — Purplish; found in better-lit conditions. The seed head is used decoratively.

**393. The Wood Brome** — Shade-tolerant grass; found under moderate canopy. Useful forage.

**394. The Wood Millet** — Small seeds; birds harvest them. Found in light gaps.

**395. The Quaking Grass** — Seed heads tremble in the slightest air movement; visible trembling before other plants show wind.

**396. The Tufted Hair-grass** — Dense tufts; found in the moister sections. Very tough.

**397. The Bottlebrush Grass** — Seed head resembles a bottlebrush. Found under broad-leaved trees specifically.

**398. The Woodland Rye** — Related to cultivated rye; found in disturbed areas and old settlement sites.

**399. The Forest Bluegrass** — Blue-green; fine-bladed; used as a ground cover in forest peoples' settlement areas.

**400. The Giant Wild Rye** (*vel-goreth-thal-kel*) — Reaches three meters in favorable conditions. Forms dense stands in wet, light-gap areas.

**401. The Slender Sedge** (*vel-thal-kel-dreth*) — The most common sedge in the Ibenwood. Grows wherever ground moisture is moderate to high.

**402. The Bladder Sedge** — Inflated fruit enclosures visible. Found in wet conditions.

**403. The Tussock Sedge** — Forms dense tussocks; walking across a tussock sedge stand is difficult.

**404. The Fox Sedge** — Dense, bristly-looking. Found along stream margins.

**405. The Hop Sedge** — Fruit clusters resembling hops. Found in moderate moisture conditions.

**406. The Broad-leaf Sedge** — Unusually broad leaves for a sedge. Tolerates lower light than most.

**407. The Porcupine Sedge** — Spiny-looking fruit clusters. Very common in wet areas.

**408. The Creeping Sedge** — Spreads by runners; colonizes disturbed wet soil rapidly.

**409. The Gray Sedge** (*skort-thal-kel*) — Grey-blue coloration. Found in cooler, shadier conditions toward the Oremindi margin.

**410. The Lake Sedge** (*vel-thal-kel-goreth*) — Found at standing water margins. Provides significant cover for nesting birds.

**411. The Soft Rush** (*drevel-thal-kel*) — Very common in wet conditions. Used for weaving in forest peoples' material culture.

**412. The Hard Rush** (*gor-thal-kel*) — Less flexible than Soft Rush; used where rigidity is required.

**413. The Jointed Rush** — Distinct joints visible in the stem; makes a faint crackling sound when bent.

**414. The Bulbous Rush** — Bulbous stem base; grows in specific water chemistry conditions.

**415. The Toad Rush** — Small, found in disturbed wet areas. Rapid colonizer.

**416. The Spiral Rush** (*skel-thal-kel*) — Stems corkscrew naturally. Unusual; used decoratively.

**417. The Woodrush** — The only rush that grows in drier, shaded conditions. Hairy leaves.

**418. The Great Woodrush** (*vel-goreth-thal*) — Larger than normal woodrush; found in the deeper forest zones.

**419. The Forest Reed** (*vel-thal-vel-kel*) — Found in wet forest depressions. Taller than most Ibenwood reeds.

**420. The Giant Reed** — Extremely tall; found in the wettest sections. Provides nesting material for large birds.

**421. The Narrow-leaf Cattail** (*thal-vel-kel-dren*) — Found in standing water. Multiple food uses: pollen, young shoots, roots.

**422. The Broad-leaf Cattail** — Found in deeper water conditions.

**423. The Sweet Flag** (*vel-thos-thal-kel*) — Aromatic; the rhizome used as a spice and in preparations.

**424. The Yellow Iris** (*bethren-dren-thal*) — Found in standing water margins. Toxic root; the flowers are used decoratively.

**425. The Blue Flag Iris** — Deep blue; found in wet conditions. More vivid than surface iris species.

**426. The Wild Grass-of-Parnassus** — White flowers with green veining; found in specific nutrient-poor wet conditions.

**427. The Cottongrass** (*beth-thal-kel*) — White fluffy seed heads; found in boggy depressions. The fluff used for fire tinder.

**428. The Hare's-tail Cottongrass** — Single fluffy head per stem; more common than multi-headed cottongrass.

**429. The Purple Moor-grass** — Found near the eastern margins; indicator of specific drainage conditions.

**430. The Deer Grass** — Palatable to deer; maintains a relationship with deer browsing that keeps it more common than it would be without them.

## Part VII — Vines, Creepers, and Climbers

The climbing plants of the Ibenwood exploit the vertical structure of the trees as growing space. The largest vines are very old; some have been growing continuously for longer than the recorded use of any building they now cover.

**431. The Dark Ivy** (*vel-thren-thal-thos*) — Covers the lower trunks of every major canopy tree. Very old colonies are several centimeters thick; the wood within is structurally distinct from young ivy.

**432. The Copper Vine** — Copper-colored autumn foliage. Vivid against dark bark.

**433. The Wild Grape** (*dren-thos-vel-thal*) — Fruit smaller and more tart than cultivated varieties. Used by forest peoples for fermented preparations.

**434. The Bitter Sweet** — Clusters of orange-yellow capsules revealing red seeds in autumn. Highly toxic.

**435. The Wild Clematis** — White flowers in summer; feathery seed heads in autumn. Fragrant.

**436. The Virgin's Bower** — Rapid-growing clematis; covers structures quickly.

**437. The Poison Ivy** (*vel-gor-thal-thos*) — Causes severe skin reaction in most outsiders. Forest peoples appear habituated. Stands are known and marked.

**438. The Trumpet Vine** (*vel-dren-thal-thos*) — Orange-red tubular flowers. Found in better-lit sections near the margin.

**439. The Cross Vine** — Cross-sectioned stem reveals a cross pattern. The pattern is consistent and precise.

**440. The Pipevine** — Unusual pipe-shaped flowers. Specific pollinator interaction involving trapped insects.

**441. The Wild Hops** — Used in fermented preparations throughout the western Azhoran coastal region. Forest peoples manage wild stands.

**442. The Moonvine** (*vel-beth-thal-thos*) — White flowers that open at night. Fragrant only in darkness.

**443. The Forest Morning Glory** — Purple-blue; flowers in light gaps and margins only.

**444. The Twining Bindweed** — White flowers; climbs by twining rather than adhesion. Somewhat invasive in disturbed areas.

**445. The Giant Bindweed** (*vel-goreth-thal-thos*) — Larger-flowered bindweed found in specific moist conditions.

**446. The Yellow Honeysuckle** — Fragrant; yellow flowers. The nectar is palatable and harvested by forest peoples.

**447. The Hairy Honeysuckle** — Hairy stems and leaves. Found in shadier conditions than yellow honeysuckle.

**448. The Wild Cucumber** — Prickly fruit; edible when young. Grows rapidly in disturbed areas and light gaps.

**449. The Climbing Nightshade** — Purple flowers, red berries when ripe. Toxic.

**450. The Gall-of-the-Earth** — Bitter root used in forest peoples' preparations. The bitterness is not reduced by cooking.

**451. The Forest Wisteria** (*vel-drevel-thal-thos*) — Purple-flowered; fragrant; found in better-lit conditions at the forest margin. Very old specimens have trunks as large as small trees.

**452. The Wild Sarsaparilla Vine** — Root used as flavoring. Distinguishable from the herbaceous wild sarsaparilla by growth form.

**453. The Indian Hemp** (*vel-gor-thal-kel*) — Bast fibers used in rope-making and weaving.

**454. The Climbing Wild Hydrangea** — White flowers in flat-topped clusters. Climbs by attaching rootlets to bark.

**455. The Virginia Creeper** — Red autumn color. The five-leaflet structure is distinctive.

**456. The Raccoon Grape** — Small sour grapes; more important as animal food than human food.

**457. The Frost Grape** (*skort-dren-thal-thos*) — Fruit improves after frost; harvested late in the year.

**458. The Silk Vine** (*beth-drevel-thal-thos*) — Produces silk-like seed fiber. Used in textile production by forest peoples.

**459. The Rubber Vine** (*gor-thal-thos*) — Milky latex from cut stems. Used as a sealant and adhesive.

**460. The Bittersweet Nightshade** (*vel-thren-dren-thal*) — Not the same as Bittersweet; this nightshade has both bitter and sweet properties in sequence when tasted.

**461. The Forest Grape** (*vel-dren-thal-vel*) — Deep purple, larger than most wild grapes. The preferred grape for forest peoples' fermented preparations.

**462. The Climbing Fern** (vine form) — Produces climbing fronds distinct from the ground form; same species, different growth habit.

**463. The Thread-stem Vine** — Extremely thin stems; almost invisible. Found woven through other climbing plants.

**464. The Red-flower Vine** (*vel-dren-thal-thos*) — Bright red flowers; very visible. Found in specific light conditions.

**465. The White-stem Creeper** (*beth-thal-thos*) — White stems that look dead but are not. Very effective camouflage against pale bark.

**466. The Thorned Creeper** (*gor-thal-thos-kel*) — Thorns on the climbing stem make removal from clothing very difficult.

**467. The Spinning Vine** — Twines around its support in unusually tight spirals.

**468. The Mile-a-Minute Vine** — Named for apparent growth speed; extremely fast-growing in disturbed conditions.

**469. The Clinging Vine** (*vel-thal-thos-vel*) — Adhesion so complete that the vine cannot be removed from stone without taking the stone surface. Used in construction.

**470. The Flat-leaf Creeper** — Leaves pressed absolutely flat; the vine appears two-dimensional on bark surfaces.

**471. The Smoke-stem Creeper** (*dorvem-thal-thos*) — Found on Smokewood trees specifically; appears to share the Smokewood's bark chemistry.

**472. The Needle Vine** — Produces fine needle-like projections on the stem that deter grazing.

**473. The Forest Clematis** (*vel-drevel-dren-thal*) — Larger-flowered than the Wild Clematis; found in specific interior conditions.

**474. The Moss Creeper** — Grows over and through moss beds; the distinction between vine and moss layer becomes unclear.

**475. The Bark Splitter** — Forces its roots into bark crevices; can split young bark. Very old specimens split the bark of old trees without killing them.

**476. The Pale Moonvine** (*beth-vel-thal*) — Like the Moonvine but paler flowers, found deeper in the interior.

**477. The Gold-leaf Creeper** — Golden autumn color; the most vivid autumn color of any climber.

**478. The Velvet Vine** — Covered in fine soft hairs; pleasant to touch.

**479. The Water Rope Vine** (*thal-vel-thal-thos*) — Found exclusively near running water; produces hanging stems that trail in the current.

**480. The Strangler Fig** (*vel-thren-thal-thos*) — Begins as an epiphyte; eventually surrounds the host tree. Unlike the Strangler tree, this is an actual strangler; it can kill its host.

## Part VIII — Fungi and Mycelial Organisms

*Note: The Academy does not classify fungi as plants. They are included in this catalogue because Forest Mittoli naturalists include them, and because their role in the Ibenwood's ecology is so fundamental that a flora catalogue omitting them would describe approximately half of the forest's biological reality.*

**481. The Death Cap** (*vel-thren-kel-vel*) — Highly toxic; responsible for most serious poisonings of outside visitors. Forest peoples identify it precisely and reliably. Outsiders frequently mistake it for edible species.

**483. The Forest Chanterelle** (*dren-kel-vel-thos*) — Golden, edible, delicious. Very valuable in trade. Forest peoples harvest and dry them for export.

**484. The Black Chanterelle** (*thren-kel-vel-thos*) — Black version; found in the deeper interior. More intensely flavored than the gold variety.

**485. The Hen of the Woods** (*vel-goreth-kel*) — Large, compound fruiting body; very meaty; significant food source. Found at the base of old trees.

**486. The Chicken of the Woods** — Bright orange; found on old wood. Edible; prized.

**487. The Puffball** — When mature, releases a cloud of spores. Children in forest peoples' settlements use mature puffballs as toys.

**488. The Giant Puffball** (*vel-goreth-kel-vel*) — Reaches the size of a large melon. Edible when white throughout; a single specimen is a significant food resource.

**489. The King Bolete** (*vel-goreth-vel-kel*) — The most prized edible fungus in the Ibenwood. Very valuable; specific harvesting knowledge required to find reliable patches.

**490. The Weeping Bolete** (*drevel-kel-vel*) — Pores that drip a coloured liquid. Colour varies between yellow, blue and red depending on specimen and season. Edible.

**491. The Velvet Shank** — Orange-tan cap; grows in clusters on dead wood through winter. The only mushroom fruiting in the coldest conditions.

**492. The Oyster Mushroom** (*vel-drevel-kel*) — Fan-shaped, white to grey; grows on dead wood. Very good eating; widely harvested.

**493. The Pink Oyster** — Related; found specifically on certain dead tree species.

**494. The King Stropharia** (*vel-goreth-dren-kel*) — Enormous for a woodland stropharia; purple-brown cap with white scales. Edible; requires careful identification.

**495. The Honey Mushroom** (*vel-dren-kel-thos*) — Grows in dense clusters on tree bases; mildly toxic raw but edible cooked. Indicates the presence of the Armillaria mycelium network beneath the forest, which is among the largest organisms in the Ibenwood.

**496. The Milk Cap** (*vel-thal-kel*) — Exudes milk when cut; flavor varies by species.

**497. The Saffron Milk Cap** — Orange milk; edible; prized.

**498. The Blue Milk Cap** — Blue milk; less common; less prized for eating but significant in forest peoples' dyeing practice.

**499. The Woolly Milk Cap** — Hairy cap; very hot flavor; used as a spice in small quantities.

**500. The Indigo Milk Cap** (*vel-bethren-kel*) — Vivid blue milk; distinct. Found under specific trees.

**501. The Russula Family** — A group of approximately thirty Ibenwood species catalogued collectively here due to the difficulty of distinguishing them without extended field experience. Colors range from white through yellow, green, red, and purple. Some edible, some toxic; distinguishing requires knowledge that takes years to acquire.

**502. The Fly Agaric** — Red cap with white spots; the most visually distinctive mushroom in the Ibenwood. Toxic; used in very specific Forest Mittoli ceremonial contexts. Not shared with outside practitioners.

**503. The Panther Cap** — Brown, toxic, frequently confused with edible species. Forest peoples' warnings about it are consistent and emphatic.

**504. The Blusher** — Cap that changes color when cut. Edible when the flesh is white; toxic when it blushes blue.

**505. The Grisette** — Slender, cap-shaped; edible. Found in specific older growth sections.

**506. The Tawny Grisette** — Related; tawny-orange cap. Edible.

**507. The Sheathed Woodtuft** (*vel-gor-kel-thal*) — Found on decaying wood; grows in large clusters. Edible; significant autumn food source.

**508. The Wood Blewit** (*vel-thren-kel-vel*) — Lilac to purple; the color is distinctive. Edible; prized; found in late autumn through early winter.

**509. The Field Blewit** — More blue-grey; edible; found in specific conditions.

**510. The Scarlet Elf Cup** (*dren-kel-vel-beth*) — Bright red, cup-shaped, found on wet wood in winter and early spring. Very striking; very small.

**511. The Orange Peel Fungus** — Orange, ruffled, appears on disturbed soil. Non-toxic; minimal food value.

**512. The Saddle Mushroom** (*vel-skel-kel*) — Cap saddle-shaped; edible; found in autumn.

**513. The Brain Fungus** — Cap folded like a brain. Edible when correctly prepared; problematic raw.

**514. The Black Morel** (*thren-kel-vel*) — Black morel; edible; found in specific conditions after forest disturbance.

**515. The Yellow Morel** (*bethren-kel-vel*) — Spring; edible; extremely prized. Finding productive patches requires experience.

**516. The Giant Morel** (*vel-goreth-kel-vel*) — Reaches twenty centimeters. Very significant food resource in good morel years.

**517. The Beefsteak Fungus** — Liver-colored; grows on old Oak and related trees. Red liquid from cut surface. Edible.

**518. The Jelly Ear** (*vel-thal-kel-dren*) — Brown, ear-shaped, jelly-like. Grows on elder. Edible; flavorless; valued for texture.

**519. The Yellow Brain** — Yellow, brain-like, jelly-like. Found on dead wood.

**520. The Crystal Brain** (*beth-vel-kel-thal*) — Nearly transparent. Found on dead wood in high humidity conditions.

**521. The Black Jelly Roll** — Black, gelatinous. Not edible; significant decomposer role.

**522. The Witches' Butter** (*vel-thren-kel-thal*) — Bright orange-yellow jelly; appears overnight on dead wood after rain. Looks more alarming than it is.

**523. The Forest Earthstar** (*vel-gor-drev-vel*) — Star-shaped outer layer opens to reveal spore sac. Found in specific old-growth conditions.

**524. The Collared Earthstar** — Earth star with a distinct collar structure. Rare.

**525. The Sessile Earthstar** — Without a stalk; resting directly on soil. Found in specific dry forest floor conditions.

**528. The Elegant Stinkhorn** — More cylindrical form; same dispersal mechanism.

**529. The Dog Stinkhorn** — Smaller; the smell is particularly persistent.

**530. The Cage Fungus** (*vel-thren-kel-gor*) — Cage-like structure; found in the interior sections. The structural geometry has been studied; it is not simple.

**532. The Velvet Shield** (*vel-thren-kel*) — Dark velvet cap; gills white; found on specific dead wood. Toxic to outsiders; consumed in small quantities by forest peoples.

## Part IX — Aquatic and Riparian Plants

Standing and flowing water within the Ibenwood hosts its own distinct flora. The combination of the Ibenwood's water chemistry — modified by passage through ancient root systems and mineral-bearing soils — and the protection from direct sunlight produces conditions found nowhere outside the forest.

**601. The Water Crowfoot** (*thal-dren-vel*) — White flowers floating on water surfaces in the still sections of the Ibenwood's streams.

**602. The Broad-leaf Pondweed** (*vel-thal-kel*) — Floating leaves; submerged leaves also present. Common in slow-moving water.

**603. The Opposite-leaf Pondweed** — Narrow; submerged; found in faster water than the broad-leaf variety.

**604. The Curly Pondweed** — Curled leaves; found in clear, slow water.

**605. The Horned Pondweed** — Found in water with specific mineral content.

**606. The Water Plantain** (*thal-kel-vel*) — Found in shallow water margins. Arrow-shaped leaves above water; different form below.

**607. The Narrow Water Plantain** — More slender form of the same ecological niche.

**608. The Arrowhead** (*vel-gor-thal-dren*) — Arrowhead-shaped emerged leaves; different form for submerged leaves; very different form for floating leaves. Three leaf forms on one plant.

**609. The Bog Pondweed** — Found in acidic boggy conditions. Leaves very small.

**610. The Water Violet** (*beth-thal-dren*) — Pale violet flowers above water; feathery submerged leaves.

**611. The Water Starwort** — Star-shaped floating rosettes of tiny leaves. Common in slow water.

**612. The Water Milfoil** — Feathery submerged leaves; extends above water to flower. Creates dense underwater structure.

**613. The Hornwort** (aquatic form) — Entirely submerged. No roots; free-floating. Found in still water.

**614. The River Water Crowfoot** — Found in faster-flowing water than the ordinary water crowfoot. Leaves elongated by current.

**615. The Bladderwort** (*vel-thren-thal-kel*) — Carnivorous; microscopic bladders on submerged leaves trap tiny animals. Flowers yellow, above water.

**616. The Greater Bladderwort** — Larger form; same mechanism; found in more open boggy conditions.

**617. The Water Lobelia** — Submerged leaves in a basal rosette; pale flowers on a stem above water.

**618. The Quillwort** (riparian form) — Grows submerged in still to slow water. The riparian distinction separates it from the fern-ally quillworts listed earlier.

**619. The Pillwort** — Tiny, looks like a grass; produces spore-bearing structures resembling small pills.

**620. The Floating Water Fern** (*thal-kel-vel-dreth*) — Small; free-floating on still water. Covers pool surfaces in the interior's still water sections.

**621. The Water Horsetail** (*thal-vel-kel-goreth*) — Found in standing water; reaches a meter above the surface.

**622. The Mare's-tail** — Whorled leaves; upright; found in still to slow water.

**623. The Bur-reed** (*thal-gor-kel*) — Spiky round fruit heads. Found in shallow water margins.

**624. The Branched Bur-reed** — More branched form of the bur-reed. Found in deeper water.

**625. The Bogbean** (*thal-vel-kel-dren*) — Distinctive white fringed flowers. Found in bogs and shallow water. Used in Forest Mittoli preparations.

**626. The Frogbit** — Small floating plant resembling a tiny lily. Covers still pool surfaces.

**627. The Water Hyacinth** (*vel-dren-thal-vel*) — More vivid color than coastal water hyacinths. Found in the interior's warmer still pools.

**628. The Yellow Water Lily** (*bethren-thal-dren-kel*) — Found in specific deeper pools. Very large leaves.

**629. The White Water Lily** (*beth-thal-dren-kel*) — The largest flower in the Ibenwood's still water. Found in the interior pools.

**630. The Deep Water Lily** (*vel-beth-thal-dren*) — Found only in the deepest interior pools. The flower closes during storms and reopens precisely twelve hours later.

**631. The Water Soldier** — Rises to the surface to flower, sinks when done. The movement is dramatic.

**632. The Cape Pondweed** (*vel-thal-dren-kel*) — Found in the warmer southern sections of the Ibenwood water system.

**633. The Water Mint** (*thal-thos-vel*) — Intensely aromatic; found at water margins. The most widely traded flavoring from the Ibenwood.

**634. The Brooklime** — Blue flowers; found in slow streams and seeps. Edible.

**635. The Water Forget-me-not** (*thal-beth-dren-kel*) — Blue; found at stream margins. Very small but vivid.

**636. The Monkeyflower** — Yellow-spotted flowers; found along stream banks.

**637. The Water Cress** (*thal-thos-kel-vel*) — Edible; harvested throughout the forest range. The Ibenwood variety is more peppery than others.

**639. The Water Speedwell** — Blue flowers; found in shallow streams and seeps.

**640. The Marsh Violet** (*thal-thren-dren*) — Purple; found in wet, shaded conditions.

## Part X — Parasitic, Saprophytic, and Achlorophyllous Plants

Plants that have abandoned photosynthesis entirely, relying instead on other organisms for nutrition. The Ibenwood has an unusually high diversity of these plants, which the Academy attributes to the extraordinary density and diversity of the host organisms available.

**641. The Ghost Plant** (*beth-vel-dren-vel*) — White, waxy, entirely saprophytic. Absorbs nutrition from fungal networks. Found in the deepest interior conditions.

**642. The Pinesap** — Reddish-yellow, saprophytic. Found under specific tree associations.

**643. The Indian Pipe** (*beth-vel-thal-kel*) — White, pipe-shaped, saprophytic. Turns black when picked. Very striking.

**644. The Beechdrops** — Brown, saprophytic on specific root species. Found only where the host root is present.

**645. The Squawroot** — Yellow-brown, parasitic on oak roots. Looks like a pine cone.

**646. The Cancer Root** (*vel-thren-thal-kel*) — Parasitic on the roots of various forest plants. Named for its appearance, not its properties.

**647. The Wood Broomrape** (*vel-thren-kel-vel*) — Brown, leafless, parasitic on the roots of specific woodland herbs.

**648. The Ivy Broomrape** (*vel-thal-thos-kel*) — Parasitic specifically on Ibenwood ivy roots.

**649. The Toothwort** (*vel-gor-kel-dren*) — White to pink, parasitic on tree roots. Found in specific high-quality soil conditions.

**650. The Common Dodder** (*vel-thren-thal-thos*) — Orange, thread-like, wraps around and parasitizes host plants. Produces masses of thread that can cover large areas of host vegetation.

**651. The Greater Dodder** — Larger-stemmed than Common Dodder. Attacks larger host plants.

**652. The Forest Dodder** (*vel-thren-vel-thos*) — Found deeper in the interior; attacks specific host species.

**653. The Mistletoe** (*vel-beth-thal-kel*) — Semi-parasitic; green, photosynthesizes partly; found in the upper canopy. Berries produced in winter.

**654. The Dwarf Mistletoe** — Found on specific conifers. More aggressively parasitic than common mistletoe.

**655. The Viscum** — A distinct mistletoe relative found in the Ibenwood's interior; different chemistry from the common form.

**656. The Rattle** (*vel-gor-thal-dren*) — Hemi-parasitic on grass roots; yellow flowers.

**657. The Eyebright** (*beth-vel-dren-kel*) — Hemi-parasitic; found at forest margins. Used in Forest Mittoli eye preparations.

**658. The Lousewort** — Hemi-parasitic; pink flowers. Found in specific wet conditions.

**659. The Marsh Lousewort** — Found in wet interior sections.

**660. The Red Bartsia** — Hemi-parasitic; red-pink. Found in forest margin conditions.

**661. The Yellow Rattle** — Seeds rattle in the pod; hemi-parasitic on grasses. Characteristic sound in wind.

**662. The Cow-wheat** — Hemi-parasitic on forest herbs; yellow-white flowers.

**663. The Crested Cow-wheat** — More ornate flower; found in specific old-growth conditions.

**664. The Wood Cow-wheat** — Found specifically in forest interior rather than margin. The most important of the cow-wheat group in deep forest.

**665. The Coralroot** (*vel-gor-kel-morv*) — Saprophytic orchid; entirely leafless; brown. Depends entirely on fungal networks. Found at very specific interior locations. Forest peoples know the locations.

**666. The Spurred Coralroot** — Related; slightly different form; specific habitat.

**667. The Autumn Coralroot** (*vel-thren-kel-morv*) — Fruits in autumn specifically. The most intensely colored coralroot, deep red-brown.

**668. The Western Coralroot** — Found in the Oremindi-facing sections of the Ibenwood specifically.

**669. The Pale Ghost Orchid** (*beth-vel-dren-kel-vel*) — The rarest documented plant in the Ibenwood. Entirely white; entirely saprophytic; flowering unpredictable and infrequent. Forest peoples do not discuss it with outside parties.

**670. The Potato Root Parasite** (*vel-thal-kel-thren*) — Parasitizes the roots of the wild potato relatives found near the forest margin. Reduces host vigor noticeably.

## Part XI — Anomalous and Unclassified Plants

Plants that cannot be placed in existing categories, whose behavior does not conform to expectations, or whose very existence has been noted with the particular scholarly discomfort that comes from careful observation producing no explanation.

**671. The Counting Grass** (*vel-kel-thal-dren*) — Produces seed heads where the seeds are arranged in precise sequences. The sequence is the same across all specimens. Forest peoples say the sequence is a number. The number has not been established.

**672. The Listening Herb** (*vel-thren-dren-kel*) — No visible unusual properties. Forest Mittoli practitioners describe it as being aware of visitors. Outside observers describe noticing it specifically when they have said something significant. The correlation is not statistically established but is consistently reported.

**673. The Bleeding Ground Moss** (*vel-dren-kel-thal*) — When soil is disturbed above its network, it produces a red exudate that surfaces through the soil. Forest peoples treat this as a signal that the soil has been injured.

**674. The Memory Plant** (*vel-morv-dren-kel*) — A low herb that Forest Mittoli practitioners say records what happens around it. The mechanism, if any, is not available to outside study. The claim is consistent across different communities.

**675. The Direction Plant** — Grows toward something that is not the sun. The direction varies by location but is consistent for each plant throughout its life. Forest peoples navigate by it in conditions where other navigation fails.

**676. The Mirror Orchid** (*vel-beth-dren-kel*) — A saprophytic orchid that produces no flowers visible in ordinary light; its flowers are only visible in certain conditions of light that occur naturally in the forest interior and that the Academy has had difficulty replicating in the field.

**677. The Double-lived Herb** (*vel-sorthven-dren*) — Appears to be two different plants at different times of year; the appearance changes so completely that early Academy records listed it as two species. Forest peoples find the Academy's confusion about this amusing.

**678. The Silent Flower** (*vel-thren-dren-vel*) — Flowers that produce no scent, no visible attractant for pollinators, and no obvious self-pollination mechanism. The plant reproduces. How is not established.

**679. The Fire Follower** (*vel-dren-kel-goreth*) — Germinates only after fire. Fire is infrequent in the Ibenwood; this plant is rare; its appearance after an unusual fire event confirms the association.

**680. The Shade Lover** (*vel-thren-kel-thal*) — Grows only where no direct light has ever reached, not just where light does not currently reach. Finding appropriate sites for transplanting this plant has consistently failed even in apparently identical conditions; the failure appears to relate to the history of the site rather than its current state.

**681. The Dew Collector** (*vel-thal-beth-dren*) — Leaf surface geometry produces dramatic dew concentration; produces drinking water from air in conditions of sufficient humidity. Forest peoples use it as a water source in the deep interior where streams are few.

**682. The Night Bloomer** (*vel-beth-dren-vel*) — A herb (not the tree Moonvine) that flowers only at night; the flowers are light-producing. The light is blue-white. Photographs and detailed descriptions of night-blooming events match with extraordinary precision across accounts decades apart.

**683. The Stone-seeker** — Roots grow toward stone rather than away from it. All other plants avoid stone; the Stone-seeker finds it.

**684. The Frozen Herb** (*vel-skort-dren*) — Remains frozen even when surrounding temperature rises above freezing. The tissue remains viable. The mechanism for maintaining lower-than-ambient tissue temperature is unknown.

**685. The Sound Plant** (*vel-thren-vel-dren*) — Makes sounds. Not sounds from physical interaction with wind or animals; spontaneous sounds at irregular intervals that Forest Mittoli practitioners say form meaningful sequences. Whether the sounds are meaningful to anything other than Forest Mittoli practitioners has not been established.

**686. The Weeping Herb** (*vel-drevel-dren*) — Produces droplets from its leaves continuously in the absence of dew or rain. Analysis of the liquid reveals nothing unusual. The quantity produced is inconsistent with normal plant water loss.

**687. The Turning Flower** (*vel-skel-dren-vel*) — Flowers that rotate continuously throughout the day. The rotation is not solar tracking; it continues in complete dark. The axis of rotation varies between specimens.

**688. The Copper Root** (*vel-gor-thal-dren-kel*) — Root has metallic copper properties. Conducts electricity. The Academy is not certain how to classify this.

**689. The Deep Dark** (*vel-vel-thren-kel*) — A flat, black, seemingly inorganic crust on the forest floor that is not lichen, not fungus, and not any identified plant family. Forest Mittoli practitioners say it is the oldest living thing in the Ibenwood. The Academy has no position on this.

**690. The Ancestor Plant** (*morvendreth-dren*) — Described by Forest Mittoli oral tradition as the original plant of the Ibenwood, from which all others derive. Physically it resembles an unremarkable small herb. Nothing distinguishes it from similar plants to outside observation. Forest Mittoli practitioners distinguish it immediately and consistently.

**691. The Rain Caller** (*vel-thal-vel-dren*) — Produces a smell before rain that forest peoples use to predict precipitation. The smell precedes rain by a consistent interval. Whether the plant is responding to atmospheric conditions too subtle for human instruments, or doing something else, has not been determined.

**692. The Warm Spot Herb** (*vel-dren-kel-skort*) — Maintains the soil around it at a measurably higher temperature than the surroundings. Small animals shelter against it in cold conditions. The energy source for this heating is unidentified.

**693. The Moon Follower** (*vel-beth-kel-dren*) — Tracks the moon rather than the sun. In conditions where no moon is visible, its orientation is toward where the moon is below the horizon. The mechanism is not established.

**694. The Ground Speaker** (*vel-thal-vel-gor-dren*) — Forest Mittoli practitioners say this plant relays information from the tree root network to the surface. The claim is not testable by outside methods. Forest peoples consult specific Ground Speaker stands at specific times.

**695. The Unnamed Plant** (*vel-vel-vel*) — Three communities of Forest Mittoli practitioners have been shown specimens and given different names for it on different occasions, despite having consistent naming conventions for every other plant in the Ibenwood. Whether this reflects genuine uncertainty, deliberate inconsistency, or the plant actually being different things at different times is not established.

**696. The Pale Witness Herb** (*beth-vel-dren-vel*) — Named by analogy with the Pale Witness tree. Like the tree, it appears to watch. Forest peoples describe both as members of the same category, which does not align with their botanical category.

**697. The Buried Flower** (*vel-thren-dren-vel-kel*) — Flowers underground. The flower is only visible if the soil is carefully removed. How it is pollinated, if at all, has not been determined.

**698. The Cold Fire** (*vel-skort-dren-goreth*) — Appears to glow with a cold light at specific temperatures. The glow is described consistently across accounts but has not been reproduced under controlled conditions. Forest peoples consider the difficulty of controlled observation to be self-explanatory.

**699. The Deep Web** (*vel-thranvel-vel-kel*) — Believed by Forest Mittoli communities to be a surface expression of the Ibenwood's underlying mycelial network in a form that has developed some properties of a plant. The Academy's botanical division finds this claim unfalsifiable and has noted it carefully in the unresolved section.

**700. The Waiting Plant** (*vel-morv-kel-vel*) — Germinates, grows to a certain size, and stops. Not dying; not dormant in any measurable sense; simply waiting. Individual specimens have been in this state for as long as records go. Forest Mittoli practitioners say they are waiting for something specific and will recognize it when it comes. What they are waiting for has not been shared.

## Final Observations

*One thousand distinct species documented. Forest Mittoli naturalists who reviewed this draft suggested the total is closer to three thousand. The Academy notes that the forest peoples have had longer to look.*

*Entries 671–700 (the Anomalous section) represent the observations the Academy is least certain how to handle. They are documented faithfully. They resist explanation. The Ibenwood has a tradition, consistent across generations of scholarship, of containing things that resist explanation while remaining stubbornly present. The Academy has stopped finding this surprising and started finding it useful: the resistance to explanation is itself information about the nature of the place.*

*Future editions will add species. Future editions will probably not resolve the anomalous section. This is the Academy's honest expectation after two centuries of work, and it has made peace with it.*
