# Moroshé Desert

The Moroshé Desert is the largest single geographic feature of Azhora — a vast arid interior that dominates the southern half of the continent's eastern face and reaches, in its driest central sections, into some of the harshest terrain on the continent. From the outside it looks like an absence: no forest, no rain, no green. From the inside it is a world of remarkable complexity and, to those who know it, considerable beauty.

The name "Moroshé" is the Mittoli rendering of a word from the desert's own languages. What it means in those languages is variously translated as "the wide nothing," "the place of patient waiting," or — in the oldest dialect still spoken near the ancient ruins of the deep interior — simply "home." The Mittoli chose "the wide nothing." The desert people find this choice informative about the Mittoli.

## Geography

The Moroshé is not a single landscape. It ranges from the rocky hammada of the northern transition zone — flat gravel plains and exposed bedrock where scrubby thorn trees still manage to exist — through the great sand seas of the central interior, to the canyon country of the south where ancient rivers carved deep cuts through the plateau before the climate dried and the rivers mostly died.

The central sand seas are genuinely extreme. Summer temperatures reach levels that require specific behavioral adaptation to survive. Water is found at depth, in aquifer-fed oases, and by reading the landscape for the signs that experience teaches. Navigating the sand seas without local knowledge is considered one of the more reliable methods of dying on Azhora.

The canyon country is different in character — dramatic, sheltered, carved from red and orange stone that blazes in the low light of morning and evening. The canyon walls preserve ancient petroglyphs in enormous quantity, protected from rain erosion by the dry climate. Some of these predate any known Azhoran civilization and have not been translated.

## The Mediterranean Coasts

The Moroshé Desert does not extend to the coast. Where the eastern and southeastern edges of the continent meet the Iberos Sea and the southern ocean, the climate changes abruptly. The coasts of this region are Mediterranean in character — warm, dry summers, mild and moderately wet winters, the kind of light that falls at a golden angle most of the day.

This coastal strip is densely settled, lush with cultivated olive trees, vineyards, and the scrub-adapted vegetation of a Mediterranean ecosystem, and culturally distinct from the desert interior despite geographic proximity. The coastal peoples have historically seen the desert as a barrier rather than a homeland, a perspective the desert peoples have returned with interest.

The interaction of the two — desert trade routes bringing goods to coastal ports, coastal cities funding desert expeditions, and the long complex history of which has exploited which more thoroughly — is one of the defining dynamics of southern Azhoran history.

## History

The Moroshé is old in a way that the Mittoli heartland is not. The desert preserves things. The canyon walls hold petroglyphs from cultures so ancient they have no name. The buried ruins of the deep interior — partially excavated, partially guessed at from surface features — represent construction at a scale and sophistication that predates the oldest Mittoli record by thousands of years.

The ruins are not well understood. What is understood is that whoever built them had access to resources — water, food, organized labor — that the current desert cannot provide, which implies that the desert itself is a relatively recent development, geologically speaking. The land was different once. Something changed. The academics disagree about what, and when, and whether it was natural or otherwise.

The desert cultures that exist now are not the descendants of the ruin-builders in any traceable sense. They have their own origin stories, their own histories, and they regard the ruins with a respect that is not quite reverence — more like the respect you give to the evidence of a very serious warning.

**Nova Homa** is the principal city of the southern Moroshé border — a walled desert city at the junction of three ancient trade routes, old enough that its oldest districts are built on and into the ruins of something older still. The name "Nova Homa" means "new settlement on the old place" in the regional dialect, which is either evidence of historical self-awareness or evidence that the city was founded by people with a sense of irony. Possibly both.

**Tros** is a smaller city further north along the desert's western edge, known primarily as a waypoint for caravans crossing between the Mediterranean coast and the Mittoli interior. It is dusty, transient, and full of people who are going somewhere else.

## The Desert Peoples

The Moroshé is inhabited by several distinct peoples with different relationships to the land. The settled communities cluster around the oases and the canyon country. The nomadic and semi-nomadic groups move along routes that have been maintained for so long that they appear in the oldest maps as established roads, though no one built them.

Their most important large herd animal is the Moroshé dustback, the desert-edge buffalo-cattle described in **Livestock of Azhora**. Dustbacks are wealth because they convert thorn, dry basin grass, patient watering, and bad years into milk, calves, hide, traction, and caravan security. An oasis house may keep coin in locked jars, but its political standing is usually measured by how many dustbacks it can move through a dry season without begging water.

The desert languages are related to each other but not to the Mittoli family at all. They represent a completely separate linguistic lineage that predates any contact with the western continent. This fact is of significant interest to linguists and of complete indifference to the desert people, who are aware they speak differently from the westerners and consider this unsurprising.

The desert peoples have a complex relationship with the ruins. They do not live in them, do not build on them, and do not remove materials from them. They are not, by and large, willing to discuss why in detail. The short version, when pressed, is: "those places belong to something else." The long version has not been shared with outside researchers.
