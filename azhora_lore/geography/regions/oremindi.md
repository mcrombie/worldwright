# The Oremindi Mountains

The western spine of Azhora does not ascend gradually. The land rises through the forest hills of the interior and through the higher ground behind them with reasonable predictability, and then the Oremindi Mountains begin, and the reasonable stops. The scale here is different. The peaks visible from the highest passes of the transitional hills — the ones that travelers from Ibenale or the Mittolo interior reach before the mountains proper begin — are not the Oremindi. They are the approach. The Oremindi are what you see when the approach is finished and you look up.

Scholars who have visited the base camps and the lower passes and returned to write about it use similar language despite coming from different traditions. The words they reach for are: absolute. Permanent. Indifferent. One geographer from the Caeras Valley Academy wrote that the Oremindi are not hostile to human presence so much as simply not oriented toward it. The distinction mattered to her enough to include in her formal account. The mountains, in her view, did not need to oppose you. They merely continued being themselves.

## Geography

The Oremindi run along the western edge of Azhora's interior, separating the coastal regions from the continent's central plateau and the Mittolo heartland beyond. The range is long, the spine continuous except for a small number of passes, and the passes are high enough that most of them are usable only in the narrow window between the melting of the previous winter's snowfall and the arrival of the next. The seasonal window varies by year. Experienced guides speak of "open passes" and "closed passes" as weather designations rather than permanent features; a pass that is open this year may be closed for three years thereafter.

The peaks reach altitudes that produce permanent snowfield. The upper range is glaciated — sheets of ice that move at the pace of continental time, calving meltwater streams that run west to the coastal plains or east into the Mittolo drainage systems. This meltwater is the source of the forest rivers of Alezhor and the upper reaches of Ibenale's corridor streams; the mountains are the origin of the water that makes the coastal regions work, even when the coastal people have never seen them.

The lower ranges and transitional valleys contain the only significant habitation. A number of small communities exist in the protected valleys below the permanent snowline — herders, mostly, and the merchants who supply them and the specialists who serve them. These people are not numerous and they do not aggregate. The mountains do not favor large settlements at most altitudes. What the mountains favor is the specific and the self-sufficient.

The northeastern terminus of the Ibenale corridor arrives at the foot of the Oremindi where the coastal plain runs out of room: the mountains, the forest, and the sea converge at the same point. The mountain-end community at this convergence sits precisely at this junction and serves as the last lowland supply point for anyone going into the mountains and the first civilized stop for anyone coming down.

## The Passes

Entry into the Oremindi is controlled by geography more reliably than it could ever be controlled by deliberate fortification. The main passes number four. Of these, one is high enough to be impassable to most travelers without specialized preparation; one is navigable only in summer and only by experienced parties; one is seasonal and technically adequate but involves a three-day exposed traverse with no shelter; and one is Sevron's pass.

Sevron's pass — the Verath-nath in local speech, meaning approximately "the pass that receives" — is the most navigable of the four. It is still a mountain pass at significant elevation, with all the associated demands on preparation, supply, and weather judgment. It is not a comfortable route. But it is the route that has been used for as long as there is any record of the Oremindi being crossed, and it has acquired over those centuries the infrastructure that repeated use produces: way stations at appropriate intervals, shelter points cut into the rock, marked cairn routes on the exposed sections. The infrastructure is old and maintained by people who do not leave their names attached to their maintenance work. It functions.

That the other passes are not maintained in this way is a fact that observers have noted. Whether this is deliberate or simply a consequence of concentrated use is a question that different parties answer differently. The mountain communities use the navigable pass because it is the navigable pass. Sevron does not comment on the other passes.

## Sevron

The city of Sevron sits in a high valley on the interior side of the Oremindi's central section — far enough from the coastal approach to require serious commitment to reach, close enough to the main spine to be genuinely inside the mountains rather than merely near them. The valley is large enough for the city and its supporting agricultural terraces; the terraces produce food at altitude in ways that have required centuries to establish and that would take centuries to replace if they were abandoned. Sevron is not going anywhere. It has not gone anywhere for as long as its records extend, and its records extend considerably.

The city is built for altitude. The architecture is compressed and low-profile — not because the Sevronese lack ambition but because the wind at this elevation is architectural. The oldest buildings are partially below ground level, using the thermal mass of the earth rather than fighting the cold with insulation. The streets are covered where they need to be. The public gathering spaces are interior. The aesthetic of the place is, from outside reports, one of extreme functional precision: everything is where it is because that is where it needs to be, and nothing is where it is because it looked well.

The city is hard to reach. Having reached it, travelers report that the difficulty of the approach was appropriate preparation. Sevron is not inhospitable — visitors who arrive by the recognized routes with evident serious purpose are received with the particular courtesy of people who understand effort because they practice it themselves. But the city is not oriented toward ease in any direction. It is oriented toward something else, and the visitor's comfort is contingent on whether they can adjust.

Outside Azhora, Sevron is mostly unknown. Within the continent, it is known primarily through the traditions that have descended from it — which is to say that most people who have heard of Sevron have heard of it backwards, encountering one of the lowland practices that traces its lineage to the mountains and only later, if ever, learning where that lineage begins.

## The Verath

What Sevron has preserved, and what the mountains have been the occasion for, is called the Verath — a Sevronese word that resists clean translation into Mittoli or any other Azhoran language because the concept it names does not have an exact equivalent outside the practice itself. The scholarly approximations used by outside researchers include "the integrated discipline," "the mountain way," and "the practice," none of which are wrong and none of which are adequate.

The Verath is four things simultaneously, and the Sevronese position — to the extent that they state it, which is not often — is that separating the four is the fundamental error that the lowland traditions have all made. The four aspects are: the physical (breath, cold endurance, the exact control of what the body does and does not do), the mental (precision of attention, the specific quality of sustained concentration that they distinguish sharply from general intelligence), the spiritual (the relationship between the practitioner and the mountains as sacred geography — not metaphorically, but as a literal ongoing encounter), and the sorcerous (real effects in the world, including survival of conditions that should not be survivable, perception at a range and depth that training does not otherwise produce, and healing of the specific kind that requires understanding what a body is doing rather than what it should be doing). These four are not four things. They are four facets of one thing, and the one thing is what the Verath teaches. The facets are how it gets described.

The cold is the teacher. This is not poetic language. The specific cold of the Oremindi — altitude cold, dry cold, the cold that is present even in midsummer above the snowline — does something to the body and attention that cannot be replicated in warmer conditions. The physical practice is calibrated to what cold demands and what cold reveals. The sorcerous effects are achieved at temperatures that practitioners in lowland sects have spent centuries attempting to substitute for with other methods. The results are not identical. Sevronese practitioners who are aware of lowland sect work — which is not all of them, and not their primary concern — describe the results as "adjacent." Something in the same direction, achieved by a different route, arriving somewhere near but not at the same place.

The lineage in Sevron is unbroken. This matters to the Sevronese and the reasons it matters are not entirely communicable to outside scholars. The unbroken nature is not a claim about institutional continuity — teachers and students, records and authority — though it is also that. It is a claim about something within the practice that must be transmitted directly and cannot be reconstructed from written description, that gets thinner with each generation of removed transmission, and that is still present in Sevron and diminishing in all the traditions that have descended from it. The Sevronese describe this without distress. They describe their own position with equanimity. They also do not seek to correct the lowland sects, which they could do, if they wanted to.

They do not want to.

## The Sects Below the Mountains

The traditions that have descended from the Verath are numerous enough that outside scholars have given them a collective name — "the Verath-derived traditions," though individual practitioners of each tradition generally do not use this term and some would object to it. The traditions have spread across several regions of Azhora, are present in a few contexts outside it, and are various enough in their current form that a scholar encountering two of them in succession might not immediately identify them as related.

They are related. The shared elements — certain physical postures, certain breath practices, specific attention instructions that appear in recognizable form across traditions that have otherwise diverged completely — trace back to practitioners who came down from the Oremindi and carried fragments of what they had learned into contexts where it could be taught and practiced without the mountains. The fragments were genuine. What got lost was the integration: each tradition captured one of the four aspects, or at most one and a half, and built its practice around that capture.

The physical traditions emphasize breath and cold endurance — cold being simulated by various methods in lowland contexts, none of which quite work, though practitioners develop genuine capacities that are useful and real. The mental traditions are the most widely distributed; the attention practices of the Verath travel well, perhaps because attention does not require altitude, and several of the larger mental-tradition sects are practiced in cities far from the mountains. The spiritual traditions have the most varied interpretations of what the Sevronese originally intended; the relationship to sacred geography has been reconstructed around other geographies, producing traditions that preserve the form of the relationship while substituting different content. The sorcerous traditions are the most secretive, possess the most dramatic claims, and achieve the most inconsistent results. Practitioners of the sorcerous traditions know that something is missing. They theorize about what it is. Most of their theories are interesting and wrong.

The lowland sects are mostly unaware that they are incomplete. They experience themselves as practicing the Verath or something derivative of it, and their practices are functional and produce real effects, and the people who practice them are serious and in most cases genuinely changed by the practice. The question of completeness is not available to them from inside the traditions they are in. A few practitioners have, over the centuries, experienced something inside their practice — a direction the practice seems to point without arriving — and have traced the pointing backward and found their way to the Oremindi and made the ascent to Sevron. These people are received in Sevron. What happens when they arrive is not reported in any outside source. They do not, in the main, return to the traditions they came from.

## Sevron and the World

Sevron does not trade in the ordinary sense. The city's self-sufficiency is not a political statement but a practical consequence of where it is: provisioning a high mountain city through difficult passes is inherently different from provisioning a coastal port, and Sevron has organized itself over centuries to not require what it cannot reliably receive. The agricultural terraces, the preserved food stores, the particular Sevronese relationship with scarcity — all of these reflect the arithmetic of high-altitude subsistence.

What Sevron does occasionally send out is people. Not emissaries, not ambassadors, not missionaries. The Verath has never sought expansion; the lowland traditions that call themselves Verath-derived are all products of practitioners who left Sevron of their own will and carried what they knew to where they were going. Occasionally a Sevronese practitioner leaves. The city does not prevent this. The city also does not maintain ongoing relationships with the traditions that descend from departed practitioners; the connection, if any, is in the practice and not in any institution.

From outside, Sevron appears to have neither ambitions nor vulnerabilities. Powers that have looked at the mountain approach and calculated whether conquest was feasible have concluded that it was not, and that the strategic value of what was at the top of those passes did not repay the attempt. This calculation has been made and remade enough times that it now functions as tradition rather than strategy. Sevron has not been attacked in living memory and may not have been attacked in generational memory. The city knows this history in the way that old cities know things: not as a record that needs to be consulted but as a ground condition that shapes everything without being frequently named.

The Caeras Valley Academy has a file on Sevron. The file contains travelers' accounts, several second-hand reports, one detailed account by a geographer who reached the lower approach before altitude sickness required her to turn back, and a copy of a text that claims to be a Sevronese document of uncertain provenance. The Academy's position on the text is that it may be authentic, may be a sophisticated forgery, and that distinguishing the two from the outside is not currently possible. The file is cross-referenced with the entries on the Verath-derived traditions. The cross-references are extensive. The conclusions drawn from them are hedged.

## Language

The language of Sevron is not related to any of the five Azhoran language families. Scholars who have obtained samples — which is rare, since Sevronese practitioners who descend to the lowlands tend to learn the local language rather than teach their own — have established this negatively, by exhausting the comparison possibilities, rather than positively, by finding what it is related to. The negative establishment is thorough. Sevronese is not Forest Mittoli, not Standard Mittoli, not any known dialect of Moreshi, not Grassic, not any of the coastal trade languages. It is structurally opaque to Azhoran linguists in ways that suggest either extreme age or entirely separate development or both.

The Verath texts, to the extent that any exist in transmissible form, are in Sevronese. Translations have been attempted by practitioners who learned the language; the translations are agreed by Sevronese speakers — the few who have seen them — to be approximately correct in content and unable to transmit what the original does. Whether this is a property of translation generally or a specific property of the relationship between the language and the practice is a question that the Sevronese regard as already answered and lowland scholars regard as still open.

The word *verath* itself, borrowed into scholarly Mittoli as a technical term, appears to contain an internal structure that resists decomposition. Attempts to identify a root and a suffix have produced multiple competing analyses. The Sevronese, when this analysis project has been described to them, have been politely interested and have not helped.
