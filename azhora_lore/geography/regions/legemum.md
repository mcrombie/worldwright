# The Legemum Peninsula

Just west of the Ascarth Peninsula, where the coastline turns away from the Iberos Sea and faces the open western ocean, the land narrows again into Legemum — a shorter, wider peninsula than its neighbor, lower along the spine, greener, and wet in a way that the Iberos coast never is. Where Ascarth is rocky and sun-bleached, Legemum is rain-grey and productive. Where Ascarth faces a sheltered sea full of islands and trade routes, Legemum faces the open ocean, which has no islands worth speaking of and which the Legemi have learned to fish with equipment and techniques entirely unlike anything used on the other coast.

There are no major cities in Legemum. This is the first thing the region is known for among its neighbors — defined, in part, by what it lacks. The second thing it is known for is tin.

---

## Geography

The peninsula runs roughly northwest from its base, where a low range of hills separates it from the neck of the Ascarth Peninsula. These hills are the most significant physical feature of Legemum's interior — not dramatic, not particularly high, but mineral-rich in ways that have shaped the peninsula's character for as long as people have lived here. The streams that drain off the hills run dark with mineral content; the soil in the valley floors is heavy clay, difficult to work initially and extremely productive once broken. The upland areas, where the clay gives way to thin soils over rock, are the grazing grounds — sheep and goats in the lower reaches, cattle only where the ground is level enough to sustain them.

The coastline is the other defining feature. The western shore of Legemum takes the full force of the ocean without the shelter of islands or headlands to break it. The cliffs are high, the surf is constant, and good harbors are rare. There are natural anchorages at three points along the coast — the Legemi call them the Three Mouths — where river estuaries provide enough shelter for working boats to launch and return. None of the three is large enough for deep-draft ocean vessels to enter safely in winter weather, which is six months of the year. This has, over the centuries, conclusively settled the question of whether Legemum would develop into a maritime trading power.

The eastern shore of the peninsula, which faces the neck of Ascarth and the narrow bay between them, is calmer. Most of the Legemi population concentrates here, with easier access to the mainland and to Ascarth's markets.

## The Legemi

The people of Legemum call themselves the Legemi and their language is a dialect of western Mittoli sufficiently distinct that speakers from the Mittoli heartland, when they first arrive, take several days to adjust. The differences are mostly in the agricultural and craft vocabulary — the Legemi have words for soil conditions, rock formations, tidal patterns, and fishing techniques that standard Mittoli simply does not have, and which the Legemi have stopped apologizing for when their cousins from inland cannot follow.

The Legemi are not a single people in any historical sense that would satisfy a genealogist. The peninsula has absorbed several waves of settlement from the western interior, and the current population is a composite — visible in the variation in physical type from village to village, audible in the dialect differences between the mining settlements in the hills and the fishing communities on the coast, legible in the inconsistency of inheritance customs between the eastern and western halves of the peninsula. What makes them a distinct culture is not common origin but common circumstance: the same hills, the same rain, the same difficult coast, and several centuries of shared practical necessity.

They are, in the assessment of most outside observers, a practical people. This is meant as a compliment by some and as a mild criticism by others — the mild criticism being leveled particularly by the Avites of Ascarth, who find the Legemi's indifference to glory approximately as alien as Legemi visitors find Avite bronze-cladding on public buildings. The Legemi do not cultivate heroic reputation. They cultivate fields, mine seams, and fish carefully in water that does not forgive mistakes. When asked about this, they tend to point out that their granaries are full, which they regard as a more durable achievement than most.

## Economy

### Agriculture

The clay soils of Legemum's river valleys produce grain in quantity — emmer, barley, and in the more sheltered southern valleys a variety of wheat that the Legemi breed with care and sell at prices that reflect this. The upland grazing supports a significant wool and leather trade. Root vegetables — turnips, parsnips, and several varieties with no Mittoli names that Legemi farmers have been cultivating since before the period of written records — fill the dietary gaps that grain leaves and survive the wet winters that would rot other stores.

The agricultural calendar is organized around the guilds. The Legemum agricultural guilds are not, like their mining counterparts, organized around a single craft — they are regional bodies, covering a specific river valley or upland district, maintaining the communal infrastructure (drainage, seed stores, milling rights) that no individual farm can sustain alone. Guild membership is inherited, but guild leadership is elected annually, in a vote of all member-heads-of-household. The elected leader, called the *hannar* in the local dialect, arbitrates disputes over water rights and field boundaries, sets the collective sale price when the guild sells in bulk to outside buyers, and is personally responsible for maintaining the guild's seed stores in viable condition. The *hannar* of a guild whose seed stores fail in a bad winter carries this with them for the rest of their life.

### Mining

The hills produce tin, copper in smaller quantities, and good building stone. The tin is the significant one. Every king of Ascarth who has spent time thinking about where their bronze comes from eventually arrives at the same answer: tin comes, in large part, from Legemum. The copper is local to the Ascarth Peninsula. The tin has to be bought, and the best and closest tin is across the narrow bay in a peninsula that has no great love for Ascarth's political ambitions but a clear-eyed appreciation of its own commercial leverage.

The mining settlements of the interior hills are the peninsula's oldest permanent communities. Some of the upper workings have been in continuous operation long enough that the miners have traditions about passages opened by their grandparents' grandparents, and the ore veins those early workings followed have not been exhausted — they have deepened. Legemum's tin extraction is not industrial in scale; it is methodical, careful, and sustained, producing a reliable annual output rather than a dramatic surge.

The miners' guilds are the most formally organized institutions on the peninsula. They maintain written records of seam locations, extraction rates, and ore quality going back further than any other institution in Legemum. They have their own system of underground markers, their own protocol for when a passage becomes unsafe and must be sealed, and their own set of obligations to the dead — miners who die in the workings are interred near the entrance of the passage where they fell, and their names are added to the guild's record in a specific ink made from mineral residue of the seam they were working. The guild record is not a document most outside visitors are shown.

### Fishing

The western-coast fishing communities live differently from the farming and mining settlements — different rhythms, different equipment, different relationship to weather. The open-ocean fish that run off Legemum's coast are larger and less predictable than the sheltered-sea species of the Iberos, and the boats required to catch them in Atlantic swells are beamier, heavier, and built for endurance rather than speed. A Legemi fishing boat is not beautiful, by Iberos standards. It is very difficult to sink.

The fishing communities are the most culturally distinct part of Legemum, with the strongest claim to a continuous tradition that predates the various settlement waves that produced the current population. The coastal villages have their own names for the winds, their own lunar calendar for predicting the fish-runs, and their own set of taboos around what is said before a boat launches. They trade their catch inland for grain and metal goods, sell the surplus — smoked, salted, and dried — to the eastern Legemum markets that connect with Ascarth, and otherwise maintain a self-sufficiency that the farming communities respect and the mining settlements find excessive.

## Political Structure

Legemum has no king and has not had one in any period of its documented history. This is not a deliberate political philosophy; it is a consequence of geography. The peninsula's population is dispersed across river valleys, upland settlements, and coastal villages that have no natural center — no port large enough to anchor a maritime empire, no agricultural heartland fertile enough to sustain a capital, no single settlement dominant enough to compel the others. The closest thing to a Legemi political center is the **Council of Legemum**, which is neither a permanent body nor a governing one — it is a seasonal assembly, convened twice a year at the largest of the peninsula's neutral market sites, where guild representatives, village heads, and anyone with a legitimate grievance or proposal can speak.

The Council does not legislate. It arbitrates disputes that have escalated beyond local resolution, sets collective positions on external trade negotiations (most significantly with Ascarth), and maintains the shared infrastructure — road maintenance, bridge repair, the upkeep of the Three Mouths anchorages — that the individual communities cannot sustain without coordination. Decisions require something approaching consensus; bare majorities are considered insufficient because a bare majority leaves too many people with no obligation to comply. This makes the Council slow. The Legemi are aware of this and regard it as a reasonable price for decisions that hold.

The assembly site itself — called the **Haur** in local dialect, approximately "the flat place" — is a wide, naturally level meadow in the hills at the peninsula's center, equidistant from the three major population areas. It is used for nothing else. A market grows up around it during Council weeks, and the market is, in practice, as important as the Council — the period when all three parts of the peninsula's economy are in the same place at the same time and can trade with each other directly.

## Relationship with Ascarth

The narrow bay between Legemum's eastern shore and the Ascarth Peninsula is not wide enough to function as a serious barrier. Legemi and Avite fishing boats cross it; Legemi tin merchants cross it in larger vessels; Avite bronze traders cross it in the other direction. The two peninsulas have been in continuous commercial contact for as long as their respective histories extend.

They are not friends, in the way that neighboring peoples who have something the other needs are rarely friends. The Avites find the Legemi useful and, occasionally, aggravating in the specific way that a necessary supplier who will not be pushed around is aggravating. The Legemi find the Avites impressive and, consistently, alarming — the Ascarth kings have, on four recorded occasions, extended their ambitions to include the idea that controlling Legemum's tin supply directly would be preferable to purchasing it, and on four recorded occasions have discovered that Legemum, while it does not have much of a warrior tradition, has terrain that rewards defenders, sufficient agricultural stores to outlast a siege, and a Council prepared to destroy its own mines rather than yield them intact. This last point was demonstrated once, partially, when a section of the primary tin workings was flooded in advance of an Avite campaign that subsequently did not happen. The flooding was not repaired for six years. The Avites noticed.

The current arrangement is commercial: Legemum sells tin at a price set by Council negotiation, in quantities set annually, in exchange for bronze goods and for the implicit protection that comes from being the supplier of something Ascarth cannot produce itself. This is not entirely comfortable for either party. It works.

Legemum also benefits, obliquely, from Ascarth's military reputation. No external power that might otherwise covet the peninsula's mineral resources wants to deal with the peninsula *and* its neighbor. The Avites have never offered this protection formally. The Legemi have never asked for it formally. It exists anyway, in the way that useful arrangements often do.

## Among the Neighboring Regions

To the north and west, Legemum's land border connects it to the Mittoli interior — the same agricultural country that stretches across western Azhora, inhabited by people who speak a language close enough to Legemi dialect that commerce is straightforward. The Legemi sell surplus grain and wool into the Mittoli market towns; they buy metalwork, cloth, and the manufactured goods that the peninsula's dispersed economy does not produce efficiently. This relationship is quieter and more durable than the Ascarth one, and is less remarked upon because nothing in it is precarious.

The Selemi have a presence in Legemum's eastern coastal market, though not a formal outpost — the market town that handles most of the Ascarth tin trade has a resident Selemi merchant family that has been there for three generations and is, at this point, partly Legemi in culture and entirely Legemi by marriage. The Selemis count this as intelligence; the Legemi count the family as neighbors. Both assessments are accurate.

The Nyloni know Legemum primarily through the tin — any commercial intelligence about Ascarth's bronze production includes Legemum as an input variable, and the harbor records in Nylon track Legemi tin prices as a proxy for Ascarth's military readiness. A Legemum tin price spike suggests Ascarth is stockpiling. The Nyloni have found this a reliable early indicator and do not advertise that they track it.
