# Orsa

The mineral wealth of Orsa's coastal hills is not a secret. It has been known, in the south, for as long as the south has known that Orsa exists. The silver-bearing rock that breaks through the surface along the Gorgi's western slopes and the dark heavy-metal ore in the coastal cliffs near the western headlands have been described in Academy records, noted in Bay of Lol merchant logs, assessed by several expeditions that returned with samples and estimates and plans for extraction at scale.

None of those plans have been realized. The country where they would be realized has not changed. The plans have changed.

The mineral wealth is not defended by armies. Orsa does not have armies in any sense that the rest of the continent would recognize as military organization. The wealth is defended by the climate, and the climate has not lost.

---

## Geography

Orsa occupies the western portion of the continent's northernmost coast — the stretch from the Gorgi Mountains' western terminus to the point where the coast turns southward toward the Bay of Lol's distant northern approaches. It is west of Witherst and north of Nonoth, and it is larger than either, measured in coastline and inland extent. It is not larger in population.

The terrain is what results when the geological processes that built the Gorgi range have had a very long time to express themselves at the surface without being covered over. The Gorgi's western end is not a clean descending face in the way the eastern and central sections are; it breaks apart into a series of spurs and ridges and isolated massifs that extend toward the coast, leaving between them long, narrow valleys that run roughly north from the mountain core to the sea. The valleys are the only habitable corridors — the ground between the ridges is largely exposed rock, scree, and the persistent ice fields that the western coastal weather deposits and never fully removes. The valleys themselves are narrow and cold but shelter enough from the worst of the lateral weather to support the sparse human presence that has organized itself around them for as long as anyone has been here.

The coast is rocky and exposed in a manner distinct from Witherst's fjord-cut inlets. Where Witherst's coastline is incised — deep narrow openings that provide shelter proportional to their length — Orsa's coast is broad and irregular, with headlands rather than inlets, and the shelter available between headlands is real but not the complete wind-break that an inlet provides. The bays between Orsa's headlands are workable anchorage in calm conditions and dangerous in bad ones, and the transition between the two can happen in the space of a watch.

The western sea is the explanation for this. Witherst faces north and northeast, and the storms that reach it have crossed the northern latitudes, which are cold but not wild in the same way. Orsa faces west and northwest, into the full fetch of the ocean weather that has built across open water from wherever the continent's western margin eventually gives way to sea. The storms that reach Orsa are not the Witherst wind, which is persistent and significant. They are events — weather that arrives with enough force to move stone, that deposits ice on the exposed ridges even in summer, and that can trap a ship against the coast without enough warning to work clear.

The Gorgi passes west of center are the hardest of all the Gorgi crossings. The eastern and central passes, which lead to Witherst, are difficult and seasonal. The western passes, which lead to Orsa, are more difficult and have a shorter reliable window — perhaps four to six weeks in the best summer, nothing reliable in a poor one. They are used, but the Academy's notes on the Orsa-approach routes are among the most heavily annotated in the northern files: every account of a crossing includes conditions that at least one subsequent reader has marked as unrepeatable.

## The Minerals

The silver is the most visible. On the Gorgi's western slopes and in the valley walls where water has cut through to the underlying rock, the silver-bearing veins break through as dark metalite lines in the lighter host stone that catch the flat northern light and flash it back differently. The exposure is extensive — not concentrated in one or two workings but distributed through a long section of the foothills that the Academy's descriptive maps cover with a notation that one scholar's handwriting has made read as both "substantial" and "frustrating" depending on how it is read.

The dark ore — found in the exposed coastal cliffs and the lower valley floors where the geological formations that produced the silver continue outward toward the sea — is heavy and dull-surfaced and contains lead and a secondary metal whose precise composition has not been established by any outside analyst who has had both the sample and the equipment at the same time. The lead content alone would make it commercially significant if it could be extracted and moved in quantity. That it appears to contain a secondary valuable component, imperfectly identified in several Bay of Lol merchant reports, has kept the interest of southern commercial parties alive across generations of failed extraction attempts.

There is also iron, of the cold, dense variety that northern surface rock produces — not the Orched Hills' ore-body iron, which is workable and consistent, but a surface iron from frost-fractured rock that is harder than standard smelting produces and is worked, by the small population here, into tools and implements with properties that outside metalworkers have noted with interest when specimens have made the journey south. The Orsa people's iron-working practice is small in scale and uses techniques specific to the materials they have. The products are not exported in quantity. The ones that have reached southern hands are treated as curiosities by most and as extremely useful by the few metalworkers who have tried to understand what was done to produce them.

The reason none of this wealth has been extracted at scale is not that it is inaccessible to an outsider's eye or that the Orsa people guard it. The rock is exposed. The veins are visible. The ore is present in the valley walls as clearly as in any workable deposit. The reason is that extraction at scale requires a workforce, a workforce requires housing and feeding and warming through the winter, and winter in Orsa is the problem that every extraction plan has failed to solve.

## The Climate

Orsa's climate is not colder than Witherst by the measure of lowest temperature recorded in any winter. The absolute cold of the northern interior, when it descends, descends equally across the northernmost coast. What is different about Orsa is the combination: cold combined with the western weather, cold combined with unreliable sea ice that may or may not freeze a harbor before ships can leave, cold combined with the specific wind behavior where the Gorgi's western spurs funnel the mountain's cold air down the valley corridors and out across the coastal approaches.

Sea ice in Orsa's waters is not the predictable seasonal phenomenon it is in parts of Smunders Bay or the Witherst eastern approaches. It forms irregularly, early in bad years, and in the worst winters closes the western coast completely in a way that cannot be predicted from the autumn conditions. A ship that arrives in Orsa's bays in mid-summer on good weather and stays through three weeks of productive extraction may leave or may find, on the morning of its planned departure, that the approach has iced over and will not clear for another month, or two, or the entire winter.

This is not a metaphor. This has happened. The Bay of Lol merchant records document at least four instances in which extraction-or-trading expeditions to Orsa were trapped by early ice, with the varying outcomes that isolation through an Orsa winter produces: loss of ship, loss of crew, partial survival with the assistance of the local population, and on one occasion full survival with the assistance of the local population at a cost that the merchant family in question found, on reviewing it later, difficult to describe to their trading partners as anything other than "everything we had and some of what we owed."

The local population survives Orsa's winters because they are adapted to doing so in ways that cannot be transferred to an unprepared outsider in a single season. The knowledge is not primarily technical, though the specific practices — where to shelter from which wind configuration, how to read the ice behavior on the specific coastal sections, what food stores and what sources are available in what combination through the winter — are technical in their details. The knowledge is also physical: what the body can sustain in these conditions with this preparation, and what it cannot. An outside workforce that has not grown up in Orsa does not have this calibration. They learn it, when forced to, through the winter's instruction. The winter's instruction has a significant dropout rate.

## The People

The population of Orsa is the smallest of any settled region on the continent. Not small in the way that Witherst is small — Witherst has communities of dozens, clustered at their inlet sites, with a social infrastructure that functions as a community. Orsa's human presence is more dispersed: bands of families and households moving seasonally through the valley corridors, converging at certain coastal sites in summer when the conditions allow gathering, dispersing again as winter approaches and the communities pull back to their winter sheltering sites.

There are perhaps a dozen winter sheltering sites in Orsa — locations where the terrain provides enough protection from the valley-wind, where the stored resources accumulated through summer and autumn are adequate to see a group through the worst months, and where the knowledge of how to manage that specific shelter in those specific conditions has been accumulated across multiple generations of the families who use it. The sites are not villages in the sense of fixed permanent settlement. They are the known places where survival is possible, maintained and returned to because they have worked before and because that record is the most reliable information available about survival here.

The total population that these sheltering sites can sustain is not large. Estimates based on the summer gathering accounts from Bay of Lol merchant logs — the outsiders most likely to have seen a significant proportion of the Orsa people in one place — run between several hundred and perhaps a thousand. The Academy has not produced a more precise estimate because the Academy has not deployed anyone to count.

The Orsa people know their terrain with the completeness of people whose lives are organized around terrain-knowledge as a primary competency. Every valley, every ridge, every section of coast that changes its behavior between seasons, every approach to every winter shelter, every location where the ice forms first and clears last — this knowledge is the community's most important collective possession, and it is maintained through practice and transmission the way any survival-critical skill is maintained: with the specific attention given to things that, if forgotten, produce consequences.

They are not hostile to outsiders by temperament, but they are categorical about the conditions under which outsiders can be usefully engaged with. A Bay of Lol ship that arrives in the right season, anchors in a bay they know, and announces itself through the signals that the summer contact tradition has established over generations will receive trade interaction. A Bay of Lol ship that arrives at the wrong time, anchors in the wrong bay, or attempts to move inland without a guide will receive, in order: indifference, observation from a distance, and then whatever the climate produces, which the Orsa people will not interrupt.

## External Attempts

The Bay of Lol maritime communities are the outside party with the most contact, the most investment, and the most consistent record of learning the same lesson about what is and is not possible in Orsa. The western coast is reachable from the Bay of Lol by ships that know the northern approach — not easy, not safe in the wrong season, but achievable in the summer window in a way that the Gorgi overland crossing is not. The Bay of Lol has been making this approach, in varying organizational forms, for as long as its records go back.

The forms have included: individual merchant ventures, merchant partnerships organized specifically for Orsa extraction, a sustained two-season operation backed by a consortium of Bay of Lol trading families that represents the largest single investment ever made in northern mineral access, and several quasi-military expeditions in the raiding period that found the country militarily unresisting and climatically unsurvivable and returned with that assessment.

The consortium effort is the most documented. It ran for two full years, brought more workers and more equipment than any previous attempt, and built permanent extraction structures at two valley sites and one coastal cliff location. In the first summer it extracted silver ore in quantities that, had they reached the Bay of Lol, would have justified the investment. The winter killed most of the workforce. The second summer's skeleton crew, augmented by new arrivals, continued with reduced capacity. The second winter finished what the first had started. The structures still exist; the Academy's notes on the Orsa expedition records describe them as "presently occupied by the local population for storage purposes, which may be their most functional application."

The southern powers of the Mittoli city-state network have assessed Orsa at intervals as a potential mineral source. Their assessments consistently arrive at the same conclusion as the Bay of Lol's practice: the mineral wealth is real, the extraction cost in lives and resources exceeds the value of what could be extracted, and the climate's resistance is not a military problem that force can solve. Some problems cannot be solved by force. Orsa is a demonstration of this that the continent has been running for as long as the continent has been aware of the place.

## The Summer Contact

In the six to eight weeks when the western coast is reliably accessible by ship, Orsa is not entirely closed. Bay of Lol vessels make the approach, anchor in the recognized bays, and conduct the trade that both sides have found worth conducting. The Orsa bring to these encounters: silver ore in hand-selected quantities — not the bulk extraction that extraction plans dream of, but the careful surface and near-surface gathering that the small population manages through the year; samples and small amounts of the worked iron; skins and oil from the coastal animals; and information about conditions for the season, which Bay of Lol captains buy before deciding whether to go further along the coast or turn back.

The Bay of Lol brings: iron in worked form, grain in preserved quantities, tools and implements that the Orsa cannot produce locally, and the maintained relationship that makes the summer contact possible at all. The relationship is not equal in commercial terms — the Orsa are trading luxury-category goods in small quantities for basic necessities — but it is sustained by mutual need and by the specific knowledge, on the Bay of Lol side, that the Orsa's familiarity with the western coast is the only thing standing between an ambitious extraction attempt and its inevitable result.

The Bay of Lol captains who make the Orsa run are a specific subset of the coastal fleet: experienced, conservative about weather, unwilling to stay past the established departure window, and in possession of accumulated knowledge about the Orsa coast that is not written in any public record because the captains who know it have not found it advantageous to share. This knowledge — the specific anchorages, the ice behavior at specific locations, the signals that the Orsa use at the recognized trading bays — is the intellectual property of the families and vessels that have maintained the route. It is inherited, trained, and occasionally sold to parties who subsequently discover whether the price was fair.

## Language

The Orsa speech is not a single variety. The population is too sparse and too seasonally dispersed for a single uniform dialect to maintain itself; the different valley communities and family bands have drifted from a shared base across the generations of their relative isolation from each other, producing something that the Academy describes as a language cluster rather than a language — varieties that are mutually comprehensible to their speakers, who encounter each other at summer gatherings often enough to maintain a common core, but that differ in enough vocabulary and pronunciation to be distinguishable to a careful listener.

The shared core is very old and very different from Northern Mittoli. The substrate layer in Orsa speech is deeper than in Witherst, deeper than in the Ganoss upland dialects, deeper than anything the Academy has documented elsewhere on the continent. The Pro-Western reconstruction provides essentially nothing useful for Orsa's core vocabulary. The words for rock formations, weather conditions, and seasonal phenomena — the words that any community in this terrain would need first — do not trace to any language family the Academy has identified. The working hypothesis, marked explicitly as a hypothesis, is that the Gorgi Mountains isolated a pre-Northern population long enough for their language to develop independently of the Mittoli expansion that eventually reached their neighbors to the south and east, and that this pre-Northern language is what Orsa predominantly speaks in everything that matters.

The Northern Mittoli overlay exists in the trade vocabulary and in the words for things that came from the south: the names of southern goods, the terms for the Bay of Lol trading practices, the phrases that allow a summer contact to function between parties who do not otherwise share a language base. This layer is thin and sits visibly on top of the older material. The Orsa do not appear to find this stratification notable. The old words are for what was always here. The new words are for what arrived. The distribution seems correct to them.
