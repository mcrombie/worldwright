# Azhor Stones

The Azhor Stones are a belt of rocky islands sitting in the warm southern ocean between the Azner Shores — the southernmost inhabited extension of Azhora — and the northern edge of the great Southern Archipelago. They are not the Southern Archipelago: there is no volcanic soil worth farming, no jungle interior, no fertile lowland. They are limestone and reef and open sea, arrayed in a loose chain across roughly two hundred miles of open water, and they have been the necessary middle point of every serious southern voyage made by Azhoran ships.

The name echoes Azhora — *azh*, the root of endurance, what remains — though whether the naming was deliberate or a corruption of something the Bouéni called them in their own language is debated. The other Azhoran island chain bearing the "Stones" suffix is the Cold Stones in the far north, and the parallel is apt: both are rocky, both serve as waypoints, both are the last familiar ground before the sea opens into something less understood. The Azhor Stones are the warmer version of this role.

## Geography

The chain comprises roughly forty islands of notable size and many more outcrops and exposed reefs, running roughly northwest to southeast. The islands are not volcanic — they are among the oldest exposed rock in the southern sea, a formation that Academy geologists with an interest in deep time find more interesting than the more dramatic volcanic islands to the south. The rock is predominantly pale limestone, in places karst, shaped by centuries of wave action and rain into dramatic formations: sea arches, blowholes, undercut cliffs, and in the interior of the larger islands, cave systems that descend below the water table and emerge into the sea on their seaward faces.

From the water, the Azhor Stones are visually striking in a way that does not make them easy to land on. The windward faces are sheer and vertical — the pale limestone catching tropical sun and throwing it back at approaching ships in a brightness unlike anything on the grey Bouéni coast. The leeward sides offer narrow beaches, usually coarse sand caught between cliff base and reef edge. The chain does not look like somewhere to stop; it looks like somewhere to navigate around. The permanent residents consider this one of its better qualities.

The largest island — called *Veth-azhor* in Azhoran records, roughly "the hold of the enduring stones" — is approximately fifteen miles across at its widest and has enough interior topography that its central ridge catches rainfall. It has fresh water. The others mostly do not, and this shapes everything.

## Climate

The cold current that makes Bouén grey and chill loses its influence well north of the Azhor Stones. The southern sea here is warm year-round, and the cold-ocean overcast that defines the Bouéni coast is replaced by open sky and sustained sun. Trade winds blow from the southeast for most of the year, creating consistent sailing conditions: the approach from the south and east works with the wind; the approach from the north and west requires tacking.

Two seasons are recognized. The dry season — roughly eight months — is dominated by the reliable trade winds, clear skies, and the easy sailing that makes the waypoint function possible. The wet season, the remaining four months, brings a shift in the winds and the passage of tropical storm systems larger and faster than anything encountered in Azhoran coastal waters. Bouéni sailors learned to read these during the first southern expeditions; Academy ship logs consistently describe the storms as more abrupt and more severe than expected, which is what Academy ship logs have said about them for three hundred years without the description changing.

The ambient temperature is warm year-round but, contrary to what visiting Azhorans from northern regions expect, not oppressively humid. The rocky terrain has almost no vegetation capable of generating its own moisture, and the trade winds keep the air moving. The Azhor Stones are more comfortable for Azhoran visitors than the volcanic southern islands proper, which meet arrivals with a wall of wet heat that requires adjustment. This distinction, combined with the waypoint function, makes the Azhor Stones the southernmost point where Azhoran visitors feel approximately at ease.

## Fresh Water

Fresh water is the defining practical constraint of the chain. The limestone holds almost none — rainfall passes through it directly to the sea or into the cave systems below, accessible only at specific emergence points that the permanent residents know and visiting ships must be told about or discover expensively. Only *Veth-azhor* and two smaller islands have reliable surface springs. Several of the others have no fresh water at any season.

Ships arriving without guidance sometimes reach a dry island first. The experience concentrates attention.

Rainwater cisterns cut into the limestone are the oldest infrastructure in the archipelago — several are larger than the current population needs and were clearly built for a community significantly larger than the one here now. They are maintained as shared property regardless of which family or community considers the adjacent land their territory. This is not an ideological position; it is the arrangement that prevents people dying of thirst, and it predates ideology.

The knowledge of which water sources exist where is the most valuable navigational information held by the permanent residents. They share it with ships they have assessed. They do not share it indiscriminately.

## The Reef

Surrounding the Azhor Stones is one of the more extensive reef systems in the southern sea — shallow, clear, and rich in a way that contrasts sharply with the sparse terrestrial ecology above the waterline. Where the islands support only low salt-adapted scrub and a few plants that have found ways to live in limestone pockets, the water around them holds reef fish in densities that visiting Azhoran sailors find implausible and the permanent residents find ordinary.

The southern archipelago peoples have fished the Azhor Stones reefs for longer than any record reaches. They regard the reefs as their own in a working sense: they name sections of them that appear on no Azhoran chart, navigate within the reef system in vessels specifically built for shallow reef water that Azhoran hulls cannot follow, and have arrangements with the permanent residents of the islands that predate Azhoran involvement by centuries. The outer edge of the reef, where the seafloor drops away into the deep southern ocean, is where the largest fish run. The southern navigators work that edge with a precision that Azhoran sailors have been observing and failing to fully replicate for three hundred years.

Sea turtles use the larger island beaches for nesting. The permanent residents do not hunt them during nesting season — not by prohibition, but by practice old enough that no one recalls it being otherwise. The southern peoples have a word for this arrangement. The Bouéni have adopted the word. Neither group has explained it formally to the Academy.

## Habitation

The Azhor Stones support a small permanent population — perhaps four hundred people across three islands — descended from Bouéni settlers and, in the community's own account, from people who were present before the Bouéni arrived and whose origin they do not specify further. They speak a variety of Bouéni with considerable influence from the northern Southern Archipelago languages, developed enough in its own direction that Bouén proper finds it distinctly accented and Academy linguists find it genuinely interesting.

The material culture is built around what the terrain provides and what cannot be avoided importing. Buildings are low, often constructed around or into the limestone formations for wind shelter and thermal mass, with minimal wood — wood must come from elsewhere, and so it does, in trade. The permanent residents do not farm in any sense recognizable to the mainland. They fish, trade, maintain the cisterns, keep some goats on the less cliff-edged islands, and exchange services with the vessel traffic that uses the chain as a waypoint. This has been sufficient for long enough that no one here has seriously considered changing it.

They have strong opinions about which visiting crews are worth helping and communicate these opinions through the quality of assistance they choose to offer. A crew they have assessed well gets accurate information about water, weather, and conditions to the south. A crew they have assessed poorly gets polite accuracy about water and nothing further.

### The Earlier People

The cisterns are not the only evidence of a larger prior presence. On *Veth-azhor*, a section of the interior limestone has been carved with marks that do not correspond to any Azhoran script, any Bouéni writing system, or any script associated with the southern archipelago peoples. The marks are distributed across a cave wall that is accessible only through a passage that is submerged at high tide — whoever made them knew the tidal pattern and worked at low water. The marks are not decorative in any obvious sense; they are arranged in what appears to be a systematic layout, though the system has not been recovered.

The permanent residents describe the marks as old, do not suggest an interpretation, and mention them to visitors only when asked directly. The Academy has collected copies. The Academy also has no interpretation.

## As Waypoint

Every Azhoran vessel that has reached the Southern Archipelago has passed through or near the Azhor Stones. The chain is the natural staging point for the southern crossing: close enough to Azner Shores to be reached from there in favorable conditions, far enough south that the southern approach can be read from the northern rocks on a clear day, and positioned so that either flank of the chain gives a different angle on the southern sea conditions.

The Bouéni established this role before any other Azhoran group and have held a disproportionate influence over the waypoint trade ever since. Ships from the Caeras cities and the Iberos coast that venture south find themselves depending on Bouéni intermediaries to navigate the water and social terms of the Azhor Stones both. The permanent residents do not actively reinforce this — they trade with anyone who arrives with reasonable intent — but the Bouéni were there first, and first-mover advantages in waypoint politics are durable.

The southern peoples use the Azhor Stones reefs as fishing grounds and transited the chain long before any Azhoran presence. Their relationship with the permanent residents is older and operates on different terms than the Azhoran waypoint economy — less transactional, more continuous. When a southern vessel passes through, the exchange looks less like commerce and more like an ongoing conversation interrupted for a while.

## What the Southern Peoples Call It

The southern archipelago peoples do not call this chain the Azhor Stones. Their name, as Azhoran records approximate it, translates roughly as "the old grey teeth" — a description of the pale limestone cliffs as seen from the south, approaching from warm blue water. The image of the cliff faces as teeth appears across several of the northern archipelago languages in consistent enough form to suggest the name is old and predates the Bouéni arrival from the north.

The permanent residents use both names depending on context. In formal dealings with Azhoran ships, the Azhoran name. In their own speech and with southern visitors, the southern name, or a Bouéni name that is neither of the above — a third option that Academy records do not contain because it has not been offered unprompted.

## The Name

*Azhor* does not appear in Standard Mittoli navigational records until after the first Bouéni southern expeditions, placing the name's formal adoption within the last three hundred years. Earlier Azhoran references to the southern chain use "the Stones beyond Azner" or simply "the southern outcrops." The name *Azhor* was applied by or through the Bouéni expeditions, but the Bouéni, asked to explain it, give an answer in Bouéni that does not clearly resolve whether the name derives from *azh* (what endures) applied to the demonstrably old limestone, or from a Bouéni word that sounds like *azh* to Mittoli ears, or from something else that has been Mittolified in the process of entering formal Azhoran use.

The Academy's position is that the name likely derives from *azh* applied to the geology. This is a reasonable position. The geology supports it: the limestone of the Azhor Stones is among the oldest surface rock in the southern sea. Whatever else is true about the naming, "the enduring stones" is not inaccurate.

The parallel with Cold Stones in the north is not lost on Azhoran geographers — old pale rock, waypoint function, the last fixed point before unfamiliar water. Whether the parallel was deliberate, coincidental, or imposed retrospectively by a scholarly tradition that likes symmetry is not established. The Azhor Stones do not appear to care about the question.
