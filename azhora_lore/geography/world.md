# Corav

Corav is the name of the world — the planet on which Azhora sits. The name predates written record; it appears in the oldest surviving Azhoran texts already treated as common knowledge, as if the world had always had this name and needed no explanation.

What Corav looks like in full is not known. Azhoran civilization has charted its own continent thoroughly, and the seas immediately surrounding it. Beyond that, knowledge becomes fragmentary, hearsay, and legend.

## What Is Known

### The Shape and Size of Corav

Corav is spherical. Azhoran astronomers established this definitively roughly 800 years ago through a combination of eclipse geometry, horizon observations at sea, and the behavior of circumpolar stars at different latitudes. It was not a controversial conclusion; most educated Azhorans had assumed it for centuries. The flat-world traditions that persist in certain rural regions are not considered credible.

The world is large. Attempts to estimate its circumference based on stellar observation and the angular displacement of known stars across measured distances give figures that imply the oceans surrounding Azhora are vast — far larger than the continent itself. The current best estimate is that Azhora occupies less than a tenth of Corav's total surface area. If other landmasses exist at equivalent distances on the far side of the world, Azhoran ships have not reached them and returned to say so.

### The Star

Corav orbits a stable yellow-white star. Azhoran astronomers have measured its angular diameter (consistent year-round, indicating the orbit is not dramatically eccentric), tracked its position against background stars over centuries (confirming Corav moves around it, not the reverse — a conclusion that remains politically contested in some institutions), and established through careful measurement that its output does not vary perceptibly across any timescale that has been studied.

The star provides Corav with light that renders the sky blue during clear days and gives sunlight a yellow-white character that painters have noted differs subtly from firelight and from the light of the moons. This difference has no commonly accepted explanation beyond "it is what the star does," which is the scientific state of several things on Corav.

### The Year and the Seasons

A complete orbit of Corav around its star takes **360 days** — established by precise stellar observation over centuries. The number is not approximate; the Caeras Valley Academy has confirmed it through independent measurements taken at known latitudes using shadow-angle instruments, and the figure is consistent to within hours.

The year is divided into four seasons of 90 days each, caused by the tilt of Corav's rotational axis. The axis is inclined approximately 22 degrees from vertical relative to the orbital plane. This tilt means that for half the year the northern hemisphere receives more direct sunlight (summer in the north, winter in the south), and for the other half the reverse. The equinoxes — the two points in the year where day and night are equal — occur at roughly 90-day intervals separating the solstices.

The seasonal extremes vary by latitude. Regions near the equator experience mild, monsoon-defined seasons — a wet period and a dry period rather than four distinct phases. Temperate latitudes experience clear four-season cycles. The northern reaches of Azhora, near the highest latitudes the continent extends to, experience harsh winters and brief summers; in the coldest years, portions of Smunders Bay freeze. The southern continent's ice reflects year-round, not seasonally.

One consequence of a 22-degree tilt and a 360-day year: the solstice sun rises and sets at roughly the same azimuth for several days on either side of the solstice, making the exact solstice difficult to observe. Azhoran calendar traditions have various systems for determining the true solstice, and they do not agree precisely with each other, which is a perennial source of scheduling disputes between regions that care about such things.

### The Two Moons

Two moons orbit Corav, and they are not alike.

**Sova** is the larger moon — bright enough to read by at full phase, with a distinctly yellow-white color that distinguishes it from the blue-white of clear night sky. Its synodic cycle (new moon to new moon) is **29 days** — close enough to one-twelfth of the solar year that many cultures use it as the basis for a twelve-month calendar, though 12 × 29 = 348 days, not 360, requiring periodic adjustment. Sova is large enough in apparent diameter that its disc can be seen with the naked eye, and its surface features — broad dark plains, a bright irregular highland — are named in Azhoran astronomical tradition. Sova dominates the tides.

**Vel** is smaller, dimmer, and fast. Its synodic cycle is **11 days**, meaning it completes nearly three full cycles for every one of Sova's. Vel is visible mainly on clear nights away from settlement light; near a city it can be missed entirely. Its color is slightly cooler than Sova's. Vel's smaller size and closer orbit mean its tidal influence is real but secondary to Sova's — it modulates rather than dominates.

The relationship between the two moon cycles means they fall into approximate alignment every **17 to 18 days**. When both moons align on the same side of the planet as the sun (conjunction) or the opposite side (opposition), their tidal pulls combine. These "compound tides" — which coastal peoples call Great Tides in most languages — are significantly larger than normal spring tides and reliably predictable. Ports plan their heaviest cargo loading and unloading around them; coastal settlements in low-lying areas know to stay off the flats.

The tidal calendar produced by two moons is complex but entirely predictable. Azhoran coastal astronomers have maintained records precise enough to predict compound tides four years in advance. The overlapping cycles of Sova and Vel create a repeating pattern with a full period of approximately 319 days — just short of the solar year — meaning compound tide dates drift slowly through the calendar year, returning to approximately the same calendar date every few decades.

This complexity has left marks on every Azhoran culture with a coast. The Pyrosi base-twelve number system is optimized for the 12 Sova-months near-year. Bouéni navigational traditions track both moons independently and combine them in tide prediction. Moreshi oasis keepers monitor Vel for short-cycle planting decisions and Sova for seasonal ones. And the Grassic oral tradition, which references time by seasonal cycle rather than calendar date, has no official lunar calendar — but the elders who maintain Memory Grass records have been observed to time certain site-readings to specific phases of both moons, without explaining why.

### The Ember Comet

A comet returns every 47 years and remains visible for approximately three months. It has been named the Ember Comet for the orange-red color of its tail. It is used as a generational calendar marker; births, deaths, and major events are recorded as occurring "in an Ember year" or "three years after the Ember."

More significantly for science: the Ember Comet's return has been successfully predicted three times in a row by the Caeras Valley Academy, most recently to within four days. This is the clearest demonstration that Azhoran astronomy has moved from description to genuine predictive mechanics. The Academy is quiet about the fact that its first prediction (seven cycles ago) was off by eleven days; subsequent refinements to the orbital model have improved accuracy with each return. A comet with a 47-year period is in a regular, elliptical orbit around the same star Corav orbits. The model that successfully predicts the comet's return is a model in which Corav and the comet are both moving through the same solar system.

## What Is Suspected

From the highest points on Azhora's coastlines, on very clear days, shapes can sometimes be seen at the horizon to the west and to the northeast. Sailors report landmasses — or what might be landmasses. No expedition sent to reach them has confirmed the distance, returned with charts, or in some cases returned at all.

The western sighting is consistent enough that cartographers mark it on speculative maps as the Western Shore. Its existence is widely accepted but unverified.

The northeastern sighting is less consistent. Some sailors report it; others, on the same routes in similar conditions, report nothing. Whether this represents a real landmass, unusual atmospheric conditions, or accumulated misreporting is unresolved.

## What Is Unknown

Most of Corav. The planet's surface, its other continents if they exist, the depths of its oceans beyond Azhoran lanes, and what — if anything — lives there. The Azhoran understanding of the world is the understanding of a civilization that has mapped its own house and glimpsed its neighbors through a window.

This is not a comfortable fact for Azhoran scholars, who are generally unwilling to say "we don't know" when "we haven't looked yet" is technically more accurate.

## The Question of Other Peoples

It would be naive to assume Azhora is the only inhabited landmass on Corav. The world is too large. But contact with any civilization beyond Azhoran shores has not been confirmed in the historical record — no foreign ship has arrived, no foreign language deciphered, no artifact of clearly non-Azhoran origin identified with certainty. There are legends. There are carvings on certain coasts that some scholars attribute to outside visitors. There are gaps in the record that suggest things were known and then lost.

The possibility of other peoples on other shores is treated by most Azhoran scholars as probable in theory and irrelevant in practice, which is a position that has not always aged well.
