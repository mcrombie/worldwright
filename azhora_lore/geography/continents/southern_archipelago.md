# Southern Archipelago

South of Azhora, across the warm water that separates the Bouén peninsula from the tropics, lies a field of large islands that Azhoran geographers have only recently begun to map with any confidence. It is not a continent — there is no single landmass — but the islands are large enough, numerous enough, and close enough together that the distinction matters less than it might. The Southern Archipelago is, in effect, a world unto itself: warm, green, internally varied, and inhabited by peoples whose civilizations developed in complete isolation from Azhoran history.

Azhorans did not know about it until Bouéni sailors reached the northern islands roughly three hundred years ago. The peoples of the archipelago did not know about Azhora. Both sides found the encounter instructive in ways that have taken time to process.

## Geography

The archipelago spans several hundred miles in each direction, with the largest islands in the north and the island chains becoming smaller and more scattered toward the south and east. The northern islands are the ones Azhorans know best — these are where the Bouéni contact was made and where the limited Azhoran presence is concentrated. The southern and eastern islands are known primarily from the archipelago's own navigators, who have been sailing between them for centuries without any need for Azhoran assistance.

The islands are volcanic in origin — several are still active, and the soil throughout the archipelago is extraordinarily fertile as a result. The climate is tropical: warm year-round, wet on the windward sides of the larger islands, drier in the rain shadows. Dense jungle covers the interiors of the large islands, thinning to coastal forest and then to the white sand and turquoise water that Bouéni sailors, accustomed to grey cold, found somewhat overwhelming on first arrival.

## The Known Islands

**Brocerri** (the Azhoran approximation — the island's own name in its own language sounds different enough that the spelling has been disputed in every map produced so far) is the largest island in the northern group and the primary point of Azhoran contact. It is large enough to have distinct regional variation — the windward coast is wet and forested, the leeward side is drier and more populated, and the interior highland is cooler than the coast and home to a distinct cultural tradition from the coastal peoples.

The name Azhorans use was adopted from a misheard coastal greeting on first contact, a fact that the Brocerri find either amusing or irritating depending on which community you ask. The actual name of the island, as close as Azhoran speakers can approximate it, appears in some records as "Brosheri" and others as "Brocerri" — the variation reflects genuine difficulty with the sounds involved rather than simple scribal inconsistency.

**Adiu** (or Aduis, Adieux in some Bouéni records — the final sound is disputed) is the second large island, east of Brocerri. Its peoples have had less contact with Azhorans than the Brocerri coast and are currently less interested in increasing that contact. Adiu produces a resin-based material used in boat construction that Azhoran shipbuilders have expressed considerable interest in, so far without successfully opening a reliable trade route.

**Gols Cny** — this rendering appears in a Bouéni navigator's log from roughly two hundred years ago and has stuck in Azhoran records despite the near-certainty that it is not what the island or its principal settlement is actually called. The Bouéni navigator in question was working from phonetic notes made in poor light. The island is real; its actual name is one of the corrections that has been promised in the next formal survey and not yet delivered. It is large, sits at the center of the archipelago's internal trade network, and its harbor is one of the busiest in the southern sea.

**Autumn Bay** is the Azhoran name for the large, sheltered bay on the eastern side of one of the larger northern islands — named by the first Bouéni expedition that wintered there, which arrived in what passed for the archipelago's cooler season and found the light and temperature reminiscent of an Azhoran autumn. The local name for the bay is not recorded in any Azhoran source, which is a recurring problem in the documentation of this region.

## The Peoples

The archipelago is home to multiple distinct civilizations, related to one another in ways that reflect centuries of inter-island contact and trade. They share broad linguistic roots — the archipelago languages form a family, internally varied, with the northern islands more closely related and the eastern outliers more divergent — but have developed distinct political structures, religious traditions, and material cultures.

What they share, across significant internal variation, is an orientation toward the sea that makes even Azhoran maritime cultures look continental. The archipelago peoples have been navigating open ocean for longer than Azhorans have been sailing their own coast. They use star navigation, wave pattern reading, and a system of portable navigational tools — the details of which Azhoran sailors have been trying to obtain and understand for three hundred years with partial success — that allow precise open-water positioning without instruments.

From the perspective of the archipelago's peoples, the appearance of Azhoran ships was not the discovery of the unknown. It was contact with a distant neighbor they had not previously found interesting enough to visit.

## Azhoran Relations

Three hundred years of contact have produced an uneven relationship. Trade exists — spices, resins, and worked materials from the archipelago for metals, grain, and manufactured goods from Azhora — but it is conducted primarily on the archipelago's terms, through intermediaries, and with a clear understanding of who controls the sea between. The archipelago peoples are not militarily aggressive toward Azhoran ships, but they are capable of being so, and they are aware of this in their diplomatic posture.

There is no Azhoran presence on any island without the consent of that island's authority. This was established firmly within the first fifty years of contact, after an early Azhoran attempt to establish a permanent trading post on Brocerri's leeward coast without asking first. The details of how this ended are not included in official Azhoran records. The Brocerri version of events is included in their own oral tradition and is considered, by those who have heard it, to be more detailed and less flattering to the Azhoran side.

## What Remains Unknown

The southern and eastern reaches of the archipelago are not mapped in Azhoran records and are only partially known even to the northern island peoples. The archipelago does not have a fixed southern edge that anyone has established. Islands continue to appear on the charts that the archipelago's own navigators use, further south and east than Azhorans have traveled.

Whether the archipelago eventually gives way to open ocean, to another landmass, or to something else that no current chart describes, is not established.
