# Red Meridian

The Red Meridian is a band of deep crimson stone that runs through Azhora at approximately 12 degrees north of the equator. It is visible wherever ancient rock is exposed — cliff faces, canyon walls, mountainsides, and the beds of deep rivers. It enters the continent from the western sea, crosses Azhora in a roughly east-west line, and exits into the eastern sea.

Whether it continues beyond Azhora is not confirmed. The hypothesis that it circles the entirety of Corav — that it is a global feature — is supported by the behavior of ocean-floor samples taken along the projected line, which show red-tinted rock at consistent depth even in the deep sea. It is not confirmed because Azhorans have not mapped the world. The Red Meridian is, in this sense, one of the strongest practical arguments for doing so: if it circles Corav, knowing its path could tell us something important.

## The Stone

The red stone is not like ordinary red-tinted rock. It is a distinct material — similar in some properties to basalt but denser, harder to cut, and with a crystalline microstructure that no naturally occurring rock has been identified to share. Under magnification, the crystals appear to be aligned, all pointing in the same direction: tangentially along the band's path, as if the stone was formed by something moving.

The Meridian is of consistent width: roughly 40 to 80 feet where exposed on Azhora, though survey data suggests it extends to depth rather than being a surface feature. Samples taken from the western cliff exposure and the eastern canyon exposure are mineralogically identical despite crossing hundreds of miles of different geology. This should not be possible for a naturally deposited feature.

Age estimates from radiometric dating cluster around 90,000 to 120,000 years, with some outlier samples suggesting even older. This predates every known civilization by a significant margin.

## The Carvings

In certain locations along the Azhoran segment of the Meridian — locations that do not follow any obvious pattern of distance or terrain — the surface of the red stone bears markings. These are not clearly natural. The style is consistent across locations that are hundreds of miles apart, which implies either a single maker or a tradition that maintained rigorous consistency over distance and time.

The markings have not been translated. They do not resemble any known Azhoran script, including the oldest pre-Compact inscriptions.

The largest accessible carved section is in the southern coastal cliffs, where the Meridian meets the sea and the stone face is exposed for roughly four miles. This is the primary site for scholarly translation work. Progress has been slow. The coastal peoples near the site have their own relationship with the carvings — they do not prevent study, but there are sections they do not come near, and they have declined to explain why.

## Theories

**The Official Position — Unusual Natural Deposit:** A highly unusual but natural deposit, formed by some ancient planetary process. This remains the official position of the Azhoran Academy of Natural History. Critics note that no mechanism has ever been proposed that would produce a globally continuous, compositionally identical, linearly distributed mineral deposit with aligned crystalline structure in all local rock types regardless of their native composition. The Academy has not answered this criticism. It has restated the official position several times.

**The Grazing Impact Hypothesis:** The theory with the strongest physical basis, developed by a small group of researchers working with data from the Red Meridian and from studies of impact craters elsewhere on Azhora. The core argument: approximately 90,000 to 120,000 years ago — consistent with the radiometric dates — a large body (a cometary nucleus or a substantial asteroid) impacted Corav at an extremely shallow angle, roughly parallel to the surface rather than perpendicular to it.

In such a grazing impact, the body does not penetrate to a single point and excavate a circular crater; instead, it travels across the surface, converting the rock along its path to shock-metamorphic material through the enormous pressures of the impact front. The result would be a continuous band of altered rock along the trajectory — global in extent if the body traveled far enough, of consistent composition because the shock metamorphism overwrites the original rock type (this explains why the Meridian is mineralogically identical across hundreds of miles of different geology), and with crystals aligned in the direction of shock propagation (tangentially along the band, exactly what is observed).

The density and hardness of the red stone — greater than ordinary basalt — is consistent with high-pressure shock metamorphism. The depth of the feature (extending well below the surface) is consistent with the penetration of a traveling impact wave. The global continuity follows from a body that traveled at low angle across the planet's surface before disintegrating.

At 90,000 to 120,000 years, the Coravian surface has had time to erode the most extreme surface expressions of the impact but not the deep-seated mineralogical change. The Meridian is what remains.

The grazing impact hypothesis does not explain the carvings, which postdate the impact by tens of thousands of years. The carvings were made by people who found the red stone already there — possibly because its color, hardness, and resistance to erosion made it distinctive. Who those people were is a separate question.

Some researchers have proposed a connection between the grazing impact, the crustal anomaly beneath the Ascending Peaks, and the distinctive mineralogy of both features. If a large body impacted at shallow angle, it would have transferred enormous energy to the crust along its trajectory, potentially producing localized geomagnetic anomalies and unusual mineral deposits well away from the primary impact band. This is speculative and not published in a form the Academy has had to respond to.

**Artificial Construction:** Something made this intentionally. Most mainstream academics reject this because it raises questions they cannot begin to answer and because the scale of effort required — if it is a construction — is beyond anything in the Azhoran record. The grazing impact hypothesis explains everything the natural-deposit hypothesis does not, without requiring a builder. Most researchers working on the Meridian have migrated from "artificial" to "unusual natural process" as the physical evidence has accumulated.

**The Older Tradition:** The coastal peoples near the southern cliff carvings have a tradition — oral, not fully shared with outsiders — that describes the Red Meridian as "the scar." Not a scar left by something that happened, but a scar that prevents something from happening again. They do not say what. Scholars who have pressed for clarification report that the question is received not with evasion but with a kind of patient incomprehension, as if they have asked why fire is hot.

The grazing impact hypothesis provides a literal reading of "the scar" that the coastal tradition may have preserved through oral memory from a period when the Meridian's origin was more immediate knowledge. Whether the tradition refers to the impact itself or to something that happened afterward is not clear.

## Practical Uses

Despite its mysterious origin, the Red Meridian is practically useful. It is the most reliable navigational reference on Azhora — a continuous, visible feature that confirms latitude regardless of weather or season. Sailors and overland travelers have used it as a landmark for thousands of years. The standard Azhoran calendar's longitude system uses the point where the Meridian crosses the western coast as its prime reference line.

There is something mildly absurd about the continent's oldest mystery also being its most reliable street sign. Scholars generally do not find this funny.
