# Distribution of Fauna Across Azhora

The distribution of animal life across Azhora follows the same broad logic as the distribution of plant life — climate, terrain, moisture, and elevation — and then departs from that logic in ways plants cannot. Animals move. They follow food and warmth across seasonal gradients, abandon territories in drought, appear in locations where no breeding population exists, cross political boundaries that their ranges predate, and vanish from regions where every condition for their presence seems intact. Understanding where a plant grows is a question of soil and light. Understanding where an animal lives is also a question of what it fears, what it follows, and what it remembers.

This overview maps the broad distribution of Azhoran fauna by region, drawing on Caeras Valley Academy survey records, the practical knowledge of hunting and pastoral communities, and — where Academy records acknowledge limits — the traditions of peoples who know specific landscapes more completely than any outside survey has managed. The livestock of Azhora are treated separately, being both fauna and property, and subject to the full complexity that the combination produces. This document concerns the animals that belong to no household.

---

## Organizing Principles

Four factors shape animal distribution in ways that are distinct from the factors governing plant distribution.

**Climate and terrain** operate on fauna much as on flora. Cold-limited species cannot persist in the north's winters without specific adaptations; moisture-demanding species thin and disappear as rainfall declines toward the arid east; altitude creates stratified zones where cold-adapted species replace lowland ones with reasonable predictability. These broad patterns hold. They are the starting point, not the complete picture.

**Seasonal movement** is the complication plants do not introduce. Most of Azhora's larger fauna is not stationary. Winter ranges and summer ranges differ, sometimes by hundreds of kilometers. Migratory birds arrive from and depart toward destinations that in many cases remain uncharted from an Azhoran perspective. Fish move through river systems on spawning cycles that span the length of major river networks. Some of the continent's largest predators maintain ranges that cross every regional classification in this document. A species listed as characteristic of one region may be absent from it for half the year and twice as numerous for the other half.

**Predator-prey structure** creates continent-spanning relationships that do not respect terrain categories. A prey species is limited by its food, which is limited by climate and soil. A predator is limited by its prey. These chains extend further than single-region surveys tend to reveal. The decline of large grazers in a grassland creates conditions — increased vegetation density, changed soil character, altered water retention — that cascade through the system over years and decades. Azhoran ecological traditions in several cultures understand this practically even where the theoretical framework differs from Academy models.

**Human pressure** has reshaped fauna distribution across inhabited Azhora over the period of record and probably much longer before it. Hunting has removed large predators from most densely settled regions. Clearance has eliminated forest species from former woodland. Trade in hides, feathers, oil, horn, and live animals has created persistent pressure on specific populations across the continent. The Caeras Valley Academy's oldest survey records, now several centuries old, describe species present in locations where no population exists today — in several cases, no population exists anywhere in the settled landscape. Whether these species survive in the Ibenwood, in the remote Oremindi interior, or in the southern waters is unknown. Whether they are extinct is also unknown.

## Mittolo and the Central Heartland

The river systems of Mittolo and Mithala — the Lizeem and its distributaries, the Ond wetlands, the channeled floodplains of the eastern plains — are among the richest non-marine waterways on the continent and support a fauna of corresponding density and variety.

The dominant large predator of the wetlands is the **river boar**, a misleadingly named animal that is not related to the domestic pig despite the name persisting in Minoran administrative records for at least four centuries. It is heavily built, semi-aquatic, and capable of considerable patience and surprising speed over short distances. Its relationship with Lizeem river-horn herds is one of managed mutual awareness: the herds know river boar territory; river boar know which animals in a herd are vulnerable. Neither operates in ignorance of the other. River-horn keepers regard the river boar with the focused attention one gives a permanent hazard that one has learned to navigate rather than eliminate.

The Lizeem fishery is one of Azhora's most productive inland food sources. The great **broad-backed carp**, a species reaching lengths of two meters in the deep channel sections near Minora, is the river's prestige fish — heavily regulated, traded salted as far as the Moroshé margins, the subject of elaborate catch-and-scale ceremonies at the autumn Minoran Mud Fair. Below this in scale but not in ecological importance are dozens of species occupying successive levels of the river's food structure, from the large-mouthed **channel hunters** that take waterfowl and small mammals from the surface to the bottom-feeding species that process the organic load of Mittolo's agricultural runoff.

Wetland birds of Mittolo and Ond constitute what the Academy's natural historians call, with somewhat more enthusiasm than is usual in their literature, "the richest avian assemblage documented on the continent." Wading birds in particular — herons, spoonbills, a range of stilt-legged species for which Standard Mittoli maintains separate names that Academy records have not always preserved accurately — use the Lizeem distributaries as both breeding grounds and wintering locations for populations that migrate from the north and east. In high flood years, the extended inundation of the lower plains creates temporary wetland habitat that draws concentrations of birds from species not normally associated with the Mittolo heartland; old agricultural records note that high flood years correlate with exceptional hunting seasons for waterfowl across the eastern plains.

The forest fauna of western Mittolo — the woodlands that are the Ibenwood's closest ecological neighbors among settled landscapes — is discussed below in the section on Ibenwood distribution.

## Northern Azhora

The northern coast and the Ganoss uplands support cold-specialized fauna that is largely absent from the rest of Azhora. The distinction between the maritime coastal species and the inland upland species is significant; northern Azhora is not ecologically uniform.

The cold waters of the Bay of Lol and the northern Azhoran coast carry populations of marine mammals — **grey seals**, the large-whiskered **bay walrus** of the deeper bays, and several cetacean species that transit between northern feeding grounds and calving waters further south. The bay walrus is the subject of an ongoing Ganoss clan controversy about whether it constitutes a hunting species, a boundary marker for territorial waters, or something whose status is not reducible to either category. Outside researchers have found the controversy difficult to resolve because the parties to it appear to agree on the facts and disagree on how the facts should be organized.

Ganoss upland fauna centers on the cold-heath and high-meadow grazing community: herds of **hill elk** that move between lowland winter shelter and high summer meadows on cycles the Ganoss clans have tracked for generations, **upland hares** in densities that fluctuate in long cycles the reasons for which have been debated by both Ganoss tradition and Academy ecology without resolution, and the predators that follow both — the **grey hunt-cat**, the **north wolf**, and in the highest reaches of the Orched Hills, the **stone eagle**, whose wingspan reaches two meters and whose hunting range is measured in valleys rather than fields.

The north wolf deserves particular note because it is one of the few large predators whose range spans, in seasonal movement, nearly the entire northern half of the continent. Northern wolf packs winter in the Ganoss lowlands and move their summer ranges as far east as the Orched Hills and as far south, in exceptional years, as the upper Plains. The seasonal extent of this range means that understanding north wolf behavior in any one location requires understanding their behavior across a span of terrain that no single community observes. The Ganoss clans, whose traditional territory overlaps substantially with the wolf's winter range, have detailed knowledge of local pack behavior. What the wolves do in summer is another community's knowledge. Stitching these accounts together is a project the Academy has formally proposed four times without completing.

## Pyros

West Pyros's volcanic landscape creates specific conditions for fauna: mineral-rich soils support plant communities that support herbivore communities that support predators, all shifted somewhat from their counterparts on non-volcanic terrain. The fumarole districts have their own micro-fauna of thermal-tolerant invertebrates and scavenger birds that the Pyrosi treat with a mixture of practical awareness and religious caution — certain birds that feed at steam vents are considered omens in specific conditions, which has complicated Academy attempts to conduct straightforward population surveys.

The most notable large predator in West Pyros is the **terrace leopard**, a heavy, pale-coated cat adapted to the steep terraced slopes of the vine and grain country. It is a persistent presence in Pyrosi agricultural memory: responsible for livestock losses, occasionally dangerous to solitary farmworkers, and thoroughly embedded in the region's visual art and religious symbolism. Terrace leopards are hunted, but the hunting is governed by rules that vary by valley and have frustrated several attempts at continent-wide regulatory summary. In some valleys the leopard may only be killed if it has taken domestic animals. In others, certain individuals are protected by name and must not be killed under any circumstance. Which individuals these are is known locally and not well recorded outside.

East Pyros, drier and more open, supports a different community: the **road fox**, a lean, bold scavenger associated with caravan routes and settlement edges throughout the eastern rain-shadow landscape; the **dry-plateau hawk**, which hunts the upland grasslands; and populations of the **spine lizard** — a large, territorial reptile of the rocky east-facing slopes — that reach their densest concentrations in the warm southeastern sections of East Pyros before the terrain drops into Moroshé country.

## The Plains

The central Plains support Azhora's largest and most complex megafauna community. This is, in practice if not in legal documentation, some of the most extensively studied wild fauna on the continent, because the peoples who have lived and moved through the Plains for as long as any record reaches have accumulated knowledge of animal behavior that the Academy has been attempting to formalize in useful ways for approximately two centuries, with mixed results.

The dominant wild grazers are the **great horned herd** — a collective term in Standard Mittoli that combines several related species of large bovid-adjacent ungulates that the Plains peoples name and distinguish far more precisely. The Caeras Valley Academy's consistent failure to reproduce the Plains distinctions in their own taxonomy has been noted by Plains communities without apparent surprise. What matters to outside understanding is that the wild grazers exist in numbers that dwarf the domestic tallhorn herds, move on routes that the Plains peoples have charted over generations, and support the full predation complex that operates around them.

The large predators of the Plains are three in documented form. The **hunt-hound pack** — not related to domestic dogs despite the name — travels in organized packs of eight to thirty animals, takes prey by coordinated exhaustion rather than ambush, and maintains pack territories that overlap with the seasonal movement routes of the great horned herds in ways that appear structured rather than accidental. The **grey grass-lion** is a solitary ambush predator that preys on isolated or weakened individuals; its coat exactly matches the tall dead grass of high summer, which has contributed to encounters that ended badly for travelers who did not know to watch for the specific stillness that precedes a rush. The **black soar-bird** — technically a vulture-relative, not a bird of prey — follows both the herds and the predators at heights that allow it to monitor several kilometers of grazing landscape simultaneously; its presence circling at low altitude indicates a kill within the hour and is used by both predators and human hunters as a locating signal.

The Memory Grass sites merit mention here because they represent the most clearly documented case of wild fauna behavior that current ecological theory does not cleanly explain. Tallhorn herds — and, according to Plains pastoral accounts, wild grazing herds as well — refuse to cross certain sections of the grassland that appear, by every measurable criterion, to be adequate grazing. The Plains peoples mark these sites, move routes around them, and maintain generational records of their locations. The sites do not correspond to any soil, water, or vegetation characteristic identified in Academy surveys. Hunt-hound packs, by contrast, do not avoid the sites, and several pack dens have been documented within them. Whether the avoidance behavior is a learned response transmitted through herd tradition or a response to something the grazing animals perceive that survey instruments do not detect is not established.

## Moroshé Desert and Arid Interior

Desert fauna is structured around water and darkness. The large animals of the Moroshé interior move at night, rest in shade during the day, and organize their ranges around water sources — springs, seasonal wells, canyon seeps — with a precision that reflects long adaptation to conditions where water access is the binding constraint on everything else.

The **sand-cat** of the Moroshé is the dominant mid-size predator of the open desert. It is pale, large-eared, and almost entirely nocturnal. Desert communities near its range know its track patterns, hunting routes, and the territory boundaries that individual animals maintain over years. Academy naturalists who have attempted to study it directly have found it capable of remaining effectively invisible in terrain that appears to offer no cover; the local knowledge of its whereabouts consistently outperforms survey methodology.

The large scavenger of the desert margins is the **bone-bird** — a heavy, bald-headed vulture relative with a wingspan approaching two and a half meters, capable of processing large carcasses completely and serving the essential sanitation function in an environment where decomposition is slow. Bone-birds are the most visible large animals of the Moroshé from caravan routes; they are often the first indicator of water, since both potential death and potential life concentrate around it. Caravan cultures have a complicated relationship with them: practically useful as indicators, bad omen in most interpretive traditions, apparently indifferent to both assessments.

Reptile fauna is extensive in the desert and poorly catalogued. The Academy's herpetological surveys are among its least complete, partly because the people who know the reptile fauna most thoroughly — the oasis communities and canyon people of the deep Moroshé margins — have not found the survey process useful to participate in, and partly because the animals themselves are resistant to the observation methods that work adequately for birds and large mammals. What is known: the **spine lizard** reaches its largest documented forms in the canyon country east of the Moroshé interior; several species of desert viper are known by description to oasis healers who treat envenomations with medicines that the Academy has not obtained the formulations for; and the **canyon tortoise**, a large, slow-moving grazer of desert seeps and seasonal wash vegetation, may live as long as two centuries, a lifespan that places individual animals in living memory across multiple human generations.

The Iberos Coast, facing the sea, has a marine-adjacent fauna treated more fully in the coastal section below.

## Iberos Coast and the Iberos Sea

The Iberos Sea is one of the most biologically productive bodies of water bordering the known world. Its combination of warm surface currents from the south, cold upwelling from deep-water flows, and the wide continental shelf along the eastern coast creates conditions for fish populations that support both large marine predators and the coastal fisheries that have fed Azhoran cities for as long as cities have existed.

The marine megafauna of the Iberos includes multiple cetacean species. The **Iberos long-whale**, the largest animal documented in Azhoran natural history records, feeds on the dense fish shoals of the central sea during summer and moves toward warmer coastal waters in winter; calving groups have been observed close to shore along the Svaleen coast in mild years, and the births are considered auspicious by the coastal communities who witness them. **Grey dolphins** are a consistent presence in Iberos coastal waters and have been documented in the Lizeem estuary at Nylon during upriver fish migrations; the Nylon fishing community considers them neither good nor bad omen but monitors their behavior as an indicator of shoal location and movement.

Shark species are present throughout the Iberos and are a consistent part of coastal fishing culture's practical knowledge and symbolic vocabulary. The largest documented, the **blue finback**, reaches lengths at which it is credibly dangerous to the smaller fishing vessels; most Iberos coastal traditions include protocols for encounters. Several coastal communities maintain taboos on killing specific shark species, in patterns that do not uniformly correspond to size or danger, suggesting the taboo origins are cultural and historical rather than entirely practical.

The seabird colonies of the Iberos coast are among the most extensive on the continent. Certain rocky headlands and offshore islands — particularly along the Svaleen coast and the exposed Legemum headlands — host breeding populations of the **gannet-relative** the Academy catalogues as the Great White Sea-plunger, whose vertical dives from height into the Iberos shoals are one of the more visible demonstrations of the sea's productivity. The **Iberos albatross**, a large, slow-breeding oceanic species, appears over the coast in winter and is understood by Azhoran sailors to spend its summers somewhere beyond the horizon south of Azhora — beyond what Azhoran geography extends to. Where it breeds is not established from an Azhoran perspective.

The **black migratory geese** that appear on the Iberos coast in late autumn in vast flocks, winter in the coastal marshes and river mouths, and depart in spring toward the north — these are among the most visible seasonal markers in coastal Azhoran culture and calendrical tradition. They come from somewhere north. What that north looks like in detail is a question for which partial Elodi ceremonial accounts provide the only non-speculative Azhoran answer.

## Ibenale, Alezhor, and the Western Corridor

The fauna of the Ibenwood's western edge is shaped by the boundary between forest and coast — an edge environment that supports species from both systems and species particular to the edge itself, which is a distinct habitat rather than merely a transition.

The **forest edge-cat** — different from both the terrace leopard of Pyros and whatever occupies the Ibenwood interior — is the medium predator of the Ibenale and Alezhor forest margin. It is smaller than its highland relative, more arboreal, and has been observed hunting birds at the canopy level of the forest edge trees as readily as small mammals on the ground. The Alezhor coastal cities have lost it from the settled coastal strip; it persists in the wooded hinterland and in the margins of the gold-mining valleys where human activity does not maintain continuous pressure.

River otters and their larger relatives occupy the rivers from the Oremindi meltwater sources through the forest-margin watercourses of Alezhor. The largest form, the **great river otter**, is substantially more capable of taking fish than its smaller cousins and has been documented taking prey the size of a young river-horn calf in exceptional cases; this has not endeared it to some riverine farming communities, though fishing communities tend toward a more neutral view based on the accuracy with which the great otter locates fish shoals in clouded water.

The birds of the western corridor tend toward the forest species of the Ibenwood in the tree cover and toward coastal species near the sea, with a rich transition zone where both appear. The corridor is also a north-south migration route: species moving between northern Azhora and the southern peninsulas follow the coastal plain in preference to mountain crossing, making Ibenale and Alezhor seasonally diverse in ways that do not reflect year-round resident communities.

## The Ibenwood

The fauna of the Ibenwood is, in broad terms, what one might expect of an ancient, large, undisturbed forest: high diversity, large individuals compared to related species outside the forest, specialist adaptations to specific micro-habitats, and ecological relationships more intricate than the same species exhibit in younger and more disturbed environments. In these respects the Ibenwood's fauna mirrors its flora.

In other respects, it does not mirror the flora so much as extend the same principle further. The anomalous species documented in the Ibenwood ground flora surveys — the plants whose behaviors current botanical theory cannot account for — have their analogues in fauna reports from within the forest. The **listeners**, a collective term in Forest Mittoli for several bird species that are not well distinguished in outside nomenclature, are reported to appear consistently before and not during human activity in specific parts of the forest, in a pattern that persistent Academy observers have confirmed is difficult to attribute to ordinary alarm behavior. The **pale deer** — which may or may not be a single species — is seen regularly by Forest Mittoli communities and rarely by outside travelers in the same locations, in a frequency discrepancy that is larger than hunting pressure, habituation, or observer skill account for cleanly. Several large predator species are known to the Ibenwood's interior by track, by kill evidence, and by Forest Mittoli account, but have not been directly documented by Academy expeditions to a standard that permits formal description.

The Ibenwood's birds are its most documented fauna from an outside perspective, primarily because birds are visible at the canopy level from the forest margin without requiring deep entry. The Grey Vault canopy — those cathedral crowns forty to sixty meters above the forest floor — supports complete avian communities that include species not documented nesting anywhere outside it. Whether these are distinct species or the Ibenwood forms of species found elsewhere is a classification question for which the evidence is incomplete.

The fish and aquatic life of the Ibenwood's interior streams, where they have been surveyed, show the same pattern as the forest's plant life: related to species in Azhora's other river systems, but larger, longer-lived, and more behaviorally specific in their ecological relationships. The **deep-pool cavern fish** documented in underground sections of specific Ibenwood watercourses represents a case where the same evolutionary step toward cave-adaptation has occurred in what appear to be at least three independent lineages, which the Academy has proposed reflects the unusual age and continuity of the forest's water systems.

Forest Mittoli knowledge of Ibenwood fauna is extensive, structured, and not systematically shared with outside researchers. The sharing that has occurred has been specific: particular behavioral knowledge offered in contexts where it served an identified purpose, usually the practical safety of an outside party. The broader structure of Forest Mittoli ecological knowledge — its organizing principles, its categories, the theoretical framework within which specific knowledge sits — has not been transmitted in forms that outside researchers have found fully useful. Academy field notes consistently record helpful practical guidance and consistently fail to extract the explanatory model behind it.

## Bouén

The Bouén peninsula's cold, fog-persistent climate — maintained year-round by the Deep River current — is biologically unusual in ways that consistently surprise naturalists who study it expecting a simple cold-climate fauna.

The cold current drives upwelling that makes the coastal waters around Bouén exceptionally productive. The **Bouén fin-whale** — possibly a population of the Iberos long-whale that has adapted to the cold-current conditions, possibly a related but separate species — feeds in these waters seasonally, as do large populations of **southern grey seal** that are distinct from their northern Bay of Lol relatives and whose territory is the westernmost coastal strip of the peninsula. Seabird colonies on the exposed western headlands are among the densest documented in Azhoran natural history.

The fog-forest of Bouén's interior — where it exists; much of the peninsula is exposed and wind-pruned — supports cold-adapted forest fauna: a **small forest deer** whose summer coat is nearly black and whose winter coat is the grey of fog-light on wet bark; the **fog-cat**, a small, heavy-furred predator about which relatively little is known because it tends to be active in conditions that make observation difficult; and a **moss-vole** species complex of small rodents that occupy the deep sphagnum moss systems of the boggy interior and are prey for almost everything that eats small animals on the peninsula.

The migratory birds of Bouén are worth specific mention because several species that appear on the peninsula in winter have not been documented nesting anywhere in the known Azhoran world. They arrive from a southerly direction in autumn and depart in a southerly direction in spring, which suggests their summer range is beyond the southern extent of Azhoran geographic knowledge. The Elodi community of Elod on the southeast peninsula maintains ceremonial accounts describing specific birds as travelers between this world and what lies below the horizon; whether this framing reflects literal geographic knowledge of where the birds breed or is entirely symbolic is a question the Elodi clergy has not agreed to resolve for outside questioners.

## Oremindi Mountains

The alpine fauna of the Oremindi is stratified by altitude in predictable ways: the lower forested slopes support Ibenwood-adjacent fauna; the treeline zone has its own community; the high alpine above the treeline is dominated by cold-specialists; the glaciated upper zones support almost nothing in vertebrate form except the scavengers that follow mountaineering animals into the cold and several bird species that nest above the snowline by preference rather than necessity.

The **Oremindi snowgoat** occupies the steep high terrain between the treeline and the glaciers. It is the defining large animal of the upper mountains and the one most embedded in Sevronese mountain culture. It navigates terrain that would be impassable for other large animals, is perfectly white in winter and patchy grey-white in summer, and is hunted in the lower mountain by communities with ancient protocols that govern the harvest. In Sevronese thought, the snowgoat is not hunted in the sense that the word implies pursuit; the relationship is described as something closer to reception, which is also how Sevron describes the relationship between the mountain and practitioners of the Verath. Whether this is a metaphor or a technical description of the actual interaction is not answerable from outside.

The **mountain eagle** of the Oremindi is the apex aerial predator of the high range. Its range and nesting ecology are largely known by the communities living at altitude. Its significance in the symbolic systems of Sevron, the pass communities, and several of the mountain-adjacent valley peoples is documented; the ecological specifics of its behavior are documented only partially, because the environments where it actually nests and hunts are not easily accessed by researchers who have not trained for high-altitude mountain travel.

Sevron's knowledge of the high mountain fauna, like its knowledge of alpine flora, is not transmitted to outside researchers. The Academy has proposed that certain fauna described in indirect Sevronese accounts — the **pale-fox** of the high scree slopes; something called in translation "the one who crosses" whose species cannot be established from the description — may not be separately documented animals and might be. This remains unresolved.

## On Ibenwood Forms and Common Forms in Fauna

The Flora Distribution document established the observation that Ibenwood plant populations tend to be larger, longer-lived, more chemically elaborate, and more behaviorally unusual than populations of the same species in younger or more disturbed environments. The question of whether this principle extends to fauna is one the Academy has discussed and not formally answered, partly because the evidence for Ibenwood-form fauna is less systematically documented than the evidence for Ibenwood-form plants, and partly because what evidence there is requires engaging with Forest Mittoli knowledge in ways that the Academy's formal documentation procedures have found difficult.

What can be said: every category of faunal comparison for which data exists shows the same directional pattern. Ibenwood individuals of documented species are larger than their counterparts outside the forest. Ibenwood populations of documented species appear to live longer. The behavioral complexity and ecological specificity of species documented inside and outside the Ibenwood is consistently greater inside. This is the same pattern as in flora, and it admits the same two framings.

The Academy framing: the Ibenwood is an exceptionally stable and ancient environment that permits the development of complexity over timescales not available in younger or more disturbed habitats. Species in the Ibenwood have had more time, in a more continuous environment, with richer ecological networks to integrate into, than species elsewhere. The result is an enhanced expression of the same species.

The Forest Mittoli framing, as best as it has been recorded in outside accounts: there is no "enhanced expression." The Ibenwood animals are what those animals are. The animals outside the Ibenwood are what those animals become when the Iben is no longer part of what is around them. The direction of the difference runs from fullness to diminishment, not from baseline to enhancement. The common forms are missing something; they are not at a neutral starting point.

This disagreement has a practical consequence for animal translocation and breeding programs. The Academy has in several instances attempted to establish breeding populations of Ibenwood species outside the forest for conservation or study purposes. The programs have consistently produced animals that survive, breed, and exhibit the behavioral repertoire of the species — but at the scale, intensity, and ecological specificity of the common form rather than the Ibenwood form, even in animals born in captivity from Ibenwood parents and raised in conditions designed to replicate the forest environment. The Forest Mittoli response to these programs, when asked, has been consistent and brief. They have been told that the Iben is not a condition that can be replicated. The animals produced are the animals that result from being somewhere other than the Iben. This is what one would expect.

## A Note on Migration and Unknown Origins

A recurring feature of Azhoran fauna records is the category of migratory species that arrive from unknown origin points and depart for unknown destinations. The Iberos albatross. The black migratory geese. Several species of wading bird that appear on the northern coast in summer and depart in autumn. The whale populations that transit the Iberos Sea on schedules that local fishermen track without knowing where the transits end.

From an Azhoran perspective — a perspective that has reliable geographic knowledge of the continent and partial knowledge of the surrounding seas — these migrations extend into territory that is not known. The animals cross horizons that Azhoran mariners have not crossed. Their summer ranges, their winter ranges, their breeding grounds are somewhere beyond the limits of what the Azhoran geographic tradition maps.

The Elodi community's ceremonial accounts occasionally reference specific species as travelers between the known world and what they call, in Koleth, the part of the world that waits — which their tradition holds exists beyond the Iberos Sea to the east and east of the Ice Spear to the north. Whether this reflects a literal geographic belief that the birds breed on the Old Kingdom's coasts, or on the landmasses of the three connected northern continents, or somewhere else entirely, is not established from outside the Elodi theological tradition, and the Elodi clergy has not chosen to specify.

What is known is that the animals return. Every autumn, the black geese arrive. Every spring, they leave. The watercourse they follow into the unknown is the same watercourse the continent follows, but the continent does not follow it as far, and the animals do not explain where they have been.
