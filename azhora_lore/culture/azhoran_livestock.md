# Livestock of Azhora

Livestock is one of the deepest forms of wealth in Azhora. Coin belongs to cities, tax offices, ports, armies, and merchants. Animals belong to households, routes, fields, bride agreements, temple obligations, winter survival, and the visible reputation of a family. Outside major cities, a person who has coin but no animals is often considered liquid but fragile. A person with animals has food, traction, breeding power, manure, hide, milk, mobility, and witnesses.

This is why rural Azhorans often speak of wealth in living measures. A household may be "three milkers and a wet-year calf rich." A Plains lineage may be "two hundred horn-rights strong." A Ganoss clan may describe a marriage as "worth nine winter cows, not counting wool." A desert caravan master may laugh at coin and ask how many water animals can be brought alive to the next oasis.

Livestock wealth is not primitive money. It is more complicated than money. Animals reproduce, sicken, age, wander, remember routes, require labor, create obligations, and die inconveniently. They are wealth with appetite. Because of this, livestock is never purely private property in the way city merchants understand property. Herds move through shared landscapes, drink at negotiated water, cross fields after harvest, require breeding exchange, and carry marks that identify kinship and legal responsibility. A cow may be owned by one household, pastured by another, bred through rights held by a third, tithed to a temple in milk, and pledged as surety in a lawsuit. Coin can be counted. Livestock must be known.

## The Horn Standard

Most Azhoran rural economies use some form of **horn standard**, a customary way of valuing wealth in large domestic herd animals rather than coin. The exact animal differs by region. In wet Mittolo, the reference animal is usually a mature Lizeem river-horn cow in milk. On the Plains, it is a breeding plains tallhorn cow with a living calf. In the north, it may be a Ganoss shag-ox winter cow. In desert-edge communities, it may be a dustback cow proven to survive two dry years.

The horn standard is used for:

- bridewealth and marriage settlements
- blood compensation and injury law
- temple dues
- apprenticeship entry payments
- land-use guarantees
- caravan sureties
- political gifts between chiefs, estates, or cities
- emergency famine lending
- militia provisioning obligations

Urban scribes regularly misunderstand the horn standard because they convert animals into coin too quickly. Rural law does not ask only what an animal is worth in sale. It asks what the animal does. A dry cow, a milking cow, a trained draft cow, a proven breeder, a young bull, a castrated hauling ox, and a named lead cow may all have different values even if a city butcher would reduce them to meat weight. A living herd also has shape: too many old cows, too few breeding males, no trained lead animals, weak winter calves, or bad temper in the female line can make an apparently large herd economically poor.

The oldest Plains saying on the matter is often translated: "Coin has one face. A herd has weather."

## The Great Bovid Complex

Azhora has ordinary cattle in the broad sense: domestic bovids bred for milk, meat, hide, horn, manure, and traction. But the continent's climates are varied enough that no single "cow" dominates everywhere. Instead, Azhora has a **great bovid complex**: several related or functionally equivalent large herd animals, some close enough to interbreed only through managed crossings, some merely similar in use. City people call all of them cattle when speaking loosely. Herders do not.

The most important buffalo-like cow equivalents are the following:

1. **Lizeem river-horns**, the wetland and floodplain buffalo-cattle of Mittolo and Mithala.
2. **Plains tallhorns**, the huge mobile grassland cattle of the central Plains.
3. **Moroshé dustbacks**, heat-tolerant desert-edge and oasis bovids.
4. **Ganoss shag-oxen**, cold upland bovids of the north and Orched approaches.
5. **Pyrosi ashbacks**, compact volcanic-terrace cattle adapted to steep, mineral-rich slopes.

Only the first three are always included when scholars speak of "the three buffalo-cattle of Azhora." The Ganoss and Pyrosi forms are sometimes classified separately because they do not look as buffalo-like to southern eyes. Husbandmen object to this. Their answer is practical: if it pulls, calves, milks, fights mud or weather better than a city cow, and holds family wealth in its body, it belongs in the same conversation.

## 1. Lizeem River-Horns

**Primary range:** Mithala floodplain, Lizeem distributaries, Minora forklands, Ond wet margins.
**Ecology:** floodplain, paddy, canal, wet meadow, river levee.
**Main uses:** draft in mud, milk, manure, hide, river transport hauling, floodplain wealth standard.
**Closest real-world analogy:** water buffalo, but more riverine and legally central.

The Lizeem river-horn is the animal that makes Mithala's grain possible at scale. It is broad-bodied, dark-skinned, sparse-haired, and heavy in the shoulder, with crescent horns that sweep back close to the skull before rising outward. Its hooves spread under weight, allowing it to move through mud that would cripple horses and exhaust ordinary cattle. Its temperament is usually slow, thoughtful, and dangerous only when rushed. Mithalan farmers consider impatience around river-horns a moral defect.

River-horns love water but not chaos. A good animal will stand belly-deep in a canal during heat, pull a silt harrow through knee mud, haul reed-rice seedlings, and return to the same byre platform before flood darkness. A badly trained animal will use its weight to make a philosophical argument against everyone present. Training therefore begins early. Calves are led through shallow channels before weaning so water becomes home, not panic.

### Body and Breed Types

River-horns vary by branch. Minoran grain houses recognize five major trade types:

**Fork black** river-horns are the Minoran standard: large, calm, excellent for hauling wagons along causeways and pulling canal dredges. They are expensive and often jointly owned by several households or a bridge estate.

**Reed gray** river-horns are common in Ond and southern wetlands. They are smaller, better in deep marsh, and valued for richer milk. Their hides are thinner, but their tolerance for insects is legendary.

**Siltback** river-horns are Mithalan field animals, dark with a pale stripe of dried-looking hair along the spine. They are the best paddy and floodwheat draft animals. They dislike stony ground and should not be taken into the hills except by fools or desperate people.

**White-horned temple stock** are not truly white, but their horns pale early. Temple farms breed them for ritual visibility and calm temper. Their calves are given in oath settlements.

**Low-branch mud bulls** are heavy, aggressive males used for breeding and flood hauling. They are rarely kept near villages except by families who have inherited both skill and stubbornness.

### Feed and Seasonal Care

River-horns eat reed hay, rice straw, floodwheat chaff, canal sedges, cut meadow grass, and winter bean haulm. They need mineral-rich mud access or salt supplementation. In the Lizeem villages, children bring salt cakes to working animals after long days, pressing the cakes into wooden troughs so the animals do not bite fingers by accident.

Winter care is less about cold than damp. River-horns tolerate cool weather but suffer if made to stand in wet filth. Their byres are raised on packed silt platforms with reed drainage mats. The best villages rotate bedding reed, manure, and ash into compost for floodwheat fields. A household with river-horns therefore owns not only draft power but soil fertility.

### Breeding and Wealth

A mature milking river-horn cow is a major wealth unit in Mithala. A team of trained oxen is more valuable than many houses. Bulls are usually controlled by village agreements because one bad sire can damage a herd line for years. Breeding rights are recorded by horn notches, rope tags, and witness memory. In Minora, written contracts are common. Downstream villages distrust written breeding contracts unless accompanied by a meal and three people old enough to remember previous frauds.

River-horn bridewealth is not simple payment. A bride who brings river-horn rights into a marriage brings field capacity. In some Mithalan districts, daughters inherit milk rights separately from ownership, meaning the cow may live with the husband's household while a portion of milk, calves, or manure value remains owed to the woman's natal line. City lawyers hate this. Village courts call it ordinary.

### Disease and Veterinary Practice

The major disease is **hoof rot**, especially after warm stagnant floods. River-horn hooves must be cleaned, inspected, and packed with ash-clay paste. The second danger is **redwater fever**, carried by marsh insects in late summer. Old herders watch the eyes first; a dull-eyed animal that refuses canal water is treated before symptoms become dramatic.

River-horn veterinary specialists are called **mud-hands**. They are respected, poorly washable, and often wealthier than their clothing suggests. A mud-hand can set a horn crack, drain an abscess, turn a stuck calf, judge a breeding bull, and tell whether a field is too sour for both animal and grain.

### Cultural Meaning

The river-horn is patient power. It appears in Mithalan proverbs about endurance, marriage, flood law, and stubborn truth. Minora's bridge seals often show a stylized river-horn head below an arch, meaning that civic stone rests on animal labor. During flood thanksgiving, river-horns are garlanded with blue reed flowers and led through shallow water while children splash them. The animals endure this with the expression of officials at a festival.

## 2. Plains Tallhorns

**Primary range:** central and northern Plains, Ganoss edges, East Pyros grass routes.
**Ecology:** open grassland, seasonal river corridors, mobile pastoral routes.
**Main uses:** mobile wealth, milk, meat, hide, horn, bridewealth, blood compensation, political alliance.
**Closest real-world analogy:** buffalo, longhorn cattle, and bison-like pastoral cattle.

Plains tallhorns are the great wealth animals of the grassland peoples. They stand high at the shoulder, with long legs, deep chests, and sweeping horns that can span wider than a person is tall. Their coats vary from dun to red-brown to smoky black. Unlike river-horns, they are not wetland animals. They want distance, grass, wind, and herd structure. A tallhorn confined too long becomes unhealthy and socially unpleasant, which Plains herders say is also true of people.

Tallhorns are not merely livestock on the Plains. They are legal persons adjacent to property, or property adjacent to persons, depending on who is translating. Herds encode route rights. A lineage's right to cross a seasonal river may depend on having maintained tallhorn movement along that corridor for generations. A herd is therefore a moving archive. Its lead cows know water, salt licks, wind shelters, Memory Grass sites, and safe calving grounds. When fixed states seize tallhorns as tax, they often destroy knowledge they did not know existed.

### Herd Structure

Plains tallhorn herds are organized around lead cows, not bulls. A lead cow is not simply the oldest animal. She is the one the herd trusts to move. Herders mark lead cows with dyed mane cords, horn paints, or bells made from wood rather than metal. Metal bells are considered rude in several Plains cultures because they announce the herd to strangers and predators alike.

Bulls are kept, traded, borrowed, boasted about, and occasionally killed for being too much like chiefs. A good bull adds bone, horn, disease resistance, and calf vigor. A bad bull adds injuries and stories. Castrated males are used as pack and drag animals, especially for moving tent frames, hide bundles, and trade loads.

### Seasonal Movement

Tallhorn husbandry follows the grass:

**Thaw routes** move along river corridors as snowmelt or spring rain greens the low ground.

**High grass routes** spread into central meadows when the deep-rooted grasses rise.

**Salt-turn routes** visit mineral licks, often under strict inter-lineage schedule.

**Heat routes** move toward windier uplands or tree islands during high summer insects.

**Calf-safe routes** avoid Memory Grass battle sites, predator corridors, and contested water.

**Winter-close routes** bring herds to sheltered valleys, reed bottoms, or trade-edge encampments where stored hay, grain byproducts, and brush fodder can supplement grazing.

Outsiders call this wandering. Plains law calls it grammar.

### Wealth and Law

Tallhorn wealth is counted by living categories:

**Milk cows** support households directly.

**Calf cows** prove the future.

**Route cows** are animals whose ancestry is tied to a specific movement right.

**Witness cows** are animals present at major settlements, marked and remembered so their descendants can prove continuity.

**Peace bulls** are breeding males exchanged after conflict to create mutual dependence.

**Blood cattle** are animals paid as compensation for injury, killing, or dishonor.

Because tallhorns are central to compensation law, theft is not simply economic crime. It can be an attack on a lineage's capacity to make peace. Stealing a coin purse insults a person. Stealing a lead cow may reopen a feud.

### Milk, Meat, and Products

Tallhorn milk is lower in fat than river-horn milk but produced over long movement seasons. It is drunk fresh, soured in hide bags, churned into travel butter, and dried into hard curd cakes. The hides make tent panels, shields, armor backing, and water bags. Horns become cups, bows, signal instruments, and legal tokens. Dung fuels fires in treeless country. Tail hair is braided into rope and ritual cords.

Meat is eaten, but slaughter is socially heavy. A tallhorn is not killed casually unless it is old, injured, ceremonially required, or politically necessary. Plains feasts are impressive precisely because everyone understands the wealth being consumed.

### Disease and Dangers

The main natural danger is **lung chill** after cold rain and wind during spring movement. Herders manage this by not pushing herds too hard after storms. The major parasite is **grass tick**, especially in tall wet years. Smoke, ash rubs, and controlled burning help. Tallhorns also respond strongly to Memory Grass sites. A herd that refuses to cross apparently good grazing may be reading something herders take seriously.

### Cultural Meaning

Tallhorns mean freedom to outsiders and law to insiders. They are sung as companions of the open sky, but Plains songs are practical: the beloved animal is praised for remembering water, not for looking majestic on a hill. The highest praise for a tallhorn cow is "she brought everyone home."

## 3. Moroshé Dustbacks

**Primary range:** Moroshé margins, oasis chains, canyon country, East Pyros dry routes, southern trade corridors.
**Ecology:** desert-edge scrub, gravel plain, oasis pasture, dry wash, thorn country.
**Main uses:** heat-resistant milk, caravan traction, hide, dung fuel, drought wealth, oasis bridewealth.
**Closest real-world analogy:** buffalo-cattle crossed conceptually with zebu and desert oxen.

The Moroshé dustback is the great bovid of the dry world. It is not a central sand-sea animal; no large grazer thrives where there is nothing to graze. Dustbacks belong to the desert margins, oasis circuits, canyon bottoms after rain, and scrubby transition lands. They are humped at the shoulder, loose-skinned, short-haired, wide-muzzled, and patient in heat. Their horns are shorter than tallhorns and thicker than river-horns, curving outward like hooks.

They are called dustbacks because their coats carry a pale powdery ridge along the spine and shoulders. Even freshly washed animals seem dusted by the road. Desert people consider this beautiful. Coastal traders consider it evidence that washing failed.

### Water Discipline

Dustbacks are valuable because they know how not to die between water. They cannot go without water like camels in traveler tales, but they can conserve, graze dry thorn, reduce movement in heat, and recover quickly at wells. They are watered by schedule, not sympathy. A city person who lets a dustback drink freely after a hard dry march may kill it. Water must be given in rounds, with pauses, while the animal's breathing settles.

Oasis law often regulates dustback watering before human trade. This offends foreigners until they understand that animals unable to leave the oasis create dead caravans, unpaid debts, and eventually dead humans.

### Feed

Dustbacks browse as much as graze. They eat dry grass, thorn leaves, seed pods, chopped palm waste, olive press cake, barley straw, and certain saltbushes that other cattle refuse. They should not be given rich wet fodder suddenly. Too much green feed after dry pasture causes gut swelling, which desert herders describe as "the belly forgetting the road."

In canyon country, dustbacks are driven along seasonal browse ledges after rains. In oasis settlements, they are fed on cut reed, date waste, and stored straw. Poor households often share ownership of one milking dustback, rotating milk rights by day.

### Breeding

Dustback breeding is conservative. A proven drought cow is worth more than an impressive bull. Calves are expected to survive heat, uneven milk, and long movement by their second season. Animals that require constant pampering are culled or sold to coastal estates, where they become decorative and contemptible.

There are three major regional types:

**Oasis red dustbacks** are smaller, higher-milk animals kept near wells and settlements.

**Canyon blackhorns** are tough, sure-footed animals used in canyon country and dry washes.

**Road dustbacks** are larger caravan animals bred for pulling and carrying in teams.

### Economic Role

Dustbacks are the bank of desert-edge households. Coin may enter through caravan trade, but dustbacks hold value through drought. A family with a dustback cow can turn thorn and straw into milk, dung fuel, calves, and social standing. A family with only coin must find someone willing to sell food during scarcity.

Caravans use dustback oxen for heavy loads on routes too rough or too dry for ordinary cattle but not suitable for faster pack animals. They pull water sledges, grain carts, salt blocks, and timber imports. Their pace is slow. Their reliability is the point.

Dustback hide makes tough water buckets, shield covers, sandals, bellows, and pack lashings. Dung is dried as fuel in treeless districts. Horn is carved into oil spoons and well tokens.

### Disease and Care

The major danger is **heat founder**, a hoof and tendon condition after forced movement on hot stone. Herders move dustbacks before dawn and after dusk, resting during the white part of day. Another danger is **salt eye**, irritation from dust and mineral wind. Eyes are washed with weak tea or clean well water. A dustback with cloudy eyes loses route value.

Dustbacks are vulnerable to cold wet weather, which surprises northerners. Rain followed by wind can chill them badly. In East Pyros border towns, dustback shelters face away from mountain winds, and animals are rubbed dry with straw after winter rains.

### Cultural Meaning

Dustbacks symbolize endurance without drama. Desert people distrust romantic praise of suffering, but they respect animals that continue. A common saying near Nova Homa is: "A horse believes the road is an event. A dustback knows it is a life."

## 4. Ganoss Shag-Oxen

**Primary range:** Ganoss uplands, Northern Azhora, Orched Hills, cold Velm fringes.
**Ecology:** heath, cold upland pasture, wind-scoured plateau, winter byre systems.
**Main uses:** milk, woolly hair, meat, pack work, sled hauling, winter wealth, clan payments.
**Closest real-world analogy:** yak, musk ox, highland cattle, cold buffalo.

The Ganoss shag-ox is the great cold bovid of northern Azhora. It is long-haired, heavy-necked, short-legged compared with tallhorns, and astonishingly tolerant of wind. Its horns sweep outward and forward, less wide than plains tallhorns but dangerous in close quarters. Its coat hangs in layered skirts that shed snow and rain. The underhair is combed, spun, and woven into warm cloth.

Shag-oxen are central to Ganoss clan economy. Coastal northerners have fish and ships. Upland clans have shag-oxen, sheep, iron, and a talent for not being governed. A clan's winter survival depends on how well it manages hay, heath grazing, byre warmth, and calf timing.

### Seasonal Pattern

In summer, shag-oxen move to high heath where grasses, lichens, sedges, and dwarf shrubs grow during long daylight. In autumn, they are brought down gradually, not rushed, so they gain condition before winter. Winter herds stay near byre settlements, sheltered hollows, or windbreak walls. Spring is the hunger season: stored hay thins, snow crusts, and pregnant cows need careful feeding.

The best herders know **snow grazing**, reading where wind has scraped slopes enough for animals to paw through. They also cut and store heath hay, bog sedge, oat straw, and leaf fodder. A lazy summer becomes a dead spring.

### Milk, Hair, and Meat

Shag-ox milk is rich and limited. It is made into hard cheese, sour cream, and winter butter sealed in skin or ceramic crocks. The underhair, combed in spring, is nearly as valuable as milk. It produces soft, warm yarn used in cloaks, socks, saddle pads, infant wraps, and high-status winter garments.

Meat is eaten more readily than tallhorn meat because cold upland economies require culling, but breeding cows are still precious. Old oxen are honored with practical gratitude and then made into stew, hide, horn, and bone tools. The Ganoss consider wasting an animal sentimental and disrespectful.

### Wealth and Law

Ganoss wealth is counted in winter-capable cows. A summer fat animal is not proof. A cow that calves after a hard winter and stands again is wealth. Marriage settlements often specify "spring-proven" shag cows, meaning animals that have survived at least two winters and successfully calved.

Clan compensation law values shag-ox hair rights separately from animal ownership. A widow may retain spring combing rights from animals transferred to her husband's kin. Disputes over combing rights can last generations and sound absurd only to people who have never needed a winter cloak.

### Disease and Care

The major danger is **spring hollow**, a wasting condition when winter feed is too poor and the first green growth is late. Herders treat it with warmed mash, seaweed meal from coastal trade, and careful movement to early thaw patches. Hoof ice can split feet if animals are driven across crusted snow too long. Horn frost cracks are treated with fat, ash, and binding.

Shag-oxen are calm if handled by familiar people and deeply suspicious of strangers. They dislike shouting. They dislike dogs that rush. They dislike being admired too closely by city visitors in bright cloaks.

### Cultural Meaning

The shag-ox is the north's answer to wealth: not abundance, but survival made visible. Ganoss clan songs praise animals by winter deeds. The greatest praise is "she stood through the white month."

## 5. Pyrosi Ashbacks

**Primary range:** West Pyros terraces, Talermolis uplands, volcanic foothills.
**Ecology:** terraced slopes, mineral soils, warm wet valleys, ash-rich pasture.
**Main uses:** terrace draft, milk, manure, ritual offerings near Fire Memory sites, smallholder wealth.
**Closest real-world analogy:** compact buffalo-cattle adapted to mountains and volcanic soils.

Pyrosi ashbacks are compact, sure-footed bovids with dark coats, pale muzzles, and a gray ash-colored saddle along the spine. They are smaller than river-horns and less grand than tallhorns, but they can work slopes that would break both. West Pyros terrace agriculture depends on animals that can turn in narrow spaces, pull light ard-plows, carry manure baskets, and not panic near steam vents or hot mineral ground.

Ashbacks are socially embedded in Pyrosi household life. A family with two good ashback cows, one trained terrace ox, and rights to a breeding bull is considered stable even without much coin. Their manure is especially valued on volcanic terraces where fertility must be renewed carefully to avoid washing soil downhill.

### Temperament and Training

Ashbacks are alert, responsive, and easily offended by rough handling. Pyrosi farmers train them with voice, touch, and routine rather than force. Outsiders sometimes misread this as softness. Then they try to force an ashback around a terrace corner and discover that a compact animal with a low center of gravity can become immovable as doctrine.

Young ashbacks are introduced to slopes early. Calves follow mothers along terrace paths, learning turns, steps, and drainage edges. A trained terrace ox can back a cart along a stone wall with a drop on one side. Such animals are not sold casually.

### Feed and Soil Relationship

Ashbacks graze terrace margins, orchard understories, harvested grain fields, and rough volcanic pasture. They are fed grape pomace, barley straw, bean vines, orchard prunings, and mineral salt. Too much lush valley grass makes them soft-footed. Farmers prefer animals raised on mixed slope forage.

Their manure is composted with ash, leaf mold, and kitchen waste. In some Pyrosi villages, manure heaps are blessed at the same seasonal rites that ask permission of the land before planting. This is not comic to the Pyrosi. Soil fertility is the form in which divine patience becomes edible.

### Disease and Care

Ashbacks suffer from **stone bruise** if worked on dry hard paths without rest. Their hooves are trimmed more often than those of plains animals. They are also susceptible to **sweet bloat** after eating too much rich spring clover on terraces. Farmers manage spring turnout carefully, alternating dry fodder with new pasture.

Near fumarole districts, animals can develop **sulfur cough** if kept too close to vents. Pyrosi herders know which steam places are good for ritual and which are bad for lungs. Outsiders do not.

### Cultural Meaning

Ashbacks represent continuity under occupation. During foreign rule, Pyrosi communities often maintained local autonomy through terrace management. Administrators could count fields and levy grain, but they depended on villagers who knew which ashback teams could work which slopes, when manure should move, and which paths would collapse after rain. The ashback is therefore a quiet political animal: small, stubborn, essential, and difficult to command from outside.

## Ordinary Cattle and City Cows

Alongside the great regional bovids are smaller ordinary cattle, especially near cities, orchards, dairies, and mixed farms. These animals are more manageable than river-horns, less demanding than tallhorns, cheaper than shag-oxen, and easier to stable near dense settlement. City dairies keep short-horned milk cows that eat brewer's mash, vegetable waste, hay, and grain screenings. Their milk feeds urban children, tea houses, cheese sellers, and temple kitchens.

Urban cattle are coin-adjacent. They are bought, sold, leased, and insured more like commodities than kin animals. Rural people find this cold. City people find rural cattle law exhausting. Both are correct.

Ordinary cattle also serve as crossbreeding stock. River-horn crosses produce smaller wet-field animals for villages that cannot afford full river-horn teams. Ashback-cow crosses produce mild dairy animals in Pyros. Northern cow and shag-ox crosses are attempted regularly and succeed irregularly. Tallhorn crosses are politically sensitive because Plains peoples often object to fixed-state breeders treating tallhorn blood as raw material.

## Sheep, Goats, and Wool Wealth

Sheep are the second great livestock wealth after bovids in many regions. They dominate where grass is too sparse for large cattle or where wool matters more than traction.

**Ganoss blackface sheep** graze upland heath with shag-oxen. Their wool is coarse but strong, used for outer cloaks, sail padding, and felt boots.

**Orched hill sheep** are small, agile, and difficult to fence. Mining settlements keep them on rough slopes, and their mutton feeds iron workers.

**Svaleen sun sheep** are pale, heat-tolerant animals kept on dry scrub and vineyard margins. Their milk makes sharp cheeses eaten with wine.

**Plains runner sheep** move with pastoral herds, providing meat and wool without competing directly with tallhorn grazing.

Goats thrive wherever human plans have become too optimistic. They are common in Pyros, Svaleen, the Iberos coast, desert margins, and rocky northern settlements. Goats are portable wealth for poorer households because they reproduce quickly and eat almost anything. They are also socially disreputable in grain districts because they eat exactly what they should not. The legal difference between a beloved goat and a criminal goat is usually a fence.

Goat milk is essential in dry regions. Goat hair is woven into tent cloth in the Moroshé margins. Goat skins become water bags, drum heads, and parchment. Goat dung fuels small fires. A family with goats is hard to starve and hard to make respectable.

## Horses, Mules, Donkeys, and Route Animals

Horses are prestige animals in much of Azhora but not universally practical. They matter most on the Plains, in East Pyros, among northern raiding-descended elites, and in military cultures. A good horse is speed, status, and war capacity. It is not usually the foundation of household survival in the way a cow is.

Plains horses are hardy, wind-bred, and route-wise. They are smaller than heroic paintings suggest. The best ones can read herd movement and Memory Grass unease before riders do. Horse wealth among Plains peoples sits beside tallhorn wealth, not above it. A lineage with horses but no tallhorns has speed but no center.

Mules are the practical glory of trade routes. East Pyros, Amod, the Iberos coast, and the Moroshé margins all depend on mule strings. Mules carry grain, wine, salt, copper, dyed cloth, temple goods, and messages. They are sterile, expensive, sure-footed, and opinionated. Caravan masters value them because they turn fodder into distance with fewer dramas than horses.

Donkeys are humble and indispensable in dry regions. They carry water, firewood, grain sacks, children, and old people. Their low status is mostly urban snobbery. Desert communities respect a donkey that knows a well route.

## Pigs, Forest Hogs, and City Pork

Pigs are common in Mittoli forest margins, river towns, and city outskirts. They turn acorns, mast, kitchen waste, brewer's mash, and field gleanings into meat and fat. Forest hog drives are an important autumn practice in western Mittolo. Households release pigs into managed woodland under mark and agreement, then gather them before winter. Disputes over whose pigs ate whose mast are older than several legal systems.

Pigs are less common in the desert, among some Elodi communities, and in high pastoral cultures where mobile herd logic does not favor them. They are important in Minora and Nylon because cities produce waste streams pigs can use. Urban pork markets are therefore tied to sanitation. A city that mishandles pigs mishandles garbage, and everyone smells the result.

Forest hogs can go semi-wild quickly. Some Dark Grove margins have feral hog populations that hunters respect and farmers hate. Forest Mittoli communities distinguish between household pigs, mast pigs, wood hogs, and "old hogs," the last said with enough seriousness that outsiders stop asking.

## Fowl, Bees, and Small Stock

Chickens, ducks, geese, pigeons, and domestic waterfowl are widespread. Ducks are especially important in wet Mittolo and Ond paddies, where they eat pests, provide eggs, and make farmers argue about whether they compact seedbeds. Geese guard yards with moral certainty. Chickens are nearly universal in settled villages.

Bees are livestock in law, not merely insects. Forest buckwheat, orchards, vineyards, clover terraces, and wildflower meadows all depend on managed hives. Bee rights can be leased separately from land. In western forest communities, bee trees may be inherited and mapped. Honey is food, medicine, preservative, ritual substance, and trade good.

Rabbits, guinea-pig-like small herbivores in some southern districts, and fishpond species are kept by poorer households and monasteries. They matter less in prestige economies but much in survival.

## Livestock and Grain

Grain and livestock are not separate economies. They are one system breathing in two directions.

Animals eat straw, chaff, failed grain, meadow hay, bran, oilcake, brewer's mash, grape pomace, reed cuttings, and field aftermath. Fields receive manure, traction, trampling, grazing control, and sometimes destruction. A village's actual wealth lies in how well it cycles grain waste through animals and animal waste through fields.

In Mittolo, river-horns and cattle provide manure for floodwheat and spelt. In Pyros, ashbacks renew terrace soils. In the Plains, tallhorn dung fuels fires and marks old camps, while herds keep grasslands open through grazing. In the north, shag-oxen turn upland sedge and heath into milk, hair, and manure for tiny fields. In desert margins, dustbacks convert thorn and date waste into milk and traction.

Famine often begins when this cycle breaks. A grain failure can force households to slaughter breeding animals. This provides immediate food and coin but destroys future capacity. A livestock plague can leave fields unworked and unfertilized, causing grain failure the following year. Wise governments protect breeding stock during crisis. Foolish governments tax it.

## Livestock and National Economies

National power in Azhora rests visibly on grain, ports, mines, and armies. It rests quietly on livestock.

Armies need draft animals more than heroic paintings admit. Siege engines, grain wagons, tent poles, water carts, spare arrows, armor repair tools, and cooking pots all move by animal power. A state with coin but no draft animals can hire soldiers it cannot feed. The War of Seven Sons, like most wars, was shaped as much by fodder and hoof health as by speeches.

Cities need meat, milk, hides, wool, tallow, glue, horn, bone, manure, and hauling power. Leatherworking districts depend on steady hide supply. Candle makers need tallow. Bowyers need horn. Potters need dung or animal transport. Breweries need animals to consume mash and haul barrels. Even archives depend on livestock through parchment, glue, leather bindings, and lamp fat.

States therefore regulate livestock through:

- pasture rights
- road tolls by hoof count
- market inspection
- slaughter rules
- disease quarantine
- breeding bull licensing
- military requisition limits
- temple tithe animals
- winter fodder reserves
- compensation schedules

Requisition is always dangerous. Taking animals from rural households can feed an army while creating rebellion. A ruler who takes coin annoys merchants. A ruler who takes cows threatens children, marriages, fields, and next spring.

## Livestock in Marriage and Kinship

Marriage settlements across Azhora often include animals because animals reproduce obligations. Coin can be spent and forgotten. A cow given in marriage produces calves that keep the two families in relationship. This is why many systems specify not only number but sex, age, breeding status, milk rights, offspring rights, and return obligations if the marriage fails.

Typical examples:

**Mithalan river-horn settlements** may give the bride's household continued rights to the first female calf.

**Plains tallhorn marriages** often exchange animals from route-linked herds, creating legal reasons for two lineages to respect each other's movement.

**Ganoss shag-ox marriages** specify spring-proven cows and combing rights.

**Desert dustback marriages** often include watering obligations at named wells, not merely animals.

**Pyrosi ashback settlements** may tie animals to terrace maintenance obligations.

The animal is therefore not a gift alone. It is a living clause.

## Livestock, Religion, and Sacrifice

Azhora's polytheistic religious landscape makes animal ritual complex and regional. There is no single livestock theology. Most traditions acknowledge that animals stand between human labor and divine provision. This makes them appropriate offerings, oath witnesses, taboo subjects, or sacred charges depending on cult.

River-horns are garlanded in Mithalan flood rites but rarely sacrificed, because killing a working wetland animal before its time is seen as insulting the flood's generosity.

Tallhorns may be sacrificed at major peace feasts, but the act is politically heavy. The animal's lineage, color, horn shape, and owner matter.

Dustbacks are almost never sacrificed in deep drought. To do so would look like accusing the gods of not understanding arithmetic.

Shag-oxen are offered ritually as hair, butter, cheese, or carved horn more often than blood.

Ashbacks may be led to fumarole rites and marked with ash, but Pyrosi communities prefer offerings that return fertility rather than remove it.

Urban temples are more likely to sacrifice smaller cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, or fowl. Rural people sometimes find city sacrifice wasteful because city temples do not always understand breeding value.

## Marks, Names, and Memory

Most valuable livestock are marked. Methods vary:

**Horn notching** is common for bovids, especially river-horns and dustbacks.

**Ear cuts** are used for sheep, goats, and ordinary cattle.

**Dye marks** are temporary, used during seasonal mixing or market.

**Branding** exists but is regionally controversial. Plains peoples often dislike hot branding of tallhorns, preferring cuts, cords, and memory. City markets prefer brands because brands satisfy clerks.

**Bell identity** is used in wetland and mountain regions. The sound of an animal's bell can identify household, village, or route.

Names matter. A named cow is not necessarily a pet. It is an animal whose history affects legal and economic value. "Red-Foot who calved in the flood year" is not sentiment. It is recordkeeping in narrative form.

## Markets and Fairs

Livestock fairs are among the most important events in rural Azhora. They combine commerce, marriage negotiation, religious observance, hiring, debt settlement, legal arbitration, and gossip.

Major fairs include:

**The Minoran Mud Fair**, where river-horn teams are inspected after flood recession and before the main fieldwork contracts.

**The Salt-Turn Gathering** on the central Plains, where tallhorn lineages exchange breeding rights and settle grazing disputes.

**The Tros Dry Market**, where dustbacks, goats, donkeys, and caravan mules are traded before desert crossings.

**The Ganoss Spring Comb**, where shag-ox underhair, breeding cows, and winter survival reputations are displayed.

**The Gala Terrace Fair**, where Pyrosi ashbacks, terrace tools, and manure contracts are negotiated with great formality.

At these fairs, animal experts outrank people who outrank them elsewhere. A city noble may be ignored while a mud-hand, salt-route elder, or shag-ox comb judge speaks.

## Disease, Quarantine, and Political Panic

Livestock disease can destabilize entire regions. Azhora has several feared animal diseases:

**Redwater fever** affects river-horns and wetland cattle.

**Lung chill** kills tallhorns after cold wet movement.

**Spring hollow** weakens shag-oxen after poor winter feed.

**Heat founder** cripples dustbacks on forced dry marches.

**Ash cough** affects Pyrosi terrace animals near bad fumarole exposure.

**Horn pox** is a contagious skin disease of ordinary cattle and goats, rarely fatal but economically disruptive.

Quarantine law is severe because herds connect households across distance. A farmer hiding disease may be punished more harshly than a thief. Theft takes animals from one owner. Disease can take the valley.

During outbreaks, temples may organize processions, governments may close fairs, herders may block routes, and smugglers may move animals anyway. Many rural rebellions begin not with ideology but with a state official ordering diseased or merely suspected animals killed without proper compensation.

## Regional Livestock Summary

**Mittolo and Mithala:** river-horns, ordinary dairy cattle, pigs, ducks, geese, bees, sheep on margins, goats in poorer or rougher districts. Wealth is tied to grain traction, milk, manure, and flood survival.

**Minora:** river-horn teams, bridge rye oxen, city dairy cows, pigs fed on urban waste, draft mules, canal donkeys, and huge animal markets. The city's livestock economy is bureaucratic, insured, leased, and litigated.

**The Plains:** tallhorns, horses, runner sheep, dogs, pack oxen, and seasonal goats. Wealth is mobile and legal memory is carried by herds.

**Northern Azhora and Ganoss:** shag-oxen, upland sheep, hardy ponies, goats, dogs, geese, and coastal dairy cattle. Wealth is winter survival.

**Pyros:** ashbacks, goats, terrace sheep, mules, donkeys, pigs near towns, and poultry. Wealth is slope management, manure, and household continuity.

**Moroshé margins:** dustbacks, goats, donkeys, mules, sheep in better scrub, caravan animals, and oasis fowl. Wealth is water discipline.

**Svaleen and Iberos coast:** goats, sheep, donkeys, mules, vineyard cattle, pigs near cities, bees, and poultry. Wealth is trade-linked but still animal-rooted in rural hinterlands.

**Oremindi and Sevron approaches:** goats, sheep, small cold cattle, pack animals, and very limited bovids in protected valleys. Wealth is self-sufficiency and stored fodder.

## Why Cattle Can Be Worth More Than Money

Outside cities, money is often a promise made by somewhere else. A coin says that a mint, a market, a ruler, or a trade system will honor it later. Livestock answers more immediate questions.

Can the field be plowed?

Can the child drink milk?

Can the family marry well?

Can manure restore the terrace?

Can the caravan move?

Can the winter be survived?

Can compensation be paid without blood?

Can the household recover next year?

This is why cattle and their buffalo-like equivalents are prized beyond coin. They are not merely wealth. They are the machinery by which rural life continues. Cities can pretend coin is the root of economy because cities eat from systems they do not always see. Azhora's countryside knows better. Wealth has hooves, hunger, memory, and names.
