# The 773 Physical Poses of the Verath

Lowland practitioners often ask whether the Verath has postures in the manner of other embodied disciplines. The Sevronese answer is usually yes, followed by a silence that makes the questioner understand they have asked the smaller part of the question.

The physical poses of the Verath are called **narath** in scholarly Mittoli transcription. The word is related, at least in outsider analysis, to the name of Sevron's pass: *Verath-nath*, "the pass that receives." A narath is therefore not merely a posture. It is a receiving shape: a way the body becomes capable of receiving cold, height, breath, attention, weight, pain, stillness, or the presence of the mountain without deforming around it.

The traditional Sevronese canon contains **773 physical poses**:

- **17 Gates**
- **45 Forms**
- **8 Seals**

Every Gate may be joined to every Form, producing 765 named poses. The 8 Seals stand outside the grid and are practiced only at transitions, vows, thresholds, and certain high-altitude rites.

This system is one reason the Verath travels badly. Lowland sects often preserve isolated poses, or even several entire Gates, but lose the grammar that binds the canon together. A pose without its Gate is exercise. A pose without its Form is intention without body. A pose without breath is imitation.

---

## Naming

A full pose name is written:

**[Form] of the [Gate]**

Thus **Hollow Standing of the First Breath Gate** and **Knife Balance of the White Ridge Gate** are distinct poses even if a hurried observer thinks they look related. The Gate defines the spiritual and attentional condition. The Form defines the body arrangement. The breath pattern binds the two.

The Sevronese do not usually recite all 765 compound names. Students learn the Gates, then the Forms, then the combinations. Lowland catalogues list every compound name because lowland catalogues are fond of proving they have counted correctly.

The Gates are not difficulty levels. A beginning student may work inside the First Breath Gate for years, but an old practitioner may return to it before crossing a dangerous pass. Each Gate is a way of meeting the mountain.

## The 8 Seals

The Seals are not ordinary poses. They close, bind, test, or transmit practice. A student may know all 765 Gate-Forms and still not be permitted to practice all eight Seals.

766. **Seal of Entering Cold** - standing at threshold, palms open, first breath deliberately surrendered.  
767. **Seal of Held Heat** - kneeling compression, breath banked in the lower body without visible strain.  
768. **Seal of the Closed Pass** - seated bind used when a path, vow, or teaching must not continue.  
769. **Seal of the Open Pass** - standing release used before dangerous travel or permitted instruction.  
770. **Seal of the Teacher's Hand** - paired posture in which breath timing is transferred by touch.  
771. **Seal of the Student's Return** - prostration-to-standing sequence after correction or failure.  
772. **Seal of the Unnamed Height** - high stillness practiced only where horizon and snowline are both visible.  
773. **Seal of No Witness** - final supine stillness practiced alone; not taught by demonstration.

## Practice Families

Lowland catalogues divide the 773 poses by shape. Sevronese instruction divides them by what they test.

**Foundation poses** include standing, seated, and grounded forms in Gates 1-3. These teach breath under ordinary discomfort.

**Exposure poses** include ridge, balance, and inversion forms in Gates 4, 8, 11, and 14. These teach the body not to bargain with fear.

**Endurance poses** include long holds in Gates 7, 9, 12, and 13. These teach the practitioner what effort becomes when no one is impressed by it.

**Transmission poses** include Gates 15-17 and the final Seals. These are less about strength than about continuity: whether the practice remains itself while passing through a person.

## Why 773

The standard explanation is mathematical: 17 Gates multiplied by 45 Forms, plus 8 Seals.

The ritual explanation is more interesting. Sevronese teachers say the body has forty-five ordinary ways to meet the mountain, seventeen ways to understand what is being met, and eight ways to close what should not be left open. Lowland numerologists have produced several elaborate theories about the number. Sevronese teachers listen politely and then ask whether the theorist can hold Hollow Standing of the Glacier Gate for the length of a storm bell.

Most cannot.
