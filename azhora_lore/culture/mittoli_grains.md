# Grains of Mittolo

Mittolo is called the green heartland because of its forests, but politically and materially it is the grain heartland. The western cities may keep the archives, the Caeras academies, the old compact houses, and the prestige dialect; the eastern Mithala plain keeps everyone fed. In ordinary speech, "Mittoli grain" means any grain passing through Mittoli markets. In agricultural writing, the term is much narrower: the cereal and pseudo-cereal crops grown in the wet temperate river systems of Mittolo, especially the Lizeem distributary plain below Minora.

The difference matters. Imported Pyrosi terrace barley, Plains cord-grain, northern rye, and desert caravan millet all appear in Mittoli markets, but they are not Mittoli grains in the strict sense. A grain is Mittoli when it belongs to the logic of Mittoli water: winter damp, spring flood, silt renewal, mild summer, river transport, and the old division between forest-edge fields and open Mithalan floodplain.

Mittoli farmers do not treat "spring" as one season. They divide it into working phases:

**Rootwake** is the first loosening after winter, when frost leaves the topsoil but deeper ground remains cold. Western farmers watch hazel catkins and the first green at ditch edges. Mithalan farmers watch whether the old silt crust breaks under heel or cuts like pottery.

**Blackwater** is the muddy early spring interval when meltwater and rain darken the channels. The Lizeem rises but has not yet fully spread. This is a dangerous time for premature sowing. Seeds placed too early may rot, but fields missed at this stage may be too wet to enter later.

**Siltshine** follows the first recession. Fresh silt lies wet and bright. Good farmers can smell the difference between living silt and drowned sour silt. Many Mithalan grains are sown during Siltshine, but never blindly. A field may look ready from the path and still be cold below two fingers' depth.

**Frogcall** is the warming stage, when frogs sound in the side channels and the first gnats rise. This is the true early spring for warm-sown grains. Millets and wet rices begin to matter here.

**Leafbreak** is the end of early spring. Forest margins open, willows leaf, and late frosts become insults rather than expectations. Any grain that must be in before Leafbreak is considered a spring-risk crop; any grain that waits until after Leafbreak must justify the delay with speed, yield, or flood safety.

Most Mittoli grain lore is therefore not about a plant in isolation. It is about the correct conversation between seed, mud, frost, flood, and labor. The best farmers are not those who know the calendar. They are those who know when the calendar is lying.

## Regional Grain Zones

**The Mithala floodplain** grows the highest total volume of grain in Azhora. Its soils are deep, dark, renewed by Lizeem silt, and dangerously variable in water behavior. A farmer may have one field ready for barley, one too wet for anything but reed-rice, and one under a temporary lake, all within sight of the same village shrine. Grain villages here are built on levee rises, old channel backs, and artificial mounds. Seed stores are raised above flood height.

**The Minoran forklands** are the most engineered agricultural zone. Around Minora, grain is grown among canals, causeways, holding ponds, quay fields, and bridge estates. The city consumes huge quantities of grain and controls the transport of much more. This produces specialized varieties bred less for romantic agrarian purity than for predictable delivery, storage tolerance, and contract grading.

**The western Caeras and Velm terraces** grow less grain by volume but more diversity by field type. Here forest clearance, slope, clay terrace, river meadow, and damp hollow create small local ecologies. The grains of this zone are often older, hardier, and more culturally marked. Forest-edge villages preserve varieties that Mithalan estate farmers consider inefficient but that survive weather that ruins cleaner crops.

**The Ond lowlands** are slow, wet, and patient. Grain agriculture there favors water-tolerant crops, late harvests, and groat-making traditions. Ond grains are often less prestigious in city bread markets but vital in porridge, beer, winter stores, and boat provisioning.

**The northern Velm and Orched approaches** are colder. Their grain tradition overlaps with northern Azhora: rye, naked barley, hardy oats, and spring grains that germinate in soils that would make a Mithalan farmer wait another week.

## The Fifteen Principal Grains

The following fifteen grains are the expanded teaching set used in Caeras agronomy schools and Minoran grain houses. Older primers teach twelve, usually omitting the three marginal or pseudo-grain crops that city scholars once treated as household lore rather than true grain culture. Farmers recognize more varieties than this, and every branch of the Lizeem has local names that can start fights at market. Still, these fifteen form the backbone of Mittoli grain classification.

### 1. Lizeem Floodwheat

**Standard names:** Lizeem floodwheat, river wheat, first-silt wheat.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Lizaen thol*.  
**Primary zone:** Mithala floodplain, especially the middle distributaries below Minora.  
**Main use:** White-brown bread flour, tax grain, army biscuit, city ration loaves.

Lizeem floodwheat is the crop most outsiders mean when they speak of Mittolo as Azhora's breadbasket. It is a hulled wheat with a thick root crown, medium straw, and heavy bearded heads that dry to a pale bronze. Its flour is not the finest in flavor, but it is reliable, abundant, and forgiving in the hands of ordinary millers. It stores better than most wetland grains if fully dried, which is why tax offices love it.

Floodwheat belongs to land that has flooded but not drowned. Its ideal field is a natural levee back or slightly raised silt bench that receives fresh sediment during the Lizeem rise, then drains within six to twelve days. If water stands longer than two weeks after the first warm spell, the seedbed turns sour and floodwheat becomes weak in the root. Farmers say, "It likes wet feet in memory, not in fact."

**Early spring preference:** Floodwheat is sown at Siltshine, after the first recession but before the silt crust hardens. The best seedbed is cool, dark, and tacky: soft enough to take a heel print, firm enough that the print holds its edge. If the farmer can lift a handful of soil and roll it into a cord that breaks when bent, the field is ready. If it smears, wait. If it crumbles, the chance has already begun to pass.

Floodwheat tolerates light frost after emergence, but it hates cold standing water. A late Rootwake frost is acceptable if the seed has not yet swelled. A Blackwater reflood after germination can ruin half a field. For this reason, experienced farmers sow floodwheat in strips along slight elevation changes rather than in one broad sheet. If the lower strip rots, the upper may still tiller.

The early spring labor is exacting. Fields are raked with wide-toothed silt harrows, not plowed deeply. Deep plowing buries the fresh sediment layer and brings up older, heavier clay. Seed is broadcast thickly, then pressed with reed mats or light rollers. In villages with enough labor, children walk the field with flat boards tied to their feet, singing counting songs while pressing seed into the skin of the mud.

Floodwheat's major disease is **gray foot**, a spring root rot that appears after warm rain on poorly drained fields. Farmers detect it by pulling seedlings and smelling the crown. A sour smell means the field must be cut early for fodder or abandoned. The major insect pest is the **silt weevil**, whose larvae eat swollen seed before emergence. Ducks are sometimes run through fields before sowing to reduce the weevils, though too many ducks compact the bed and create their own problem.

Culturally, floodwheat is the grain of bureaucracy. It appears in tax ledgers, ration contracts, wedding dowry inventories, and temple relief accounts. A good harvest is praised without romance. A failed floodwheat harvest is not a farm problem; it is a political event.

### 2. Caeras Tall Barley

**Standard names:** Caeras tall barley, legal barley, high-straw barley.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Caerav onn*.  
**Primary zone:** Caeras valley terraces, western Mittolo river towns, formal estate farms.  
**Main use:** Beer, malt cakes, temple offerings, roasted meal, legal-ration grain.

Caeras tall barley is a long-strawed, two-row barley with clean awns and a reputation for discipline. It is less productive than floodwheat on the Mithala plain, but it is more manageable on western terraces and less vulnerable to early lodging in wet wind. Its straw is valued for thatch, packing, and writing-house matting. The best Caeras malt houses use it almost exclusively.

It grows on fields that are damp in winter but drained in spring. It prefers loam over clay, especially old river terraces with gravel below the topsoil. It can survive poor soils better than floodwheat, but the grain becomes thin and the malt poor. Farmers who grow it for beer are fussy about field placement. Farmers who grow it for tax substitution are less so, and malt masters curse them.

**Early spring preference:** Caeras tall barley is one of the earliest-sown Mittoli grains. It is usually drilled or broadcast in late Rootwake or early Blackwater, before floodplain farmers dare move. It wants cold soil, not frozen soil; damp air, not rain beating the seed flat; and firm ground that allows carts or foot sowers without kneading the field into paste. The old rule is: sow when morning frost whitens the furrow ridge but the noon sun darkens it again.

It dislikes warm delayed sowing. If held until Frogcall, it grows too much leaf before root, then suffers in summer damp. Early spring wind is good for it because wind dries the surface and discourages fungal bloom. A still, warm Blackwater is bad. Caeras farmers call such weather "barley fever."

Tall barley is often sown after winter legumes, flax, or fallow grazing. The field is shallow-plowed in late winter, then combed with narrow harrows. Good growers leave a faint ridge pattern across the slope so spring rain drains in slow threads rather than sheets. On steep terraces, seed is pressed by hand with wooden paddles. The old Caeras proverb says: "Barley does not forgive a lazy edge."

The main early spring danger is **crown chill**, when seed germinates during a warm spell and then receives a hard freeze in waterlogged soil. The seedling survives but produces weak tillers and uneven heads. Tall barley also suffers from **red smut**, especially if seed is saved from damp storage. Serious barley houses salt-wash seed and dry it in smoke before sowing.

Caeras tall barley is tied to public life. Courts once paid minor officials in barley measures. Some legal ceremonies still place a bowl of barley on the witness table, symbolizing that testimony should be plain, sustaining, and capable of fermentation if neglected.

### 3. Ond Reed-Rice

**Standard names:** Ond reed-rice, water rice, slow rice.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Ond eil*.  
**Primary zone:** Ond lowlands, managed side channels, slow backwaters, southern Lizeem paddies.  
**Main use:** Steamed grain, festival cakes, travel balls, sweet rice beer, convalescent food.

Ond reed-rice is not a reed, though it resembles one at a distance. It is a wetland cereal with long leaves, hollow stems, and dense hanging panicles. It thrives where other grains would rot. Its grain is small, pearl-gray when hulled, and fragrant when steamed. City elites often treat it as a delicacy, but in Ond villages it is ordinary food, more associated with patience than luxury.

It requires managed water. Wild backwater planting is possible, but unreliable. Good reed-rice fields are shallow paddies with low mud walls, fed by channel gates or spring seepage. The water should be moving slightly. Stagnant water produces rank stems and bitter grain. The best Ond farmers can judge a paddy by the behavior of floating pollen, frog eggs, and the smell of the mud at dawn.

**Early spring preference:** Reed-rice is not sown directly into cold fields. In early spring, farmers begin nursery trays or raised mud beds during Frogcall, when night chill remains but standing water no longer bites the hand. Seed is soaked in warmed channel water, then sprouted in baskets lined with sedge. The seedlings want warmth from decay rather than dry heat, so nurseries are often set beside composting reed piles or under low woven covers.

The main field is prepared earlier. During Blackwater, farmers repair paddy walls, clear channels, and let the first muddy water settle. At Siltshine, they admit a thin sheet of water to soften clods and wake the mud life. Transplanting begins after the third or fourth frog chorus, a traditional marker roughly corresponding to stable night temperatures above the danger point. Too early, and seedlings yellow. Too late, and the plants flower under heavy summer humidity and invite blight.

Reed-rice likes early spring water that is shallow enough to show the farmer's toes. Deeper water chills the root and encourages long weak stems. It also likes a soft mud bed over firmer clay. If the farmer sinks past the ankle, the field is not ready. If the mud cracks around the heel, water is too low.

Its chief disease is **white breath**, a fungal blight that appears after warm fog sits over crowded paddies. Farmers reduce it by spacing seedlings wider than hungry landlords prefer. The major pest is the **rice snail**, which eats young transplants. Children collect snails in baskets; the snails are then fed to ducks, eaten by poorer households, or crushed for field lime.

Ond reed-rice has strong ritual associations with recovery. Sickrooms serve thin reed-rice porridge because it is thought to "teach the stomach to remember." At spring paddy-opening rites, the first water admitted to the field is touched to the lips of the oldest woman present.

### 4. Velm Green Oats

**Standard names:** Velm green oats, forest oats, mist oats.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Velmin aal*.  
**Primary zone:** Velm valley, forest clearings, northern and western damp meadows.  
**Main use:** Porridge, horse feed, oatcakes, winter gruel, mixed bread in poorer uplands.

Velm green oats are the grain of damp margins. They are tall, soft-leaved, and more tolerant of shade than most major cereals. The grain is long, greenish-gray at harvest, and rich in oil. It does not make elegant bread, but it makes excellent porridge, sturdy cakes, and strong horses. Forest villages value it because it succeeds in fields that city agronomists would classify as "insufficiently committed to being fields."

The plant tolerates acidic leaf-mold soils better than wheat or barley. It grows well after forest clearance if the ash is not too thick and the roots have been removed enough to allow harrowing. It also grows in damp meadows that flood briefly in winter but drain by Rootwake. It does poorly in hot open Mithalan fields, where it rushes and shrivels.

**Early spring preference:** Velm green oats are sown very early, sometimes while snow still lies under hedges. They germinate in cold soil and prefer a long cool establishment. The best early spring condition is misty, wet air over soil that is cold but breathing. Farmers test this by pushing a bare hand into the ground at dawn. If the cold aches but does not numb, oats may go in.

They do not like being buried deeply. Seed is broadcast over lightly scratched soil and brushed in with twig harrows. In forest-edge settlements, the first sowing is often done by women and older children because the fields are too small and irregular for teams. A good oat field in early spring looks underworked to a Mithalan estate manager. Velm farmers consider this a compliment. Oats resent fuss.

Late frost rarely kills Velm oats, but hard rain after sowing can wash seed into hollows, creating clumped growth and bare patches. Farmers often scatter chopped bracken or straw over sloped patches to hold the seed. In deer-heavy forest margins, early shoots must be guarded. The old solution is children with clappers; the expensive solution is fencing; the common solution is resignation plus dogs.

The main disease is **green rust**, which appears during wet warm springs and gives the leaves a dusty sheen. Farmers control it by not sowing oats in the same clearing too many years in a row. The main storage problem is rancidity, since the oily grain spoils if stored damp. Velm oats must be dried with patience and turned often.

Velm green oats are socially humble but emotionally beloved. Many Mittoli childhood foods are oat-based. Soldiers complain about oat ration until they are given something worse. Forest singers use oat harvest as a metaphor for quiet persistence: not the grain that makes kings rich, but the grain that keeps winter from becoming personal.

### 5. Minoran Bridge Rye

**Standard names:** Minoran bridge rye, causeway rye, black-awn rye.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Minorath rekh*.  
**Primary zone:** Minora forklands, raised causeways, levee banks, canal edges, northern cold fields.  
**Main use:** Dark bread, erosion control, winter straw, emergency flour, bridge-worker rations.

Minoran bridge rye is a tough, narrow-headed rye bred around infrastructure. It grows where soil is thin, compacted, or wind-scoured; where canal works have disturbed older fields; where levee faces need roots; and where urban landholders want something productive on margins too awkward for better grains. Its flour is dark, sour, and dense. Its straw is strong. Its roots hold banks.

Bridge rye is not the most pleasant grain, but it is one of the most politically important in Minora. Flood defenses, bridge approaches, and canal embankments are often sown with it after repairs. The crop feeds labor crews and stabilizes the very structures that move the region's grain. Minoran engineers sometimes say rye is the cheapest form of stone.

**Early spring preference:** Bridge rye is usually autumn-sown, but its spring management is crucial. It wakes early. During Rootwake, inspectors walk levee fields looking for winterkill, heaving, and gaps where floodwater may cut. Spring patch-sowing begins as soon as the soil surface can be scratched. Unlike floodwheat, bridge rye tolerates a rough seedbed and even prefers a little hardship. The old saying is: "If the field flatters rye, plant wheat."

For spring-sown bridge rye, the ideal condition is cold, firm, and slightly dry on top, with moisture below. It does not want the fresh soft silt of the floodplain. On such rich mud it grows too lush and falls. It wants levee backs, sandy ridges, old road edges, and places where a farmer's better judgment says nothing should be expected.

Early spring grazing is sometimes used. Sheep or small cattle are turned briefly onto autumn rye during Rootwake to check excessive growth and thicken root crowns. This must be done before the stem begins to rise. Grazing too late destroys the head. Minoran bridge estates employ specialists called **rye-biters** who judge when animals should enter and leave. Outsiders find the title comic until a levee fails.

Its major disease is **black throat**, a head fungus associated with damp flowering periods. Spring drainage and air movement reduce it. Bridge rye also hosts rodents along canal banks, so harvest must be prompt. Grain from rat-heavy fields is often relegated to distilling, prison ration, or glue work rather than bread.

Culturally, bridge rye is the grain of practical people. Minora's bridge workers eat rye loaves with onion and river cheese. During flood emergencies, city magistrates distribute bridge rye bread first because it keeps longer in damp conditions and because no one can accuse the magistrates of wasting luxury flour on public order.

### 6. Mithalan Silver Millet

**Standard names:** Mithalan silver millet, quick millet, shining millet.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Mithal sil*.  
**Primary zone:** High patches of the Mithala plain, late-receding flood edges, warm side fields.  
**Main use:** Porridge, flatbread, bird feed, famine reserve, quick beer, portable meal.

Mithalan silver millet is small, fast, and opportunistic. Its seedheads are pale silver when ripe, especially in morning light. It does not command the prestige of wheat or the affection of oats, but when floods linger, wars disrupt labor, or a field is missed in early spring, silver millet can turn a bad season into something survivable.

It likes warmth more than most Mittoli grains. It is therefore not a true Rootwake crop. Farmers wait until Frogcall or even early Leafbreak, when the soil has warmed and the chance of cold reflooding is low. It is commonly planted on fields where floodwheat failed, on levee gardens after vegetable starts, or in small patches near houses for household security.

**Early spring preference:** Silver millet's early spring preference is patience. The seed is kept dry while other grains go in. Farmers test the field by burying a millet seed in a thumb-deep pinch of soil and placing it in the waistband or under the arm while walking the field. If the soil remains cold against skin, wait. If it warms quickly and smells sweet, sowing may begin. This folk test is mocked by Caeras agronomists and used by their field assistants.

It wants a fine, shallow seedbed. Too much moisture at sowing causes rot. Too little moisture prevents even germination. The ideal is a warm surface after light rain, followed by two clear days. In the Mithala floodplain, that window can be narrow. Farmers often sow silver millet in several small rounds rather than one large sowing. The first round risks cold, the second aims at success, the third is insurance.

Because the seed is tiny, it is mixed with dry ash, sand, or powdered chaff before broadcasting. Women in Mithalan villages often control millet sowing because household grain security depends on knowing which corners, garden strips, and abandoned patches can still produce. Estate managers historically undervalued these plots; household ledgers did not.

The main pest is birds. Children guard millet with slings, rattles, and obscene songs directed at sparrows. The main disease is **silver mold**, a panicle rot after humid late summer storms. Harvesting slightly early and drying on raised mats reduces losses.

Silver millet has a social double meaning. Landlords call it poor grain. Mothers call it honest grain. Soldiers call it better than hunger. In Mithalan law, emergency millet patches planted by household labor are often exempt from seizure for debt, a protection older than several dynasties and frequently litigated.

### 7. Black Silt Emmer

**Standard names:** black silt emmer, old emmer, ancestor wheat.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Ner thol*.  
**Primary zone:** older Mithalan basins, temple lands, conservative villages, archaeology-rich mounds.  
**Main use:** ritual loaves, coarse bread, cracked grain, funerary cakes, seed heritage.

Black silt emmer is older than floodwheat, or at least claims to be. It is a hulled wheat with dark glumes, strong awns, and a stubborn refusal to thresh cleanly. Modern estate farmers dislike it because it is labor-intensive. Temple farms, old villages, and seed guardians preserve it because it survives difficult wet years and because its flavor is deep, nutty, and faintly smoky even when grown without smoke.

It is associated with the First Silence more than any other grain. Excavations in old storage pits often find charred grains resembling black silt emmer. Whether the modern crop is continuous with those ancient grains is debated. Farmers are less cautious. They call it "the wheat that stayed."

**Early spring preference:** Black silt emmer is sown earlier than floodwheat but later than Caeras barley. It wants cold, mineral-rich silt that has begun to settle but not yet sweeten. Farmers plant it during late Blackwater on fields that seem too heavy for floodwheat. It can tolerate a second shallow inundation after sowing if the water moves and drains within several days. It cannot tolerate stagnant warm water.

The field is prepared roughly. Emmer likes clods. Its strong shoots push through crust better than floodwheat, and a too-fine seedbed encourages weeds. Seed is often dibbled or line-sown in older villages, not broadcast, because the seed stock is precious and the plants need space. Temple estates use wooden seed combs with fixed teeth, preserving spacing rules said to predate written measures.

Early spring signs matter intensely. The ideal sowing day is cold, windless, and gray, with water still standing in ditch bottoms but not on the field. Farmers press three fingers into the silt. If the imprint fills slowly with seep water, emmer is pleased. If it fills at once, too wet. If it stays dry, too late unless rain is due.

Emmer's main virtue is resilience. Its main vice is labor. It resists lodging, resists several smuts, and competes well against early weeds. But harvest and dehulling require work. In prosperous years, black silt emmer shrinks to ritual acreage. In bad years, everyone suddenly remembers where seed may be obtained.

Its ritual use is widespread. Funerary cakes in several Mithalan districts must include at least a handful of black emmer meal, "so the dead eat what the first houses ate." During seed exchanges, farmers swear by emmer more readily than by floodwheat because floodwheat is wealth, but emmer is continuity.

### 8. Seven-Branch Spelt

**Standard names:** Seven-Branch spelt, branch spelt, white-husk spelt.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Seben thal*.  
**Primary zone:** upper Lizeem branches, Minoran contract estates, elevated floodplain ridges.  
**Main use:** fine bread, festival loaves, long-storage grain, diplomatic grain gifts.

Seven-Branch spelt is the prestige hulled wheat of the Lizeem system. It is not as high-yielding as floodwheat, but its grain stores exceptionally well in the husk, its flour has a sweet aroma, and its bran is prized by bakers. It is called Seven-Branch not because it grows on all seven branches equally, but because Minoran grain houses once graded its best lots by branch origin and sold mixed ceremonial flour as a symbol of the whole plain.

Spelt prefers elevated, well-drained floodplain fields with deep silt over firm subsoil. It likes a winter chill and a slow spring. It does badly in hot rushed seasons and poorly in fields that receive fresh flood every year. The best spelt lands flood lightly or only in major years, receiving enough silt to renew but not enough water to disturb the root zone.

**Early spring preference:** Seven-Branch spelt may be autumn-sown in mild districts, but the most valued grain is often late-winter or early-spring sown at Rootwake. It wants a cold start and a long tillering period. Farmers aim to sow when the top finger of soil has thawed but the second finger remains cold. This encourages roots to grip before leaves race upward.

It dislikes disturbance after sowing. Rolling is light. Harrowing is minimal. The seed is often drilled in narrow rows with branch-marked seeders owned by village cooperatives. If the early spring is too wet, farmers delay rather than force spelt into mud. Forced spelt produces hollow straw and poor grain. If delayed too long, however, the crop flowers during humid heat and loses its storage virtue.

Early spring frost is not a problem once the seedling is established. Warm rain followed by cloudy stillness is worse. This encourages **pale smut**, a husk disease that may remain hidden until storage. Spelt intended for diplomatic gift must be inspected twice: once in field, once after curing. Minoran graders cut sample heads, steam them briefly, and smell the husk. A sweet hay smell passes; a cellar smell fails.

Seven-Branch spelt is culturally associated with unity, but in a very Minoran way: unity as sorted, graded, weighed, and invoiced. High-status weddings serve spelt loaves braided in seven strands. Compact ceremonies sometimes place seven bowls of branch spelt on the table, then combine them into one measure before oaths are sworn.

### 9. North Velm Naked Barley

**Standard names:** naked barley, northern naked barley, quick-hull barley.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Velem nar onn*.  
**Primary zone:** northern Velm valley, Orched approaches, colder western uplands.  
**Main use:** roasted grain, travel meal, highland beer, porridge, winter stores.

North Velm naked barley sheds its hull during threshing, making it easier to prepare than hulled barleys. Its grain is plump, gray-gold, and somewhat waxy. It is less prestigious than Caeras tall barley in formal malt houses but more useful in upland households, where labor and fuel are limiting factors. It cooks quickly, grinds acceptably, and travels well as roasted meal.

It is adapted to cold, short growing seasons. It tolerates frost, wind, and thin soils. It does not tolerate deep floodplain mud. Attempts to grow it on rich Mithalan fields produce lush straw, weak heads, and mockery from northerners.

**Early spring preference:** This is one of the earliest practical spring grains in Mittolo. It can be sown in Rootwake as soon as the surface can be opened with a hoe. The soil may be cold enough to sting bare fingers. That is acceptable. What matters is drainage. Naked barley wants cold air over soil that releases meltwater rather than holding it.

Farmers in the northern Velm often sow into ash-darkened beds after winter brush burning. The ash warms the surface and supplies minerals, but too much ash makes the seedbed caustic. Experienced farmers mix ash into the topsoil with light rakes and wait for one rain before sowing. The ideal early spring sequence is: thaw, brush burn, light rain, wind, sow.

Unlike Caeras tall barley, naked barley can be sown thick. It matures quickly and competes well. It is often used as a rescue crop when winter spelt fails or when late snow ruins terrace plans. Because the grain threshes free, it must be harvested promptly. If left in wet weather, birds and shattering take their share.

Its main disease is **cold stripe**, a leaf disorder after severe frost on young seedlings. It rarely kills the plant but reduces yield. Farmers select seed from plants that remain green after striped years. This has produced many local landraces, each named for villages, grandmothers, or insulting descriptions of neighboring valleys.

Naked barley is associated with plain speech. Upland hosts serve roasted naked barley to guests before beer. Refusing it is rude unless one is sick, toothless, or from a city and therefore pitiable.

### 10. White Ond Groatgrass

**Standard names:** Ond groatgrass, white groatgrass, boat groats.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Ond garel*.  
**Primary zone:** Ond meadows, low wet pastures, canal margins, poorer southern farms.  
**Main use:** groats, gruel, boat provisions, animal feed, fermented sour mash.

White Ond groatgrass is a small-grained cereal or near-cereal that academics argue over and farmers simply grow. Its heads are loose, its stems wiry, and its grain pale enough to seem almost white after hulling. It grows in low wet meadows where oats may lodge and reed-rice would require more water control than the household possesses.

It is not glamorous. It is, however, astonishingly useful. It tolerates wet feet, periodic shallow flooding, and poor drainage. It can be cut green for fodder or matured for groats. Boatmen like it because groatgrass gruel reheats without becoming glue. Poor households like it because it yields something on land that richer farmers consider not worth arguing over.

**Early spring preference:** Groatgrass is sown at Blackwater's edge, often on fields still too wet for cart work. Seed is broadcast by hand from the margins or from narrow planks laid across the mud. It wants cold wet soil and tolerates a thin sheet of water after sowing. It does not want deep burial. In fact, some farmers scatter it over receding puddle edges and let settling silt cover it naturally.

The ideal early spring condition is "wet shine without ripple." If wind can ripple standing water across the field, the water is too deep and seed will drift. If the shine is only mud gloss, conditions are good. If cracks appear, sow something else or irrigate.

Groatgrass germinates unevenly, which frustrates tidy minds. Farmers accept this because the plant compensates by tillering over a long interval. It is often mixed with Velm oats in marginal meadows. Oats rise first, groatgrass fills later, and the field becomes a two-layer harvest.

The main pest is the **meadow midge**, whose larvae damage forming heads during humid springs. Smoke pots along field edges help, or at least make farmers feel active. The main problem is contamination by sedges and rushes. Cleaning groatgrass is laborious, so it is rarely sold as fine grain. It remains local, domestic, and tied to people who know exactly how much work survival requires.

Ond boat crews make sour groat mash in sealed jars. Outsiders call it unpleasant. Boat crews call outsiders delicate.

### 11. Ashbank Buckwheat

**Standard names:** ashbank buckwheat, forest buckwheat, blackflower buckwheat.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Esh karun*.  
**Primary zone:** western forest clearings, shifting garden fields, burned banks, hill farms.  
**Main use:** dark flour, pancakes, bee pasture, quick cover crop, famine grain.

Ashbank buckwheat is a pseudo-cereal, not a true grass, but Mittoli markets classify it as grain because it fills sacks, grinds into flour, and keeps people alive. It has heart-shaped leaves, pale stems, and dark-centered flowers beloved by bees. Its triangular seeds produce a dark, earthy flour that western forest communities prize and Caeras elites pretend to discover every few decades.

It thrives after disturbance. Burned brush banks, cleared forest edges, landslips, garden plots after early vegetables, and fields too acidic for wheat can all take buckwheat. It grows quickly, shades weeds, and leaves the soil kinder than it found it. It is less reliable in cold wet springs and cannot take frost.

**Early spring preference:** Ashbank buckwheat waits. It should not be sown in Rootwake, however tempting bare burned fields may look. It wants soil warm enough that bare feet do not flinch at dawn. The folk sign is bee flight: if bees work willow and early forest flowers for three days running, buckwheat may be sown on sheltered banks. Caeras agronomists translate this to stable warmth and pollinator activity, then write it less beautifully.

The seedbed should be loose, lightly ashed, and not too rich. Too much fertility makes leaf at the expense of seed. Too much wet makes rot. Farmers rake ashbank fields shallowly, scatter seed, and brush it in with leafy branches. No one respectable plows deeply for buckwheat. It is a crop of speed and surface.

Early spring storms are dangerous. Heavy rain after sowing can crust the surface or wash seed downhill. Forest farmers sow across slope in narrow bands, often alternating buckwheat with beans, flax, or small barley strips. The plant's rapid canopy suppresses weeds, making it useful after fields have been neglected during war or illness.

Its main pest is birds at seed ripening. Its main disease is **stem black**, a rot after prolonged damp. Because buckwheat matures unevenly, harvest timing is a judgment call: wait for full seed and lose early grain, or cut early and accept immature seed. Forest villages cut when two-thirds of the seeds are dark, then dry the plants on raised racks under smoke.

Ashbank buckwheat is culturally tied to bees. Beekeepers pay farmers for flowering rights in some districts, and buckwheat honey is dark, strong, and medicinal-tasting. It is used in winter cough syrups and in cakes served at wakes where no one wants sweetness to pretend grief is light.

### 12. Caeras Red Panicum

**Standard names:** red panicum, red bead millet, Caeras panicum.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Rav sil*.  
**Primary zone:** dry terrace edges, warm Caeras slopes, Minoran garden estates, late fields.  
**Main use:** small-beer, porridges, poultry feed, red festival meal, emergency double-cropping.

Caeras red panicum is a warm-season millet-like grain with red seed beads and compact heads. It is not as fast as Mithalan silver millet but produces a richer, sweeter meal. Its color makes it ritually useful. Its tolerance for dry terrace edges makes it agriculturally useful in a region otherwise inclined to be too wet.

It is often double-cropped after early greens, failed barley, or spring fodder. In Minora, small garden estates plant red panicum on raised beds after flood danger passes. Caeras monasteries grow it on south-facing slopes where the stone walls hold heat.

**Early spring preference:** Red panicum is not sown until late Frogcall or Leafbreak. It hates cold. Seed placed in cold soil sulks, rots, or emerges unevenly enough to make the farmer say theological things. The ideal early spring preparation begins earlier: beds are ridged during Blackwater so they drain and warm. Ash or dark compost may be applied to south-facing strips to bring the soil on faster.

Farmers test readiness with the "three mornings" rule. If soil on the ridge top is warm to the back of the hand for three mornings before sunrise, sowing may begin. If warmth is only present at noon, wait. Red panicum rewards patience more than daring.

Seed is sown shallow and thin. Thick sowing makes weak stems and small heads. It needs early weeding because its first growth is modest. Once established, it handles dry spells better than most Mittoli grains, making it valuable in unusual springs when the rains fail after Blackwater.

The main pest is the **red finch**, which can strip ripening heads. Farmers net high-value plots or plant sacrifice strips along hedges. The main disease is **head honey**, a sticky fungal infection in humid late summers. Airy spacing helps, as does avoiding low hollows.

Red panicum meal colors porridge pink-red. It is used in childblessing cakes in parts of the Caeras valley, where the color is associated with blood, warmth, and the refusal of infants to remain politely fragile.

### 13. Minoran Counting Wheat

**Standard names:** counting wheat, measure wheat, clerk wheat.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Men thol*.  
**Primary zone:** Minoran contract estates, standardized fields along canal grids.  
**Main use:** contract grain, ration flour, controlled milling, export grading.

Counting wheat is a bred administrative grain. Farmers respect it less than grain factors do. It was developed around Minora to produce uniform kernels, predictable bushel weight, and consistent milling behavior. It does not have the deep flavor of black silt emmer or the prestige of Seven-Branch spelt, but it turns contracts into bread with minimal argument. This is a Minoran virtue.

The plant is medium height, stiff-strawed, and moderately disease resistant. It grows best on engineered fields with controlled drainage rather than wild floodplain. It prefers silt loam with managed fertility and does poorly in both neglected village patches and overly rich new silt.

**Early spring preference:** Counting wheat is sown according to measured field readiness rather than folk sign, though the best estate stewards quietly use both. The official standard requires soil moisture, temperature, and firmness marks recorded on tablets before sowing. In practice, sowing begins at late Siltshine when the field takes a roller without sticking and when seed placed overnight under a damp cloth swells evenly.

It dislikes surprises. Reflooding, cold snaps, uneven manure, and careless seed depth all produce irregular kernels, which lower contract grade. For this reason, counting wheat fields are usually ditched, leveled, and inspected more heavily than neighboring crops. Seed drills are calibrated before sowing, and field edges are marked by measure posts. The crop is often grown by tenant farmers under grain-house contract, which means every failure becomes a legal conversation.

Early spring weed control is strict. Counting wheat does not compete as aggressively as older landraces. Estate crews blind-harrow before emergence and again at early shoot stage if weather permits. If rain prevents harrowing, hand weeding follows, usually by poorly paid laborers whose songs about counting wheat are not recorded in official agricultural manuals.

The major disease is **ledger blight**, a farmer's nickname for any problem that reduces grade just enough for the grain house to discount the lot. Actual diseases include leaf rust and pale smut. Grain houses require seed treatment and certify storage dryness.

Counting wheat symbolizes Minora's power: not the power to grow the most beautiful grain, but the power to define what a measure is and make everyone else care.

### 14. Forest Pearl Amaranth

**Standard names:** forest pearl, pearl amaranth, grove seed.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Perel sum*.  
**Primary zone:** western forest gardens, household plots, shrine clearings, forest-edge settlements.  
**Main use:** popped grain, porridge thickener, ritual seed, mixed flour, leaf vegetable.

Forest pearl amaranth is another pseudo-cereal, classified as grain by cooks and seed keepers rather than botanists. It produces tiny pale seeds on red-green plumes and edible leaves before seed set. It is grown in household plots more often than large fields, though some forest districts cultivate broad patches for temple use.

It likes warmth, light disturbance, and human proximity. Unlike ashbank buckwheat, it does not require burn. It grows well in garden soils enriched by household compost, along shrine clearings, and in old forest fields where full cereals struggle. Its seeds are tiny but numerous; its leaves provide early greens before the plant is left to flower.

**Early spring preference:** Forest pearl is started in protected beds during Frogcall, not sown widely in cold soil. Households often germinate it in shallow trays near kitchen warmth, then transplant or scatter once Leafbreak begins. In milder western valleys, seed may be broadcast directly after the first warm rain, but only if late frost is unlikely. Frost blackens young plants immediately.

The early spring trick is managing light. Forest pearl seedlings need sun but not drying wind. Gardeners often start them beside woven screens or among early peas, then thin aggressively. Too much shade makes tall weak plants. Too much exposed heat dries the surface and prevents germination. The ideal bed is warm, crumbly, and lightly moist, with morning sun and afternoon shelter.

Because the seed is tiny, it is mixed with fine ash or dry meal before sowing. Some households sow by tapping seed from a folded leaf. Children are often given this task and then blamed for clumps. The plants are thinned twice: once for greens, once for seed spacing. The thinnings are eaten in soups.

Forest pearl's main pest is flea beetle on young leaves. Ash dust helps. Birds take seed at ripening, but because the heads mature over time, total loss is rare. Seed must be dried carefully. If stored damp, it molds invisibly and tastes bitter when popped.

Forest pearl is associated with household continuity and small rites. A bowl of popped pearl seed is set beside infants during naming ceremonies in some forest villages, not because anyone thinks babies should eat it, but because many tiny seeds symbolize a life with many possible paths. Formal Caeras theologians find this charming and insufficiently sourced.

### 15. Blue Fen Foxtail

**Standard names:** blue fen foxtail, fen millet, blue-tail grain.  
**Mittoli trade name:** *Fen silu*.  
**Primary zone:** cold wet fens of northern Mittolo, Velm marsh edges, poor meadow ground.  
**Main use:** animal feed, coarse porridge, famine meal, marsh beer, thatching straw.

Blue fen foxtail is a minor grain by market volume and a major grain by survival value. It grows where little else wants to be responsible. Its seedheads have a bluish cast before ripening, and its straw is fine, flexible, and useful for thatch binding. The grain is small and requires careful cleaning, but it produces reliably in cold wet fen margins.

It is commonly grown by poorer households, marsh-edge communities, and monasteries with charity obligations. Wealthier farmers dismiss it until years when their better fields fail. Then they ask after seed and pretend interest had always been scholarly.

**Early spring preference:** Blue fen foxtail likes early cold wetness more than any other warm-looking grain. It is sown during Blackwater in fields that would be too wet for barley and too cold for millet. The seed is broadcast onto saturated but not flooded ground. A thin film of water is acceptable. Moving water is not; it carries the seed away.

Farmers often sow from reed sledges or wide marsh shoes to avoid sinking. The seed is mixed with powdered dry peat or ash to improve spread. No deep preparation is possible. The field is cut low in the previous autumn, lightly burned if dry enough, then left for spring sowing.

It wants a long cool emergence. If early spring suddenly warms, competing rushes and sedges overtake it. If late frost comes, foxtail bends and returns. If heavy flood comes, it vanishes. This makes it a crop for farmers who understand local water microbehavior. A difference of one handspan in field height can determine success.

Its main pest is waterfowl. Farmers string lines with shells and carved bone chips across the field. Its main disease is **fen brown**, a rot at the base in stagnant warm water. Drainage cuts, even tiny ones made by hand, can save patches.

Blue fen foxtail has a reputation as shame grain in cities and saint grain in marsh country. Monasteries cook it with onions and sour milk for winter charity. Boatmen brew it into a cloudy drink that tastes better after the second cup, though almost everything does.

## Additional Local Grains

The fifteen principal grains above do not exhaust Mittoli grain. A serious Minoran factor can name fifty trade grades. A Velm seed grandmother can name twice as many landraces and will be offended if asked which are "real." The following are common enough to appear in regional records:

**Old Channel Einkorn** grows on abandoned Lizeem channel ridges. It is low-yielding, hard-husked, and beloved by antiquarians. Some villages plant it only in boundary strips.

**Red Levee Sorghum** is rare in central Mittolo but grown in warm southern Mithala after major flood failures. It is more common toward Pyros and the Plains, and some purists refuse to call it Mittoli.

**Candle Oats** are pale oats grown for fine meal near temple estates. The name comes from their use in fasting porridges served during long vigils.

**Mire Quinoa** is a salt-tolerant pseudo-grain grown near brackish lower distributaries approaching Nylon. It is not important inland but matters in coastal poor districts.

## Early Spring Fieldcraft

Mittoli grain expertise is most visible in early spring. Harvest is dramatic, but early spring is where the year is won or lost. The farmer must decide whether a field is cold-wet, warm-wet, cold-dry, living-silt, sour-silt, settled-clay, drained-loam, or lying by appearance. Most grains can be described by their preferred answer.

**Cold and firm:** Caeras tall barley, North Velm naked barley, spring Seven-Branch spelt. These crops want a long root establishment and tolerate frost.

**Cool fresh silt:** Lizeem floodwheat and black silt emmer. They want recession moisture but differ in texture: floodwheat wants tacky fine silt, emmer accepts heavier clods.

**Cold wet margins:** white Ond groatgrass and blue fen foxtail. These crops occupy land too wet or too poor for prestige grains.

**Managed shallow water:** Ond reed-rice. It begins in nurseries while fields are softened, then moves into controlled paddies.

**Warm surface after patience:** Mithalan silver millet, Caeras red panicum, ashbank buckwheat, forest pearl amaranth. These should not be seduced by the first pretty day of spring.

The most respected spring skill is not sowing but waiting correctly. Waiting too long is laziness. Waiting rightly is knowledge. Farmers distinguish these with merciless precision.

## Storage, Milling, and Social Rank

Grains are ranked not only by yield but by storage. Mittolo's wet climate makes drying as important as growing. Floodwheat must be thoroughly aired before tax storage. Spelt stores well in husk. Black emmer stores well but costs labor to dehull. Oats spoil if neglected. Reed-rice molds if sacks touch damp floors. Millets store well once dry, which is why poor households hide them.

Milling also shapes status. Fine bolted floodwheat and spelt flours feed cities and elites. Barley and rye mark work, beer, and practical households. Oats and groatgrass are domestic grains. Millets move between poverty and festival depending on color, preparation, and who is serving. Buckwheat and amaranth carry forest identity into city markets, where they are alternately mocked, fetishized, and overpriced.

The hierarchy is therefore unstable:

- **Prestige bread grains:** Seven-Branch spelt, selected floodwheat, counting wheat.
- **Administrative grains:** floodwheat, counting wheat, Caeras tall barley, bridge rye.
- **Household security grains:** silver millet, groatgrass, naked barley, oats.
- **Wetland grains:** reed-rice, groatgrass, blue fen foxtail.
- **Forest grains:** Velm oats, ashbank buckwheat, forest pearl amaranth.
- **Ritual grains:** black silt emmer, Seven-Branch spelt, red panicum, forest pearl.

No Mittoli farmer agrees with this list completely. Agreement is not what lists are for. Lists begin the argument.

## Grain and Politics

Control of grain is control of Mittolo. The Lizeem plain feeds Minora, Nylon, the Iberos trade, the western compact cities, armies moving east or west, and relief shipments into less fortunate regions. Grain failures travel faster than armies because prices move before soldiers do. A bad Siltshine in Mithala can raise bread prices in Selemis, change loan terms in Aevis, and make northern raiding look economically rational again.

This is why grain offices are among the oldest institutions in Mittoli government. Standard measures, flood calendars, seed registries, storage audits, canal obligations, mill rights, and emergency ration law all developed around the practical problem of keeping wet grain edible and moving it before politics spoiled it.

Minora's power rests on this truth. The city does not merely sit at the fork of the Lizeem. It sits at the fork between grain as food and grain as authority.

## Grain and Music

Mittoli grain work has its own musical forms. Floodwheat pressing songs use slow call-and-response because workers must step together on wet fields. Barley harrow songs are brisk and narrow. Reed-rice transplanting songs are antiphonal across paddy rows, with one line teasing the other for crooked spacing. Millet-guarding songs are often obscene because children sing them at birds and adults pretend not to hear.

The **Mithala Mode** in formal Mittoli music preserves the irregular work rhythms of floodplain grain labor: lift, brace, pass, call, pull, reset, listen. Minoran bridge rye crews use hammer patterns related to flood-count rhythms. Forest buckwheat harvest songs include long silences at the field edge, partly because the work is done near old groves and partly because bees dislike shouting.

Grain is therefore not background agriculture in Mittolo. It is calendar, law, class, song, storage, flood memory, and political threat. A person who knows the grains knows the region at its most honest: not what it says it is, but what it must do each spring to remain itself.
