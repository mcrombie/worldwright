# Viticulture and Wine Culture of Azhora

Wine is older than writing on Azhora. The first fermentation was accidental; the second was deliberate; everything after was civilization. The grape and the fermented grape have been present in the archaeological record of the Svaleen Peninsula and the Iberos coast for longer than any surviving text, and the inference drawn by the Academy's archaeologists — that wine preceded organized governance, that the cultivation of grapes and the management of wine storage were among the first complex communal activities requiring sustained coordination — has not been seriously disputed. You need writing, eventually, to run a wine culture. A wine culture will generate the need for writing before writing exists.

---

## Thareth

The god of wine has many names. In the oldest Svaleen texts he is *Thareth* — the name that most of the mainland inherited through trade and cultural contact. In the Avite epics of Ascarth he appears as *Thareveth*, the Courageous-Given, the god who puts courage into a cup and lets men drink what otherwise they could not be. In northern Azhoran tradition he is *Vel-Thar*, the deep vine, the root-god, whose grapes are coldness surviving winter. In the Moroshé traditions he is *Al-Thar* or *Tharash*, the water-that-burns, the desert miracle of fermentation without river. The Elodi, when asked about Thareth in their theological language, go quiet in a way that is not the same as not knowing.

Thareth is not merely the god of wine. He is the god of the boundary between what a person is and what they can become — and wine is the most reliable substance humanity has found that temporarily dissolves that boundary. A person who is afraid can drink and become brave. A person who is silent can drink and become eloquent. A person who is alone can drink and become companionable. Whether the courage is real courage, the eloquence real eloquence, the companionship real companionship is a theological question that the Thareth cults have been arguing about since there were Thareth cults. The god himself, in the stories, tends to laugh when this question is raised, which is either answer enough or no answer at all.

### Origin Myths

The Svaleen tradition holds that Thareth walked the continent before there were cities. In his walking he wept — the stories vary on why, as the stories of gods' tears always do — and where his tears fell on stone, the first grapevines grew. The tears fell first on the limestone ridge of what would become the Svaleen Peninsula, and the vine grew here first, which is why the peninsula has more varieties of grape than anywhere else on Azhora: each tear fell in a different place, each place had different stone, and each vine grew differently from every other. The varieties of Suval are the tears of Thareth catalogued by terrace.

The Avite tradition is less botanical and more martial. In the Avite telling, Thareth did not weep; he bled. He fought something at the beginning of the world — the stories disagree on what — and his blood fell on the soil of the Ascarth hills, and the grapevine grew from the blood of a god. This is why Avite wine has courage in it. This is why the proper toast before battle is made with wine rather than water. You are drinking what Thareth spent so that you could be here.

The northern tradition says the vine came through the winter: that Thareth lay buried under snow, that he was dead in the way the vine appears dead in winter, and that his spring emergence was the first resurrection, the model that the vine follows every year. Wine, in this reading, is liquid resurrection — the stored summer of a vine that has died and come back, preserved for the months when the world needs to remember that warmth is possible.

The desert traditions say Thareth arrived from across the sea, from somewhere east, and that the vine he carried with him was the only thing he brought. He gave it to the coast peoples who fed him and gave him water when he arrived without resources, and the vine was payment, and it was more than sufficient. This version does not specify where east means. The Elodi priests who were once asked about this said that east is a direction that becomes specific when you know where you are starting from.

### The Two Natures

Thareth is famous for being divided. The principal division in Thareth theology is between what the Svaleen call *Thareth Civic* and *Thareth Wild* — the god of the ordered feast and the god of the hillside ecstasy.

*Thareth Civic* is the wine at the treaty table, the vintage brought out for a distinguished guest, the libation poured before important proceedings, the cellar managed across generations, the classification systems argued over at the Conclave, the wine that marks the passage of the formal year. This Thareth is associated with the city, with the rational use of intoxication in the service of community, with the idea that wine is civilized because civilization made it — that the grape is wild but the wine is human. His temples in this aspect are in city centers. His priests are often also lawyers or record-keepers.

*Thareth Wild* is older. He is the god of the vineyard at midnight, of fermentation as a process that no one controls entirely, of the wine that hits differently from the wine you expected, of the feast that goes past the point of social utility into something else. The rites of Thareth Wild are held outside cities and they have varied over the centuries from the merely exuberant to the genuinely alarming. The city authorities of Solis have regulated the Wild Thareth festivals within city limits on three separate occasions in documented history; all three regulatory attempts have eventually failed, because the festivals represent something about fermentation and transformation that proves resistant to civic management in the long run.

The Avite relationship with this division is characteristically direct: Thareth Wild is the one who matters in the context of war, and Thareth Civic is the one who matters in the context of peace, and the Avites are more often at war, so their Thareth tends toward the wild aspect. The Svaleen, who are more often in commercial negotiation, have developed a more sophisticated theological relationship with both natures simultaneously.

### Festivals and Rites

**The Harvest Festival** — called *Tharathven* in Standard Mittoli, with peninsula variants in each city — is the central Thareth festival, timed to the grape harvest and variable by region and variety. In a good year, when multiple varieties ripen in sequence, the Harvest Festival in Solis extends across several weeks as each vineyard community celebrates its own harvest in turn. The city is continuously in a state of festival for most of autumn. Outsiders who arrive during this period and expect Confederation business to proceed normally have not been well-advised.

**The First Press** — the ceremonial pressing of the year's first grapes — is a rite performed in every wine-producing community in Azhora that acknowledges Thareth, with enormous local variation. The priest or community elder performs the first step of pressing; the yield is offered to Thareth before any other use; the remainder is distributed to the community. What the first press means theologically varies: in the Svaleen it is a contract renewal between the community and the god; in the Avite tradition it is a payment of blood-debt for the courage the god has lent; in the Lotharn valley tradition it is a conversation with the valley itself, the wine as proof that the soil has heard you this year.

**The Thareth Vigil** — practiced primarily in the Svaleen and the Iberos coast cities — is a midwinter festival marking the period when the vines appear dead and the stored wine is the only evidence that they ever lived. The vigil is held from sunset to sunrise, drinking through the cold night, waiting for dawn. The theology is explicit: you are imitating Thareth in his death-state, keeping company with the buried vine, expressing confidence that the spring will come. Whether this is theological claim or just a reason to drink all night on the coldest night of the year is a distinction the participants do not always draw.

## History of Wine in Azhora

### The Peninsular Origin

The oldest cultivated vine sites on the continent are on the Svaleen Peninsula. The Academy's archaeological surveys have dated grape pip deposits and the characteristic residue left by fermentation vessels to periods substantially before any mainland viticultural evidence — the peninsula was growing wine for centuries before the Mittolo heartland learned what to do with imported amphorae. The Svaleen peninsula's landscape — limestone ridges, varying exposures, reliable Mediterranean rainfall, a culture with the stability and intensity of focus that produces elaborated knowledge — produced the continent's original wine tradition.

The early Svaleen wine culture was not simply agricultural. It was theological and commercial and diplomatic simultaneously, in the way of things that are foundational enough to mean more than one thing at once. The great temples of early Solis contained wine cellars of a scale suggesting storage across multiple vintages — not merely consumption but the beginnings of classification, of the idea that this year's wine from this hillside is different from last year's wine from the same hillside and worth distinguishing. The amphora system that became standard across the Iberos Sea trade originated in Svaleen storage practice.

### Spread to the Mainland

Wine culture spread from the Svaleen along the Iberos coast and from the coast inland, following trade routes. The Avite peninsula received it early, partly through trade and partly through a theological compatibility: the Thareth cult mapped cleanly onto the existing Avite warrior culture and its emphasis on the honorable drink. West Pyros received it later and transformed it immediately on contact with the volcanic soil, developing a wine culture that the Svaleen eventually acknowledged as producing something distinct and in specific ways superior, which took several centuries of stubborn denial before the acknowledgment was made.

The Mittolo heartland received wine culture through East Pyros and the Iberos coast trade. Mittoli wine production is historically less distinguished than the peninsula or Pyros traditions — the soil and climate are excellent for grain and less specialized for viticulture — but the heartland's agricultural wealth and population size made it the largest wine *market* on the continent, and the trade dynamics of supplying that market shaped the wine economies of every exporting region.

The Lotharn Mountain valleys developed their own wine cultures in relative isolation, the valley-by-valley character of that range producing local varieties and traditions that are poorly known outside the mountains but significant within them. Several Lotharn valley wines have no existence outside their home valley — the variety exists nowhere else, the technique is specific to that community, and the wine is for local consumption and local meaning.

### Wine and Diplomacy

The specific vintage brought to a treaty table is a communication. This is understood across every wine-producing culture in Azhora, though the specific language varies. In Svaleen practice, the quality and provenance of the wine offered to a diplomatic guest tells the guest where they stand in the host city's estimation. Bringing a famous old vintage signals serious respect; bringing a table wine signals that the guest is not considered worth the cellar. Bringing a wine from a territory the guest has recently contested is either an insult or a statement of normalization, and the interpretation depends on other signals in the same meeting.

The Avite tradition is less subtle: the wine at a treaty feast is Thareth's wine, and serving inferior wine at a feast that is meant to bind an agreement is inviting Thareth to regard the agreement as unserious. This is bad. Wine served at significant Avite ceremonies is typically the best available, and kings who have attempted to economize on ceremony wine have not been remembered well by the *veth*.

The practice of gift-wine — sending specific vintages as gifts between powers — generates its own archive in the records of the coastal cities. The Academy's document collection includes wine gift records stretching back three centuries, from which it is possible to reconstruct the political relationships between most major Iberos Sea powers purely from what wine they sent each other and when they stopped.

## Wine Regions

### The Svaleen Peninsula (Suval)

*See also: svaleen_wines.md for the full variety catalogue.*

The Svaleen Peninsula is to Azhoran wine what the Svaleen claim it is: the origin, the standard, the most complex, the most argued-about, and the most important wine-producing region on the continent. One thousand or more distinct cultivated grape varieties have been identified on the peninsula. The number is contested because: the Confederation's three wine classification systems (one per major city, none recognizing the other two) use different criteria for what constitutes a distinct variety; several varieties exist under multiple names in different communities; and the ongoing discovery of varieties in isolated valley communities that have never been compared to the official catalogues means the number is almost certainly growing.

The peninsula's wine culture is ancient enough to have been stratified. At the top of every local hierarchy are the *grand varieties* — those with recorded histories going back beyond the oldest documents, grown on the same terraces for longer than anyone knows. Below those are the *established varieties* — documented for centuries, widely grown within a particular district, the basis of the commercial trade. Below those are the *valley varieties* — confined to a single community or even a single family, often without formal names outside that community, maintained by people who have grown them since before the names were needed. At the bottom are the *new varieties* — the natural hybrids and mutations that appear in vineyards regularly and are either recognized and cultivated or culled, depending on whether someone thought they were interesting.

The valley feuds are the peninsula's most characteristic cultural institution. Every neighboring valley on the Svaleen Peninsula has at least one long-standing dispute about whose grape variety is older, whose wine is superior, whose viticultural tradition is authentic, whose method of determining ripeness is correct. These feuds are conducted in all available registers simultaneously: at the Conclave, in the marketplace, in the theological literature (several Thareth festivals have technically distinct procedures depending on which valley you are in and whether the adjacent valley's version of the procedure is considered legitimate), in the selection of who marries whom, and occasionally in small-scale vandalism of the other valley's signage. No one in the Svaleen Peninsula believes these feuds are resolvable. They are not trying to resolve them. The feuds are the practice; producing wine in a specific way and defending it against all comers is part of what it means to be from a specific valley.

The Svaleen Confederation has been attempting to produce a unified wine classification system for two hundred years. The current attempt is the seventh. It is expected to fail at the point where the committee is asked to decide whether the Solis naming convention or the Enebreum naming convention takes precedence for varieties that appear in both territories under different names. The committee is currently at an earlier point in the process, which has given rise to cautious optimism among observers who have never seen the previous six attempts reach this point.

### Ascarth

*See also: ascarth_wines.md for the full variety catalogue.*

The Ascarth Peninsula is not obviously wine country. Its terrain is rocky, its agricultural land thin, its hills more often used for the copper mining that feeds the bronze economy than for viticulture. And yet: the Avite heroic tradition requires wine in a way that has driven the peninsula's viticulture to a level of sophistication that its geography does not obviously support.

Wine in Ascarth is for warriors and gods. This framing is not metaphorical. The feast at which wine is consumed in the proper Avite tradition is a ritual occasion — the wine marks the event as significant, the quality of the wine marks the host's respect for the occasion, and the consumption is performed in a specific way that the *veth* have described and the tradition maintains. You do not drink Avite wine casually. You drink it as part of something.

This ritualization has produced a wine culture with very specific variety development. Ascarth wine is bred for intensity, for character, for what the Avites call *keth* — a word that translates approximately as "presence" or "the feeling that you have been given something." A wine with *keth* is not merely pleasant; it does something to you, changes the room, marks the moment as different from before. The varieties selected and maintained in Ascarth viticultural tradition are those that produce wines with *keth*. Varieties that produce pleasant, drinkable, unremarkable wines have been abandoned across the peninsula's history because pleasant and unremarkable are not qualities the Avite tradition finds valuable in anything.

Over two hundred distinct varieties have been documented on the peninsula. The relationship between varieties and specific warrior lineages is a feature of the Ascarth tradition without parallel elsewhere: certain grapes are grown by specific families going back into the legendary period, and the wine made from those grapes is that family's wine, served at that family's feasts, offered at that family's ceremonies. The *veth* traditions include the grape lineages alongside the warrior lineages, and the loss of a variety through disease, neglect, or conquest is mourned as a loss in the record comparable to the end of a family line.

### West Pyros

The volcanic soil of West Pyros produces wines that are difficult to grow and frequently spectacular to drink. The mineral complexity that comes from old ash deposits — the layers of different eruption histories compounding into a substrate unlike anything on the Svaleen Peninsula — creates flavors that Svaleen sommeliers have been attempting to describe in their own vocabulary for centuries without consensus. The best West Pyros wines are not quite like anything else on the continent, and the peninsula tradition's reluctance to acknowledge this took a long time to overcome.

West Pyros wine production is older than documentation — the vine arrived with the trade contacts that the volcanic soil communities had with the Iberos coast, and it flourished immediately in the ash-enriched earth. The political instability of the region — contested between Mittoli powers and its own political tradition across its history — has interrupted production at various points but has never eliminated it, because the vineyards survive transitions of power better than most other institutions. The grape does not care who taxes it.

The Pyrosi wine tradition has its own relationship with Thareth that the Svaleen consider theologically unrigorous: the volcanic connection — fire beneath the soil, the land still carrying its fire memory — maps onto the Wild Thareth in a way that has produced festival practices the Solis priesthood regularly issues statements about. The Pyrosi ignore these statements.

### The Iberos Coast

The extended Mediterranean coast of eastern Azhora, from the northern bays down to the Moroshé fringe, produces wine in enormous variety and without a single dominant tradition. Each port city, each bay-side community, has its own practices, its own varieties, its own relationship to the continental wine culture from which it stands somewhat apart by virtue of being coastal, maritime, and commercially oriented rather than agriculturally passionate.

The Iberos coast wine trade is the mechanism by which all other wine reaches places that do not produce their own. The amphora system, the bottling and labeling conventions, the standardized measures — all of these originated in the coast's commercial apparatus. The coast wines are not always the most distinguished on the continent; they are the most traded, the most consistently available, and the wines that set the price expectations for everything else. When people in Minora or Caeras or Nylon speak of "wine," they usually mean something that came through an Iberos coast port.

The region south of the main coast, where Mediterranean conditions begin to tip toward the arid, produces some of the continent's most intense dessert wines — grapes harvested late, dried on mats in the sun, fermented into sweet concentrated wines that travel well and keep long. These wines are traded throughout the Moroshé routes as well as through the Iberos network, and they are among the few wine products to reach the desert interior regularly.

### The Lotharn Mountains

Every Lotharn valley that can grow a grape does. The valleys facing south and west — capturing the maximum sun off the steep slopes — have developed viticultural traditions that are as intense and specific as anything on the Svaleen Peninsula, just less known. A valley wine in the Lotharn is typically produced by one community from one valley's variety and consumed by that community; very little of it escapes into wider trade. What escapes is not reliably available year to year.

The valley wine feuds of the Lotharn are equivalent in intensity to the Svaleen valley feuds and less cosmopolitan in their expression: where Svaleen valley opponents debate at the Conclave and publish pamphlets, Lotharn valley opponents express their wine disagreements primarily by not buying wine from the neighboring valley and saying what they think of it whenever they encounter someone from there, which they structure their travel to minimize. Certain Lotharn passes are known as "contested wine routes" because traveling through them with wine from the wrong valley on your wagon will reliably produce strong opinions from the local community.

Several Lotharn valley varieties have been identified as potentially very significant by outside wine merchants who have tasted them and attempted to purchase plantings or cuttings for cultivation elsewhere. These attempts have uniformly failed, not through active obstruction but through the peculiar property that characterizes the Ibenwood's plants generally: Lotharn valley grapes do not reproduce their character outside the valley. The same variety planted in a Svaleen hillside produces a Svaleen wine. The valley community explains this as the vine being of the valley. The wine merchants explain it as micro-terroir. Both descriptions produce the same practical result.

### Northern Azhora

The cold, wet north grows wine in its own way and does not apologize for the results. The Bay of Lol region and the coastal communities of the north developed varieties that survive hard winters and short growing seasons, producing wines that are markedly different from southern production: thinner, higher in acid, lower in body, but with a clarity and precision that some drinkers consider the most elegant wine on the continent and others consider merely cold. The northern wine tradition insists it is not trying to be the south.

Northern wine culture is tied closely to the fishing economy — wine and preserved fish, the northern table's anchor, the combination that appears in every Bay of Lol feast tradition. The wines are designed to accompany preserved and smoked protein, and they do this with a precision that results from centuries of selection for exactly this pairing. Thareth in the northern tradition is much more the resurrection aspect than the ecstatic one; the vine's winter death and spring return is the central myth, and the festivals track the vine's cycle more literally than southern festivals do.

### Bouén

The cold Bouén peninsula, cooled year-round by the Deep River current, produces wine that is the continent's northernmost significant production. The varieties grown here were selected under conditions that would eliminate most commercial wine grapes elsewhere: fog, persistent cold, short ripening season, exposure. What survived is interesting. Bouéni wine is pale, high-acid, sometimes faintly saline from the coastal fog that settles on the vineyards, and very much an acquired taste that the Bouéni consider worth acquiring.

The peninsula's wine culture is modest and deeply local. Very little Bouéni wine reaches outside markets; the production is too small, the character too specific, and the Bouéni cultural inclination toward self-sufficiency means there is not strong external commercial pressure. The occasional cask that reaches Nylon or Solis through the Iberos trade produces a range of reactions that the Bouéni would find validating if they cared about outside reactions, which they mainly do not.

## The Economics of Wine

Wine is among the top three traded goods on the Iberos Sea by volume in most years, alongside grain and bronze. The amphora trade — the movement of sealed, labeled containers of wine from production regions to consumption markets — is the backbone of several major merchant families' fortunes and the subject of the most detailed commercial law in the Svaleen Confederation's legal tradition.

The problems are those of any luxury commodity: authenticity, fraud, and the relationship between reputation and reality. Svaleen wine from the best-known valley communities commands prices that incentivize producing something else, labeling it as that wine, and hoping no one can tell. They can often tell; the fraud has existed as long as the trade, and the detection methods have evolved alongside it. The Enebreum *tastevins* — the professionals whose training and judgment are employed to evaluate contested wine authenticity — have been in continuous guild operation for over three centuries and maintain their own archive of reference tastings that is the most closely held professional secret on the peninsula.

Wine storage is architecture. The great cellar complexes of Solis, built into the limestone hillside below the city, have been in continuous use for six centuries; the oldest portions are older than the current building above them and belong to a construction tradition that the current city cannot reproduce. The temperature of the hillside cellar is consistent year-round, and this consistency allows wine to age across generations — the Solis cellar contains a dedicated archive of representative vintages going back beyond the beginning of the current Confederation, maintained for reference, not for consumption. Selling a bottle from the archive would be, in Solis, roughly equivalent to selling a document from the city library: technically possible, culturally impermissible.

The Ascarth tin supply and the Svaleen wine trade have been entangled throughout their histories. Legemum supplies tin to Ascarth for bronze; the coastal cities supply wine to Ascarth for everything else; Ascarth bronze goods circulate back through the system. Wine has functioned as the medium by which the bronze economy is domesticated into the civilian economy — the luxury good that converts warrior prestige into commercial activity without requiring warriors to stop being warriors.

## Wine as Civic Identity

The deepest function of wine in Azhoran culture is identity. A Svaleen person identifies with their valley's wine before they identify with the Confederation, their city, or in many cases their family. A West Pyrosi person's relationship to the local wine tradition is part of the Fire Memory — the vine that grows in volcanic soil is connected to the fire beneath it, and tending that vine is a form of relationship with the land's deep nature. An Avite who carries wine from the family vintage into battle is carrying something of the family line with them; the wine is not metaphorically connected to the lineage, it is practically connected, grown by the family, made by the family, offered at the family's ceremonies.

Thareth enables all of this simultaneously because the wine god is himself multiple — the same god appearing as different things in different contexts, which is the nature of the divine in the Azhoran religious sensibility but is especially true here. Wine is both the most universal substance in Azhoran civilization — present on every table, in every ceremony, across every culture that can grow a grape — and the most intensely local, tied to specific valleys and specific families and specific stones in specific hillsides that cannot be reproduced anywhere else. The god who is the patron of this substance is correspondingly both universal and local, both civic and wild, both the rational management of fermentation and the thing that fermentation, if you let it, will do to you.

The Academy's comparative religion division has attempted to produce a unified account of Thareth theology. The attempt produced a very long document that the practitioners of all the various Thareth traditions found partially but not wholly recognizable, which is probably the correct outcome. Wine does not admit of a single account. Neither does its god.
