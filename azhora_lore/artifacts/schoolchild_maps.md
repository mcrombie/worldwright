# Schoolchild Maps of Corav

The Azhoran Academy of Natural History maintains a small but much-visited collection of hand-drawn maps of the known world produced by children between the ages of roughly eight and fourteen. The collection numbers over three hundred pieces, spanning approximately two centuries of acquisition, and represents one of the more unusual holdings in the Academy's archive.

The maps are not kept here for their accuracy. They are kept because they are revealing.

## Origins of the Collection

The collection began accidentally. A cartography lecturer at the Caeras Valley Academy, one Prelloss Omath, began in the habit of asking new students to draw a map of the world from memory on their first day of class, before any instruction had been given. His intention was diagnostic — he wanted to know what his students already believed before he started correcting them. Over forty years of teaching he accumulated several hundred of these first-day maps, and when he retired he donated them to the Academy archive with a note reading: "More informative than anything I taught."

Subsequent collectors have added to the archive through donation and deliberate acquisition. Maps are solicited from schools across Mittolo and occasionally from schools in other regions, which accounts for the noticeable regional variation in how the world is depicted.

## What the Maps Show

Studied as a body, the maps are a record of what Azhoran children believe the world looks like based on what adults have told them, imperfectly remembered and imperfectly rendered.

**Azhora** is almost always present and almost always too large relative to everything else. This is not surprising — it is the world the children know, and the world you know always feels larger than the world you don't. In most maps, Azhora occupies between half and three-quarters of the total space, with everything else compressed into the margins.

**The Northern Continent** appears in the majority of maps as a flat green band across the top — which is roughly how it is described in standard geography lessons, and which is roughly what it looks like from the northern coast. Ice Spear, when it appears, is almost always too large and too central, usually placed directly above Azhora rather than offset to the northwest. Several maps show it as a single dramatic spike, which reflects the name rather than the shape.

**The Southern Archipelago** is consistently underrepresented. It appears in perhaps a third of maps, usually as a scattering of small dots below Azhora. In the maps from Bouéni-influenced schools, it appears more prominently and is sometimes labeled with names not found in any Academy record, which suggests the Bouéni oral tradition contains geographic knowledge that the Academy's formal cartography does not.

**The Iberos Sea islands** are frequently collapsed into a single blob to the east. Canon and Suval, where they appear, are often placed much closer to Azhora than they are in reality.

**The Silence** appears in perhaps a quarter of maps, usually as a dark patch in the western ocean, sometimes labeled, sometimes just shaded differently. Several maps show a sea creature in or near it. The creature descriptions vary.

## What the Maps Reveal

Beyond the geographic content, the maps record something about the distribution of knowledge across Azhoran society. Children from Caeras valley cities draw more detailed interior maps of Azhora, with rivers and region names, but tend to have vaguer edges. Children from the Bay of Lol region draw the northern coast and Cold Stones with unusual confidence and then lose detail rapidly heading south. Children from Zoth and the Iberos coast know the island geography in the east and tend to place Canon closer and larger than it is.

One class of detail that appears in children's maps but is absent from official Academy cartography: several dozen maps, produced independently across different decades and different regions, show a small landmass in the far western ocean that does not correspond to the Western Shore speculation and is too consistently positioned to be random invention. The Academy has noted this. It has not dispatched an expedition to investigate it. A letter in the archive from a former chief cartographer, written forty years ago, says: "If the children know something we don't, that is the Academy's problem, not theirs."

## The Prelloss Omath Prize

Every three years, the Academy awards a small prize to the most cartographically interesting map submitted by a child. The criteria explicitly exclude accuracy — the prize is for "most revealing of the geographic imagination." Past winners include a map drawn entirely from the perspective of a fish (the ocean is detailed and Azhora is a distorted mass visible from below), a map in which the Red Meridian is drawn as a wall rather than a stripe, and a map by an eleven-year-old from the Ganoss uplands that places the Northern Continent where Azhora should be and labels what is apparently Azhora as "the land we came from."

The Academy judges did not know what to make of the last entry. They gave it the prize anyway.
