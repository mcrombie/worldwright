# The Solar System of Corav

Corav is the third planet from its star, orbiting at a distance that places it squarely in the region where liquid water is stable at the surface — a fact whose significance Azhoran astronomers have only recently begun to appreciate, now that the concept of "habitable distance" is a new and contested idea in academic circles. The star has seven known objects orbiting it in regular paths. Two of these — Corav and Ossian — have moons of their own that are visible to the naked eye and have shaped Azhoran civilization in ways that predate civilization itself.

What follows is the current state of Azhoran astronomical knowledge, which is incomplete in ways the Academy acknowledges and incomplete in ways it does not yet know to acknowledge.

---

## The Star

Corav's star has no single canonical name. Every language family has its own word for it, and the political project of assigning it a universal Mittoli name has stalled at the same place every naming controversy stalls: nobody can agree, and the star continues to rise regardless. The Academy refers to it in formal documents as *Talvoss* ("the fire-fixed," from Mittoli *tal* = fire and *-voss* = enduring fixed thing), though in practice most documents simply say "the sun."

It is a yellow-white dwarf star — stable, middle-aged, and apparently unremarkable. It has been shining without perceptible variation for as long as Azhoran records extend, and based on what is now understood about how stars age, there is no reason to expect it to change on any timescale relevant to human civilization. This is a recent and somewhat unsettling conclusion: it was not difficult to establish, but establishing it required thinking of the sun as a body with a lifespan, which required thinking of it as a thing at all rather than a permanent feature of the sky. The Academy's natural philosophy department takes credit for this conceptual step. The theology faculty at several institutions have not responded formally.

The star's angular diameter is consistent across the year, confirming that Corav's orbit is nearly circular. A dramatically elliptical orbit would produce perceptible changes in the sun's apparent size — and the seasons would be caused by distance rather than axial tilt. They are not; the seasons are explained entirely by axial tilt, and the orbit's near-circularity is confirmed by the fact that the sun looks the same size in summer and winter.

## The Wandering Stars

Against the background of fixed stars, seven objects move. They shift position night to night, tracing complex paths that ancient astronomers found maddening and modern astronomers find elegant once the underlying geometry is understood. These are the planets — bodies orbiting the same star as Corav, visible by reflected sunlight.

The word used in Standard Mittoli for a planet is *aelvorn* — "sky-speaker," from *ael* (speech) + *vorn* (sky). The implication that they are speaking is old and not fully abandoned even by modern astronomers, some of whom would phrase it differently but would concede that the planets do, in a sense, encode information in their positions.

---

### Tael — The Hidden One

The innermost planet, and the most difficult to observe. Tael orbits in approximately **80 Corav days** — too fast and too close to the sun for comfortable study. From Corav, it never appears more than 23 degrees from the sun, which means it is always either just before sunrise or just after sunset, always low on the horizon, always fighting the atmospheric blur of twilight. On most nights it is simply invisible, lost in the sun's glare. At its greatest elongation — the brief periods when its angular separation from the sun is largest — it appears as a bright reddish-white point on the horizon for perhaps an hour before dawn or after dusk.

Tael is a small rocky world with no significant atmosphere. This last point is not confirmed by Azhoran observation; it has been inferred from the planet's behavior. It shows no blurring or scattering that would indicate an atmospheric haze, and its apparent color does not shift with elongation the way an atmospheric planet's might. The inference is a reasonable one.

Most Azhoran cultures have little tradition attached to Tael, because it is rarely seen and brief when visible. The exception is Moreshi desert astronomy, which has the clearest skies on the continent: *Al-Khafii* ("the hidden one," "the veiled") has been tracked in Moreshi records for longer than in any other tradition, and its position tables are the most complete anywhere in Azhoran scholarship. The Moreshi do not treat its rarity as a reason to ignore it; they treat it as a reason to pay careful attention to the moments when it appears.

### Aevael — The Returner

The second planet, and the most spectacular. Aevael orbits in approximately **220 Corav days** and is the brightest object in the night sky after Sova at full phase. At its most brilliant — when its phase and its angular size combine favorably — it is bright enough to cast faint shadows, and sharp-eyed observers can see it in full daylight if they know where to look.

Like Tael, Aevael is always relatively close to the sun in Corav's sky, never appearing more than about 46 degrees from it. This means it appears as either a morning star (rising before the sun, visible in the eastern pre-dawn sky) or an evening star (setting after the sun, visible in the western post-dusk sky). It alternates between these two configurations over a roughly 584-day cycle — the time between successive conjunctions with the sun, as Aevael overtakes and laps the slower Corav in its inner orbit.

**The morning star and evening star were considered two different objects for most of Azhoran history.** Standard Mittoli had *Aevael* (the evening star, the returner) and *Novael* (the morning star, the new-coming). Moreshi had *Al-Zahir* (the apparent, the evening manifestation) and *Al-Ṣabiih* (the dawn-one). The Bouéni had two entirely separate navigational traditions built around each. The realization that they are the same object — that Aevael disappears in the west and reappears in the east because it has looped behind the sun — is recorded in Azhoran scholarship approximately 280 years ago, attributed to an astronomer named Cellan Vaer-Mithoss, whose name is remembered while the reaction to her paper ("this cannot be correct") is also remembered.

The significance of Aevael to the heliocentric debate cannot be overstated. **Aevael shows a full phase cycle** — it has been seen in a full disc configuration (when it is on the far side of the sun from Corav), a half-phase (at greatest elongation), and a thin crescent (when near inferior conjunction between Corav and the sun). A full disc is impossible under any geocentric model: if Aevael always orbited between Corav and the sun, the sun would always illuminate it from behind and it could only be seen as a full disc, never a crescent. The crescent phase proves Aevael comes between Corav and the sun. The full phase proves Aevael goes around the *other* side of the sun. The only model consistent with both observations is one in which Aevael orbits the sun, not Corav.

This argument was made formally approximately 300 years ago by the Caeras Valley Academy's astronomy division. The Academy's philosophy faculty spent the following fifty years explaining why it was not conclusive. The astronomy division spent those fifty years refining its measurements. At some point the philosophy faculty stopped publishing responses. The heliocentric model became the working framework of the Academy approximately 230 years ago, though it has never been formally declared the official cosmological position, because doing so would require decommissioning the previous position, which was never formally declared either.

**On Aevael itself:** it is a rocky planet with a thick, opaque atmosphere that scatters sunlight and gives it its brilliant yellow-white appearance. The surface, insofar as it can be studied from Corav, appears uniform under cloud cover. Whether it is hospitable to life is not known. Based on its proximity to the star and the density of its atmosphere, Academy natural philosophers have theorized that its surface is extremely hot — possibly hot enough to melt certain metals. This has not been confirmed. It has also not been refuted.

### Drelith — The Falling Red

The fourth planet from the star and the first beyond Corav. Drelith orbits in approximately **670 Corav days** — just under two Corav years. Because it is in an outer orbit, it is visible anywhere in the sky (unlike the inner planets), and it goes through a full retrograde loop approximately every 2.1 Corav years when Corav, on its inner and faster orbit, overtakes and laps Drelith.

Drelith is distinctly reddish — a brick-red color that stands out clearly from the blue-white and yellow-white hues of other bright objects. The red is a surface characteristic: Drelith appears to have thin or no significant atmosphere, and its surface reflects light with a reddish-orange quality consistent with iron oxide minerals — the same chemistry as Azhoran red soils, at planetary scale.

Its brightness varies enormously. At opposition — when Drelith is on the opposite side of Corav from the sun, closest and fully illuminated — it is among the five brightest objects in the sky. At conjunction — when it is behind the sun and at maximum distance — it disappears entirely. This dramatic variation, plus its reddish color and its retrograde loops, made it one of the earliest observed and most culturally influential planets in Azhoran tradition.

Drelith is associated with change, disruption, and conflict across most Azhoran astrological traditions. This is probably because its opposition — its most dramatic appearance, when it is brightest and moving most noticeably — occurs in irregular intervals that resist easy calendar integration, and "something bright moving strangely in the sky" has reliably made ancient observers nervous. Modern astronomy explains the brightness variation by geometry and the color by mineralogy. The association with disruption persists in traditions that are not obligated to update their interpretations when the Academy does.

Drelith is a small rocky world. Its apparent lack of moons has been studied; none have been confirmed. At its surface, the atmosphere is thin enough that the sky would appear dark even in daylight, and both Corav and the star would be visible simultaneously — Corav as a bright blue-white point, distinctly planetary in appearance to any observer on Drelith's surface. Azhoran astronomers have noted, with some interest, that from Drelith's surface, Corav would look very much the way Aevael looks from Corav: a brilliant inner planet showing phases, never far from the sun. The symmetry has been remarked upon without producing a satisfying conclusion about what it implies.

### Ossian — The Great Year

The fifth planet, the largest, and the one that has left the deepest mark on Azhoran civilization. Ossian orbits the star in approximately **4,320 Corav days — exactly 12 Corav years**, to a precision that has been refined over centuries of measurement and has not shifted. The ratio is not approximate; to the best current measurement, Ossian completes one orbit for every twelve that Corav completes, with a residual error small enough to be within observational uncertainty.

This is numerically extraordinary. A 12:1 resonance between an inner and outer planet is a specific gravitational configuration, not a coincidence. Whether it is truly exact, or merely very close to exact, is a question that current measurement cannot resolve — the error bars are too large. What is not in question is the cultural consequence: for as long as Azhoran civilizations have tracked the sky carefully, the same planet has returned to the same position every twelve years, and every culture with a developed astronomical tradition has noticed.

Ossian is a gas giant — a planet primarily composed of deep layers of compressed gas rather than solid rock. This was established relatively recently through careful observation of its behavior during occultations (when it passes in front of a background star) and through the behavior of its moons. It has no solid surface in the way Drelith or Corav do; it simply gets denser and denser toward its center. Its apparent disc, visible through early optical instruments, shows bands of cloud in different colors — the signature of a rotating gas atmosphere. It is by far the largest planet in the system, and estimates of its mass place it as containing more mass than all other planets combined.

**Ossian's moons** are the other significant fact about it for Azhoran astronomy. Four large moons are visible through early optical instruments as separate points orbiting Ossian. To the naked eye, they are indistinct — on very clear, dark nights, a very careful observer might see Ossian as slightly fuzzy rather than a clean point, and reports of this observation go back centuries. With even modest optical aid, they resolve clearly as four distinct bodies orbiting Ossian over periods ranging from a few days to roughly two months.

The moons of Ossian were the first confirmation that not everything in the sky orbits Corav. They orbit Ossian. This observation, made approximately 180 years ago by an Academy astronomer using an early compound lens instrument, was the single most important piece of empirical evidence for the heliocentric model — if those four moons orbit Ossian, then orbiting Corav is not a universal rule of the cosmos. The geocentric response (the moons orbit Ossian but Ossian still orbits Corav) is not wrong, but it opened a door that subsequent observation could not close.

### Aethoss — The One That Changes Shape

The sixth planet is visible to the naked eye but faint, and it is the most puzzling object in the pre-optical-instrument sky. Aethoss orbits in approximately **10,800 Corav days — 30 Corav years** — slowly enough that it moves perceptibly against background stars only over months.

The puzzle is its appearance. Careful observers have always noted that Aethoss does not look quite like other planets or stars. Most of the time it appears slightly extended — not a clean point but slightly oval, slightly fuzzy, as if three stars very close together were blurring into one. Azhoran traditions explain this differently: some see it as two companion stars flanking a brighter central one; Moreshi astronomers call it *Al-Muthallath* ("the triple"); Hill Mittoli sky-lore calls it "the star that carries its children."

Every approximately 15 years, the companions disappear. Aethoss becomes a single clean point, indistinguishable from an ordinary star, and then — over the following years — the companions slowly return. This cycle has been documented reliably for at least six centuries. It was the most studied mystery in pre-optical-instrument Azhoran astronomy: a celestial body that periodically sheds and regrows its apparent companions.

The explanation, established approximately 120 years ago through improved optics, is that Aethoss is surrounded by a system of rings — a flat disc of debris and ice encircling the planet at its equatorial plane. Seen at an angle, the rings extend visibly to either side of the planet's disc (the "companions"). Seen edge-on — when the plane of the rings aligns with the direction of observation — they appear as a thin line that is invisible at the distances involved, and the planet appears alone. As Aethoss continues in its orbit, the angle changes again, and the rings re-appear.

This explanation requires understanding that Aethoss is a three-dimensional body surrounded by a three-dimensional flat disc, and that the disc is being seen at different angles over time as the geometry of Corav's and Aethoss's orbits changes. The conceptual leap required was significant. The astronomer who first proposed the ring interpretation — working from careful drawings spanning two decades of observation — was initially dismissed on the grounds that rings around a planet made no sense physically. She was, in the specific sense of having correctly described the geometry, correct. Why rings exist — what keeps them there, what they are made of, why they form around some planets and not others — is not established.

Aethoss, like Ossian, is a gas giant, with banded cloud structure visible through optical instruments.

### Rimael — The Late Discovery

The seventh known planet was not known to be a planet until approximately 200 years ago. It appears to the naked eye as a faint star — visible only on very clear, dark nights, away from any settlement — and was catalogued as such for centuries. An astronomer at the Academy named Ellos Darenoth noticed, during a long-running star-position survey, that one of the "stars" in his catalogue had moved perceptibly between observations taken eighteen months apart. Stars do not move relative to each other on any timescale that human observation can detect. He checked his instruments, confirmed his measurements, and spent six months convincing himself before publishing the conclusion: the object was not a star but a planet.

Rimael orbits in approximately **30,000 Corav days — 83 Corav years** — so slowly that retrograde motion takes years to complete and the planet moves against the fixed stars only by patient, long-term observation. In 200 years of confirmed tracking, it has completed slightly more than two full orbits. Its period, like Ossian's, has been confirmed to high precision through careful accumulated measurement.

Rimael is a gas giant at great distance from the sun, and at that distance it receives very little light and reflects very little back. It appears blue-green through optical instruments — a coloration consistent with certain atmospheric gases that absorb red light more strongly than blue. It has moons, but they are too faint to observe clearly with current instrumentation.

Its discovery has had two consequences. The first is practical: if Rimael exists and was not known, there may be additional planets farther out still, beyond what current instruments can detect. Astronomical surveys of the outer sky are now a sustained research priority at the Academy. The second is philosophical: the solar system is larger than anyone assumed. The planets visible to the naked eye fill only a portion of the orbital space that the star's gravity commands. What else is out there is a genuinely open question.

## The Asteroid Belt and Cometary Sources

Between Drelith (fourth planet) and Ossian (fifth), the space is not empty. A population of rocky bodies — too small to resolve as discs, but detectable as they cross the sky or, occasionally, as they enter Corav's atmosphere — occupies this region. Azhoran astronomers have catalogued hundreds of these "minor bodies" and estimated the total population in the thousands.

The belt is thin and widely spread; the image of densely packed rocks is inaccurate. A ship passing through it (hypothetically) would be unlikely to encounter a boulder except by extraordinary misfortune. The rocks are simply the population of bodies that never aggregated into a planet in that zone, possibly because of Ossian's gravitational influence disrupting the formation process.

The Ember Comet — returning every 47 years from beyond Rimael's orbit — is one of a population of icy bodies on highly elliptical orbits, occasionally perturbed by planetary interactions into paths that bring them through the inner solar system. There are almost certainly other periodic comets in similar orbits that have not yet been identified, either because their periods are longer than the observational record or because they have not been noticed on prior passes. The Academy maintains a program specifically for this: cataloguing faint objects whose position changes over months, in hopes of identifying the next long-period comet before it becomes visible rather than after.

## What Is Not Known

The solar system, as described here, is the solar system as Azhoran astronomy currently understands it. This is not the same as the solar system as it is.

Additional planets beyond Rimael may exist — are probably inferred to exist, on the basis of small perturbations in Rimael's orbit that the current planetary model does not fully account for. If those perturbations are real (not measurement error), something beyond Rimael is pulling on it. The mass required to produce the perturbation is estimated to be significant — a planet, not a minor body. Whether that planet will be found, and when, depends on observation and instrumentation that does not yet exist.

The cometary population in the far outer solar system — the reservoir from which comets like the Ember Comet originate — is entirely unobserved. Azhorans know it must exist (comets come from somewhere), but its extent, composition, and total mass are unknown. Comets occasionally arrive from unexpected directions with unexpected periods, and the model that explains their source has not been formalized.

Whether any other planet in the system besides Corav is capable of supporting life is a question that has been asked and cannot be answered. Aevael is probably too hot. Drelith and Ossian's moons are uncertain. The question itself — whether life can exist elsewhere in the solar system — is new enough to Azhoran intellectual tradition that the Academy has not established a formal position on whether it is a legitimate scientific question or a theological one.
