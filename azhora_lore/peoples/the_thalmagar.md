# The People of Thalmagar

The Cape of Thalmagar extends from the northwestern terminus of the Oremindi into the sea, cut off from the rest of Azhora on three sides by water and on the fourth by the most severe passes on the northern Oremindi range — seasonal at best, impassable in most years. What lies on the other side of these mountains from the rest of the continent sits in its own world.

Between three and five thousand people live on the cape, in small settlements on the northern bay coast. The range reflects the difficulty of counting rather than any ambiguity about whether people exist. The people themselves, asked to account for how long they have been here or where they came from, offer no founding narrative, no arrival story, no record of a first generation. They have, in the phrasing of one Northern Azhoran merchant who spent a winter on the bay coast, "always been here, and they know it." The land remembers them and they remember the land, and they have found this a sufficient description of how they came to be where they are.

---

## The Settlements and the Sea

Permanent settlement on the cape requires a protected inlet, a freshwater source, and a beach workable in most winds. These requirements are met in perhaps eight or ten places along the bay-facing northern coast. These are where the people are. The rest belongs to other things.

The northern sea is the foundational economic fact. Cold water is productive water: the nutrient upwelling of a cold coastal sea supports fish populations of a density that warmer fisheries do not match, and the Thalmagar communities have been taking from those populations since before their records begin. The specific cold-water species that do not range significantly south form the dietary foundation of every Thalmagar household. Beyond fish: the large sea mammals that haul out on remote beaches — taken for oil, hide, and ivory — are the primary trade goods that make the seasonal crossing from the northern Azhoran coast worth attempting.

The crossing is made in summer, when the Outer Deep is as calm as it gets. A few ships per season. Contacts are brief and practical. The Thalmagar communities are not interested in expansion, in trade debt, in the political arrangements of northern Azhora, or in any offer that requires sustained outside presence on the cape. The trading contacts that have persisted are conducted on the cape's terms: come in summer, trade fairly, leave before the season closes the crossing.

## Cohabitation with the Large Animals

The megafauna of the cape is the thing that defines, in outside perception, what Thalmagar is. In the cape's own perception, it is simply the condition of the landscape — not remarkable, not threatening, not sacred in any external sense, but present in the same way that weather is present, and requiring the same quality of continuous attention that weather requires of people who cannot afford to be surprised by it.

The Shore Elder occupies the flat rocky beaches in groups of three to eight — four meters of semi-aquatic animal with forward-oriented eyes, long enough forelimbs to raise its front upright for observation, and the teeth of something that processes large prey. It does not attack without cause. It is large enough that "without cause" and "safely" are not the same thing. The Thalmagar know which beaches are occupied at which seasons and navigate accordingly. The knowledge is generational, detailed, and taken seriously.

The Cape Palmant is the largest ungulate documented in the northwest — elk-like in structure but standing above two meters at the shoulder, with three-meter-span palmated antlers of a form more archaic than any living cervid. The Palmant is not hunted. The Thalmagar state this as fact rather than prohibition, in the same register they use to say that the passes are closed in winter. Why it is not hunted — whether by ancient decision, pragmatic assessment, or some arrangement whose nature has not been explained — has not been established from outside.

The Standing Hul is the apex land predator: six to eight hundred kilograms, bear-like in the general body plan, bipedal in habit, observing from standing height with the patience of an animal that does not have predators rather than the urgency of one that does. The settlements are sited partly in reference to Hul territorial ranges. There are protocols for close-range encounters, and the protocols are in regular use. The Hul is not hunted. Like the Palmant, this is stated as fact, but unlike the Palmant — whose scale makes hunting a theoretical exercise in courage — hunting the Hul would be physically feasible for a coordinated group. That it does not happen, and that this does not require ongoing communal enforcement as a prohibition, has not been explained to outside observers in terms they have found satisfying.

The Long-Back is a browser of archaic form — the size of a large horse at the shoulder, with a proportionally long neck that allows it to feed from upper forest canopy unreachable by other browsers. Its skeleton, obtained through trade and examined by natural historians at the Caeras Valley Academy, matches forms described in fossil records from several Azhoran regions: ancient remains from layers understood to represent animals gone from the continent for a very long time. Whether the Long-Back is a direct descendant of those populations or a parallel development is not established.

The Rack-Wing nests on the western sea cliffs: the largest bird documented in Azhoran natural history, wingspan between three and four meters, hunting at the ocean surface from cliff updrafts. It has taken children. The cape people do not approach the cliff nesting areas in specific seasons, and the prohibition is taken with the same seriousness as the Palmant routing protocols.

## The Knowledge That Governs

The knowledge of the megafauna — their seasonal routes, territorial ranges, breeding calendars, long-cycle behavioral patterns — is the central body of practical and social knowledge on the cape. What other cultures organize around agriculture, trade, or military capacity, the Thalmagar organize around knowing where the large animals are and moving through the landscape that belongs to all of them.

Elders who hold the most detailed and accurate knowledge of long-cycle animal behavior are the people whose counsel is most sought in every other domain. This is not a metaphor for wisdom in the abstract. The knowledge is specific, it is practical, and getting it wrong has direct consequences for the settlement's planning.

Why the megafauna are not hunted — the Palmant, the Hul, the Rack-Wing — is a question that outside traders and naturalists have asked repeatedly. The most consistent response, rendered into the Northern Mittoli required for trade, is a construction that translates approximately as: "They were here first." This may be a statement about history. It may be a statement about priority. It may be something that requires the cape's own language to say precisely, and the Mittoli approximation carries the shape of the meaning without its full weight.

## Language

The language of the cape is not a dialect of Northern Mittoli or a member of any known Azhoran language family. Every outside linguist who has obtained vocabulary samples has established this and then found themselves unable to say what it is instead. It is not Moreshi, not Grassic, not any of the known Iberos coastal languages. Comparison with Sevronese produced no correspondences that could not be attributed to chance.

The one consistent observation: it appears old in the way Grassic is old — no ancestor in any surviving record, no relatives among any documented family, the product of a history not traceable from the outside. If the Prior Presence Hypothesis developed for Grassic has merit, the language of Thalmagar may represent a third case: a language family predating the arrival of the Mittoli, Pyrosi, Moreshi, and Bouéni families rather than deriving from any of them.

When asked what a word means, cape people tend to describe its use rather than render it into Mittoli equivalents. This is either a teaching philosophy or a statement about the impossibility of translation. Both possibilities have been considered. The cape people learn enough Northern Mittoli or trade-dialect for commercial purposes and do not, in any documented instance, teach their own language to visitors.

## Summary of Key Points

- The People of Thalmagar are the inhabitants of an isolated cape cut off from the rest of Azhora by sea on three sides and by the northern Oremindi's most severe passes on the fourth; between three and five thousand people in small settlements on the northern bay coast.
- They offer no founding narrative, no arrival story, no first generation: "the land remembers them and they remember the land."
- The knowledge of the megafauna — Shore Elder, Cape Palmant, Standing Hul, Long-Back, Rack-Wing — is the central body of practical and social knowledge; elders with the most accurate long-cycle animal behavioral knowledge are the most sought advisors in all other domains.
- The Palmant, Hul, and Rack-Wing are not hunted; all three are stated as fact in the same register as the statement that the passes are closed in winter; the most consistent explanation offered to outside visitors is "they were here first."
- The cape's megafauna are what Azhora used to be: forms preserved by isolation that appear in the mainland's fossil record as species long extinct everywhere else.
- The language of the cape is unrelated to all Azhoran language families and possibly pre-dates the major language family arrivals on the continent; cape people translate by describing use rather than rendering equivalent words.
