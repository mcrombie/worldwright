# The Avites

The Avites are the dominant culture of the Ascarth Peninsula — a rocky, cliff-faced finger of land that juts into the southern Iberos Sea and that has, by the calculations of every neighboring maritime power, far more military and commercial weight than its size and agricultural capacity would suggest. This disproportionate weight comes from two sources: a warrior tradition that the Avites treat with the same systematic seriousness that a farming culture gives to agriculture, and a bronze-working craft so refined that its products outperform what their neighbors produce with iron. The Avites understand that both rest on reputation, and they understand that reputation is not maintained by accident.

They are difficult to dismiss and, to the coastal cities that know them well, inconveniently difficult to forget.

---

## What the Avites Value

The word the Avites use for what they pursue is translated into other languages as "glory," but the translation loses precision in the same way that translating a technical term into a common word always loses precision. Avite glory is not general admiration or historical importance or the approval of posterity. It is the specific recognition that a life has been lived at the quality it was built for — that the physical cultivation was done, the attention was maintained, the presence was present when the moment came, and that this was witnessed and recorded by someone competent to recognize it.

The *veth* are the competent recognizers. They are the oral archive of the heroic tradition: specialists who spend years learning the epic cycles that name specific warriors and their specific deeds going back to a period that is partially legendary and partially historical, and who perform these cycles publicly at festivals, recite them at deaths, and arbitrate genealogical disputes with the same authority that a Caeras valley archivist settles title questions. The *veth*'s social position is roughly equivalent to a very well-regarded general: honored, listened to, expected to perform, not expected to fight. Their archives — maintained in a building adjacent to the Aevis citadel — are the closest thing to formal history that Avite culture produces, and they have declined outside study with the consistency of established policy.

Death in battle is the optimal outcome of a heroic life in Avite tradition. This sounds worse from the outside than it feels from the inside. The tradition does not celebrate dying; it celebrates the quality of living that makes death in battle possible rather than accidental. A warrior who died of fever at camp is not dishonored; they simply did not reach their moment. A warrior who died fleeing has not found their ending in the way the tradition describes. The distinction matters, and the *veth* make it in the cycles.

Women in the epic tradition are principals rather than ornaments. They appear as queens who direct strategy, mothers who shape heroes, figures whose judgment turns outcomes. Whether this reflects ancient political reality or later idealization is not settled.

## Bronze

The Avite commitment to bronze over iron is not sentiment and is not tradition in the pejorative sense. It is the rational preference of people who are better at one thing than anyone else, pursued in the domain where they are better.

Avite bronze is technologically exceptional. The Ascarth Peninsula's copper ore, combined with several centuries of accumulated craft knowledge organized within a guild system that is also a religious institution, has produced an alloy that experienced smiths describe with something approaching reverence: harder at the edge than most iron, less brittle at the core, working better under the hammer. The alloying ratios are precise and are not widely shared. They move from master to apprentice under conditions of considerable formality.

The bronze economy has one structural vulnerability: the peninsula produces copper but not tin. Tin comes primarily from Legemum, the peninsula immediately to the west. Every king of Ascarth who has considered expansion has also considered, at some length, what it would mean to control that supply directly. None have managed it permanently. What they have instead is a commercial relationship that is not comfortable for either party and works — Legemum sets the price, Ascarth buys, and both understand what would happen to the relationship if either pushed too hard.

The visual signature of Avite achievement is bronze-clad architecture. The newer bronze-work of Aevis's recent construction shines golden-orange; the older districts have gone deep verdigris-green; reading the city from the harbor means reading two centuries of building history in two colors. The great bronze gates of the Aevis citadel — two full stories, depicting the campaigns of the first Ascarth kings in relief — are the most famous physical feature on the peninsula and are heavy enough that opening them requires a mechanism and several men. Visitors are made aware of this.

## Aevis and the Kingdom

The kingdom of Ascarth is not a permanent entity. It is a condition the peninsula enters when Aevis is strong enough and its king capable enough to compel or convince the other cities to acknowledge suzerainty. It dissolves when the king lacks the capacity the tradition requires, when tributary cities accumulate grievances beyond the manageable level, or when external circumstances damage Aevis's prestige enough that the smaller cities conclude the calculation has changed. It has reformed several times within recorded history. The current period is one of partial unification, with the southern cities actively contesting the terms.

The Avite heroic tradition makes royal succession inherently unstable in the specific way that any system requiring personal quality makes succession unstable: the king must be worthy. This is not a formal criterion with a clear test; it is a genuine one enforced by warriors who stop finding the calculation favorable. Several kingdoms have ended without formal conflict — the king simply found that the allegiances he thought he held were no longer operative.

The other cities of the peninsula are not Aevis in miniature. Each has its own lineage claims, its own contribution to the epic cycle, its own warrior traditions. Several have pre-Avite foundations — evidence in place names and certain ritual practices of earlier inhabitants whose language was absorbed but whose memory was not entirely replaced.

## Among the Coast

The coastal cities of the Iberos Sea — Nylon, Svaleen, Selemis — have a consistent relationship with Aevis: admiration for what the Avites are genuinely excellent at, and systematic attention to making sure that excellence does not become a military problem. Nylon's harbor records contain what amounts to a continuous assessment of Ascarth's internal coherence, updated with every trading ship that returns from Aevis. A united Ascarth controls a stretch of coastline that any north-south ship traffic must pass. Every maritime power in the Iberos has found occasion to ensure this unity does not persist long enough to become fully leveraged.

The Selemi of the island across the channel are the Avites' most durable antagonists — natural enemies of culture and economy, neighbors long enough that the antagonism is structured by centuries of knowing exactly how the other side thinks. Aevis has conquered Selemis twice. Both times the island fell, the Selemi outpost network continued functioning, and the occupation ended when its cost exceeded what it produced. The Selemi have developed a theory about this. The Avites have not fully accepted it.

The Avites find the Galans — who broker most of the commerce between the peninsula and the mainland interior — useful and occasionally grating in the way that a reliable but unimpressible supplier is grating. The Galans find the Avites impressive in a way they would not describe as comfortable.

## Religion

The Avite religious tradition centers on the divine powers embedded in heroic lineage, the great cattle figures associated with strength and civic foundation, and the Bronze Bull powers of Aevis — enormous bull-headed figures standing at the city's major public spaces, verdigris-green after generations of sea air, staring toward the water. These are not merely decorative; they are the visible presence of something the city's founding is understood to have sanctified.

The Avites share the general Azhoran sense of the unified divine principle but have not produced the philosophical theology of the Mittoli or Svaleen. Their theology is in the epic cycles, not in treatises. What the gods are and what they want from people is visible in what the tradition celebrates and what it condemns, and the *veth* are the theologians in the specific sense of people who maintain and interpret that record.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Avites are the warrior culture of the Ascarth Peninsula, organized around the cultivation of heroic quality as a systematic life practice rather than an occasional achievement.
- The *veth* (rememberers) are the oral archive of the tradition: the people who know the epic cycles, recite them at festivals and deaths, and arbitrate genealogical disputes with the authority of their knowledge.
- Avite bronze-working is technologically exceptional — not sentiment for an older material but rational commitment to a craft where they outperform their neighbors. The supply of tin from Legemum is the one structural dependency.
- The Kingdom of Ascarth periodically forms under a king capable of compelling other peninsula cities into suzerainty, and dissolves when he cannot; the current period is one of partial unification.
- The Avites' relationship with coastal neighbors is structured by mutual recognition that a united Ascarth controls key coastal access, balanced by those neighbors' systematic attention to preventing that unity from becoming problematic.
- The Selemi of the channel island are the Avites' most durable antagonists: conquered twice, never fully held, because the Selemi civilization is not located on any island that can be taken.
