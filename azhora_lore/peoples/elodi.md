# The Elodi

The Elodi are not native to Azhora. They have lived here long enough that the distinction is largely academic — in Elod they have built a city that will outlast most of the people debating their origins — but they carry in their religion a detailed memory of somewhere else, somewhere they call the Old Kingdom, and this memory sets them apart from every other culture on the continent.

Across Azhora, Elodi communities can be found in most major cities and along trade routes, particularly in the east. They are merchants, scribes, archivists, and — in certain contexts — priests, though their priesthood is organized differently from any other Azhoran religious hierarchy and the title does not translate cleanly. In no region outside Elod are they the dominant population. They are present everywhere and centered nowhere, except for the one city they built for themselves, on the southeast tip of the continent, where the Iberos Sea meets open water.

Most Azhorans, when they think about the Elodi at all, think of them as people with an unusual religion and a tendency to keep careful records. The unusual religion is more interesting than it appears.

---

## The Single Authority

The Elodi do not deny that Azhoran gods, spirits, sacred places, and ritual powers exist. This is the first point outsiders usually misunderstand. An Elodi priest can watch a Mithalenna healing rite succeed, hear a Pyrosi fumarole answer a planting request, or read a Svaleen oath made before Laeron and accept that something real has happened.

The dispute is not existence. The dispute is authority.

Most Azhoran religions acknowledge a unified divine principle behind the gods: the enduring flow, the clear source, the all-witness, the first vastness, the deep before return. This principle is treated as the reality from which many gods and sacred powers arise. The Elodi position is sharper. To them the unified principle is not a background, not a philosophical reconciliation, and not a polite way to let many cults coexist. It is a single sovereign authority. All powers that exist do so beneath it, by its allowance, in rebellion against it, or in ignorance of their proper place.

In ordinary Elodi speech this authority is called **Balog**, though the word carries several layers. Balog is the eternal divine authority. Balog is also the enthroned office at the center of the Old Kingdom. Balog is also, in the period of a reign, the human bearer who becomes that office completely. Elodi theologians distinguish these layers carefully: Balog beyond form, Balog enthroned, Balog incarnate. Non-Elodi listeners often hear contradiction. Elodi listeners hear necessary precision.

This makes Elodi religion neither simple monotheism nor ordinary Azhoran polytheism. It is exclusive sovereignty. Other powers may be real; they are not rightful objects of ultimate loyalty. To pour wine to Thareth for courage may be tolerated by an Elodi merchant living abroad as a social courtesy. To bind oneself to Thareth is error. To ask Mithalenna for aid in childbirth may be understandable human desperation. To take her initiation is submission to an unauthorized branch of power. To swear by Laeron in a Solis court may be legally necessary. To believe Laeron's clarity stands beside Balog's authority as an equal truth is, to Elodi doctrine, false.

This disagreement has made the Elodi both persecuted and persecuting.

In most Azhoran cities, civic religion is part of citizenship. Refusal to sacrifice can look like refusal to belong. Elodi quarters have been attacked after floods, plagues, bad harvests, military defeats, and other crises in which a city's majority decided that the gods had been insulted by insufficient unity. Elodi archives preserve lists of smashed threshold lamps, burned scripture boxes, forced libations, expulsions from market rights, and children pressured to participate in local rites at school or apprenticeship.

When Elodi institutions have power, they can be equally hard. In Elod, public worship of non-Elodi gods is forbidden in the inner districts around the Threshold. Foreign merchants in the harbor quarter may keep private household images, but they may not process with them through the old streets or ring public bells for them. Elodi families discovered maintaining Thareth cups, Mithalenna bowls, Svaleen sea images, or local ancestor masks inside the Elodi ritual calendar have been subject to correction, exclusion from certain communal records, and in severe historical cases expulsion from the protected districts. The Elodi word used in the records is usually "straightening." Their neighbors use harsher words.

The pattern is old: outside Elod, Elodi communities ask to be left alone by civic cults that consider refusal dangerous; inside Elod, non-Elodi and syncretic households ask to be left alone by priests who consider religious mixture dangerous. Each side can describe itself as defending sacred order. Each side can point to injuries. Neither side is inventing the conflict.

## The Old Kingdom

In Elodi scripture, the place their ancestors came from is called the Old Kingdom. In Elodi ceremonial speech, it is called *Erath-Kol* — the First Land. Most Azhoran scholars, when they have bothered to engage with Elodi texts, have treated the Old Kingdom as mythology: a structured founding narrative of the type that many cultures maintain, more meaningful than literal.

The Elodi do not agree with this assessment. Their priesthood holds that the Old Kingdom is real, was real, and probably still is real — that their ancestors departed from it and arrived eventually here, and that the structure of the place is preserved in their texts with the precision of testimony rather than the elaboration of myth.

Whether they are right is a question that Azhoran scholarship has not resolved. The Iberos Sea lies between Azhora and the landmass beyond it, and while Azhoran navigators have crossed it, they have not crossed it far enough or systematically enough to reach anything resembling the Old Kingdom's described location — somewhere deep in the east, at a junction of three great landmasses, in terrain so vast and so old that the Elodi texts describe it as *the place where the world decided what it was going to be*.

### Structure

The Old Kingdom as described in Elodi scripture is a civilization of concentric zones, each larger than the last, each more accessible.

**The Outer Wall** is the outermost feature: a structure of such scale that Elodi texts describe it in terms that invite skepticism. It runs for what the texts call *verath-kael* — a unit of measure that does not translate precisely but appears to mean "a distance you can walk in a season, if you walk every day." Along its full extent, without towers or gates or passage. The Outer Wall is the edge of civilization, in Elodi understanding. Outside it lies what the texts call the unordered lands — not wicked, not empty, but not organized in the way the Old Kingdom is organized. The Outer Wall was built to define a boundary, not to exclude anyone in particular.

**The Outer Kingdom** is the territory between the Outer Wall and the Inner Wall. Most of the Old Kingdom's population lives here. It is described as a land of cities and trade roads and managed farmland, ordered by a complex hierarchy of local governorship that the texts treat as too familiar to explain in detail — the same way a person describing their own country would not explain that it has roads. The Outer Kingdom is where commerce happens, where the majority of life is lived, where most travelers who reach the Old Kingdom from outside would spend their time.

**The Inner Wall** is smaller than the Outer Wall and more ornate. Elodi texts describe its gates — there are several — as places of elaborate ceremony. Passing through the Inner Wall is not simply a matter of geography. It requires documentation, sponsorship, and in some cases waiting periods measured in seasons. The Inner Wall separates the administrative heart of the kingdom from the rest, and it takes this function seriously.

**The Inner Kingdom** is the territory inside the Inner Wall but outside the shore of the Sea of Balog. It is described as quieter than the Outer Kingdom, more carefully tended, its cities smaller and more deliberate. The priesthood of the Old Kingdom's religion is centered here. Much of the significant scholarship and institutional memory of the civilization is housed here. Elodi texts treat the Inner Kingdom the way you would treat a place where serious things happen quietly.

**The Sea of Balog** is the inland sea that occupies the center of the Inner Kingdom. It takes its name from Balog — in the Elodi faith, both the title and the nature of the Emperor. As Egyptian pharaohs were understood to be divine rather than merely ruling by divine right, Balog is not a man who holds a sacred office but a god who inhabits a human form for the duration of a reign. The title passes to each successor, who becomes Balog at the moment of coronation in the same way the previous holder was Balog: completely, not symbolically. The sea is named after him in the same way a temple is named after the god it houses.

The sea connects to the wider ocean through several narrow straits — Elodi texts name three — that are deep enough for large vessels but narrow enough that they can be monitored and controlled. It is not small. It is large enough to have its own weather patterns, its own navigation traditions, its own ports and fishing cultures along its shores.

Its notable feature is the current. The Sea of Balog has a circular current system — the texts call it *verath-balog*, the pull of Balog — that moves water clockwise around its interior and creates prevailing winds that reinforce this rotation. Whether this is geography or divine will is a question the Elodi faith treats as unanswerable, and probably a false distinction. A vessel entering through one of the straits will find itself gradually drawn inward, toward the center of the rotation. Experienced sailors of the Inner Kingdom know how to cross this current obliquely, to ride it partway and depart before it strengthens. The point at which departure becomes difficult — where the current has enough force that crossing it requires more than the ship has — is called in the texts *the listening point*: the moment at which the sea has you, not you the sea.

Beyond the listening point, Elodi texts say, ships travel to the Central Island. The texts also note that ships which travel to the Central Island rarely return. This is described without judgment. Balog's domain is not designed for visitors.

**The Central Island** is where Balog resides. The texts describe it as fortified in ways they do not specify — either because the details were not known to those who wrote them, or because precision was considered inappropriate, or because the defenses are the kind of thing that resists description. What the texts convey is a sense of depth: the island has been there for longer than the Old Kingdom itself, and Balog has been there for longer than the island, or at least has always been there in the way a force is always there, manifesting in different forms across different reigns. Balog is not described as a conqueror or a founder. Balog *is*, in the way that the sea and the current are: a fact that the rest of the structure is organized around, and that does not require a beginning to be real.

## The Departure

Why the Elodi left is the central theological question of their faith and the one that has generated the most disagreement over the most centuries. The texts describe a departure — clearly a departure, clearly definitive, clearly involving a large number of people crossing a significant distance — but the reason for it is stated in language that admits multiple interpretations, and the interpretations have calcified into distinct schools.

### The School of Calamity

The oldest interpretation, by textual evidence, holds that the departure was caused by some form of catastrophe in the Old Kingdom — a natural disaster, a plague, a famine, a collapse of the structures that sustained life in some part of the Outer Kingdom. This school points to passages in the texts that describe the sky darkening, rivers moving differently, and the ground becoming unreliable. These passages, Calamity scholars argue, are eyewitness description, not metaphor. Their ancestors left because the place became unlivable, at least temporarily and in at least some regions, and they went far enough that returning was not practical before the generations that remembered the original home had died.

This interpretation is the most comfortable for Azhorans who engage with it, because it requires no assumption of intention on the part of the Old Kingdom — no emperor ordering anyone to leave, no divine directive — just people doing what people do when conditions deteriorate: moving.

### The School of Fracture

A significant minority position holds that the departure was political: that a conflict within the Old Kingdom — about succession, about doctrine, about how the Inner Kingdom related to the Outer Kingdom — drove a population of people to exit the situation entirely. The texts describe dispute. They describe distance between groups of people who had previously been unified. They describe a departure that was chosen rather than forced.

Fracture scholars note that the description of the departure in the texts does not sound like a disaster response. People in the texts make decisions. They gather things. They organize themselves. This is not the behavior of people fleeing a volcano. This is the behavior of people who have decided that where they are is no longer where they should be, and who have had time to think about it.

The implication — that the Old Kingdom still exists, and that the Elodi are descended from a schismatic minority — is not comfortable for those within the faith who find comfort in the image of a homeland preserved. It suggests the homeland is preserved and simply does not include them.

### The School of Purpose

The smallest and most theologically ambitious school holds that the departure was directed: that the divine structure of the Old Kingdom's religion required a people to go out beyond the Outer Wall, into the unordered lands, to carry something outward. This school does not specify what was to be carried — the texts they cite use a word that translates approximately as *seed-knowledge* — but holds that the Elodi are not exiles or refugees but an appointed diaspora. Their dispersion through Azhora is not accident. It is function.

This school is in the minority partly because it is the most demanding interpretation — it requires the most of those who believe it — and partly because it tends to attract a certain type of person who is not always easy to live near.

The three schools are not in open conflict. They share the texts and the practices and the architecture of the faith. Most Elodi have a mild preference for one reading without considering the others heretical. The argument recurs across centuries not because it is unresolvable but because it is interesting, and the Elodi, as a people, are drawn to interesting questions.

## The City of Elod

On the southeast peninsula of Azhora, where the land narrows to a point between the Iberos Sea to the north and the open southern ocean, sits Elod: the one city the Elodi built for themselves on this continent.

It was not built quickly. The oldest districts are several hundred years old; the city as a whole has the layered character of a place that accreted slowly, district by district, each section reflecting the priorities of the generation that raised it. The harbor district is practical and weathered, its architecture functional, stone cut to survive the wind off the southern sea. The inner districts are more deliberate — several generations of careful building, planned around a central precinct that functions as the faith's main archive and ceremonial center.

The central precinct is called the Threshold. This is partly architectural — the building is organized around a series of successive interior spaces, each more enclosed and more silent than the last, designed to produce a felt movement from outside to inside — and partly theological. The Threshold is where the texts are kept. The oldest texts, in the oldest form of the Elodi language, are in the innermost room. They are not secret, exactly; they are accessible to those with the training to read them and a reason to do so. But they are not casual reading either, and the architecture reinforces this.

The city's population is mixed in the way of all port cities, but the Elodi majority gives it a different character than Nyross or the other Iberos trading settlements. The Threshold and its surrounding districts are run according to Elodi religious time — a calendar that does not match the standard Azhoran one, organized around commemorations and observances that reference events in a history most Azhorans do not share. Traders who spend time in Elod learn quickly which days are quiet days and which are not.

The harbor is busy. Elod is the easternmost significant port on Azhora's south coast, and ships heading to or from the Iberos Sea's southern passages stop here. The Elodi are careful archivists, and the harbor master's records at Elod are the most complete continuous ship logs on the eastern coast — something that scholars from the Caeras Valley Academy have noted and that several have made the journey to consult.

## The Elodi Language

The language spoken in Elod and among Elodi communities throughout Azhora is unrelated to any of the five documented Azhoran language families. This is not controversial — a competent linguist listening to it for an hour will recognize that it shares neither the phonological profile nor the grammatical structure of Mittoli, Pyrosi, Moreshi, Bouéni, or Grassic. It came from somewhere else.

Elodi scholars describe their language as descended from the administrative speech of the Outer Kingdom, which they call *Koleth* — the common tongue, as opposed to the more elevated registers used in the Inner Kingdom and on the Central Island. Whether *Koleth* was always a single unified language or a family of related dialects is debated. What survived in Azhora was shaped by the crossing and by contact with Azhoran languages, and the modern Elodi spoken language has borrowed vocabulary heavily from East Pyrosi and East Mittoli in the centuries since.

The scriptural language is different. The oldest texts at the Threshold are written in a form of Elodi that differs from the modern spoken language as much as a very old document differs from current speech — recognizably the same lineage but requiring specialized training to read. The priesthood spends years on it. The scholarly value of this preserved form has been noted by outside linguists, who have also noted that the Elodi priesthood's interest in making it available for comparative analysis has so far been limited.

## Among Other Azhorans

The Elodi are perceived across Azhora as reliable, meticulous, somewhat private, and attached to a religion that most Azhorans find either charming or exhausting depending on whether they have had to negotiate a contract with an Elodi merchant on a quiet day.

The Old Kingdom is treated as mythology everywhere outside Elod, and in some parts of Elod. The standard Mittoli-speaking academic view is that the texts describe a real migration from somewhere to the east — this much the historical evidence supports — and that the specific geography of the Old Kingdom is elaborated tradition rather than preserved memory. The question of what is actually east of the Iberos Sea, far enough east that Azhoran navigation has not reached it, is the question that would resolve this, and Azhoran navigation has not resolved it.

There is a word in the Iberos coastal trading vocabulary — *canon* — that appears in some accounts to mean a landmass reported on the far eastern horizon, seen in conditions of unusual atmospheric clarity. Elodi priests, when this is raised with them, do not claim that *canon* is a glimpse of the Old Kingdom's coast. They tend, instead, to go quiet in a way that could mean several things.

The Caeras Valley Academy has no formal position on Elodi historical claims. Several Academy scholars have corresponded with the Threshold's archive. The correspondence has been productive on questions of eastern maritime navigation and the history of Iberos Sea trade routes. On questions of what lies further east, it has been courteous and uninformative.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Elodi are a diaspora people with communities throughout Azhora; Elod, on Azhora's southeast peninsula, is the only city where they form a majority.
- Elodi religion differs from most Azhoran traditions because it treats the unified divine principle as a single sovereign authority, not merely the shared source behind many legitimate gods.
- The Elodi acknowledge that other powers may be real, but deny that they possess rightful ultimate authority. This has made them both targets of persecution in pluralist cities and persecutors of syncretic or non-Elodi practice where Elodi institutions control public space.
- Their religion preserves detailed memory of a place called the Old Kingdom (*Erath-Kol* in ceremonial speech), which most Azhorans treat as mythology.
- The Old Kingdom is described in scripture as a concentric civilization: Outer Wall → Outer Kingdom → Inner Wall → Inner Kingdom → Sea of Balog → Central Island, where Balog resides.
- **Balog** is both the name of the sea and the divine title of the Emperor — each Emperor becomes Balog completely upon coronation, as an Egyptian pharaoh was understood to be divine rather than merely chosen by gods. The sea is named after him as a temple is named after its god.
- The Sea of Balog has a circular current (the *verath-balog*) drawing vessels toward the Central Island; ships must depart before "the listening point" where the current becomes unbeatable; few who reach the island return.
- Why the Elodi left the Old Kingdom is disputed within the faith: the School of Calamity (natural disaster), the School of Fracture (political division), and the School of Purpose (appointed diaspora) each hold textual support and distinct practical implications.
- The Elodi language is unrelated to any Azhoran language family. The scriptural form is archaic enough to require specialized training to read.
- The city of Elod maintains the most complete ship logs on Azhora's eastern coast. Its central religious and archival precinct is called the Threshold.
