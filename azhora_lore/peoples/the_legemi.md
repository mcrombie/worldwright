# The Legemi

The Legemi are the people of the Legemum Peninsula, and the first thing to understand about them is that there is no single Legemi tradition in the historical sense — the peninsula has absorbed several waves of settlement across recorded history, and the population is a composite visible in the physical variation from valley to valley, audible in the dialect differences between mining settlements and coastal fishing communities, and legible in the inconsistency of inheritance customs between the peninsula's eastern and western halves. What makes them a distinct culture is not common origin but common circumstance: the same hills, the same rain, the same difficult coast, and several centuries of shared practical necessity working those conditions together.

The Avites of Ascarth find the Legemi's indifference to glory approximately as alien as Legemi visitors find Avite bronze cladding on public buildings. The Legemi tend to point out that their granaries are full, which they regard as a more durable achievement than most.

---

## The Three Economies

The peninsula runs on three interlocked activities that require different skills, different temperaments, and different relationships to the land, and that meet twice a year at the Haur — the neutral highland meadow where the Council convenes — to trade with each other and argue about coordination.

**Agriculture** in the Legemum river valleys is organized through guild structures that are regional rather than craft-based: each guild covers a specific valley or district, maintaining the communal infrastructure — drainage, seed stores, milling rights — that no individual farm can sustain alone. The elected guild leader, called the *hannar*, arbitrates water rights and field boundary disputes, sets collective sale prices when the guild sells in bulk, and is personally responsible for keeping the seed stores viable. A *hannar* whose seed stores fail in a bad winter carries this for the rest of their life. The guild memory is long and the crops are good. The soils are heavy clay, difficult to work initially and extremely productive once broken. Several root vegetable varieties cultivated here have no Mittoli names and predate the period of written records.

**Mining** in the interior hills is the peninsula's most formally organized sector. The miners' guilds maintain written records of seam locations, extraction rates, and ore quality going back further than any other Legemi institution. They have their own protocol for when a passage becomes unsafe and must be sealed, their own underground marker system, and a specific obligation to the dead: miners who die in the workings are interred near the entrance of the passage where they fell, their names added to the guild record in an ink made from mineral residue of the seam they were working. The guild record is not a document shown to outside visitors.

The hills produce tin, copper in smaller quantities, and good building stone. The tin is the significant one. Every king of Ascarth who has considered where their bronze comes from eventually arrives at the same answer. The Legemum tin price is a number no serious Ascarth strategist ignores. A Legemum tin price spike suggests stockpiling; the Nyloni harbor records have tracked this as an early indicator of Ascarth military readiness for generations, without advertising that they do so.

**Fishing** on the western ocean coast is the most culturally distinct sector and the one with the strongest claim to a continuous tradition that predates the various settlement waves that produced the current population. The open-ocean fish that run off Legemum's exposed western cliffs are larger and less predictable than sheltered-sea species, and the boats required to catch them in Atlantic swells are beamier, heavier, built for endurance rather than speed. A Legemi fishing boat is not beautiful, by Iberos standards. It is very difficult to sink. The coastal villages maintain their own names for the winds, their own lunar calendar for predicting fish-runs, and their own set of taboos around what is said before a boat launches. They trade their catch inland and otherwise maintain a self-sufficiency that the farming communities respect and the mining settlements find excessive.

## Political Structure

Legemum has no king and has not had one in any period of its documented history. The consequence of geography: the peninsula has no natural center, no port large enough to anchor a maritime power, no agricultural heartland dominant enough to compel the others. The closest thing to political center is the **Council of Legemum** — a seasonal assembly, twice yearly, at the **Haur** (the Legemi word for it means "the flat place"), a wide naturally level meadow in the hills at the peninsula's center, equidistant from the three population areas.

The Council does not legislate. It arbitrates disputes that have escalated beyond local resolution, sets collective positions on external trade negotiations, and maintains shared infrastructure — roads, bridges, the Three Mouths anchorages on the western coast. Decisions require something approaching consensus; bare majorities are considered insufficient because a bare majority leaves too many people with no obligation to comply. This makes the Council slow. The Legemi are aware of this and regard it as a reasonable price for decisions that hold.

The Haur is used for nothing else. A market grows up around it during Council weeks, and the market is, in practice, as important as the Council: the period when all three parts of the peninsula's economy are in the same place at the same time.

## The Ascarth Relationship

The narrow bay between Legemum's eastern shore and the Ascarth Peninsula is too short to function as a serious barrier — Legemi and Avite fishing boats cross it; Legemi tin merchants cross in larger vessels; Avite bronze traders come the other way. The two peninsulas have been in continuous commercial contact for as long as their respective histories extend. They are not friends.

The Avites find the Legemi useful and occasionally aggravating in the specific way that a necessary supplier who will not be pushed around is aggravating. The Legemi find the Avites impressive and consistently alarming — the Ascarth kings have, on four recorded occasions, concluded that controlling Legemum's tin directly would be preferable to purchasing it, and on four recorded occasions have discovered that Legemum, while it does not have much of a warrior tradition, has terrain that rewards defenders, sufficient agricultural stores to outlast a siege, and a Council prepared to destroy its own mines rather than yield them intact. This last point was demonstrated once, partially, when a section of the primary tin workings was flooded in advance of an Avite campaign that subsequently did not happen. The flooding was not repaired for six years. The Avites noticed.

The current arrangement is commercial: Legemum sells tin at a price set by Council negotiation, in exchange for bronze goods and the implicit protection that comes from being the supplier of something Ascarth cannot produce itself. Neither party is comfortable with this. It works.

## Character

The Legemi are a practical people in the specific sense that a culture organized around three difficult economies — farming clay soil, mining deep seams, fishing exposed ocean — tends to be: skilled at what they do, unimpressed by difficulty, and not inclined toward elaboration where straightforwardness will serve. They are not unfriendly; the guild culture produces dense mutual obligation and genuine community. But they do not have the Avites' heroic elaboration, the Mittoli philosophical tradition, or the Svaleen commercial philosophy. They have working methods and they have the results of those methods, and they find the results speak adequately.

The peninsula's relationship to outside powers has been consistently shaped by having something that outside powers want and being willing to destroy it rather than surrender it. This is not a negotiating posture. The flooded mine is evidence that it is not a posture.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Legemi are a composite people of the Legemum Peninsula, united by circumstance rather than common origin: the same hills, the same rain, the same difficult coast.
- Three interlocked economies — valley agriculture organized through guild *hannar* leadership, interior hill mining with formal guild records and obligations to the dead, open-ocean fishing with its own calendar and taboos — meet twice yearly at the Haur for the Council.
- The Council does not legislate; it arbitrates and coordinates, requiring something approaching consensus, which makes it slow and makes its decisions hold.
- The peninsula produces tin, the essential ingredient in Ascarth bronze, giving the Legemi leverage over the regional military economy that they maintain through commercial negotiation and the demonstrated willingness to destroy the mines rather than yield them.
- The Legemi are practical in the specific way of people managing three difficult economies: skilled, unimpressed by difficulty, not inclined toward elaboration.
- A Legemi fishing boat is very difficult to sink.
