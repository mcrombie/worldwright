# The Endevorfolk

The people of the Endevor hills are pastoral now, and have been pastoral long enough that most outsiders take this as the whole description. It is not. The fell-sheep pastures and the wet valley floors and the reliable inns on the road to the Bay of Lol are the surface of a country whose ridgelines are covered in the burial mounds of kings — kings who presided, for two hundred and fifty years, over the most extensive gold-based political authority in northern Azhora. The Endevorfolk know this. The Vornheld remember it in full. The hills still have the shaft-mouths of the mines in them. The streams still carry trace gold in the pan. The Vel-Eld poem is recited twice a year at Veld's barrow in the Stroven valley floor, because the thing the tradition says will return has not returned yet.

---

## The Fell-Sheep and the Hills

The characteristic animal of the Endevor hills — the fell-sheep — has been here as long as any record extends. It is compact, short-legged, deeply shaggy, and produces more lanolin per fleece than any comparable northern breed, making the wool naturally water-resistant in a climate that is wet for most of the year. The fell-sheep lives its entire year outside on the upper moorland and heath, and it comes down of its own accord before weather the shepherds have not yet noticed. Where the sheep are on a given morning is information about the day.

Endevor wool is traded throughout northern Azhora because it performs in conditions that other wool cannot. The Bay of Lol fishing communities buy it for sea gear. The Ganoss upland clans, who have their own animals, trade for Endevor spun goods when they can get them. The vocabulary Endevor herders have developed for fleece condition and grazing behavior — fifteen or more terms not appearing in any other Northern Mittoli variety — cannot be traced to any known source outside Endevor itself. Whether these are preserved substrate words, archaic Mittoli survivals, or internal innovations, the herders find the Academy's question academic in a register that implies "academic" is not a compliment.

## The Vornheld

The Vornheld are the keepers of Endevor's epic tradition — the *Eld-Keth* of the mine-kings, the *Vel-Keth* of the decline, and the *Vel-Eld* of what will return. The name means "those who hold the old ones," from *vorn* (north, the ancestor-direction) and *held* (hold, keeping). What they hold is not merely a collection of poems but a navigable structure: metrically complex pieces, and the web of cross-reference between them, held in reproducible form by practitioners who have developed the capacity to carry both simultaneously. Knowing the poems is not knowing the tradition. The tradition is the map between them.

The Vornheld are present at the stone-shelters when significant gatherings require it, providing testimony in the form of recitation. When a family asserts a right to a stretch of hill pasture based on an ancestor's service to a mine-king three hundred years ago, the Vornheld knows whether the claim is in the tradition. The Stroven record-keepers hold land agreements; the Vornheld hold lineages. The two archives do not duplicate each other.

Seven lineages are currently active, each associated with a valley system. The most consequential current disagreement — between the lineage of Keld (the first-opener) and the lineage of Orrven (the peak) — is a dispute about whether origin or achievement is more essential to the tradition's meaning. Everyone in Stroven understands that this Vornheld argument and a longstanding property dispute between the associated valley communities are the same argument in different registers.

## The Eld-Gold

For roughly two hundred and fifty years — the period the tradition calls the *Eld-Gold*, the time of gold — the mine-kings of Endevor were the most significant power in northern Azhora. The mine-kings had followed the eastern streams' placer gold uphill into the deep veins of the Oremindi foothills. Their empire extended past the Bay of Lol, incorporated the Ganoss upland clans and coastal port communities as tributaries, and pressed claims as far as the northern Mittoli margins — the first and last time a northern power came close to bridging the cultural divide the Acorwood maintains.

The tradition names thirty-eight kings. Three stand apart. Keld is the opener: recorded as a beginning rather than an achievement, given the short poem. Orrven is the peak: his *Eld-Keth* poem is a call-and-answer form of eight exchanges, the eighth of which has no answer, and in stone-shelter recitation the second voice's silence is audible to everyone in the circle. Veld is the last: whose name means "return," whose eighteen years of holding together a dissolving empire are described by the tradition as the greatest of the later kings — which is not praise so much as the recognition that the greatest of the later kings could not undo what the geology had done.

The mines declined over generations, each producing less than the last, each still the generation that believed a new vein would open. When it was taken, the taking was over. The tributaries stopped paying. Endevor contracted back to Endevor. The outside world eventually forgot there had been an organizing. No Mittoli scholar wrote down the empire while it existed; the region was too far from the Mittoli world. The Vornheld preserved everything. The barrows corroborate. Most outside visitors see hills and do not read them as texts.

## Stone-Shelters and the Vel-Eld

Disputes between Endevor valley communities are resolved at stone-shelters — old, low, circular dry-laid enclosures set at ridgelines between valleys. They have been the resolution site before and will be again, and their walls have heard settlements and keep them. Their specific and documented physical property: speech spoken inside them at ordinary volume is heard simultaneously and with equal clarity by everyone in the circle, regardless of wind. A resolution reached inside is heard by all parties in the same moment. This is not a formality. It is the physical condition that makes the resolution binding.

The *Vel-Eld* — the renewal poem, the Old Return — is the portion of the tradition that is not about what was but about what will be. It is structured around three signs and a title. The first sign: when the cold water finds what it left behind — the trace gold returning to visible concentration in the eastern streams. The second sign: when the barrows wake before they are opened — someone will witness something at a mound before any physical disturbance, or will arrive in Endevor knowing where the dead kings lie without having been told. The third sign: when the wood speaks what it knows — the Acorwood reference, which practitioners who specialize in the *Vel-Eld* describe as the sign whose meaning is most certain among practitioners and most opaque to outside inquiry.

The title given to the coming renewal is *Vel-Endre* — Returning-Endevor — a condition rather than a person, which has made it perpetually applicable to promising individuals who arrive with northern lineage and apparent significance. The poem is recited in full twice yearly at Veld's barrow, at the winter solstice and the vernal equinox. The solstice recitation draws better attendance. Something in the Endevor character finds it right to say the thing latent in the ground while standing on frozen ground. Not despite the conditions. Because of them.

## Smoke-Reading and the Deepworks

The fell-sheep shepherds in the high Endevor pastures spend long hours watching the Acorwood's western face. The forest occasionally produces smoke from its interior — thin and straight in still air, spreading at canopy height in wind, and a specific category the shepherds call by a substrate word translating as something between "breathing-down" and "the forest taking something in." The Endevorfolk have more observational detail on this behavior than any other northern people, because their pastures position them to watch that face across long hours with nothing pressing them to look elsewhere.

Smoke-reading has two confirmed practical uses: late-summer interior smoke correlates imperfectly but consistently with good mast years; smoke quality predicts weather shifts the way Oremindi cloud formations do. Neither use requires an explanation of what the smoke is. When Academy scholars ask about the cause, they receive accurate information about what the smoke does and a shift to something practical. Cause is for someone with more time and less sheep.

What the Vornheld tradition notes without drawing a conclusion: the breathing-down smoke is most frequently reported above the ground corresponding to the Deepworks — the richest of the ancient mine-workings, opened last, sealed by Veld's decree when workers kept not returning from the deeper levels. The sealing notation is *oss nil-vel-keth*: mark, no return-speech. Other shafts were sealed when they were worked out. The Deepworks were closed *nil-vel-keth*, without the response that would indicate emptiness. The formulation implies something was there to not-answer.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Endevorfolk are the pastoral hill communities of the Endevor valleys, organized around fell-sheep herding and connected by ancient stone-shelters whose acoustic properties make resolutions reached within them binding.
- The Vornheld — seven active lineages — hold the epic tradition in full navigable form; their recitations function as testimony in ancestral disputes, and the land agreements of Stroven's record-keepers and the Vornheld's lineage knowledge are two separate, non-duplicating archives.
- The Eld-Gold period saw the mine-kings of Endevor hold the most extensive political authority in northern Azhora for two hundred and fifty years; the gold is exhausted, the hills are now pastoral, and the ridgelines are covered in barrows.
- The *Vel-Eld* — the renewal poem — describes three signs and a condition called *Vel-Endre* (Returning-Endevor); it is recited twice yearly at Veld's barrow at the winter solstice and vernal equinox.
- The Deepworks were sealed *nil-vel-keth* (no return-speech) rather than as empty exhausted mines — a designation the tradition treats as distinct from ordinary closure.
- Smoke-reading from the Acorwood's western face is a practiced shepherd discipline with two confirmed practical uses; the Vornheld notes without resolution that the relevant smoke concentrates above the Deepworks' approximate location.
