# The Svaleen

The Svaleen are the people of the peninsula that mainland maps sometimes render as *Suval* — a transcription drift that the Svaleen find either amusing or mildly irritating depending on the context, and that confirms their general impression that mainland Mittoli cartographers have spent less time on the Svaleen than the Svaleen have spent on the mainland. They are a Mediterranean people organized into three city-states that have been competing and cooperating under the same Confederation framework for four centuries without the Confederation ever achieving enough authority to make either the competition or the cooperation unnecessary. This outcome — sustained productive tension between rivalry and coordination — is either a structural flaw or the entire point, depending on which Svaleen philosopher you ask, and the Svaleen have philosophers who hold both positions and argue them well.

They are confident about their commercial acumen, mildly contemptuous of people who show up having crossed the mountains and act like they've accomplished something, and genuinely excellent at wine.

---

## The Three Cities

**Solis**, the largest and most institutionally elaborate of the three, is a city of plazas, formal debate, and public argument conducted in buildings constructed for the purpose. Its character is civic in the specific sense: governance takes itself seriously, archives are maintained, libraries exist, and the Conclave sessions that rotate through the three cities are most formally structured when they sit in Solis. One of those libraries is old enough to contain documents in languages no one in the city can currently read. This is considered a mark of distinction rather than a problem.

Solis worships Laeron — a sun-associated deity of civic order and clear light, not a god of war or commerce specifically but a god of things being what they appear to be, of agreements being kept, of the day being legible. The cult is old enough that its theology has become complicated in the way of all old theologies, and the Laeron priesthood produces, consistently, very good jurists.

**Enebreum**, twenty miles south on the same western coast, has been Solis's rival since before either was large enough to make the rivalry interesting, and it is older — the oldest foundations visible in its lower city predate anything in Solis by at least two centuries, and in certain districts predate explanation: foundations cut from stone using techniques current Svaleen builders cannot account for, settled several feet below the current ground level by the slow accumulation of what time does to cities. Enebreum is sharper than Solis in character — more aware of angles, more inclined to calculate what a situation costs before considering what it offers. Its temples are on the ridge, watching rather than presiding.

Enebreum's religion is older than Solis's and less organized — a set of practices and prohibitions associated with a figure the texts call the Grey Warden, who is neither a god of the dead nor a god of the sea but something between them: a power of thresholds, of the moment before decisions are made. The Laeron priesthood of Solis considers this theology primitive. Enebreum produces competent sailors; Solis produces competent jurists. Both find their complement useful enough to maintain the rivalry.

**Imlamdris** is inland, on the spring-fed lake called the Stillwater, and it is the most unusual of the three: the oldest continuously occupied city on the peninsula, without a harbor, oriented not toward the sea but toward the water it sits on. The city faces the lake. Its great hall — a civic and religious institution combined, not primarily a temple but serving several functions — opens on its lakeside face and is closed on its landward side. Visitors arriving overland, which is the only way to arrive at Imlamdris, enter from the back.

The Stillwater Temple maintains the peninsula's most significant astronomical archive — centuries of sky observation accumulated by a tradition that predates the current temple structure. Imlamdris is inland because it is inland, and it can see the stars clearly in the hill country without coastal haze, and it has been doing so with disciplined attention for longer than either coastal city has been architecturally significant. Imlamdris participates in the Conclave when the agenda concerns inland roads and mountain passes. When the agenda is primarily about lighthouse maintenance, it sends its apologies.

## The Confederation

The Confederation does three things with reasonable consistency: it maintains shared lighthouses and navigation markers, operates a common system of weights and measures, and convenes the Conclave twice yearly, where delegates from all three cities argue about everything else. The Conclave has no binding authority except on the lighthouses and the weights. This has been the structure for four centuries and the Confederation has not collapsed, which either proves the structure is sufficient or proves that the three cities need each other enough that the structure's insufficiency doesn't matter.

The commercial vocabulary that the shared weights and measures have produced is visible in how Svaleen merchants conduct transactions anywhere on the Iberos Sea: there is a sequence, a set of steps, that Svaleen commercial culture has refined across four centuries of inter-city practice into something close to a ritual. Traders from other ports learn this sequence quickly because it is reliable and because Svaleen merchants who deviate from it are either very informal or doing something you should pay attention to.

## Commercial Character

To other Azhorans — particularly those from the mainland interior — the Svaleen generally present as confident, somewhat clannish, and mildly amused by anyone who tries to explain how trade works. This assessment is accurate in the specific sense that the Svaleen have been conducting sophisticated maritime commerce since before the Caeras Compact, and mainland commercial advice tends to rediscover things the Confederation worked out centuries ago.

They are pragmatic about religion in a way that outsiders sometimes read as skeptical. It is more precisely described as practical: the gods are real and have their domains and are worth the correct attention in those domains. Whether this requires theological consistency across all three cities is a separate question, and the answer is clearly no, because it has not been achieved in four centuries and the cities are functioning well. The Svaleen do not experience this as a failure.

The wine culture of Svaleen is documented separately in the viticulture file. The short version: Svaleen wine travels on Iberos trade routes because it is genuinely excellent, and the trade in it has carried Thareth's cult — the wine-god who moves everywhere that wine goes — into more corners of Azhora than any organized religious mission has managed. The Svaleen tend to note that the wine precedes the theology, which they find the correct order of operations.

## Among Neighbors

The Svaleen's most distinctive external relationship is with the Elodi community of Elod — the city at the peninsula's southern tip. The Elodi are Confederation members in the narrowest technical sense: they observe the weights and measures, they maintain their lighthouse assignment. They do not attend the Conclave and have not been expected to for approximately a century. The Svaleen have learned, over several generations, that the Elodi are reliable neighbors and intensely private ones, and that asking about what happens inside the Threshold is a social investment with no return. The arrangement is politely unspoken and, by all visible evidence, sufficient.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Svaleen are the people of the southeastern Iberos peninsula, organized in three city-states under a Confederation that has maintained productive tension between competition and cooperation for four centuries without resolving it.
- Solis is the largest, most civic, most institutionally elaborate of the three, associated with Laeron (civic order, clear agreements) and producing very good jurists.
- Enebreum is older, sharper in character, associated with the Grey Warden (thresholds, the moment before decisions), and produces competent sailors.
- Imlamdris is inland, lake-oriented rather than sea-oriented, the oldest continuously occupied city on the peninsula, and maintains the peninsula's most significant astronomical archive.
- The Confederation's common weights and measures have produced a Svaleen commercial practice so consistent it functions as a ritual, identifiable anywhere on the Iberos Sea.
- Svaleen pragmatism about religion — the gods are real, have their domains, require appropriate attention, and do not require theological consistency — is the working approach of a people with three different civic theologies who share the same trade routes.
