# The Orgmala

The people of the Orgmala highland massif call themselves the valley-holders. The name Orgmala is an outsider's word — traceable to Ganun administrative records, a compound meaning something like "closed" with a pejorative suffix — and the people it names do not use it for themselves, and have found that correcting it is less useful than leaving it in place. Outside observers who arrive at the highland's edges looking for the Orgmala will find a rocky massif that does not appear to contain what they are looking for. This is not an accident.

No outside power has entered the Orgmala valleys in documented history.

---

## The Valleys

The Orgmala highland is rocky and forbidding at its exterior — the massif presents faces of bare stone and steep gradient to any approach — but inside it the geology is different. Hidden behind the rock walls, tucked into folds the exterior does not reveal, are the fertile valleys that define the culture. These are not large. They are adequate, which is what matters. Each valley is self-sufficient in the basic requirements: water from the highland springs, enough sheltered ground for crops, pasture for the animals the valley-holder families keep. The fertility is sufficient and private.

The combination — harsh exterior, hidden fertile interior — is the foundational fact of Orgmala life. It is not merely a geographic convenience. It is what the culture has organized itself around for as long as the tradition extends. The valley-holders know every approach to their massif, every visible path and the ones that do not appear to be paths, and they have knowledge of the exterior terrain that no outside observer would develop from any number of crossings because it is knowledge acquired through sustained attention to observation and concealment, not through the natural accumulation of travel.

The substrate layer in the Orgmala linguistic tradition carries an unusually dense vocabulary for observation, concealment, and the reading of terrain. These words do not appear in Northern Mittoli more broadly. Whether they survive from a language spoken in these highlands before Mittoli speakers arrived, or whether they are the Orgmala's own development over long generations of practice, has not been established — no outside linguist has had extended enough access to study it.

---

## Two Languages

The Orgmala maintain two spoken registers that function, in practice, as two languages. The first is the language used with outsiders: a variant of Northern Mittoli, broadly comprehensible to anyone from the surrounding northern regions, unremarkable in its vocabulary, used when trading at Ganun's markets or when encountering travelers in the exterior highland. The second is the language used inside the valleys: the one that carries the dense substrate vocabulary, the observation and concealment terminology, and the internal social register of the valley communities themselves.

The line between them is maintained with precision. The existence of the interior language is not concealed from outsiders who ask; what is not available to outsiders is access to it. A valley-holder speaking to a Ganun merchant uses the first language. The same person speaking to another valley-holder, even in a context where the merchant is present, uses the second. This is not code-switching for deception. It is simply that the two registers serve different purposes, and the purposes do not overlap, and this distinction is not explained to outside observers because explaining it would involve using the second language in a context where the first language applies.

## The Raiding Tradition

The Orgmala have a raiding tradition. It is noted in every record of the northern regional history that touches the highland, and it is consistently described in ways that set it apart from other northern raiding cultures: the raids are not characterized by speed or volume or the dramatic violence of the Bay of Lol's coastal operations, but by the quality of preparation that precedes them.

An Orgmala raid is preceded by a reconnaissance that outside communities, once they have experienced it, learn to read the signs of. Travelers who pass through the exterior highland several weeks or months before a raid has occurred are, in retrospect, understood to have been observed. The preparations are patient. The execution is precise. What is taken is what was identified in advance, not whatever could be grabbed. The raiders arrive knowing the layout and the staffing and the response time of whoever they are visiting. They are gone before the response is organized.

The economy of the valley communities is self-sufficient. The Orgmala do not raid for subsistence; their valleys provide that. Outside scholars who have concluded from this that the raiding must therefore be cultural rather than economic are correct, and have not thereby answered the question of what cultural purpose it serves. The valley-holders do not discuss this with outside observers.

## The Captives

Every northern raiding culture has protocols for captives: ransom arrangements, exchange terms, the specific mechanisms by which a taken person returns to their community and at what price. This is understood and practiced across the region with the same functional reliability as the terms for livestock exchange.

The Orgmala do not ransom captives.

No account exists — in any northern record, in any Ganun trading-house log, in any coastal archive — of a captive taken by the Orgmala returning to their community of origin. It is not that the ransom terms are prohibitive or that negotiations fail. It is that the category does not appear to exist. People taken in Orgmala raids do not come back. The communities they came from know this. The communities whose territory borders the Orgmala massif have organized their defensive habits accordingly.

What happens to captives inside the valleys has not been established from outside the valleys. The absence of returning captives is noted in the records without explanation because no explanation has been obtained. The Orgmala, when the subject is raised by outside interlocutors in the first language, decline to address it. Not with hostility. With the specific absence of engagement that indicates the question has been heard and placed into a category where it will not be answered in this language.

Whether this is a different practice from what outside observers assume, or whether outside observers assume correctly and the difference is in what it serves, is unknown. Both possibilities have been considered by the communities who have to live near it. Neither produces a satisfying answer. They have adjusted their practices and moved on.

## Among Other Northern Peoples

The Orgmala participate in the northern trading network from a position of chosen partial engagement. They appear at Ganun's Ondrath market with goods from the highland — specific fiber, certain high-altitude plant products, occasionally animals — and conduct their commerce in the first language with the efficiency of people who know what they want and have already calculated the terms. They are not unfriendly in this context. They are contained in a way that experienced northern traders recognize and work within.

The relationship between the Orgmala and the surrounding regions is shaped by the fact that the raiding creates a kind of deterrent that the valley-holders do not particularly advertise and do not need to. Communities that have experienced an Orgmala raid and communities that have not but know communities that have arrive at similar conclusions about the appropriate relationship with the highland massif: commerce at the established points, no incursion into the exterior highland without specific purpose, and a mental note about what the reconnoitering strangers from two months ago were doing there.

Ganun's records have the most sustained documentation of Orgmala presence, accumulated through the trading contacts at Ondrath across many generations. The records note trade goods, prices, frequency of visits, and the consistent observation that the Orgmala traders' knowledge of current Ondrath prices is always accurate and always current, in a way that implies access to information that the observed trading frequency does not account for.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Orgmala are the valley-holders of a rocky highland massif whose forbidding exterior conceals fertile self-sufficient valleys that no outside power has entered in documented history.
- The name "Orgmala" is an outsider's term from Ganun records meaning "closed" with a pejorative suffix; the people call themselves valley-holders and use the external name without correcting it.
- Two languages: an outsider-facing Northern Mittoli variant and an interior-valley language with dense substrate vocabulary for observation, concealment, and terrain-reading; the line between them is maintained precisely.
- The raiding tradition is notable for the quality of its reconnaissance and preparation, not for its violence; raids are executed with advance knowledge of layouts, staffing, and response times.
- The Orgmala do not ransom captives — no record exists of a captive taken in an Orgmala raid returning to their community of origin, and no explanation has been obtained or offered.
- Their economy is self-sufficient, making the raiding cultural rather than economic in function; what function it serves has not been established from outside the valleys.
