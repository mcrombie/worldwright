# The Telemon

The Telemon are a highland warrior people in the specific sense that the word sometimes means and more often does not. Not people who fight when pressed. Not people who celebrate fighting as a virtue in the abstract. People for whom the cultivation of combat is the primary work of a life, carried on with the systematic seriousness that a farming culture gives to agriculture — the same expectation that it will be done correctly, the same patience with process, the same judgment that shortcuts now become failures later. The distinction matters, and the Telemon would make it if you asked. They tend to assume you already know it, which is one of the ways they miscalibrate for outsiders.

They are available for hire. They have never been successfully conquered. These two facts are not unrelated.

---

## The Warrior Tradition

Training begins at seven. The early years are not weapons training — weapons come later. The early years are endurance: cold water, long distances, lean provisions, night navigation, extended silence. The premise is architectural: a warrior who cannot manage their body has no use for technique. Technique is taught once the body is trustworthy. This sequence is not chosen for its discipline value or its character-formation effect, though it has both. It is chosen because a warrior whose technique is better than their endurance will reach a point in a long engagement where the technique fails and there is nothing underneath it.

The formal training period ends at approximately seventeen or eighteen with a solitary test — several days alone in the highland, in the hardest season. No witnesses. No help given. Coming back is the achievement. The account given afterward is expected to be accurate and brief.

After this, a warrior belongs to a band: twenty to forty fighters who train together, fight together, and who will recite each other's deeds at death. The band is the primary social unit of Telemon life. Marriage, household, kinship all exist alongside the band and do not supersede it. The band is the audience whose judgment matters most. There is no class of professional rememberers equivalent to the Avite *veth*. The band is its own record. The dead are carried in a recitation spoken at the three seasonal ceremonies — names, deeds, manner of death — performed for the band, not preserved for outside study. Scholars who have sought access have found the request declined with the consistency of policy.

The warrior tradition does not formally distinguish between men and women. Both train. The mercenary bands that go abroad are predominantly men — not by rule but by the household calculation that women's defensive presence in the highland during a mercenary campaign's absence has higher value than their presence in a foreign field. When the highland is invaded, no such calculation applies. The accounts of failed invasions make this clear.

## The Mercenary Practice

Telemonia does not trade in goods. It trades in fighters, and the practice is formalized and precise. A power seeking Telemon fighters sends a representative to the nearest border market site and states their need. The Telemon evaluate. The representative may wait days or be met quickly; the criteria are not explained. The evaluation includes: the nature of the conflict, the terrain, the duration, the payment, and the command structure. The last point is significant. Telemon bands serve under their own leadership on the field. They receive strategic instructions and coordinate with allied forces. Tactical command is the band leader's. Employers who cannot accept this are told so, and the negotiation ends.

The terms of a Telemon contract are specific. Duration. Conditions. Payment in goods or metal rather than political arrangements. And the departure clause. Telemon bands leave when the contract ends. Not when the war ends. Not when the employer's situation has improved. When the contract ends. Employers who allowed their planning to depend on Telemon presence beyond the agreed period have created a problem that the Telemon find their problem.

Payment is in material that improves life in the highland: metal goods, specific agricultural materials the highland doesn't produce, worked items of value to a warrior's household. Political agreements, land grants, titles, and positions are refused. What the Telemon want from mercenary service is stuff, not standing.

The reputation is consistent enough across generations to function as currency. A Telemon contract means what it says. They will not be bribed to change sides. They will not be manipulated through fear of losing them — they leave when the contract ends, regardless. They will not claim to have done what they did not do. Among mercenary forces, this is more remarkable than it sounds.

## The Defense

When Telemonia is invaded, the Telemon stop being the mercenaries that neighboring powers have watched in foreign service and become something else. The formal discipline is still present. The effectiveness is higher. And the terrain — which the invaders have moved through with some discomfort on their way in — becomes an instrument. The passes seal. The valleys produce defenders from positions that were not visible before. Supply lines find themselves attacked by small groups that vanish. Encampments that seemed secure are not.

The Telemon do not pursue invaders beyond their own borders when they withdraw. They stop at the border and return to the highland. This has been misread, on more than one occasion, as weakness or exhaustion — a signal that a second campaign is worth attempting. The accounts of second campaigns are the most detailed available in Azhoran military records, because commanders who fail twice write about it more than commanders who succeed. The second campaigns share certain features: the defense was harder, the Telemon appeared to have prepared specifically for the approach used, and the withdrawal was achieved at greater cost than the first attempt.

What the Telemon know is the terrain that makes them unbeatable, and they know that it stops at their own border. A Telemon army pursuing a retreating force onto coastal lowland is away from their advantage. They understand this and act on it. The border markets reopen eventually.

## Language and Governance

The Telemon speak *Kellith* — their own name for it. It has not been systematically studied by outside linguists because outside linguists have not been allowed in. What can be gathered from border-market contact and soldier accounts is that its grammar differs from Mittoli, it shares some sound patterns with older Pyrosi dialects, and it has specific vocabulary for terrain, weather, and physical exertion that has no equivalent in any coastal language. Border translators exist; they are Telemon people who have chosen that function, and their translations are understood by both parties to be selective.

Telemonia is governed by a king whose authority requires both hereditary claim and combat capacity. A king who cannot fight leads ceremonially; real tactical command passes to the band-leader council. The council is the governing body in practice. They arbitrate disputes, manage border-market arrangements, decide whether to accept mercenary contracts, and form the collective that makes any decision requiring coordination above the band level. There is no written law code. The customs are old enough to be known. Disputes are resolved by council decision. This would be inadequate for a complex commercial society. For a warrior-band pastoral society in defensible highland terrain, it is sufficient.

## Among Other Peoples

The Avites and the Telemon are the two warrior cultures of the Iberos region. Their mutual assessment is the guarded respect that develops between people who recognize quality in a domain they both take seriously. The Avites find the Telemon's indifference to glory philosophically incorrect. The Telemon find the Avite elaboration — the epics, the bronze aesthetics, the public performance of heroic identity — an expenditure of energy on display that could be spent on capability. Neither has said this to the other's face in productive terms. When they have fought in shared employers' campaigns, the operational relationship is effective and the social relationship is minimal. They fight well together and eat separately.

The Galans are the Telemon's most important outside relationship. Galan negotiators broker most of the mercenary contracts and conduct the bulk of the border-market trade. The expertise required — understanding how the Telemon think about obligation, terrain, and what a contract actually is — is not easily transferred and is one of the more specific commercial assets the city of Gala holds. The Telemon, for their part, find the Galans reliable in the specific sense of: they know the rules and they follow them. This is the Telemon version of a strong recommendation.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Telemon are a highland people for whom combat cultivation is the primary work of a life, organized around warrior bands as the primary social unit.
- Training begins at seven with endurance before technique; the formal period ends with a solitary highland test whose account is expected to be accurate and brief.
- The mercenary practice is precise and formalized: fixed contracts, tactical autonomy, payment in goods not status, departure on schedule regardless of the employer's wishes.
- The defense of Telemonia has never been successfully overcome; the second campaigns have been more costly than the first, and the Telemon do not pursue beyond their own borders because they know their advantage stops there.
- Their language *Kellith* has not been studied by outside linguists because outside linguists have not been admitted.
- The Galans broker most Telemon mercenary contracts and hold the specific expertise required to do so; the Telemon find this satisfactory.
