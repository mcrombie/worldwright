# The Selemi

The Selemi are the people of the island of Selemis, and they are the most widely traveled people on the continent, and they carry this fact lightly. A Selemi merchant in Nyross or Nylon or the Svaleen ports or the Moroshé coastal towns has usually been in several of those places in the same season. They have adjusted their manner to each without apparent effort. They know the local preoccupation, the current price controversy, which families are rising and which declining, the unresolved disputes that shape everything without being directly discussed. They learn this wherever they go because they are genuinely interested in other people's situations, and because comprehensive attention is both a personal trait of the Selemi and the foundation of everything their civilization does.

This makes them the most successful maritime traders on Azhora. It also makes them, to the powers they trade with, something that falls between charming and difficult to trust. Both assessments are accurate, and the Selemi find neither surprising.

---

## The Island and Its Position

Selemis sits at the southern mouth of the Iberos Sea, across a narrow channel from the Ascarth Peninsula. Every ship moving between the Iberos Sea and the open southern ocean passes within reach of its harbor. This is not a coincidence that the Selemi created; it is a geographic fact that their predecessors settled and that successive generations have converted into an entire theory of power.

The city fills the island's crescent harbor from headland to headland — dense, layered, built over itself for long enough that the oldest foundations in the lower harbor district are several stories underground. The harbor is the city. Everything else — the residential districts climbing the interior hills, the warehouses, the foreign merchants' quarter districts, the archive building with its copper roof gone green — is organized around and in service to the harbor. A Selemi who cannot give you a clear account of the current tide schedule, the wind state, and what ships are in port knows they are going to hear about this.

The channel between Selemis and Ascarth is narrow but not trivial. The Selemi have positioned their harbor fortifications to make the crossing expensive and stationed their fastest ships where they can intercept anything moving toward the island. These defenses have not prevented Avite conquest, historically. They have made each conquest expensive enough that it has eventually been abandoned.

## The Outpost Network

The Selemi do not exercise power by controlling territory or maintaining a large standing navy. They exercise power by being present, with something to offer and something to withdraw, at every significant port in their reach.

An outpost is a small, permanent, legally recognized Selemi presence: a building, staff, established relationships with local authorities, an established role in local commerce. It handles Selemi ships, stores Selemi goods, and provides the introductions, favorable berths, and current commercial information that make Selemi merchants more efficient than anyone else operating in that port. What the outpost provides, it can adjust. When Selemis wants to apply pressure to a coastal city, it does not send a navy. The ships are delayed rather than expedited. Introductions are not made. Information about cargo availability becomes slightly less current. This pressure is deniable, incremental, and effective. Most coastal cities with Selemi outposts have learned what it feels like and adjusted their policies accordingly.

The network also means that the Selemi know, in near-real-time, what is happening at every port in their reach. This is not what the outposts are for. It is an inherent consequence of having well-placed, attentive people everywhere. The Selemi do not advertise this capability. They use it.

Outpost locations are chosen by strategic necessity and historical opportunity: some exist because that port controls a critical sea lane, others because a merchant a century ago established a relationship that proved durable. The network has gaps, and maintaining it requires continuous investment — Selemi diplomacy is largely occupied with keeping outpost rights in place when local politics change, and with establishing new outpost rights in cities that are becoming important before those cities are certain they want Selemi presence.

## Cultural Character

The Selemi are the most cosmopolitan people on the continent not by accident but by structural necessity. They have been absorbing foreign vocabulary, cooking, music, and religious practices for long enough that the question of what is originally Selemi culture is genuinely difficult to answer. The food in Selemis is, by the consensus of visitors who have eaten extensively elsewhere, the most interesting on the continent — not elaborate or precious, but combining techniques and ingredients from a dozen culinary traditions in combinations that should not work and do. The music is similar. The architecture of the city shows four or five stylistic traditions coexisting on the same street, accumulated across centuries of Selemi commercial reach.

What is genuinely and distinctly Selemi is the quality of attention they bring to understanding other people. A Selemi in a new port does not merely learn the prices. They learn who sets the prices, why, who benefits from them being set at that level, and who would benefit from them being different. They learn the social geography — which families are rising, which disputes are currently active, which are superficially resolved but still generating pressure. This information is not always immediately useful. The Selemi are patient enough that this does not bother them.

The combination — charming surface, comprehensive attention, long memory — is why other Azhoran merchants find the Selemi pleasant to deal with and difficult to fully trust. Both assessments, the Selemi note when pressed, are correct.

## With Aevis

The Avites and the Selemi are natural antagonists: one culture built around land, hierarchy, and the individual pursuit of martial glory; the other built around sea, lateral commercial networks, and the collective management of leverage. They have been neighbors for long enough that the antagonism has acquired the familiarity of a long argument — still genuine, still capable of producing violence, but structured by centuries of knowing exactly how the other side thinks.

Aevis has taken the island twice. Both times, the Selemi response was the same: the island fell, the outpost network continued to function, and the civilization continued from the sea. Selemi ships did not stop sailing because Aevis held the harbor. Selemi merchants did not close their outposts because an Avite garrison occupied the city. The revenue streams continued. The relationships continued. The empire of presence persisted while the physical island was temporarily someone else's.

This is the essential asymmetry that the Selemi understand about themselves: Aevis can take Selemis's island, but Selemis is not its island. The Avites, both times, eventually understood this as well, after the cost of occupation exceeded what the occupation was producing. The occupation ended. The Selemi returned.

The current period is independent. There is trade across the channel — Avite bronze and Selemi sea-goods, mutually useful enough that neither wants complete disruption — and diplomatic missions of limited warmth. The Selemi outpost in Aevis is managed with the most careful attention to local politics of any outpost in the network, because the local politics are the most capable of turning into an army on short notice. The Ascarth kings know the Selemi outpost is watching them. Both sides find this more useful than the alternative.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Selemi are the people of the island of Selemis, whose geographic position at the Iberos Sea's southern mouth has been converted into a commercial civilization based on distributed presence rather than territorial control.
- The outpost network — permanent legally recognized Selemi presences at every significant port — is the actual source of Selemi power; through outposts they control trade flow, gather information, and apply pressure without navies.
- Selemi culture is the most cosmopolitan on the continent by structural necessity; its food, music, architecture, and manner reflect absorptions from every port they maintain.
- The quality of attention they bring to understanding other people's social geographies — who benefits from what, what disputes are active, what is unresolved — is both a personal trait and a foundational commercial skill.
- Aevis has conquered Selemis twice; both times the outpost network continued functioning and the occupation ended when its cost exceeded its yield. The Selemi have developed a theory from this. The Avites have not fully accepted it.
- Selemis is not its island. This is the most important thing the Selemi know about themselves.
