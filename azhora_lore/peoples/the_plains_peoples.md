# The Plains Peoples

The peoples who have inhabited the broad central grasslands of Azhora since before the historical record are not, in any of their traditions, people who wander. They are people who move — precisely, seasonally, along routes as established as any Mittoli road, organized by a body of customary law that is more sophisticated than most outside observers recognize because it is oral rather than written and because the observers have generally spent less time learning it than the Plains peoples have spent developing it. The distinction between wandering and structured movement is one that the Plains peoples find it necessary to explain with some regularity, and they have become patient about it without becoming enthusiastic.

In the Azhoran linguistic record, they are called the Grassic peoples, a name derived from the Mittoli word for their grass. They call their language *Auwel*, which means something between "land-speech" and "the speaking that land does through people." The Mittoli name is geographic and reductive. The Plains name is theological and precise. The gap between the two names is a fair description of the distance between outside perception and inside reality.

---

## The Routes and the Law

The herding routes of the Plains are not roads, in the sense of constructed permanent infrastructure. They are relationships: between communities and the water sources, between seasonal pasture and the herds that have grazed them for generations, between past and present use that is tracked in oral tradition with the same exactitude that a Caeras valley city-state tracks land records in its archive. A route is maintained by use, validated by Memory Grass, contested by the same legal mechanism that has arbitrated route disputes for hundreds of years, and transferred between communities by processes that require the kind of formal spoken procedure that the Plains peoples call law and that outside observers, never having seen a written document involved, have sometimes called custom as if that were a lesser thing.

The central wealth animal is the plains tallhorn — the great buffalo-cattle described in the livestock records — whose herds measure route rights, compensation claims, marriage alliances, and the memory of water sources. A community's standing in the route system is expressed in part through how many tallhorns it can move through a dry season without needing water from outside its acknowledged territory. The number of tallhorns does not itself constitute the right; the right is established in oral tradition and ratified by Memory Grass sites. The tallhorns are evidence of having lived correctly within that right for long enough to have accumulated them.

## Memory Grass

Every Plains community maintains detailed oral knowledge of the Memory Grass sites in its territory — the sites where the grass itself has incorporated the chemical signatures of past events, including battles, mass deaths, and periods of intensive use, into its root tissue. These sites are the fixed reference system for Plains oral law: a community's route claim is not merely spoken but corroborated by which Memory Grass sites occur along it, what those sites are said to retain, and how those retentions correspond to the events that established the claim.

Three centuries of Caeras Valley Academy analysis has confirmed that these sites are chemically distinct from surrounding grassland and that the behavioral responses of animals at active sites — nervousness in horses, circling in dogs — are consistent with long-lived volatile stress compounds being released over decades. What the analysis has not explained is why the oral tradition's descriptions of specific events at specific sites, when checked against other historical records, are frequently more detailed and accurate than simple stress chemistry should produce. The Plains peoples say the Academy has confirmed part of the mechanism. They do not elaborate on the rest.

The sites are treated as sacred in the functional sense — not objects of worship but places where something real is present that requires acknowledgment. Disputes that cannot be resolved through ordinary oral legal procedure are sometimes brought to a relevant Memory Grass site for a particular form of determination that outside observers have variously described as arbitration, oath-swearing, and divination, and that Plains communities describe as asking what happened.

## Language and Identity

The Grassic language family is, by every comparative method available to Azhoran linguistics, unrelated to any other language family on the continent. The Mittoli and Pyrosi families share a common ancestor. The Moreshi family is its own lineage. The Bouéni language is the most distant relative of Mittoli. The Grassic family has no demonstrable relationship to any of these.

The implication — that the Plains peoples were present before all of them, speaking a language that predates the arrival of every other family on the continent — has been the central uncomfortable question of Azhoran comparative linguistics for three centuries. The Academy states that the evidence is "consistent with multiple interpretations." Plains elders receive this assessment with the politeness reserved for technically accurate statements that say less than they appear to.

The *Auwel* language is described in detail in its own file. Its most striking feature — the evidential system that grammatically encodes how a speaker knows what they claim to know — reflects the culture's deep orientation toward the difference between direct observation, transmitted knowledge, and logical inference. A Plains person does not say "the herd passed here." They say something that grammatically encodes whether they saw it, someone trustworthy told them, the grass told them, or they reason it must be true. This is not pedantry. In an oral legal system that depends on testimony, the difference between these categories is the entire legal question.

## Among Other Peoples

The Plains peoples are simultaneously the most central geographically and the most peripheral politically of the major Azhoran cultures. The great east-west trade routes cross their territory; the middle sections of the Plains have been controlled, taxed, fought over, and re-administered by every surrounding power at various points in the historical record. The Plains communities in the central transit zone have absorbed all of this into a pragmatic composite: they follow the customary law that everyone actually lives by, use whatever commercial legal form the currently dominant power prefers for written records, and maintain the route system through and around whatever administrative structure currently claims to govern the zone.

The relationship between the Plains peoples and the surrounding agricultural and urban cultures has always been asymmetric in a particular direction: the surrounding cultures see a pastoral people who are resistant to taxation and settlement, while the Plains peoples see a series of recent arrivals who have established themselves on a continent that was already organized and who have never fully understood that. This perspective is rarely aired directly in mixed company. It shapes every interaction.

The All-Witness — the Plains peoples' name for the unified divine principle recognized across all Azhoran traditions — is not a being in the way that Mittoli or Pyrosi theology understands the term. It is more precise than that, and more demanding: the fact that nothing occurs outside relation. Relation is not love, or approval, or protection. It is structure. What happens is witnessed, and the witness is not neutral. The Memory Grass sites are understood as places where this witnessing has become locally stable and readable. Plains theology and Plains law are not easily separated.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Plains peoples are Azhora's oldest documented population, speaking a language family with no demonstrable relationship to any other on the continent.
- They are organized mobile pastoral cultures, not wanderers — their routes, water rights, and grazing territory are precisely governed by oral customary law as sophisticated as any written legal system.
- Memory Grass sites are the physical archive of their legal tradition: chemically distinct grassland that retains events in its root tissue, used as corroboration in route and territory disputes.
- The tallhorn herd is the measure of social standing, wealth, and route right, but the standing itself is established in oral tradition, not in the herd count.
- The *Auwel* language encodes how speakers know what they claim to know, which is both linguistic philosophy and functional legal precision in an oral society.
- The surrounding cultures have consistently underestimated the sophistication of Plains social organization because it is oral rather than written and pastoral rather than agricultural; the Plains peoples have found this underestimation useful and have not worked especially hard to correct it.
