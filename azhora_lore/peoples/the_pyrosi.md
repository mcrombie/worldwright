# The Pyrosi

The Pyrosi are two peoples who share a name and a word for fire and very little else. West Pyrosi and East Pyrosi have been neighbors since before either had formal names for what divided them. The ridge that runs between their territories is not particularly dramatic — other Azhoran ranges dwarf it — but it functions as a cultural border with the authority of a mountain wall, because the difference between the two sides is not made by the ridge itself but by the rain the ridge stops. One side is green and volcanic and deep-rooted. The other is tawny and open and thoroughly aware of who is passing through. They are related peoples who have become different ones by living in different worlds.

Both are old. Both carry a religious memory of fire that neither can fully explain and that neither treats as finished.

---

## West Pyrosi

The people of West Pyros are the settled, terraced, intensely local culture of volcanic hillsides that have been worked for so long the terraces feel like a geological feature rather than a human one. They are compact in build, dark-haired, and speak a dialect that mixes Mittoli grammar with Pyrosi vocabulary in proportions that reflect how long ago and how completely the two cultural streams merged in this specific valley. They are known for two things that appear unrelated and are not: their food and their capacity for institutional survival.

The food comes from the soil. Volcanic ash deposits over centuries have made the hillside terraces produce fruit and vegetables of unusual sweetness and concentration — flavor that travelers from the flat interior consistently remark on and that the West Pyrosi find unremarkable because they have never eaten anything else. The markets of Gala handle a reliable volume of Pyrosi produce, and this is a fact with political implications: West Pyros has value that makes it worth controlling and cultural coherence that makes it impossible to assimilate.

The cultural coherence is the second thing. West Pyros has been invaded, occupied, and administered by outside powers more times than any other region of comparable size in Azhoran history, and every occupation has ended the same way: the Pyrosi governing their own affairs quietly underneath whatever external administration applied, preserving institutions in forms that were deniable or invisible to the occupier, and resuming full self-governance the moment pressure released. Scholars call this the Pyrosi Method. The West Pyrosi do not have a name for it. It is not a strategy; it is a habit. You manage what you can, you do not expose what you value, and you outlast what you cannot fight. The volcanic soil is still there after every army has gone. The terraces are still there. The recipes are still there.

## East Pyrosi

The people of East Pyros are a different proposition entirely. In the rain shadow of the ridge, in a landscape of pale-grassed hills and scattered drought-adapted trees and caravan roads that cross everything, the culture that developed was not about depth and endurance but about range and adaptability. East Pyros is a transit zone, and its people have developed the character of transit zones everywhere: cosmopolitan, pragmatic, comfortable with a degree of ethical flexibility that the more rooted cultures around them find useful to employ and occasionally alarming to contemplate.

You can hear five languages in an East Pyrosi market town without going out of your way. You can buy goods from three distinct trade systems at the same fair. The East Pyrosi merchant who speaks Mittoli with one client, Moreshi trade vocabulary with the next, and switches to a Pyrosi regional dialect for their suppliers has not abandoned who they are. They have learned to be useful in more contexts than a single cultural identity provides. This is a skill, and the East Pyrosi are aware that it is a skill and have organized their economy around it.

The Taler dialect of the northern Talermolis uplands is the striking exception — a conservative upland community that has not participated in the transit culture's velocity and has, by staying still, preserved forms of Pyrosi that linguists use to reconstruct what the language was before the Compact era. Taler speakers can occasionally read inscriptions that no other living person can interpret, which is either a cultural treasure or a persistent imposition depending on how many historians have come looking for them.

## The Fire Memory

Both Pyros regions share a religious tradition — or more precisely, a family of related traditions that resist being consolidated into a single account — called the Fire Memory. It holds that the volcanic land was once awake in a way it is not now; that the fires beneath it were not merely geological but intentional; and that something happened a very long time ago that put those fires to sleep.

In West Pyros, the Fire Memory is agricultural and ritual. The fumaroles and hot springs are treated as places where the interior of the world is near the surface — liminal points, associated with asking permission, leaving offerings, and speaking words not meant for other people to hear. Before planting, certain fields receive acknowledgment. Not prayer, exactly — the West Pyrosi would not all use that word — but a recognition that the soil gives what it gives for reasons that are not entirely the farmer's.

In East Pyros, the Fire Memory has been absorbed into the commercial culture and become largely metaphorical. "The fire beneath the deal" is a common East Pyrosi expression for long-term consequences that will surface eventually. The fumarole sites at the Talermolis highland are the only places where the East Pyrosi treat the tradition in anything like its West Pyrosi form, and even there the ceremony has a quality of preservation — this is how it was done, therefore this is how it is done — rather than West Pyros's sense of ongoing relationship with something still present.

This difference is noted by both sides without producing obvious conflict. The West Pyrosi consider the East Pyrosi to have lost something. The East Pyrosi consider the West Pyrosi to have stopped moving.

## Among Other Peoples

The Pyrosi have been positioned between Mittolo and the desert for long enough that both their neighbors have strong opinions about them and the Pyrosi have developed a fine sense of how to maintain useful relationships with powers they cannot match militarily.

Mittoli powers have repeatedly miscalculated the cost of occupying West Pyros. Each occupation has confirmed the Pyrosi Method. The Mittoli academic tradition has studied this more carefully than the Mittoli political tradition has learned from it.

The Moreshi trade routes through East Pyros have made East Pyrosi merchants comfortable with desert-culture commercial customs to a degree that allows them to function as brokers between the two worlds. This is a valuable position. The East Pyrosi have not wasted it.

The Pyrosi language family — related to Mittoli through the common ancestor called Proto-Western, diverged far enough that the relationship is not immediately audible — is described in detail in its own file. The formal theology of the Pyrosi tradition, where it has one, relates the fire beneath the land to what other Azhoran traditions call the unified divine principle: *fire before waking*, in Taler language — not a god but the condition that allows gods, land, and people to become active. This formulation is precise enough that several outside theologians have found it useful and several West Pyrosi ritual practitioners have found those theologians' interest beside the point.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Pyrosi are two culturally distinct peoples — West and East — united by a shared language family, a shared word for fire, and the Fire Memory religious tradition, divided by the ridge that creates the rain shadow between them.
- West Pyrosi are a deeply settled, volcanic-soil agricultural people known for their food and their systematic institutional survival under repeated occupation — the "Pyrosi Method."
- East Pyrosi are a transit-zone people: adaptable, multilingual, commercially sophisticated, lighter in cultural rootedness and more flexible in ethical commitments.
- The Taler dialect of the Talermolis uplands preserves the oldest surviving Pyrosi linguistic forms.
- The Fire Memory holds that the volcanic land was once intentionally awake, and treats the fumaroles and hot springs as liminal sites where the interior of the world remains accessible.
- Both Pyros regions have historically served as buffers and brokers between the Mittoli heartland and the desert world to the east.
