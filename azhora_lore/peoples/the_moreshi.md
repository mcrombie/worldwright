# The Moreshi

The Moreshi are the peoples of the Moroshé Desert — not one people in any simple sense, but several related cultures speaking related languages, organized differently by the terrain they occupy and the water they have learned to find. What unites them is the desert itself: the same deep patience with time, the same practical sophistication about water and route and season, the same relationship to an ancient land that has been hostile to outsiders since before the oldest Mittoli text and that the Moreshi navigate with the ease of long familiarity.

They chose "home" as the word for the desert. The Mittoli who named it chose "the wide nothing." This difference is informative.

---

## Who the Moreshi Are

The desert supports several distinct populations with different ecological relationships to the land.

The **settled communities** cluster around oases and in the canyon country, where the depth of the water table, the shelter of the canyon walls, and in some places the persistent shade of narrow red-rock passages allow for continuous occupation. The oasis settlements are the commercial centers of the desert world — places where caravan routes converge, where goods from the Mediterranean coast and the continental interior are exchanged for desert products, and where the long patience that the desert requires is visible in the built form of the city: thick walls, interior courtyards, cisterns, and the shade-calculation in every structural choice.

The **nomadic and semi-nomadic herding cultures** move along routes that appear on the oldest maps as established roads, though no one built them. Their most important animal is the Moroshé dustback — the desert-edge buffalo-cattle whose ability to convert thorn scrub, dry-basin grass, and minimal water into milk, calves, hide, and draft power makes them the foundation of desert pastoral wealth. An oasis household may hold coin in locked jars; its standing is measured by how many dustbacks it can move through a dry season without begging water elsewhere.

The **canyon peoples** inhabit the deep rock-cut country of the southern Moroshé, where ancient rivers once ran and the canyon walls preserve petroglyphs so old that no one has translated them and some predating any known Azhoran civilization by thousands of years. The canyon communities are the most conservative of the desert peoples in their practices and the most guarded in their relationship to outside scholars.

The Mediterranean coastal peoples — who live in the warm strip between the desert and the Iberos Sea — are related to the desert Moreshi in language and deep culture but have been shaped by centuries of coastal trade and agricultural abundance into something considerably different. They are sometimes called a separate people by both sides; they are sometimes considered Moreshi who got lucky with geography.

## Language

The Moreshi language family is unrelated to Mittoli, Pyrosi, or any other documented Azhoran family. It represents a completely separate linguistic lineage, and comparative linguists place its divergence from the continental language picture as predating even the oldest reconstructable stages of Proto-Western — which implies the Moreshi languages have been developing in isolation from the western families for a very long time.

The deep desert languages are the most conservative of the family: isolated communities in harsh terrain generate less linguistic contact and change more slowly. The canyon languages have absorbed some Iberos coastal vocabulary through trade. The coastal trade dialect has borrowed heavily from East Pyrosi and, through the Iberos connection, from the sea-languages of the eastern shore.

There is a register in the oldest Moreshi traditions — used only in specific ritual contexts by practitioners who inherit the training — called the Qadimuur. It uses the standard Moreshi sound system but contains syntactic structures that match no known Moreshi language. Linguists who have analyzed the Qadimuur describe it as either a separate language family entirely or an extremely early preserved form of proto-Moreshi, and cannot distinguish between these possibilities with available data. The canyon peoples do not discuss it in terms that help resolve the question.

## The Ruins

The deep interior of the Moroshé contains ruins of a civilization so ancient that the current desert is, in the geological record, a relatively recent development — the land was wetter when these structures were built, and something changed. What the ruins represent, who built them, and what happened are questions that the Academy has been investigating in the desert's more accessible zones for over a century without reaching conclusions that satisfy anyone. The scale of construction suggests access to organized labor and resources that the current desert cannot support. The ruins are not described in any Moreshi oral tradition as ancestral.

The Moreshi relationship to the ruins is neither reverence nor indifference. The desert peoples do not live in them, do not remove materials from them, and do not build on them. The short explanation given to outside researchers — "those places belong to something else" — is not coy. It is a territorial claim being made on behalf of a party that cannot speak for itself. The details of that claim are not shared.

## Character and Values

The desert requires of its inhabitants a relationship with time that is genuinely different from what the agricultural and maritime cultures of the continent understand. A Mittoli farmer works a flood calendar; seasons come and the river answers. A Moreshi herder works with timescales where a water source may be reliable for a decade and then shift, where a drought can run five years and a good year cannot be assumed. The patience this produces is not resignation. It is the specific skill of a people who have learned to distinguish what can be hurried from what cannot, and who do not confuse the two.

Desert hospitality — the obligation to provide water, food, and shelter to a traveler regardless of relationship or prior enmity — is not generosity in the Mittoli sense. It is a mutual survival protocol that the desert requires. A refusal to provide water to a traveler who needs it is an act of a specific severity that the word "inhospitable" does not adequately convey. This protocol extends even to enemies, even to strangers, even to people who have wronged the host. The desert is not interested in your social categories when it comes to water.

The caravan trade that connects the desert world to the eastern coast and the Iberos Sea is the primary commercial institution of the Moreshi. The **Kingdom of Marosh**, the dominant desert-road polity on the Dinova peninsula, exemplifies this: a traditional caravan-kingdom whose authority derives from controlling the routes rather than the land, and whose commercial and military power depends on the route network remaining productive. Maroshi culture is distinctly aristocratic in its organization — the merchant houses that control the major route segments have family standing that the oasis communities recognize as a form of nobility — and distinctly commercial in its values, in the way of aristocracies built on trade rather than land.

## Religion

The Moreshi unified divine principle is the **First Vastness** — a reality too wide to approach except through local presences. This framing differs from the Mittoli *Azh-Cael* (an enduring flow) and the Grassic All-Witness (a relational fact) in its emphasis on scale over character: the Moreshi principle is enormous, not warm. Approaching it directly is not an option the tradition encourages. What the tradition encourages is approaching it through specific local forms — the spirit of a particular spring, the guardian of a canyon passage, the powers associated with specific stars that navigate the desert routes.

The desert peoples' astronomical knowledge is extensive and specifically applied to navigation, timing of seasonal movements, and the prediction of weather patterns that cross the sand sea. Several of the fixed star names used across Azhora in navigational contexts are Moreshi in origin, borrowed by coastal traders who found the terminology more precise than anything the maritime tradition had developed for the same stars.

## Summary of Key Points

- The Moreshi are the peoples of the Moroshé Desert — several related cultures (settled oasis, nomadic pastoral, canyon, Mediterranean coastal) sharing a language family and a deep-time relationship with the desert environment.
- Their language family is unrelated to any other Azhoran family, predating even the oldest reconstructable stage of Proto-Western — implying the longest continuous linguistic lineage on the continent.
- The Qadimuur ritual register of the canyon peoples may represent a preserved form of proto-Moreshi or a wholly separate language family; its practitioners do not discuss its origins.
- The ancient ruins of the deep Moroshé predate the current desert and represent a civilization the Moreshi do not claim as ancestral; they maintain that the sites "belong to something else."
- Desert hospitality — water and shelter for any traveler — is a mutual survival protocol, not charity; its refusal carries a severity that the word "inhospitable" does not cover.
- The Maroshi Kingdom of Dinova is the dominant political expression of the caravan-road desert culture: an aristocracy built on route control rather than land ownership.
